## Summary

This paper characterizes the MET copy number alteration (CNA) landscape across 50,748 MSK-IMPACT-profiled solid tumors and develops an optimized bioinformatic approach (read-depth + FACETS allele-specific copy number + focality analysis <25 Mb) that achieves 91% concordance, 97% sensitivity, and 89% specificity versus FISH as the gold standard. Among 408 cases with MET CNA/amplification, 88% (n=358) had true MET amplification (copy number ≥6). MET amplification was most frequent in papillary RCC (8.4%), high-grade glioma (5.3%), and GEJ adenocarcinoma (5.0%). In NSCLC, 40% of MET amplifications were acquired resistance events to EGFR TKIs. Clinical response to c-MET inhibitors was best in concurrent MET ex14 + MET amplification (ORR 67%), with attenuated responses in isolated de novo MET amplification. Data are publicly available at cBioPortal study `mixed_impact_subset_2022`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Bioinformatic approach (read-depth + FACETS + focality) achieves 91% concordance with FISH for MET amplification

**Finding:** Adding FACETS allele-specific copy number and a focal amplification criterion (<25 Mb) to standard read-depth analysis improved MET amplification calling from 81% to 91% concordance, 97% sensitivity, and 89% specificity versus FISH.

**Detail:** Standard read-depth alone: 81% concordance, 81% sensitivity, 82% specificity. FACETS alone: 86% concordance, 100% sensitivity, 74% specificity (false-positive polysomy calls). Read-depth + FACETS + focality (<25 Mb): 91% concordance, 97% sensitivity, 89% specificity. Six discordant cases at best performance: 5 were NGS+/FISH− (likely FACETS overcalling in fragmented genomes), 1 was NGS−/FISH+ (broad amplification exceeding 25 Mb focality cutoff). Minimum tumor content required: 20% for reliable copy number reporting.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MET`, pan-solid tumor (validation cohort: NSCLC 59%, GI 13%, RCC 7%, glioblastoma 7%)

**Data availability:** `available`
> Validation cohort data publicly available at `mixed_impact_subset_2022` on cBioPortal.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> FACETS and focality analysis require custom bioinformatic pipelines; MSK-IMPACT copy number outputs accessible in cBioPortal; standard FISH comparison requires external data.

### F2: MET amplification frequency and landscape across >50,000 solid tumors across 23 cancer types

**Finding:** Of 50,748 MSK-IMPACT-profiled patients, 408 (0.8%) had MET CNA/amplification. MET amplification was most frequent in papillary RCC (8.4%), high-grade glioma (5.3%), and GEJ adenocarcinoma (5.0%), and was enriched in pleomorphic/sarcomatoid NSCLC (8.5% vs 1.9% overall NSCLC).

**Detail:** 88% of 408 cases had true MET amplification (integer copy number ≥6 by FACETS); 12% were classified as MET gains (copy number 3–5). Total MET copy number inversely correlated with amplified segment size (Spearman rho=−0.5, p<0.0001): high-level amplifications are more focal, while low-level amplifications span broader chromosomal regions. Gastric adenocarcinomas had the highest copy number and most focal MET amplification among all cancer types.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MET` · papillary RCC, high-grade glioma (glioblastoma/anaplastic astrocytoma), GEJ/gastric adenocarcinoma, NSCLC (all histologies), pan-solid tumor

**Data availability:** `available`
> MET copy number and amplification data in `mixed_impact_subset_2022`; landscape data queryable in MSK-IMPACT studies on cBioPortal.

**Tool support:** `native`
> CNA frequency by cancer type viewable natively in cBioPortal Cancer Summary and OncoPrint; focality information requires custom analysis.

### F3: In NSCLC, 40% of MET amplifications are acquired TKI resistance events, predominantly EGFR-driven

**Finding:** In 140 NSCLC cases with MET amplification, 56 (40%) were acquired resistance events to non-MET targeted therapy (89% EGFR-mutant), with de novo isolated MET amplification (group 1) accounting for 36% and MET amplification concurrent with MET ex14 skipping (group 2) accounting for 11%.

**Detail:** NSCLC classification: Group 1 (de novo isolated): n=51 (36%); Group 2 (concurrent MET ex14/fusion): n=16 (11%); Group 3 (de novo + non-MET driver): n=17 (12%); Group 4 (acquired resistance, mostly EGFR): n=56 (40%). In group 4, concurrent drivers: EGFR (n=50/56, 89%), ALK (5%), RET (4%), KRAS G12C (2%). Groups 1, 2, and 4 showed significantly more focal MET amplification than group 3 (p=0.002, p=0.03, and p=0.002 respectively), consistent with a functional driver role requiring high focality.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MET`, `EGFR`, `ALK`, `RET`, `KRAS` · Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)

**Data availability:** `available`
> MET amplification and co-occurring driver mutations in `mixed_impact_subset_2022` and MSK-IMPACT NSCLC studies.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Co-mutation analysis (MET + EGFR, ALK, etc.) visualizable in OncoPrint; clinical group assignment requires custom attribute annotation.

### F4: Best clinical responses to c-MET inhibitors in concurrent MET amplification + MET ex14 (ORR 67%); attenuated in isolated amplification (25%)

**Finding:** Among 45 patients treated with c-MET inhibitors, ORR was 67% (95% CI 39–86%) for concurrent MET ex14 + MET amplification (group 2, n=12 evaluable), 44% for acquired MET amplification after EGFR TKI (group 4, n=18), 25% for de novo isolated MET amplification (group 1, n=4), and 25% for MET amplification with non-MET driver (group 3, n=4).

**Detail:** Response by copy number: ORR 29% (<6 copies), 53% (6–9 copies), 50% (≥10 copies). Median TTD: group 1: 7.8 months, group 2: 8.3 months, group 3: 4.3 months, group 4: 6.1 months. Primary progression rate was low across all groups (5%). One patient with broad (non-focal) MET amplification + MET ex14 achieved a partial response. All but one patient with evaluable clinical responses met the focality criterion for MET amplification.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MET` (amplification), `MET` (exon 14 skipping), `EGFR` · NSCLC (primary), RCC, gastric, cholangiocarcinoma (5 patients total)

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Treatment and response data for 45 patients available in `mixed_impact_subset_2022`; some clinical outcome data available upon request to authors.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Response data downloadable from `mixed_impact_subset_2022`; treatment and outcome analysis requires custom analysis scripts.

### F5: FISH has significant technical limitations for MET amplification assessment, especially in heterogeneous tumors

**Finding:** FISH signal clustering, overlapping, and tumor heterogeneity frequently led to over- or underestimation of MET copy number; NGS (with FACETS + focality) is superior for assessing overall amplification status in heterogeneous tumors and for determining amplification focality.

**Detail:** FISH limitations included: (1) tight clustering of MET signals per cell precluded counting in high-amplification samples; (2) high intratumoral heterogeneity in signal distribution led to enrichment bias when selecting areas for counting (especially in TKI-resistant cases with acquired MET amplification); (3) FISH uses CEP7 as the single denominator, providing no arm-level or chromosomal-level context. NGS/FACETS concordance with FISH for quantitative copy number: R²=0.66 (acceptable but imperfect). Minimum tumor content required for reliable FACETS calling: 20%.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MET` · All solid tumor types (primarily NSCLC, GI, RCC, glioblastoma in validation cohort)

**Data availability:** `available`
> FISH validation cohort (n=70) data available in `mixed_impact_subset_2022`.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> FACETS focality analysis requires custom bioinformatic pipeline; FISH data not queryable in cBioPortal natively.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Does the high ORR (67%) in concurrent MET ex14 + MET amplification (group 2) represent a truly synergistic oncogenic co-dependency, or is the ex14 alteration the sole driver with amplification being an amplifier of the signal?
- Can the focality threshold (<25 Mb) and FACETS copy number ≥6 definition be prospectively validated as companion diagnostic criteria for c-MET inhibitor trials across cancer types beyond NSCLC?
- What is the clinical significance of the 12% "MET gain" (copy number 3–5) cases—are these functionally distinct from polysomy, and do they benefit from c-MET inhibition?
- As liquid biopsy (cfDNA) becomes standard, can MET amplification focality be reliably assessed from plasma?
- Are non-focal (broad, chromosome 7 aneuploid) MET gains ever clinically actionable, or is focality always required for targeted therapy benefit?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Clinical response cohort is small (n=45) with heterogeneous treatment regimens; ORR confidence intervals are wide, especially for smaller groups
- Single-center (MSK) FISH validation cohort (n=70); institutional FISH criteria may differ from other laboratories
- Treatment cohort includes many combination c-MET + EGFR TKI regimens, confounding single-agent MET inhibitor response interpretation
- FACETS tumor purity estimation unreliable below 20% tumor content; may miss amplification in very low-purity samples
- FISH may overcall amplification in heterogeneous tumors (selection bias toward amplified regions); NGS overcalls in fragmented, complex genomic backgrounds
- Retrospective design; prospective validation of the NGS-based MET amplification calling algorithm is needed
