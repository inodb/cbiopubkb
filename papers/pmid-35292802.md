## Summary

MAPPYACTS (NCT02613962) is a prospective international precision medicine trial enrolling 787 pediatric and young adult patients with recurrent/refractory malignancies across 18 centers in France, Italy, Ireland, and Spain (February 2016–July 2020). On-purpose tumor biopsies were sequenced with WES and RNA-seq; cfDNA was analyzed as an ancillary study. Actionable alterations were identified in 69% of patients with successful sequencing. Only 10% of alterations were classified as "ready for routine use" (ALK, NTRK1/2/3, BRAF, ROS1, RET fusions; BRAF, NF1, IDH1 mutations; PTCH1 alterations). Of 356 patients with ≥12 months follow-up, 30% received matched targeted therapy — 56% within phase I/II trials (mainly AcSé-ESMART). Overall ORR was 17%; for patients with "ready for routine use" alterations, ORR was 38% vs 14% for investigational-level alterations. In extracerebral tumors, 76% of actionable SNVs detected in tumor were also found in cfDNA, establishing liquid biopsy feasibility in this population.

## Key Findings

### F1: 69% of pediatric recurrent tumors harbor actionable alterations; only 10% are "ready for routine use"

**Finding:** Molecular profiling identified ≥1 "potentially actionable" alteration in 436/632 (69%) patients with successful sequencing. Of 1,144 actionable findings, 533 were SNVs (484 somatic, 49 germline), 527 CNAs (212 amplifications, 315 deletions), 59 targetable fusions, and 25 elevated tumor mutational burden. Using an adapted ESCAT framework, 44 patients (10% of those with actionable alterations) had "ready for routine use" alterations. The most frequent "investigational" targets were: TP53 (n=215), CDKN2A/B (n=157), MYCN/MYC (n=28), NF1 (n=21), KRAS/NRAS/HRAS (n=27), FGFR1/2/3/4 (n=16).

**Detail:** "Ready for routine use" alterations included fusions involving *ALK* (n=10), *BRAF* (n=7), *NTRK1/2/3* (n=6), *ROS1* (n=2), *COL1A1/PDGFB* (n=1), *RET* (n=1); and mutations in *BRAF* (n=4), *NF1* (n=2), *IDH1* (n=1), or deletion/mutation in *PTCH1* (n=4). Critically, 42% (19/44) of these high-evidence alterations were previously unknown in the patient's diagnostic workup — underscoring the value of comprehensive molecular profiling over targeted panel tests. Treatment recommendations involved WEE1 (n=150), mTOR (n=123), CDK4/6 (n=105), MEK (n=95), PARP (n=64), BET (n=59), EZH2 (n=38), FGFR (n=31), and PD-1/PD-L1 (n=31) inhibitors.

### F2: Matched targeted therapy achieves 17% ORR overall; 38% ORR in "ready for routine use" alterations vs 14% in investigational

**Finding:** Of 107 patients receiving matched targeted therapy, 109 had evaluable disease: 18 achieved partial response (ORR 17%, 95% CI 10–25%) and 27 stable disease (disease control rate 41%). For "ready for routine use" alterations (single-agent targeted therapy): ORR was 38% (5/13, 95% CI 18–65%). For investigational-level alterations: ORR 14% (12/86, 95% CI 7–23%). Including patients who received the same alteration-matched treatment prior to MAPPYACTS inclusion (n=12 evaluable), global ORR for "ready for routine use" was 56% (95% CI 37–73%).

**Detail:** The 38% ORR for targeted-to-alteration therapy compares favorably with the ~4% ORR in pediatric phase I/II chemotherapy trials and is comparable to or better than the emerging targeted therapy era in pediatrics. Single-agent matched targeted therapy achieved 13% ORR, combination with chemotherapy 18%, and targeted combination 0% — though sample sizes were small. Median treatment duration for controlled disease was 129 days (range 58–697). The MAPPYACTS results informed the parallel AcSé-ESMART platform trial, which enrolled 78% of MAPPYACTS-referred patients. The large gap between "ready for use" (38%) and "investigational" (14%) ORR confirms that evidence-based actionability tier directly predicts clinical benefit and should guide treatment prioritization.

### F3: cfDNA detects 76% of actionable tumor SNVs in pediatric extracerebral solid tumors; neuroblastoma shows highest cfDNA yield

**Finding:** In 225 patients with extracerebral solid tumors with cfDNA analysis, 128/190 (67%) had successful cfDNA WES. Among 94 potentially actionable SNVs detected in tumor, 71 (76%) were also identified in cfDNA. Among 14 patients with cfDNA-exclusive actionable SNVs, 35 additional somatic alterations (median 1, range 1–13 per patient) were found in plasma that were absent from tumor WES — including an ALK p.Arg1275Gln mutation detected only in plasma. Neuroblastoma had the highest cfDNA quantity and ctDNA fraction (median 35% ctDNA, range 0–95%) compared to other tumor types (median 16%, P=1.4e-8).

**Detail:** cfDNA analysis was more frequently successful in patients with metastatic vs localized disease (66% vs 47%, P=0.04), consistent with higher tumor shedding in advanced/metastatic settings. For patients where tumor WES was not done or failed (n=35), cfDNA WES succeeded in 11 (34%), identifying actionable SNVs in 5. These results establish liquid biopsy as a feasible and complementary profiling approach in pediatric solid tumors, consistent with findings from adult cancer (see pmid-37769223 for HCC). CNS tumors were excluded from the cfDNA analysis as blood-brain barrier limits cfDNA shedding from brain primaries, highlighting a key limitation of liquid biopsy in pediatric oncology given CNS tumors comprising 28% of the cohort.

### F4: Tumor mutational burden is low in most pediatric cancers; hypermutation rare but associated with constitutional MMR deficiency

**Finding:** TMB was retrospectively calculated for 451 patients with solid malignancies. Median TMB was 0.6 mut/Mb (range 0–195.3). Only 7 patients (1.6%) had TMB >10 mut/Mb: 3 high-grade glioma (including 1 ultrahypermutator >100 mut/Mb), 3 neuroblastoma, 1 medulloblastoma. Constitutional mismatch repair deficiency (cMMRD) involving MSH2, MLH1, or MSH6 was identified in 3 of these cases — previously unknown. Five tumors showed microsatellite instability.

**Detail:** The low overall TMB in pediatric cancers contrasts sharply with adult cancers (where TMB-high tumors may respond to PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy). This confirms that immune checkpoint inhibition is unlikely to benefit most pediatric patients based on TMB alone. The identification of 3 unrecognized cMMRD cases through comprehensive molecular profiling demonstrates a direct clinical impact beyond treatment selection: germline counseling for Lynch syndrome-related conditions. The 25 cases of elevated tumor mutational load identified as potentially actionable (per the 1,144 total actionable findings) likely represent these hypermutator cases and tumors with MSI.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MSH2`, `MLH1`, `MSH6` (cMMRD); pan-pediatric · High-grade glioma, neuroblastoma, medulloblastoma

### F5: Molecular profiling led to diagnosis revision in 1.9% of cases; germline findings warranted genetic counseling in 7.6%

**Finding:** RNA-seq and integrated profiling led to revision of the initial diagnosis in 12/624 patients (1.9%), mainly through detection of gene fusions in CNS tumors and sarcomas, or identification of a secondary cancer diagnosis. Among 674 patients with WES, 51 (7.6%) had a potentially relevant germline finding warranting genetic counseling. Resistance mutations to specific targeted therapies were identified in 10 patients who had previously progressed on the corresponding agents, providing mechanistic insight into treatment failure.

**Detail:** Diagnosis revisions demonstrate the clinical value of comprehensive molecular profiling beyond treatment selection, particularly for histologically ambiguous pediatric tumors where specific fusions (e.g., EWSR1 in Ewing sarcoma, SS18-SSX in synovial sarcoma) establish diagnoses with prognostic and therapeutic implications. The 7.6% germline finding rate aligns with data from adult precision oncology programs and highlights the need for systematic germline counseling within molecular profiling programs. Detected resistance mutations (e.g., ALK, NTRK, SMO, BRAF/MEK resistance alleles in 9 post-treatment patients) provide a clinical rationale for profiling at treatment failure to guide next-generation agent selection.

## Open Questions

- What proportion of pediatric patients with "investigational"-level alterations benefit from matched therapy when enrolled in appropriately designed basket trials (e.g., AcSé-ESMART)?
- Can cfDNA-based liquid biopsy replace or complement tumor biopsy for molecular profiling in relapsed pediatric solid tumors with high ctDNA shedding (e.g., neuroblastoma)?
- Do genomic alterations in pediatric tumors identified at first recurrence differ systematically from those at later relapses, limiting the predictive value of single-timepoint profiling?
- Should WES + RNA-seq + methylation array be the standard molecular profiling approach at first relapse for all pediatric tumors, or can risk-stratified approaches reduce costs without loss of actionable finding rates?
- How do MAPPYACTS-identified resistance mutations (ALK, NTRK, SMO) inform the design of next-generation inhibitor trials?
- Can TMB thresholds be refined for pediatric tumor types to identify subsets likely to respond to PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy?

## Limitations

- Multicenter trial with heterogeneous tumor biopsy quality and DNA/RNA yield; 9% WES failure rate may enrich for lower-cellularity samples
- "Actionable" and "investigational" classifications reflect 2016–2020 evidence; many targets (e.g., WEE1, EZH2) lack proven pediatric activity
- cfDNA analysis excluded CNS tumors (28% of cohort) due to poor blood-brain barrier shedding — limiting utility for the largest single disease category
- Only 30% of patients with actionable alterations received matched therapy; multiple barriers (patient death, other treatments, drug access) preclude assessment of the full program impact
- Most matched treatments were investigational-level; ORR data for "ready for routine use" tier based on only 13 evaluable patients
- No paired initial-diagnosis vs relapse profiling; actionable alterations at relapse may not represent the truncal clone or founding event
- cfDNA WES success rate was 67%; tumor coverage at low VAF may miss low-frequency subclonal alterations
- The trial was not designed as a randomized controlled study of matched vs unmatched therapy; survival benefit of molecular profiling program not directly assessed
