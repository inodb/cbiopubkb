## Summary

MSK-IMPACT hybridization capture-based NGS analysis of 83 enucleated retinoblastoma specimens assaying 468 genes for somatic alterations, copy number changes, and 88 genes for germline analysis (42 patients consented). *RB1* biallelic inactivation was identified in 97.5% of specimens, with copy-neutral LOH (CN-LOH) disproportionately common as the second-hit mechanism. The most frequent non-*RB1* somatic alteration was *BCOR* (22.9%), which was nominally associated with worse metastasis-free survival. 1q gain and 16q LOH were independently associated with vitreous seeds — the primary reason for enucleation. Five of 42 patients (11%) consented for germline testing harbored non-*RB1* cancer predisposition gene mutations. No two specimens had identical genetic profiles, underscoring tumor individuality. Full data in `rbl_mskcc_2020` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: RB1 biallelic loss in 97.5% of specimens; copy-neutral LOH is the predominant second-hit mechanism

**Finding:** Pathognomonic *RB1* alterations were found in 97.5% (79/81) of patients. Biallelic loss was identified in 79.5% (66/83) of specimens. Among 42 specimens with LOH at the *RB1* locus, 73.8% (31/42) showed copy-neutral LOH (CN-LOH) rather than heterozygous deletion LOH. CN-LOH at *RB1* in retinoblastoma is significantly higher than in other cancers with *RB1* mutations (OR=44.4, P<2.2×10⁻¹⁶).

**Detail:** The predominance of CN-LOH (where two mutant alleles are present but 13q copy number is intact) rather than allele loss may reflect a selection advantage of preserving other genes on 13q essential for retinal cell survival. Of 66 specimens with two hits, 10 had somatic homozygous deletion, 38 had mutation + LOH, 15 had two mutations, and 2 had hypermethylation + LOH. Two specimens had no detectable *RB1* alteration; one had only 6p gain, suggesting an alternative tumorigenesis pathway. Three patients had evidence of three RB1 hits, suggesting intertumoral heterogeneity with multiple clones in the same eye. Mutation spectrum was dispersed — hotspots account for only 40% of mutations.

**Genes / cancer types:** RB1 · Retinoblastoma (pediatric intraocular tumor)

### F2: BCOR mutations (22.9%) are the most common non-RB1 alteration and associate with metastasis risk

**Finding:** *BCOR* somatic mutations were found in 19/83 (22.9%) specimens — higher than previously published series (13%). All *BCOR* mutations were putative loss-of-function. *BCOR* mutations were nominally associated with worse metastasis-free survival (p=0.03) from date of enucleation, though not with high-risk histopathological features (massive choroidal invasion, optic nerve involvement).

**Detail:** *BCOR* (BCL6 corepressor) is located at Xp11.4 and is highly expressed in the lens and retina during development, potentially explaining its enrichment in retinoblastoma. *BCOR* mutations are associated with poor prognosis in multiple malignancies. The association with metastasis but not histopathologic risk features suggests a distinct biologic mechanism of aggressiveness beyond local invasion. The authors propose that *BCOR* mutation detection — either in enucleated specimens or via cell-free DNA (cfDNA) in vitreous or blood — could guide enhanced systemic surveillance. No other non-*RB1* genes occurred at sufficient frequency to assess clinical correlations.

**Genes / cancer types:** BCOR, RPTOR, TERT, MSH3, ARID1A, MYCN, TSC2, CREBBP · Retinoblastoma

### F3: 1q gain and 16q LOH independently predict vitreous seeding

**Finding:** Recurrent arm-level CNAs included 1q gain (67%), 2p gain (32%), 6p gain (59%), 13q LOH (51%), and 16q LOH (43%). Gains on 1q (BH-corrected p=0.008; OR=12.6) and LOH on 16q (BH-corrected p=0.004; OR=26.7) were each independently associated with the presence of vitreous seeds — the primary driver of eye removal.

**Detail:** 1q gain and 16q loss were not significantly correlated with each other (p=0.37), indicating independent contributions. A logistic model including both showed significant association (model p=8.2×10⁻⁸; 1q p=1.3×10⁻⁴; 16q p=2.3×10⁻⁴). Loss of 16q has previously been linked to diffuse vitreous seeding with CDH11 proposed as the candidate gene; however, CDH11 mutations were not identified in this cohort, leaving the mechanism unclear. 6p gain, while recurrent, was not associated with aggressive features in this enucleation cohort, unlike prior reports. Vitreous seeding is clinically critical as it determines eligibility for intra-arterial or intra-vitreous chemotherapy.

**Genes / cancer types:** CDH11 (16q candidate), MYCN (2p24.3), TSC2 (16p13.3) · Retinoblastoma

### F4: 11% of patients carry non-RB1 germline cancer predisposition gene mutations

**Finding:** Of 42 patients consented for germline MSK-IMPACT testing, 5 (11.9%) harbored pathogenic or likely pathogenic germline mutations in non-*RB1* cancer predisposition genes: *NTHL1* (base excision repair), *ERCC3* (nucleotide excision repair), *MSH3* (mismatch repair), *CHEK2* (cell cycle), and *MITF* (transcription factor).

**Detail:** Two patients had heterozygous variants in genes associated with autosomal recessive cancer predisposition syndromes (*NTHL1*, *MSH3*); no MSI was found in the *MSH3* patient. The germline *MITF* p.E419K variant is associated with melanoma predisposition. To date, none of these 5 patients had developed extraocular disease at the time of publication, but these germline variants raise the possibility of increased lifetime risk for additional cancers (Lynch-related cancers for *MSH3*, breast/ovarian for *CHEK2*, melanoma for *MITF*). The prevalence (11%) is consistent with other pediatric cancer cohorts in which 12–17.5% of patients harbor cancer predisposition germline mutations.

### F5: Intertumoral genetic heterogeneity — bilateral tumors and multi-hit RB1 specimens reveal tumor individuality

**Finding:** No two of the 83 specimens had identical genetic profiles (mutations, LOH type, or CNA pattern). Two patients with bilateral retinoblastoma had distinct somatic *RB1* second-hit events in each eye despite identical germline mutations. Three patients showed evidence of three RB1 hits (germline mutation + LOH + additional somatic mutation), consistent with multifocal tumors with distinct clones in the same eye.

**Detail:** In bilateral patient 1, both eyes shared germline *RB1* loss but had different somatic second hits (splice site p.X566_splice vs truncating p.V654Cfs*4). In bilateral patient 2, the germline alteration was a splice mutation (*RB1* X775_splice), with one eye acquiring somatic LOH and the other a somatic truncating mutation (p.S807*). This demonstrates that the nature of the germline event does not predict which somatic event will occur. The presence of three RB1 hits in some specimens (with low-VAF additional somatic mutations) provides molecular evidence for intraocular tumor heterogeneity — consistent with the clinical observation that multifocal tumors within the same eye often have distinct morphologies.

**Genes / cancer types:** RB1 · Retinoblastoma (bilateral and multifocal)

## Open Questions

- Can *BCOR* mutation be detected in vitreous humor or blood cfDNA as a non-invasive biomarker to guide systemic surveillance without requiring enucleation?
- What is the candidate driver gene at 1q responsible for vitreous seeding — and can its inhibition reduce dissemination?
- Do the non-*RB1* germline mutations (CHEK2, MITF, MSH3, NTHL1) in retinoblastoma patients increase risk for second primary cancers, and what surveillance is appropriate?
- Is the predominance of CN-LOH at the *RB1* locus a reflection of a gene on 13q that is essential in two copies — and can this gene be identified to reveal a new therapeutic vulnerability?
- Do the three-hit *RB1* specimens (with three detectable alterations) represent convergent evolution or microenvironmentally driven selection within the intraocular space?

## Limitations

- Retrospective enucleation cohort: specimens from patients who failed eye-conserving therapy, introducing selection bias toward aggressive or resistant tumors
- FFPE DNA extraction with minimum 250 ng required; small biopsies and endoscopic specimens not assessable
- Germline analysis limited to 42/83 patients (only those consented under the relevant IRB protocol)
- Methylation testing not performed systematically on all specimens; prevalence of *RB1* promoter hypermethylation as a silencing mechanism is therefore underestimated
- Metastasis-free survival analysis for *BCOR* is underpowered (nominal p=0.03 without BH correction reaching significance) — requires prospective validation
- RNA expression, methylation, and proteomics data not generated — limits understanding of downstream pathway consequences
- TMB was uniformly low (0–3 mt/Mb) across all specimens; MSI was present in only the *MSH3* germline patient
