## Summary

This comprehensive whole-genome sequencing (WGS) study of 112 Ewing sarcoma samples with matched germline DNA — the largest genomic analysis of Ewing sarcoma at the time — defines the secondary genetic landscape of this pediatric bone tumor driven primarily by *EWSR1–ETS* gene fusions. Ewing sarcoma has an exceptionally low background mutation rate (median 2.4 × 10⁻⁷ per base; ~10 coding mutations per tumor). The most frequent recurrent mutations are *STAG2* (17%), *CDKN2A* deletion (12%), and *TP53* (7%). *STAG2* and *CDKN2A* are mutually exclusive, while *STAG2* and *TP53* mutations are significantly co-associated (p=2.4×10⁻⁴) and together define an aggressive subtype with the worst clinical outcomes. Subclonal *STAG2* mutations at diagnosis can clonally expand at relapse. Data are available in cBioPortal as `es_iocurie_2014`.

## Key Findings

### F1: STAG2 is the most frequently mutated gene in Ewing sarcoma (17%) and encodes a cohesin complex component

**Finding:** *STAG2* is mutated in 17% (19/112) of Ewing sarcomas by WGS, with 13.2% (41/299) overall in the extended clinical cohort. Mutations include nonsense (15), frameshift indels (17), missense (4), splice site (4), in-frame deletions (2), and one exon duplication. The recurrent R216* mutation was observed in 7 independent cases.

**Detail:** STAG2 (Stromal Antigen 2) is an integral subunit of the cohesin multiprotein complex, which plays an essential role in sister chromatid cohesion and nuclear chromatin organization. STAG2 loss was initially linked to aneuploidy in a wide range of cancers. In Ewing sarcoma, STAG2-mutated cases have a significantly greater number of structural variants (SVs) than wild-type cases (p=0.006, Mann-Whitney), although this correlation is attenuated when TP53 co-mutations are considered. STAG2 mutations are X-linked and were validated by immunostaining (loss of STAG2 protein) in cell lines.

### F2: STAG2 and TP53 mutations co-associate and define the most aggressive Ewing sarcoma subtype

**Finding:** *STAG2* and *TP53* mutations are significantly co-associated in both the discovery (WGS) and extended clinical cohort (p=2.4×10⁻⁴, Fisher's exact). Patients with mutations in both genes have the worst overall survival, while patients with mutations in neither have the best outcomes. Patients with only one mutated gene have significantly lower survival than those with wild-type status for both genes (p significant for the combined group).

**Detail:** In the 299-patient clinical cohort, 41 patients (13.2%) had STAG2 mutations and 16 (5.2%) had TP53 mutations. The presence of a STAG2 mutation alone was not associated with known adverse prognostic factors (tumor size, chemotherapy response, resection quality, metastatic spread), yet still conferred worse overall survival. This suggests that STAG2-TP53 co-mutation increases genetic instability, creating a particularly aggressive molecular subtype requiring alternative therapeutic approaches. Cell lines derived from aggressive/relapsed cases show much higher STAG2 and TP53 mutation frequencies, consistent with this model.

### F3: STAG2 and CDKN2A alterations are mutually exclusive in Ewing sarcoma

**Finding:** *STAG2* mutations and *CDKN2A* deletions (12% frequency) are mutually exclusive in both primary tumors (299 cases: only 2 tumors with both) and 19 Ewing sarcoma cell lines (p=0.0079, Fisher's exact). All STAG2-mutant cell lines express p16 (CDKN2A protein) and vice versa.

**Detail:** STAG2-containing PBAF-type cohesin complexes interact with CTCF, a transcription factor known to regulate the *CDKN2A* locus via chromatin boundary insulation. STAG2 loss may epigenetically deregulate CDKN2A expression in CDKN2A-wild-type tumors, functionally substituting for genomic CDKN2A deletion. This mutual exclusivity suggests these alterations are at least partially redundant in their oncogenic contribution, pointing to a convergent pathway — possibly related to cell cycle control (CDKN2A/p16 function) — targeted by both alterations. CDKN2A status alone was not significantly associated with overall survival in the extended cohort.

### F4: Chromosomal copy number alterations with prognostic significance — 1q gain and 16q loss are co-associated and adverse

**Finding:** The most frequent CNAs are chr8 gain (47%), chr12 gain (21%), chr1q gain (18%), chr16q loss (17%), and CDKN2A deletion (12%). Chr1q gain (p=2×10⁻⁵) and chr16q loss (p=0.0037) independently associate with shorter overall survival (log-rank). These two events are highly co-associated (p=10⁻⁸), consistent with a derivative chr16 from unbalanced t(1;16) translocation.

**Detail:** Chr8 and chr12 gains are co-associated (p=1.63×10⁻³) but do not independently predict survival. A higher number of SNVs/indels (tertile-based stratification) is associated with shorter survival (p=0.04). Eight patients with extreme outlier numbers of structural variants had very poor outcomes (p=0.003). Older age at diagnosis correlates with higher SNV count (r²=0.42, p=2.7×10⁻⁵), suggesting age-related mutagenesis contributes to the genomic landscape.

### F5: Recurrent epigenetic regulator mutations (EZH2, BCOR, ZMYM3, SETD2) and subclonal STAG2 expansion at relapse

**Finding:** Beyond STAG2 and TP53, recurrent mutations affect epigenetic regulators: *EZH2* (2.7%; gain-of-function Y646F/Y646H/A682G in SET domain), *BCOR* (2.7%), *ZMYM3* (2.7%), and rare mutations in *SETD2*, *MLL2*, *MLL3*, *PRDM9*. Overall, 15.2% (17/112) of tumors harbor recurrent mutations in epigenetic regulators. Additionally, subclonal *STAG2* mutations at diagnosis (allele frequency <0.25 in 7 cases) can clonally expand to become the dominant clone at relapse.

**Detail:** EZH2 gain-of-function mutations (SET domain) are mechanistically similar to those in B-cell lymphoma and enhance H3K27 trimethylation. These mutations are targetable by EZH2 inhibitors (e.g., tazemetostat). STAG2 subclonal expansion at relapse was confirmed by STAG2 immunostaining paired primary/relapse samples: 3 of 21 relapse pairs showed loss of STAG2 staining (homogeneous negative) at relapse when only a minority of cells were negative at diagnosis, with LOF mutations at high allele fractions confirmed at relapse.

## Open Questions

- Can EZH2 inhibitors (e.g., tazemetostat) provide clinical benefit in the 2.7% of Ewing sarcoma patients with EZH2 gain-of-function SET domain mutations?
- Does the mutual exclusivity of STAG2 and CDKN2A reflect a functional equivalence through epigenetic CDKN2A silencing via STAG2-CTCF axis disruption?
- What are the mechanisms of STAG2-mutant subclonal expansion at relapse — is this driven by selection pressure from chemotherapy?
- Can STAG2 immunohistochemistry serve as a clinical stratification tool to identify patients who need more intensive/novel therapy?
- Do co-mutations of STAG2 and TP53 represent a target for synthetic lethal approaches (e.g., ATR inhibitors given genomic instability)?

## Limitations

- WGS discovery cohort (N=112) predates more comprehensive sequencing efforts; extended cohort uses targeted capture for STAG2/TP53 only, not genome-wide
- No transcriptomic or proteomic data in clinical cohort; mechanistic insights about STAG2-CDKN2A mutual exclusivity are inferential
- Pediatric cohort (mean age 15 years); adult Ewing sarcoma may have different secondary mutation profiles
- STAG2 subclonal dynamics analyzed in only 21 primary-relapse immunostaining pairs — limited statistical power
- Functional significance of BCOR and ZMYM3 mutations in Ewing sarcoma not experimentally validated in this study
- EWSR1-ETS fusion type (FLI1 vs. ERG) not systematically correlated with secondary mutation burden
