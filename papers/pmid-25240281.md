## Summary

This study characterizes the genomic landscape of malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors (MPNSTs) — highly aggressive sarcomas arising from Schwann cells — through whole-exome sequencing (WES), SNP array copy-number profiling, and RNA-seq of 15 discovery MPNSTs, validated in 37 additional FFPE MPNSTs using MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing. The key discovery is that PRC2 (Polycomb Repressive Complex 2), the major histone H3K27 trimethylase, is recurrently inactivated through loss-of-function mutations or deletions in its core subunits *EED* or *SUZ12* in the vast majority of conventional MPNSTs across all three clinical subtypes (NF1-associated: 70%, sporadic: 92%, radiotherapy-associated: 90%). Three co-occurring pathways define conventional MPNST pathogenesis: NF1 loss, PRC2 inactivation, and CDKN2A loss — collectively altered in the large majority of cases. Functional studies demonstrate that PRC2 loss leads to global H3K27me3 reduction and aberrant activation of developmentally regulated targets (HOX genes, *IGF2*, *PAX2*, *FOXN4*, *TLX1*), and that restoration of SUZ12 suppresses MPNST cell growth. Epithelioid MPNSTs are a genetically distinct entity lacking these alterations.

## Key Findings

### F1: PRC2 is recurrently inactivated in the vast majority of conventional MPNSTs across all three clinical settings

**Finding:** PRC2 genetic alterations (mutations or deletions in *EED* or *SUZ12*) were identified in 70% (19/27) of NF1-associated, 92% (12/13) of sporadic, and 90% (8/9) of radiotherapy-associated MPNSTs in the combined discovery and validation cohorts. PRC2 alterations involve both *EED* (frameshift mutations, point mutations) and *SUZ12* (homozygous deletions, heterozygous deletions with structural rearrangement of remaining allele).

**Detail:** *SUZ12* deletions include 2 homozygous and 5 heterozygous deletions in the discovery cohort; in 3 of the 5 heterozygous cases, RNA-seq revealed structural alterations of the *SUZ12* transcript (truncation at exon 4, 6, or 10), suggesting biallelic functional inactivation through combined deletion and rearrangement. All 3 structural variation cases occurred in radiotherapy-associated MPNSTs. The high prevalence of PRC2 loss across clinically distinct MPNST subtypes establishes this as a fundamental, pan-subtype driver of MPNST pathogenesis, distinct from NF1 which may be a permissive event for PRC2-driven malignant transformation.

### F2: NF1, PRC2 (EED/SUZ12), and CDKN2A define three co-occurring critical pathways in conventional MPNST pathogenesis

**Finding:** In the combined MPNST cohort, *CDKN2A* alterations were found in 81% (42/52), *TP53* mutations in 42% (22/52), and *NF1* mutations/deletions in 87.5% of sporadic/radiotherapy-associated MPNSTs. Significant co-occurrence of *NF1*, PRC2 components, and *CDKN2A* was confirmed (Fleiss' Kappa=0.21, p=0.001), suggesting these three pathways are independently necessary for MPNST development.

**Detail:** The co-occurrence (rather than mutual exclusivity) of NF1, CDKN2A, and PRC2 loss distinguishes MPNST from cancers where pathway alterations are redundant. This implies that all three defects — derepression of RAS signaling (NF1 loss), loss of CDK inhibition (CDKN2A), and epigenetic deregulation via PRC2 inactivation — are required cooperating events in conventional MPNST pathogenesis. In 7 NF1-associated neurofibromas (benign precursors), PRC2 and CDKN2A alterations were rare, suggesting these are associated with the malignant transition from neurofibroma to MPNST.

### F3: PRC2 loss causes global H3K27me3 reduction and aberrant activation of developmental master regulators

**Finding:** RNA-seq analysis of the discovery cohort showed that 95% of differentially expressed genes (455/479) are upregulated in PRC2-loss MPNSTs vs. PRC2-intact MPNSTs. Upregulated genes are enriched for PRC2 suppression targets including homeobox transcription factors and developmental genes. GSEA confirms downregulation of PRC2 module genes and H3K27me3 target genes from neural precursor cells and brain tissue.

**Detail:** Key upregulated PRC2 target genes in PRC2-loss MPNSTs include *FOXN4*, *IGF2*, *PAX2*, and *TLX1*. ChIP-qPCR in MPNST cell lines confirms that at baseline, these genes in PRC2-deficient ST88-14 cells lack H3K27me3 and instead gain H3K4me3 and H3K27ac activation marks. Restoration of exogenous FH-SUZ12 (but not FH-EED) re-establishes H3K27me3 at these promoters and suppresses their transcription, confirming that SUZ12 is the limiting PRC2 subunit whose loss drives the epigenetic derepression program.

### F4: SUZ12 restoration suppresses MPNST cell growth, confirming PRC2 loss as a driver event

**Finding:** Reintroduction of exogenous Flag-HA-tagged SUZ12 (FH-SUZ12) into the SUZ12-deficient ST88-14 MPNST cell line restores H3K27me3 protein and H3K27me3 at target gene promoters, leading to significant growth retardation. FH-EED reintroduction does not rescue H3K27me3 in the absence of SUZ12. FH-SUZ12 has no effect on growth of PRC2-intact MPNST724 cells.

**Detail:** This gain-of-function experiment in a clinically derived MPNST cell line (ST88-14, NF1-associated) provides direct functional evidence that SUZ12 loss is a driver rather than a passenger event in MPNST. The specificity (FH-EED does not rescue in SUZ12-null cells) suggests SUZ12 is the structural scaffold for the PRC2 complex, and its loss cannot be compensated by restoring EED alone. The growth suppression by SUZ12 restoration has therapeutic implications: EZH2 inhibitors (which reduce H3K27me3 in cells with intact PRC2) would be predicted to worsen PRC2-null MPNSTs, while approaches that restore PRC2 function could be therapeutic.

### F5: Epithelioid MPNSTs are a genetically distinct entity lacking conventional MPNST drivers

**Finding:** None of the three epithelioid MPNSTs analyzed harbored genetic alterations in NF1, PRC2 components, or CDKN2A — the three critical pathways defining conventional MPNST. This complete absence of the canonical co-alteration signature suggests epithelioid MPNST is a fundamentally distinct tumor entity.

**Detail:** Epithelioid MPNST is a rare histological variant composed of exclusively epithelioid malignant cells with diffuse S100 immunoreactivity, and is not associated with NF1. The genetic distinction from conventional MPNST (NF1-associated, sporadic, radiotherapy-associated) has clinical implications: treatment strategies based on NF1/PRC2/CDKN2A biology are unlikely to apply to epithelioid MPNST, which may require distinct molecular characterization to identify its drivers and therapeutic targets.

## Open Questions

- Does EZH2 inhibitor therapy worsen outcomes in PRC2-null MPNSTs (by further reducing residual H3K27 methylation) and should PRC2 status be tested before administering EZH2 inhibitors?
- What is the mechanism by which PRC2 loss cooperates with NF1 loss to drive malignant transformation from neurofibroma to MPNST — is there a specific sequence of events?
- Can restoration of H3K27me3 marks through alternative epigenetic approaches (e.g., CDK7/BRD4 inhibitors to suppress activated developmental genes) be therapeutic in PRC2-null MPNSTs?
- Are the structural rearrangements of the remaining *SUZ12* allele in radiotherapy-associated MPNSTs (all three occurring in that subtype) a direct consequence of radiation-induced genomic instability?
- Do epithelioid MPNSTs harbor SMARCB1 loss (as in epithelioid sarcoma) or other chromatin remodeling defects that distinguish them from conventional MPNSTs?

## Limitations

- Discovery cohort is small (n=15 MPNSTs) with limited statistical power; validation cohort used FFPE/targeted sequencing without RNA-seq
- RNA-seq performed only in the discovery cohort; transcriptomic characterization of the validation cohort is absent
- WES sensitivity may miss structural rearrangements of *SUZ12* that were detected only by RNA-seq; such events may be underascertained in the IMPACT-sequenced validation cohort
- Functional studies performed in a limited number of cell lines (ST88-14, MPNST724); in vivo validation of growth suppression by SUZ12 restoration not shown
- Patient-level clinical outcomes (OS, PFS) by PRC2 status not reported; prognostic significance of PRC2 loss is not assessed
- Only 7 neurofibromas included; larger neurofibroma cohort needed to confirm that PRC2/CDKN2A alterations are specific to the malignant transition
- Mechanistic studies focus on SUZ12; EED loss functional consequences are less thoroughly characterized
