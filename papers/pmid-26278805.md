## Summary

This study used MSK-IMPACT targeted next-generation sequencing (300 genes) to define the genomic landscape of 83 upper tract urothelial carcinomas (UTUC) and compare them to 102 high-grade urothelial carcinomas of the bladder (UCB). While both cancer types share a broadly similar mutational spectrum, UTUC showed significantly higher rates of *FGFR3* and *HRAS* mutations and markedly lower rates of *TP53* and *RB1* alterations. These findings suggest that UTUC and UCB are biologically distinct diseases with different oncogenic driver hierarchies, implying potential benefit from site-specific therapeutic strategies.

## Key Findings

### F1: FGFR3 and HRAS are more frequently altered in UTUC than in bladder cancer

**Finding:** High-grade UTUC shows significantly higher rates of *FGFR3* and *HRAS* alterations compared to high-grade UCB.

**Detail:** *FGFR3* alterations were present in 35.6% of high-grade UTUC vs 21.6% of high-grade UCB (p=0.065), and *HRAS* mutations in 13.6% vs 1.0% (p=0.001). Additionally, *FGFR3-TACC3* gene fusions were identified in 8.5% of UTUC vs 2.0% of UCB. All five FGFR3-TACC3 translocations occurred in high-grade UTUC tumors and were confirmed by Sanger sequencing. These differences remained significant after adjustment for T stage (except FGFR3).

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR3` · `HRAS` · `TACC3` · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma · Bladder urothelial carcinoma

**Data availability:** available — mutation and fusion data for utuc_mskcc_2015 is on cBioPortal; UCB comparison cohort not in same study

**Tool support:** native — oncoprint and comparison plots accessible via cBioPortal UI

### F2: TP53 and RB1 alterations are significantly less common in UTUC

**Finding:** *TP53* and *RB1* mutations, hallmarks of high-grade UCB, are substantially underrepresented in high-grade UTUC.

**Detail:** *TP53* was mutated in 25.4% of high-grade UTUC vs 57.8% of high-grade UCB (p<0.001). Strikingly, *RB1* mutations were absent in the entire UTUC cohort (0.0%) compared to 18.6% in UCB (p<0.001). Combined *TP53/MDM2* pathway alterations were also less common in UTUC (35.6% vs 62.7%, p=0.001). *ARID1A* was less frequently altered in UTUC (13.6% vs 27.5%, p=0.050). These differences remained significant after multivariable adjustment for T stage.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` · `RB1` · `MDM2` · `ARID1A` · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma · Bladder urothelial carcinoma

**Data availability:** available — somatic mutation data in utuc_mskcc_2015 on cBioPortal

**Tool support:** native — mutation frequencies and oncoprints accessible via cBioPortal UI

**Concepts:** #TP53 #urothelial #upper-tract #molecular-subtypes #driver-context

### F3: FGFR3 mutations are near-universal in low-grade UTUC and mark an early clonal event

**Finding:** Activating *FGFR3* mutations are present in 22 of 23 (96%) low-grade UTUC tumors and are concordant across spatially and histologically distinct tumor components.

**Detail:** Microdissection of four UTUC cases showed complete concordance of *FGFR3* mutation status between low-grade and high-grade components, invasive and non-invasive components, and primary tumor versus matched lymph node metastasis. Chromatin-modifying gene (CMG) mutations (*KDM6A*, *KMT2D*) were also concordant across components. In high-grade UTUC, *FGFR3* and *HRAS* mutations were mutually exclusive with *TP53* mutations, and *TP53* mutations were found exclusively in high-grade tumors, consistent with two distinct pathways of urothelial carcinogenesis.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR3` · `KDM6A` · `KMT2D` · `TP53` · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma

**Data availability:** available — mutation data in utuc_mskcc_2015; microdissection data referenced in supplementary tables

**Tool support:** native — mutation co-occurrence and oncoprint views in cBioPortal UI

### F4: TP53/MDM2-altered UTUC tumors exhibit markedly higher copy-number instability

**Finding:** *TP53/MDM2*-altered high-grade invasive UTUC tumors have significantly more copy-number gains and total copy-number alterations than *FGFR3/HRAS/KRAS*-mutant UTUC.

**Detail:** Compared to *FGFR3/HRAS/KRAS*-mutant high-grade invasive UTUC (n=21), *TP53/MDM2*-altered tumors (n=16) had a 2.91-fold higher rate of copy-number gains (95% CI 1.13–7.71, p=0.028) and 2.49-fold higher rate of total CNAs (95% CI 1.19–5.33, p=0.017). Nearly all *TP53/MDM2*-altered tumors harbored at least one copy-number gain or loss. High-grade tumors also had more CNAs than low-grade (p=0.004), and invasive more than non-invasive (p<0.001).

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` · `MDM2` · `FGFR3` · `HRAS` · `KRAS` · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma

**Data availability:** available — copy-number alteration data in utuc_mskcc_2015 on cBioPortal

### F5: Chromatin-modifying gene mutations are common in both low- and high-grade UTUC, suggesting early pathogenic role

**Finding:** Mutations in chromatin-modifying genes (*KDM6A*, *KMT2D*, *KMT2A*, *KMT2C*, *CREBBP*, *EP300*, *ARID1A*, *SMARCA4*) are frequent in UTUC across all grades, implying these are early events in disease pathogenesis.

**Detail:** CMG alterations were common in both low-grade and high-grade UTUC, in contrast to *TP53* mutations which were exclusively in high-grade tumors. Microdissection confirmed CMG mutations were concordant across histologically and spatially distinct tumor components including primary tumor, lymph node metastasis, noninvasive and invasive components. With the exception of *ARID1A*, there was no significant difference in CMG mutation frequency between UTUC and UCB, suggesting these are shared early drivers across urothelial subsites.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KDM6A` · `KMT2D` · `KMT2A` · `KMT2C` · `CREBBP` · `EP300` · `ARID1A` · `SMARCA4` · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma

**Data availability:** available — mutation data in utuc_mskcc_2015 on cBioPortal

### F6: A POLE-mutant ultramutated UTUC case provides first report in urothelial cancer

**Finding:** One UTUC tumor harboring a *POLE* V411L hotspot mutation displayed an ultramutated phenotype (422 somatic mutations) with no focal copy-number alterations — the first reported case in urothelial carcinoma.

**Detail:** The *POLE* V411L exonuclease domain mutation had been previously associated with ultramutation in endometrial and colorectal cancers. This tumor was excluded from the main statistical analyses due to the overwhelming number of likely passenger mutations. The remaining 82 UTUC tumors had a median of 5 mutated genes per sample. This case highlights UTUC as a potential site for POLE-driven hypermutation as an alternative carcinogenic mechanism.

**Genes / cancer types:** `POLE` · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma

**Data availability:** partial — case is likely included in utuc_mskcc_2015 but ultramutation phenotype requires mutation-count filtering to isolate

**Tool support:** api — requires filtering via API or custom query; not prominently surfaced in native UI

### F7: CDKN2B, TSC1, and PIK3CA show differential mutation rates in UTUC vs UCB

**Finding:** Beyond the major driver genes, several additional cancer genes show differential alteration frequencies between UTUC and UCB, with *CDKN2B* significantly higher in UTUC.

**Detail:** *CDKN2B* was more frequently altered in high-grade UTUC than UCB (15.3% vs 3.9%, p=0.016). *TSC1* showed a non-significant trend toward higher frequency in UTUC (11.9% vs 3.9%, p=0.100), while *PIK3CA* trended lower in UTUC (10.2% vs 21.6%, p=0.084). *CDKN2A* deletions were among the 14 most frequently altered genes in the full UTUC cohort. These differences suggest that the mTOR pathway (TSC1) and cell cycle regulation (CDKN2B) may be particularly relevant therapeutic targets in UTUC.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDKN2B` · `CDKN2A` · `TSC1` · `PIK3CA` · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma · Bladder urothelial carcinoma

**Data availability:** available — mutation and CNA data in utuc_mskcc_2015 on cBioPortal

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Does the higher prevalence of *FGFR3* mutations in high-grade UTUC (vs UCB) reflect more frequent progression of *FGFR3*-mutant low-grade tumors to high-grade disease in the upper tract due to detection/resection challenges, or a distinct biology of upper tract FGFR3 signaling?
- Would FGFR inhibitors (e.g., erdafitinib) have greater efficacy in UTUC than in UCB, given the higher baseline *FGFR3* alteration frequency?
- Do *POLE*-mutant ultramutated UTUC tumors respond to immune checkpoint inhibitors, as has been demonstrated in other POLE-mutant tumor types?
- Are the CMG mutations (KDM6A, KMT2D) in UTUC functionally equivalent to those in UCB, or do they drive different downstream epigenetic programs in the distinct anatomical/embryological context of the upper tract?
- What molecular mechanisms underlie the near-complete absence of *RB1* mutations in UTUC, and does this explain any clinical or therapeutic differences compared to UCB?
- Could Lynch syndrome–associated microsatellite instability (noted to be more common in UTUC) identify a UTUC subgroup with distinct mutational landscape and potential immunotherapy sensitivity?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Targeted panel (300 genes) captures only genes included in MSK-IMPACT; rare mutations and structural alterations not in the panel would be missed.
- Study size (n=83 UTUC) is the largest NGS-profiled UTUC cohort at time of publication, but statistical power to detect co-mutation patterns or less frequent events is limited.
- Epigenetic differences (e.g., promoter methylation) and gene expression changes not captured by targeted DNA sequencing — these may be more important drivers of UTUC biology.
- The UCB comparison cohort was sequenced separately and not prospectively matched; residual confounding by stage or other factors is possible despite multivariable adjustment.
- Ultramutated POLE case was excluded from analysis; its exclusion limits understanding of this rare but potentially clinically important subtype.
- Absence of whole-exome or whole-genome sequencing means the full mutational signature landscape and all structural variants could not be characterized.
