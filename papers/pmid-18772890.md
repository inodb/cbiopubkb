## Summary

The inaugural TCGA publication characterizes the multi-dimensional genomic landscape of glioblastoma (GBM) — the most common and lethal primary adult brain tumor — in 206 cases using DNA copy number (3 platforms), gene expression, and DNA methylation, with targeted sequencing of 601 genes in 91 matched tumor-normal pairs. The study establishes three core pathways (RTK/RAS/PI3K, p53, RB) as nearly universally co-disrupted in GBM (74% of cases show aberrations in all three), definitively validates NF1 as a GBM tumor suppressor, identifies frequent novel PIK3R1 mutations as a new mechanism of PI3K activation, and discovers a clinically important link between MGMT methylation, temozolomide treatment, and acquisition of a mismatch-repair (MMR)-defective hypermutator phenotype. This landmark study established the TCGA framework and the GBM data resource used by the research community for over a decade.

## Key Findings

### F1: Three core pathways co-disrupted in 74% of GBMs — RTK/RAS/PI3K (88%), p53 (78%), RB (87%)

**Finding:** Integration of copy-number and sequencing data reveals that three major signaling axes — RTK/RAS/PI3K signaling, and the p53 and RB tumor suppressor pathways — are each disrupted in the vast majority of GBMs, and 74% of sequenced cases carry alterations in all three pathways simultaneously. Components within each pathway show mutually exclusive alterations (consistent with pathway-level selection), but cross-pathway co-occurrence is greater than random.

**Detail:** RTK/PI3K pathway alterations include EGFR amplification/mutation (45%), PDGFRA amplification/mutation (13%), MET amplification/mutation (4%), ERBB2 mutation (8%), PTEN deletion/mutation, PIK3CA mutation, and novel PIK3R1 mutations — affecting 86% of samples. p53 pathway: ARF deletion (55%), MDM2 amplification (11%), MDM4 amplification (4%), TP53 mutation (37.5% untreated). RB pathway: CDKN2A/B deletion (55%/53%), CDK4 amplification (14%), RB1 mutation (9%). This integrated pathway view is now the canonical framework for understanding GBM biology and for rationally designing combination therapies.

### F2: NF1 definitively established as a GBM tumor suppressor gene; 23% of cases affected

**Finding:** Despite prior reports of rare NF1 mutations in GBM, their pathogenic significance remained controversial. This study identifies NF1 somatic mutations in 14% (13/91 sequenced) and NF1 heterozygous deletions in 23% (47/206 total) of GBM, with multiple confirmed loss-of-function mutations matching known germline neurofibromatosis variants, definitively establishing NF1 as a GBM tumor suppressor.

**Detail:** 19 somatic NF1 mutations identified: 6 nonsense, 4 splice site, 5 missense (several matching germline NF1 loss-of-function alleles), 4 frameshift indels. Five specific mutations (R1391S, R1513*, e25-1, e29+1, Q1966*) previously reported as germline pathogenic in neurofibromatosis patients. Some samples showed NF1 expression loss without genomic alteration (epigenetic silencing). NF1 loss activates RAS signaling, making GBM with NF1 mutations rational candidates for RAF or MEK inhibitor therapy.

### F3: Frequent novel PIK3R1 mutations activate PI3K independently of PIK3CA, defining a new cancer driver mechanism

**Finding:** *PIK3R1* (encoding the p85α regulatory subunit of PI3K) is frequently mutated in GBM with 9 somatic mutations in 9/91 (10%) of sequenced cases. These mutations cluster in the iSH2 domain at the C2-iSH2 interface of p110α/p85α, are mutually exclusive with PIK3CA mutations, and are predicted to constitutively activate PI3K by disrupting the inhibitory contact of p85α on p110α.

**Detail:** PIK3R1 was extremely rarely reported as mutated in cancer prior to this study. Eight of nine mutations lie within the iSH2 domain; four are 3-bp in-frame deletions. Clustering around residues D560 and N564 (p85α contact points with p110α C2 residue N345) based on crystal structure analysis supports the mechanistic model of constitutive PI3K activation. This mutual exclusivity with PIK3CA (and with PTEN deletions in many cases) highlights PI3K pathway convergence. Therapeutic implication: PIK3R1-mutant GBMs may respond to PI3K or PDK1 inhibitors.

### F4: MGMT promoter methylation combined with alkylating therapy drives MMR-deficient hypermutator phenotype in treated GBM

**Finding:** Among 19 post-treatment GBM samples, 7 were hypermutated. *MGMT* promoter methylation in treated GBMs is associated with a profound shift in mutation spectrum toward G:C-to-A:T transitions at non-CpG sites (81% of mutations in MGMT-methylated treated tumors vs. 23% in non-methylated treated), consistent with unrepaired alkylated guanine from temozolomide. 6 of 7 hypermutated tumors harbored MMR gene mutations (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, or PMS2), arising with the same alkylation-characteristic mutational spectrum — suggesting temozolomide selects for MMR loss.

**Detail:** MGMT methylation → unrepaired alkylated guanine → G:C-to-A:T transitions → MMR gene mutations → loss of MMR → hypermutator phenotype. This "two-hit" mechanism creates an acquired hypermutator state as a resistance mechanism to alkylating therapy. Clinical implication: patients with MGMT-methylated GBM who initially respond to temozolomide may evolve an MMR-defective state, potentially conferring resistance to subsequent targeted therapies and raising the hypothesis that MSI-targeted therapies could be relevant in recurrent GBM.

### F5: EGFR family activation is prevalent and complex; co-amplified RTKs suggest mechanisms of adaptive resistance

**Finding:** EGFR is the most commonly altered RTK in GBM: 45% (41/91) of sequenced samples carry EGFR focal amplification with or without point mutations. ERBB2 somatic mutations found in 8% (7/91). Together with PDGFRA (13%) and MET (4%), 10 of 91 samples show genomic co-activation of two or more RTKs. Novel AKT3 amplification identified as a PI3K-pathway activation mechanism.

**Detail:** EGFR vIII deletion (extracellular domain) is most common structural alteration; cytoplasmic domain deletions also detected via exon-level expression profiling. ERBB2 mutations cluster in extracellular domain (unlike breast cancer amplifications) — 8 extracellular domain mutations including recurrent V777A site (also in lung, gastric, colon cancers). Genomic co-amplification of multiple RTKs provides a mechanism for the observed phenomenon of multiple phosphorylated RTKs in individual GBM specimens, suggesting that anti-RTK cocktails tailored to individual RTK mutation patterns may be needed. AKT3 amplification (novel finding) provides an alternative mechanism for PI3K pathway activation that may be refractory to PI3K inhibitors.

## Open Questions

- Does the MMR-defective hypermutator phenotype in treated MGMT-methylated GBM confer sensitivity to PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibition at recurrence?
- Can PIK3R1 mutation status predict response to PI3K inhibitors independently of PIK3CA/PTEN status?
- Which GBM patients with NF1 mutations/deletions show RAS pathway activation and would benefit from MEK inhibitors?
- Do co-amplified RTKs in individual GBMs signal independently or synergistically, and what rational combination anti-RTK regimen prevents adaptive resistance?
- How does AKT3 amplification affect sensitivity to PI3K vs. AKT inhibitors compared to PTEN deletion or PIK3CA mutation?
- The 2008 TCGA GBM analysis used targeted sequencing (601 genes), not WES/WGS; what additional driver events in the remaining genome were missed?

## Limitations

- Targeted sequencing limited to 601 genes — whole-exome and whole-genome mutations not captured; TERT promoter mutations (not in targeted panel) identified only in later studies
- Small sequenced cohort (n=91) limits power for rare mutation discovery
- Predominantly primary (de novo) GBMs; secondary GBM biology (IDH-mutant progression) underrepresented
- No RNA-seq; fusion transcripts and splice variants (e.g., EGFR vIII) detected via exon arrays, which is less sensitive than RNA-seq
- Strict biospecimen quality criteria excluded 65% of samples screened; may introduce selection bias
- Tissue microarray-era study: many subsequent findings (IDH1/2, TERT promoter, ATRX mutations) not measured here
- DNA methylation platform (Illumina GoldenGate) covers only 2,305 loci vs. genome-scale EPIC arrays used in later studies
- Treatment outcome data in post-treatment cohort is limited to small n for clinical conclusions
