## Summary

This study establishes *CDH1* loss-of-function as the pathognomonic molecular event in plasmacytoid-variant bladder cancer, an aggressive histologic subtype with high rates of local recurrence and cancer-specific mortality. Using whole-exome sequencing (WES) and targeted MSK-IMPACT sequencing of 31 plasmacytoid tumors, *CDH1* alterations (somatic mutations or promoter hypermethylation) were found in 100% of cases, compared with 0% in conventional urothelial carcinoma. CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockout of *CDH1* in bladder cancer cell lines confirmed that E-cadherin loss drives enhanced cellular migration, consistent with the peritoneal spread pattern of this disease. Co-alterations in *ERBB2*, *PIK3CA*, and *TSC1* suggest clinical actionability despite CDH1 being undruggable directly.

## Key Findings

### F1: CDH1 truncating mutations are pathognomonic for plasmacytoid-variant bladder cancer

**Finding:** Somatic loss-of-function alterations in *CDH1* (encoding E-cadherin) are present in 84% of plasmacytoid bladder cancers by sequencing and 100% by protein expression, absent in conventional urothelial carcinoma.

**Detail:** WES of 6 plasmacytoid tumors found *CDH1* nonsense mutations in all 6; targeted sequencing of 19 additional cases found *CDH1* mutations in 14/19 (74%). In the prospective institutional cohort, all 6 patients with *CDH1* mutations had plasmacytoid histology; 56 urothelial NOS cases had zero *CDH1* alterations. E-cadherin IHC confirmed protein loss in all 31 plasmacytoid cases. Five *CDH1* wild-type plasmacytoid tumors showed promoter hypermethylation by bisulfite sequencing, accounting for the remaining cases (P<0.001 for enrichment vs. urothelial NOS).

### F2: CDH1 loss is a truncal event arising in a common cell of origin shared with urothelial NOS

**Finding:** In mixed-histology tumors, *CDH1* alteration is a subclonal event specific to the plasmacytoid component, while earlier truncal mutations are shared with the co-existing urothelial NOS component.

**Detail:** Deep sequencing of spatially separated plasmacytoid and urothelial NOS regions from one tumor revealed shared truncal mutations in *CDKN1A* (A45fs) and *PIK3C2G* (S48R), indicating a common precursor. *CDH1* Y68fs and mutations in *PTEN*, *NOTCH2*, and *FAT4* were exclusive to the plasmacytoid region, demonstrating that CDH1 loss drives a distinct evolutionary branch from a shared urothelial cell of origin.

### F3: CDH1 loss drives enhanced cell migration, explaining peritoneal spread pattern

**Finding:** CRISPR/Cas9-mediated *CDH1* knockout in two urothelial carcinoma cell lines (RT4, MGHU4) significantly increased migratory capability, providing a mechanistic explanation for the peritoneal dissemination pattern characteristic of plasmacytoid tumors.

**Detail:** CDH1 knockout MGHU4 cells showed increased scratch-assay migration (P<0.05) and both RT4 and MGHU4 CDH1-KO cells demonstrated enhanced Boyden chamber migration versus parental lines (P<0.05). E-cadherin protein loss was confirmed by immunoblot in both knockout lines. These results link the molecular alteration directly to the distinctive invasive phenotype of plasmacytoid carcinomas, including their high rates of local recurrence and peritoneal spread.

### F4: Plasmacytoid-variant histology is associated with worse cancer-specific mortality and local recurrence

**Finding:** Patients with plasmacytoid-variant bladder cancer had significantly higher cumulative incidence of cancer-specific mortality (CSM) and local recurrence compared with urothelial carcinoma NOS after radical cystectomy.

**Detail:** Clinical analysis of 53 plasmacytoid patients vs. 978 urothelial NOS controls showed higher CSM and local recurrence rates by competing-events analysis (Gray's test, P<0.05). Multivariable competing risks regression confirmed plasmacytoid histology as an independent predictor of local recurrence and cancer-specific death. Peritoneal spread was more frequent in plasmacytoid patients. Median follow-up 71.8 months among survivors.

### F5: Co-mutations in ERBB2, PIK3CA, and TSC1 identify actionable therapeutic targets

**Finding:** Beyond CDH1, plasmacytoid-variant tumors harbor recurrent actionable alterations in *ERBB2*, *PIK3CA*, and *TSC1* at frequencies similar to conventional urothelial carcinoma, suggesting that targeted therapy could be combined with standard treatment.

**Detail:** Frequency of *TP53*, *RB1*, *ARID1A*, *ERBB2*, and *PIK3CA* mutations in plasmacytoid tumors was not significantly different from TCGA urothelial NOS cohort (P>0.05 for each), except *CDH1*. Actionable alterations in *ERBB2* (~15%), *PIK3CA* (~10–15%), and *TSC1* (~10%) were present. Given the aggressive natural history and high mortality of plasmacytoid carcinoma, early integration of targeted agents into multi-modality therapy was proposed.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can *CDH1* mutation status serve as a clinical diagnostic biomarker to identify plasmacytoid histology non-invasively (e.g., from urine or ctDNA)?
- Does *CDH1* loss confer sensitivity or resistance to specific systemic therapies (e.g., immune checkpoint inhibitors, ErbB2-directed agents)?
- What is the prevalence and clinical significance of CDH1 promoter methylation versus somatic mutation in determining clinical phenotype?
- Can early identification of plasmacytoid histology via prospective MSK-IMPACT sequencing improve treatment allocation and outcomes?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=31 sequenced tumors), limiting power to detect lower-frequency co-alteration associations.
- Survival comparison is retrospective; plasmacytoid patients had higher stage at presentation, potentially confounding outcome analysis.
- Promoter methylation data (bisulfite sequencing) not available in cBioPortal.
- CRISPR functional experiments performed in cell lines; in vivo validation of CDH1 loss driving peritoneal spread not provided.
- Germline testing performed but no germline CDH1 alterations found; study was not powered to detect rare germline events.
