## Summary

This landmark study performed whole-exome and targeted sequencing on 103 microsatellite-stable (MSS) African American (AA) colorectal cancers (CRCs) versus 129 Caucasian CRCs to characterise ethnicity-associated differences in the somatic mutational landscape. Twenty new genes were identified as significantly mutated in CRC — none previously reported in MSS nonhypermutator CRCs. A 15-gene subset was 3.3-fold more frequently mutated in AA CRCs than in Caucasian CRCs. Strikingly, *EPHA6* (5.8% vs 0%) and *FLCN* (2.9% vs 0%) mutations were exclusive to AA CRCs and are proposed as AA-specific CRC driver genes. These findings suggest distinct routes of colon carcinogenesis across ethnicities and may partially explain the disproportionately higher CRC incidence and mortality in African Americans. Data are available in cBioPortal as `coad_caseccc_2015`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Twenty new significantly mutated genes identified in CRC, preferentially in African American cases

**Finding:** Whole-exome sequencing of 29 AA CRCs followed by targeted resequencing in 74 AA CRCs identified 20 genes as significantly mutated (P < 0.05, FDR < 0.05) in both discovery and validation cohorts — none previously described as mutated in MSS nonhypermutator CRCs.

**Detail:** The 20 genes include *EPHA6*, *FLCN*, *HTR1F*, *CDK8*, *JAK1*, *CHD5*, *TCEB3CL*, *MAGEB10*, *CPT1C*, *ANKRD36*, *MGAT4C*, *ZNF717*, *ZNF862*, *CP*, *KIAA1551*, *EML6*, *ATP8B2*, *WASH1*, *GPR149*, and *WDR87*. These were discovered in a cohort that was absent or underrepresented in major prior CRC sequencing studies (e.g., TCGA had only 2 annotated AA cases of 333 total). A 15-gene subset was statistically 3.3-fold more frequently mutated in AA CRCs compared to Caucasian CRCs (P = 1.8 × 10⁻⁸ from subset analysis), accounting for 41% of AA CRCs vs 15% of Caucasian CRCs.

### F2: EPHA6 mutations are exclusive to African American CRCs and likely represent an AA-specific driver

**Finding:** Somatic mutations in *EPHA6* (ephrin type-A receptor 6) were detected in 5.83% of AA CRCs and in 0% of Caucasian CRCs (P = 0.007), confirmed by Sanger sequencing. The mutation pattern — including a canonical splice-site and four missense mutations predicted to alter protein function — is consistent with tumour-suppressor activity.

**Detail:** *EPHA6* belongs to the largest RTK subfamily (Eph receptors), members of which function as oncogenes or tumour suppressors depending on context. *EPHA3* and *EPHB6* were previously identified as mutated in Caucasian MSS CRCs, making *EPHA6* an intriguing ethnicity-specific variant of the same pathway. The finding implies that different ephrin family members are targeted for inactivation in CRCs arising in distinct ethnic groups — a previously unsuspected level of cancer biology diversity.

### F3: FLCN mutations are exclusive to African American CRCs and link Birt-Hogg-Dubé syndrome biology to colon cancer

**Finding:** Inactivating mutations in *FLCN* (folliculin) — including two frameshift and one nonsense mutation — were detected in 2.91% of AA CRCs and 0% of Caucasian CRCs (P = 0.086). Germline *FLCN* inactivation causes Birt-Hogg-Dubé syndrome, which includes increased risk of renal, thyroid, and (in some cases) colon neoplasia.

**Detail:** FLCN encodes a tumour suppressor that interacts with FNIP1/2 and regulates mTORC1 and AMPK signalling. All three *FLCN* somatic mutations in AA CRCs are likely loss-of-function events, consistent with known Birt-Hogg-Dubé syndrome biology translated to a somatic context. Of 18 *FLCN* CRC mutations previously listed in COSMIC, 17 were in hypermutator CRCs (primarily MSI) — highlighting the importance of separating MSS and MSI CRCs before assessing gene-specific mutation frequencies.

### F4: CDK8 and JAK1 are recurrently mutated in African American CRCs with potential therapeutic relevance

**Finding:** *CDK8* (1.94% AA, 0.78% Caucasian) and *JAK1* (3.88% AA, 2.33% Caucasian) are among the 15 genes preferentially mutated in AA CRCs, both encoding kinases with known roles in oncogenic signalling and potential therapeutic tractability.

**Detail:** CDK8 is a kinase component of the Mediator transcriptional co-activator complex and has been implicated as an oncogene in colorectal cancer (amplification on chromosome 13q) and as a regulator of WNT/β-catenin signalling. JAK1 is a cytokine receptor-associated kinase; in MSS CRCs, JAK1 activating mutations can promote immune evasion by reducing IFN-γ responsiveness. Both genes represent potential therapeutic targets: CDK8 inhibitors are in development; JAK inhibitors are approved in other indications.

### F5: The mutational landscape of African American CRCs is fundamentally distinct, suggesting different routes of carcinogenesis

**Finding:** The 15-gene mutation signature that is enriched in AA CRCs accounts for 41% of AA CRCs but only 15% of Caucasian CRCs, suggesting that a substantial fraction of AA CRCs follow distinct carcinogenic routes not captured by existing Caucasian-derived CRC genomic models.

**Detail:** Prior major CRC sequencing studies (TCGA, CCLE) were conducted predominantly on Caucasian-derived samples (only 2 of 333 sequenced CRCs were annotated as AA in prior work). This study demonstrates that the mutational landscape in AA CRCs includes both the canonical CRC driver genes (APC, KRAS, TP53) and an additional stratum of genes preferentially or exclusively mutated in AA patients. Environmental, dietary, and socioeconomic factors may contribute alongside genetic factors to these differences. The geographic restriction to northeastern Ohio limits generalisability to AA communities across the US with different migration histories.

## Open Questions

- Are the 15 AA-enriched mutated genes present at similar frequencies in AA CRC cohorts from other US regions or African-origin populations globally (e.g., West Africa, Caribbean)?
- Do mutations in EPHA6 or FLCN in AA CRCs associate with distinct clinical outcomes (stage at diagnosis, survival, treatment response)?
- Do the newly identified genes interact functionally with canonical CRC pathways (WNT, MAPK, PI3K, TGFβ), and do they represent synergistic or alternative oncogenic mechanisms?
- Can the 15-gene panel serve as an ethnicity-adjusted biomarker in clinical sequencing panels to improve molecular subtyping of AA CRC?
- What environmental or epigenetic factors co-operate with EPHA6 and FLCN loss in AA CRC carcinogenesis?
- Does CDK8 mutation in AA CRCs have functional consequences similar to CDK8 amplification (activating vs passenger event)?

## Limitations

- Cohort drawn exclusively from northeastern Ohio; migration history of AA communities may produce regional genetic heterogeneity
- Caucasian comparator cohort consists primarily of late-stage cases, whereas AA validation cohort is predominantly early stage — potential stage-related confounding
- Statistical significance thresholds vary between AA-enriched vs Caucasian comparisons; per-gene comparisons for most of the 20 genes are nominally non-significant
- Targeted resequencing (not WES) used for validation; novel genes outside the 78-gene panel are not characterised
- Clinical outcome data (survival, treatment response) not reported — the prognostic impact of the ethnicity-enriched gene set is unknown
- Limited sample sizes reduce power to detect rare mutations with OR < 3 in individual genes
