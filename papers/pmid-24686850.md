## Summary

Integrated genomic analysis of 139 paired ESCC cases using WES/targeted deep sequencing, plus somatic copy number variation (SCNV) analysis of >180 ESCCs across multiple platforms (SNP array, array-CGH). Key findings: 13 significantly mutated genes identified including novel tumor suppressors FAT1, FAT2, ZNF750, and KMT2D; RTK-MAPK-PI3K pathways are dysregulated via multiple simultaneous mechanisms in the same tumors; FGFR1 is a novel recurrently amplified therapeutic target; APOBEC3B drives mutagenesis; and XPO1 is identified as a novel druggable target through both somatic mutation and protein overexpression. Study provides a comprehensive molecular foundation for ESCC — a cancer with dismal survival despite decades of treatment. Full mutation and SCNV data in `escc_ucla_2014` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: 13 significantly mutated genes including novel tumor suppressors FAT1, FAT2, ZNF750, and KMT2D

**Finding:** MutSigCV analysis identified 13 significantly mutated genes (FDR q < 0.2). Beyond previously known TP53 (most frequent), PIK3CA, and NOTCH1, novel SMGs included FAT1, FAT2, ZNF750, KMT2D, FBXW7, and others. FAT1 and FAT2 (cadherin superfamily members) showed frequent, mutually exclusive truncating mutations; functional assays confirmed both as tumor suppressors. ZNF750, a squamous differentiation regulator, was predominantly truncated and showed specific enrichment in squamous cell carcinomas across cancer types.

**Detail:** Total 1,847 non-silent somatic mutations were identified across all cases (average 15/case). APOBEC3B trinucleotide signature dominated the mutational spectrum — APOBEC3B mRNA was significantly upregulated in tumor versus normal esophageal tissue. FAT1 loss-of-function was supported by bi-allelic inactivation (LOH in SNP-array cases), siRNA-mediated FAT1 knockdown increased cell proliferation, and FAT2 shRNA promoted in vivo tumor growth. ZNF750 knockdown similarly increased proliferation and suppressed late differentiation gene expression. Both proteins were downregulated by IHC in ESCC tumors versus normal esophageal epithelium.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, PIK3CA, NOTCH1, FAT1, FAT2, ZNF750, KMT2D, KMT2C, FBXW7, ERBB4, PTEN · ESCC

### F2: RTK-MAPK-PI3K pathway dysregulated in nearly all ESCCs via multiple simultaneous mechanisms

**Finding:** The MAPK (p = 0.0005) and PI3K (p = 0.0004) pathways are dysregulated in virtually all ESCCs through three concurrent mechanisms: (i) amplification and overexpression of RTKs (EGFR, ERBB2, FGFR1) and KRAS; (ii) activating mutations of ERBB4 and PIK3CA; (iii) inactivating mutations of PTEN, MAP3K13, and MAP3K15. JAK-STAT3 signaling is additionally activated via IL7R amplification and JAK1 mutations.

**Detail:** KRAS amplification, EGFR amplification, and ERBB4 activating mutations co-occur in individual tumors with PIK3CA mutations and PTEN deletions, creating layered pathway activation that likely contributes to therapeutic resistance. p-STAT3 protein was confirmed elevated in ESCC, consistent with JAK-STAT activation. SOX2 amplification (3q26, a well-known ESCC amplicon) cooperatively transforms esophageal epithelial cells with activated STAT3. The RTK-MAPK-PI3K network analysis emphasizes that single-agent pathway blockade may be insufficient without understanding the full mutational context.

**Genes / cancer types:** EGFR, ERBB2, ERBB4, FGFR1, KRAS, PIK3CA, PTEN, MAP3K13, MAP3K15, JAK1, IL7R, SOX2 · ESCC

### F3: FGFR1 amplification as novel recurrent therapeutic target; CCND1 most frequent focal amplification

**Finding:** Focal SCNV analysis across 184 ESCC samples identified 14 recurrent focal amplifications. The most frequent peak spans CCND1 (11q13.2). FGFR1 amplification — not previously reported in ESCC — was confirmed in 11 of 53 additional samples by FISH (20.8%); FGFR1 protein was overexpressed by IHC in 17.3% of tumors. Cell cycle progression is altered by CCND1 amplification, CDKN2A deletion/mutation, and TP53 mutation in overlapping but not identical tumor subsets.

**Detail:** Additional focal amplification peaks included EGFR, MYC, KRAS, and CDKN2A deletion. FGFR amplification predicts sensitivity to FGFR-targeted inhibitors in other solid tumors (breast, lung), making FGFR1 a priority therapeutic target in ESCC. FBXW7 (a c-Myc ubiquitin ligase and negative regulator) was recurrently mutated; FBXW7 protein was lost by IHC in 33% of ESCC tumors in an additional cohort, providing functional confirmation that FBXW7 loss-of-function promotes c-Myc stabilization and cell cycle progression. The G1-S cell cycle pathway was significantly altered (p = 1.63×10⁻⁵) in a high fraction of tumors.

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR1, CCND1, CDKN2A, EGFR, MYC, KRAS, FBXW7 · ESCC

### F4: Epigenetic regulation frequently disrupted — SWI/SNF, histone methyltransferases, demethylases

**Finding:** Epigenetic modification genes were significantly enriched for mutations in ESCC (p = 0.0013). Recurrently altered genes include SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex members (ARID2, PBRM1), histone methyltransferases (KMT2D/MLL4, KMT2C/MLL3), and histone demethylase KDM6A. These alterations affect the same epigenetic regulatory axis disrupted in other squamous malignancies (HNSCC, lung SCC), suggesting a shared epigenetic vulnerability in squamous cancers.

**Detail:** KMT2D was among the 13 significantly mutated genes. ARID2 and PBRM1 are canonical SWI/SNF subunits whose loss is known to affect global chromatin accessibility and transcription factor binding. KDM6A (UTX) is a histone H3K27 demethylase that opposes PRC2 repression; its mutation may enable aberrant polycomb silencing at squamous differentiation loci. These epigenetic alterations overlap with the ZNF750 squamous differentiation pathway, suggesting a convergent mechanism suppressing terminal differentiation across multiple gene classes.

**Genes / cancer types:** KMT2D, KMT2C, ARID2, PBRM1, KDM6A, ZNF750 · ESCC

### F5: XPO1 mutation (D624G) and protein overexpression as novel therapeutic target

**Finding:** A recurrent D624G missense mutation in XPO1 — identical to the mutation previously found in chronic lymphocytic leukemia — was identified in ESCC. Structural modeling showed Asp624 forms a salt bridge with export cargo (Snurportin Lys144); D624G disrupts this interaction and likely enhances nuclear export efficiency. XPO1 protein was overexpressed in ESCC tumors (mRNA and protein), with overexpression correlating with larger tumor size (p = 0.016). Treatment with KPT-330 (selinexor), an oral XPO1 inhibitor targeting Cys528, inhibited ESCC cell proliferation at submicromolar concentrations.

**Detail:** XPO1 shRNA knockdown induced apoptosis (cleaved PARP) and reduced proliferation. KPT-330 treatment altered downstream cargo proteins including P53, Cyclin D1, c-Myc, PUMA, and BIM. The mutated XPO1 tumor also showed protein upregulation relative to matched normal, supporting a gain-of-function phenotype that may complement its export-enhancing mutation. XPO1 inhibitors (selinexor) have subsequently received FDA approval for multiple myeloma and DLBCL, making this finding prescient for ESCC clinical development.

**Genes / cancer types:** XPO1, TP53, CCND1, MYC · ESCC

## Open Questions

- Can FGFR1 amplification in ESCC predict benefit from FGFR inhibitors (e.g., erdafitinib) in a prospective biomarker-stratified trial?
- Does APOBEC3B upregulation in ESCC create an immunogenic neoantigen burden that could be exploited with checkpoint immunotherapy?
- Do concurrent epigenetic alterations (KMT2D, ARID2, KDM6A) sensitize ESCC to EZH2 inhibitors or BET bromodomain inhibitors?
- Can ZNF750 restoration or downstream differentiation program activation reverse the malignant phenotype in ESCC cells?
- How does the layered RTK-MAPK-PI3K co-activation pattern affect therapeutic resistance to individual targeted agents, and what combination strategies are most rational?

## Limitations

- Chinese patient cohort only; mutational frequencies may differ from Western ESCC, where alcohol and tobacco are more prominent etiological factors
- Functional validation (siRNA, shRNA, IHC) performed on a subset of findings; comprehensive in vivo validation of all novel SMGs is lacking
- RNA-seq performed on only 4 discovery cohort samples; transcriptomic characterization is minimal
- Proteomics data limited to IHC for selected proteins; no mass spectrometry–based proteomic profiling
- SCNV analysis performed across multiple platforms (SNP array, array-CGH) with potential for batch effects; WGS-based structural variant analysis not performed
