## Summary
This study performed the first comprehensive multi-platform genomic analysis of 40 oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC) tumors, integrating copy number, gene expression, DNA methylation, and whole-exome sequencing data. Integrated analysis identified four major driver pathways—mitogenic signaling, Notch, cell cycle, and TP53—plus two additional key genes (*FAT1* and *CASP8*). Notably, the Notch pathway was found to be tumor-suppressive (not oncogenic) in OSCC, disrupted in 66% of cases. Despite the dominance of tumor suppressor gene loss, 80% of tumors harbored at least one genomic alteration in a targetable gene.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Notch Pathway Tumor-Suppressive and Disrupted in 66% of Tumors
- **Detail**: Notch pathway members were altered in 66% (23/35) of tumors. *NOTCH1* was mutated in 9% of the primary cohort and in 16 of 44 HNSCC cell lines, predominantly via loss-of-function mutations (frameshift, nonsense, splice site). Functional experiments demonstrated that activated NOTCH1 (ICN1) inhibited OSCC cell proliferation, induced G1 arrest via p21 (CDKN1A) upregulation, and dramatically reduced tumor growth in orthotopic xenograft models—confirming a tumor-suppressive role. Additional Notch pathway alterations included gains in *JAG1*, *JAG2*, *NUMB* and losses in *MAML1*.
- **Genes/Alterations**: NOTCH1 (mutation 9%, pathway disruption 66%), TP63 (alteration 34%), JAG1/JAG2 (gain), MAML1 (loss)
- **Data availability**: available — mutations and CNA data present in hnsc_mdanderson_2013
- **Tool support**: native — Mutations OncoPrint and single-gene mutation query supported in cBioPortal UI
- **Concepts**: #driver-genes #squamous #HNSC #molecular-subtypes

### Finding 2: Cell Cycle Pathway Altered in 94% of Tumors
- **Detail**: Amplification of *CCND1* (22%, 8/38) and homozygous/heterozygous deletion of *CDKN2A* were the two most common genomic alterations, with combined cell cycle alterations present in 94% (33/35) of tumors. Focal 9p deletions targeting *CDKN2A* sometimes occurred against a background of 9p arm-level gain, suggesting a concurrent oncogenic driver on 9p (candidate: *NFIB*). *EGFR* was focally amplified in 16% (6/38) of tumors. Gene expression strongly correlated with relative (rather than absolute) copy number, suggesting ploidy compensation.
- **Genes/Alterations**: CCND1 (amplification 22%), EGFR (amplification 16%), CDKN2A (deletion ~32-74% combined with gain), CDKN2B
- **Data availability**: available — CNA and expression data in hnsc_mdanderson_2013
- **Tool support**: native — CNA query, oncoPrint, co-occurrence analysis all native in cBioPortal
- **Concepts**: #copy-number #CNA #driver-genes #actionability

### Finding 3: FAT1 as Third Most Frequently Inactivated Tumor Suppressor
- **Detail**: *FAT1* was mutated in 30% (12/40) of patients, with two-thirds being inactivating mutations (nonsense, frameshift, splice site). Including focal deletions, the combined inactivation rate reached 46% (16/35). This was the highest frequency of *FAT1* mutation reported in HNSCC at the time. *FAT1* is a large cadherin involved in cell-cell signaling and is cleaved by furin-like convertases and γ-secretase. FAT1 inactivation was linked to nuclear β-catenin localization and Wnt pathway activation, suggesting a mechanism involving cell polarity and migration control.
- **Genes/Alterations**: FAT1 (mutation 30%, combined inactivation 46%)
- **Data availability**: available — mutations in hnsc_mdanderson_2013; functional studies not in portal
- **Tool support**: native — single-gene mutation frequency query; co-occurrence with other alterations
- **Concepts**: #driver-genes #mutation #HNSC #oral

### Finding 4: CASP8 Mutations Define a Novel OSCC Subtype with Low CNA Burden
- **Detail**: *CASP8* was mutated in 10% (4/40) of OSCC tumors and was validated in TCGA HNSCC data. CASP8-mutant tumors showed significantly fewer total CNAs (p=0.0136), defining a novel molecular subtype. Strikingly, three of four CASP8-mutant tumors also had *HRAS* mutations, and this co-mutation was validated in TCGA (7/8 HRAS-mutant HNSCC had CASP8 mutations). CASP8-mutant cell lines were more tumorigenic and generated larger, more lethal orthotopic tumors, consistent with a loss-of-function mechanism.
- **Genes/Alterations**: CASP8 (mutation 10%), HRAS (co-mutation, ~10%), FADD, BIRC2, BIRC3
- **Data availability**: available — mutations in hnsc_mdanderson_2013; TCGA HNSCC also in cBioPortal
- **Tool support**: native — co-occurrence analysis, mutation frequency query; subtype stratification requires custom query
- **Concepts**: #molecular-subtypes #mutation #driver-genes #HNSC

### Finding 5: TP53 Pathway Altered in 60% with Predominant Missense and Splice Site Mutations
- **Detail**: *TP53* mutations occurred in 60% (24/40) of tumors. Splice site mutations were enriched at 13%, far above the 2% background rate across tumor types. TP53 mutations correlated with more CNAs (p=0.0051). The TP53 pathway is a near-universal feature of OSCC, and splice site mutations—which typically abolish protein expression—represent a distinct and clinically relevant mutation class.
- **Genes/Alterations**: TP53 (mutation 60%), splice site enrichment (~13% of mutations)
- **Data availability**: available — in hnsc_mdanderson_2013
- **Tool support**: native — mutation type breakdown, lollipop plots available in cBioPortal
- **Concepts**: #TP53 #mutation #driver-genes #HNSC

### Finding 6: 80% of Tumors Harbor Targetable Alterations
- **Detail**: Seventy-six targetable genes were identified with at least one sample showing copy gain plus high expression or an activating mutation. Of these, 45 were targets of FDA-approved cancer agents. In total, 80% (28/35) of tumors had at least one targetable alteration and 54% (19/35) had two or more. Most notable candidates include PI3K signaling (*PIK3CA*, *AKT1/2/3*; 34%), SRC-family kinases (*SRC*, *LYN*, *YES1*; 29%), aurora kinases (*AURKA/C*; 14%), *TNK2* (17%), and *PTK2/FAK* (14%). EGFR downstream alterations may explain limited clinical benefit from single-agent cetuximab.
- **Genes/Alterations**: PIK3CA/AKT (34%), SRC-family kinases (29%), EGFR (16%), AURKA/C (14%), TNK2 (17%), FAK (14%)
- **Data availability**: available — CNA and expression data in hnsc_mdanderson_2013
- **Tool support**: native — actionability queries via OncoKB integration; pathway view
- **Concepts**: #actionability #targeted-therapy #driver-genes #copy-number

### Finding 7: Two Expression Subtypes Linked to Differentiation and Tobacco Use
- **Detail**: Hierarchical clustering of mRNA and microRNA data revealed two major expression clusters correlating with tumor differentiation state (p<0.002). Cluster 1 (7/8 poorly differentiated tumors) showed fewer point mutations, higher ploidy, and included all non-tobacco users (p<0.007). Cluster 2 (6/6 well-differentiated) showed more mutations and lower ploidy. Methylation clustering similarly revealed two groups, with tobacco use enriched in methylation cluster 2.
- **Genes/Alterations**: Expression subtypes defined by squamous differentiation, stromal, and immune gene signatures
- **Data availability**: partial — expression data in portal; methylation subtypes not queryable natively
- **Tool support**: api — RNA-seq expression data queryable via API; subtype assignment requires custom computation
- **Concepts**: #molecular-subtypes #RNA-seq #immune #mutation

## Open Questions
- Do the CASP8/HRAS co-mutant tumors respond differently to EGFR inhibitors or HRAS-targeted therapies, and can this be queried in the combined TCGA + hnsc_mdanderson_2013 datasets?
- Can the two expression subtypes (cluster 1 vs. cluster 2) in the MD Anderson cohort be mapped to subtypes defined in TCGA HNSCC to enable cross-cohort prognostic analysis in cBioPortal?
- Does Notch pathway disruption (multi-gene composite) correlate with clinical outcomes in the portal data?

## Limitations
- Small primary cohort (N=40 tumors) limits statistical power for subgroup analyses; most findings were validated in TCGA data
- HPV status was not systematically reported or analyzed (important confound for HNSCC prognosis)
- Proteomics data not available; gene expression and CNA correlations are molecular surrogates
- microRNA expression showed weak correlation with CNAs; this data type is not in cBioPortal
- Functional studies performed in cell lines/xenografts; clinical validation of targetable events remains to be done
