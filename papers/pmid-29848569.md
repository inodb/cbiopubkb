## Summary

This study prospectively profiled 195 patients with intrahepatic (IHC, 81%) or extrahepatic (EHC, 19%) cholangiocarcinoma using MSK-IMPACT, a targeted NGS panel covering 410 cancer-associated genes. The most common alterations in IHC were IDH1 (30%), ARID1A (23%), BAP1 (20%), TP53 (20%), and FGFR2 fusions (14%); several of these were mutually exclusive, suggesting distinct molecular subtypes. Actionable alterations were identified in 47% of patients, enabling biomarker-directed therapy or clinical trial enrollment in 16%. CDKN2A/B and ERBB2 alterations were independently associated with shorter overall survival and reduced time to progression on first-line chemotherapy. The study supports routine molecular profiling in advanced cholangiocarcinoma to guide targeted therapy selection.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: IDH1 and FGFR2 fusions are the dominant actionable alterations in IHC and are mutually exclusive
- **Finding**: IDH1 hotspot mutations (30%) and FGFR2 gene fusions (14%) are the most frequent actionable alterations in intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma and occur in a mutually exclusive pattern.
- **Detail**: 38 structural alterations were identified in 35 patients (18% overall); the majority were in-frame FGFR2 fusions with multiple partners (most frequent: BICC1, KIAA1217). IDH1:FGFR2 mutual exclusivity suggests they define biologically distinct IHC subtypes.
- **Genes**: IDH1, FGFR2, BICC1, KIAA1217
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: #FGFR #IDH1 #actionability #molecular-subtypes #cholangiocarcinoma

### Finding 2: CDKN2A/B and ERBB2 alterations carry negative prognostic value
- **Finding**: Deletions in CDKN2A/B and amplifications in ERBB2 were significantly associated with reduced overall survival and shorter time to progression on first-line chemotherapy in patients with advanced disease.
- **Detail**: CDKN2A deletions were found in 8% and ERBB2 amplifications in 4% of the cohort. These associations held in both univariate and multivariate Cox regression adjusting for age, sex, and sample type.
- **Genes**: CDKN2A, CDKN2B, ERBB2
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: #ERBB2 #copy-number #actionability #cholangiocarcinoma

### Finding 3: IHC and EHC have distinct mutation landscapes
- **Finding**: IHC is enriched for IDH1, ARID1A, BAP1, and FGFR2 fusions, whereas EHC has a different genomic profile with higher rates of TP53 and KRAS and fewer IDH1/FGFR2 alterations.
- **Detail**: The overall most commonly mutated genes were IDH1 (25%), TP53 (24%), ARID1A (21%), BAP1 (15%), KRAS (13%), PBRM1 (12%), SMAD4 (9%), and ATM (8%). Fisher's exact tests with FDR correction identified significant differences between IHC and EHC.
- **Genes**: IDH1, TP53, ARID1A, BAP1, KRAS, PBRM1, SMAD4, ATM
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: #molecular-subtypes #classification #cholangiocarcinoma #KRAS #TP53

### Finding 4: Multiple co-mutation pairs show mutual exclusivity in IHC
- **Finding**: Several gene pairs showed a tendency toward mutual exclusivity in IHC: TP53:IDH1, IDH1:KRAS, TP53:BAP1, and IDH1:FGFR2.
- **Detail**: These patterns suggest that distinct oncogenic driver pathways define non-overlapping subsets of IHC and may have implications for trial stratification and combination therapy design.
- **Genes**: TP53, IDH1, KRAS, BAP1, FGFR2
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: #molecular-subtypes #FGFR #KRAS #TP53 #cholangiocarcinoma

### Finding 5: 47% of patients harbor actionable alterations; 16% received matched therapy
- **Finding**: Using OncoKB classification (level ≥3B), actionable alterations were found in 93/195 patients (47.6%); 25 patients (16% of advanced-disease patients) received matched targeted therapy with a 64% response or clinical benefit rate.
- **Detail**: Level 2B alterations (FDA-approved in another tumor type) included ERBB2 amplification (6 pts), TSC1/2 (3 pts), BRCA1/2 (2 pts), BRAF V600E (1 pt), and MET amplification (1 pt). Level 3 was driven primarily by IDH1 mutations (43 pts) and FGFR2 fusions (17 pts). Additional low-frequency actionable mutations included PIK3CA, NRAS, and ERBB2 hotspots.
- **Genes**: IDH1, FGFR2, ERBB2, TSC1, TSC2, BRCA1, BRCA2, BRAF, MET, PIK3CA, NRAS
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: #actionability #targeted-therapy #FGFR #ERBB2 #PIK3CA #cholangiocarcinoma

### Finding 6: Mismatch repair deficiency is rare in advanced cholangiocarcinoma
- **Finding**: Only 1 patient (0.5%) showed a signature of mismatch repair (MMR) deficiency / MSI-H, a prevalence substantially lower than prior small retrospective series had suggested.
- **Detail**: The low prevalence was observed in a prospective, largely unselected metastatic cohort profiled with clinical NGS (MSK-IMPACT), which detects MSI signatures. Despite the rarity, the finding carries practical significance given the FDA approval of pembrolizumab for MSI-H tumors.
- **Genes**: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 (MMR genes; not individually called out)
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api

## Open Questions

1. Do acquired resistance mechanisms to FGFR inhibitors (e.g., secondary FGFR2 kinase mutations) differ across the diverse FGFR2 fusion partners identified, and can fusion partner identity predict response duration?
2. Would whole-genome or whole-exome sequencing plus RNA-seq identify additional actionable alterations (e.g., NTRK, ROS1 fusions) missed by the targeted panel, and at what clinical cost in turnaround time?
3. Do the negative prognostic implications of CDKN2A/B and ERBB2 alterations persist when controlling for EHC vs IHC subtype and prior surgical resection status?
4. Can co-mutation patterns (e.g., IDH1 with ARID1A or PBRM1) be leveraged to predict epigenetic vulnerability or sensitivity to combination targeted therapy?

## Limitations

1. **Targeted panel only**: MSK-IMPACT does not capture the full transcriptome; NTRK and ROS1 fusions, splice variants, and gene expression subtypes were not assessed, potentially underestimating the actionable fraction.
2. **Archival FFPE tissue**: All sequencing was performed on archival samples, so temporal evolution of the tumor genome between biopsy and treatment decision was not captured.
3. **Single-institution cohort**: The predominantly Caucasian (89%) MSK population may not represent global cholangiocarcinoma epidemiology (e.g., Asian populations with higher hepatitis B/fluke exposure).
4. **Limited statistical power for survival subgroup analyses**: Clinical and genomic heterogeneity, mixed IHC/EHC disease stages, and varying prior treatment histories constrain definitive prognostic conclusions for individual genes.
