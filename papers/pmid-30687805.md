## Summary

This 2018 prospective study performed real-time clinical MSK-IMPACT sequencing on 96 tumor samples from 80 patients with metastatic well-differentiated pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors (panNETs). Somatic alterations were detected in 95% of patients, with dominant hits in three functional pathways: chromatin remodeling factors (CRFs: *MEN1*, *DAXX*, *ATRX*, *ARID1A*), histone methyltransferases (HMTs: *MEN1*, *SETD2*, *KMT2C*), and the mTOR pathway (*TSC2*, *PTEN*). Loss of heterozygosity (LOH) across ≥50% of the genome was identified in 58% of samples. Survival analysis showed improved OS for *MEN1*- and *DAXX*/*ATRX*-altered tumors, and significantly worse OS for *BRAF*-altered tumors. Serial sequencing in 12 patients revealed tumor grade progression, clonal evolution, everolimus resistance mechanisms (*PTEN*, *RHEB*, *TSC2*+*AKT2*), and alkylating agent–associated hypermutation. Germline analysis of 76 cancer susceptibility genes in 88 patients identified pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants in 16%, including high-penetrance genes *MEN1*, *VHL*, and *TSC2*. This study is the primary data source for the `panet_msk_2018` cBioPortal study.

## Key Findings

### F1: Dominant somatic alterations cluster in chromatin remodeling, histone methyltransferase, and mTOR pathways

**Finding:** Somatic alterations were identified in 95% (76/80) of patients. The most commonly altered genes were *MEN1* (56%), *DAXX* (40%), *ATRX* (25%), and *TSC2* (25%), with pathway-level enrichment in CRFs (65%), HMTs (66%), and mTOR (43%).

**Detail:** CRF alterations included *DAXX* (40%), *ATRX* (25%), and *ARID1A* (11%), while HMT alterations were dominated by *MEN1* (56%) and *SETD2* (18%). mTOR pathway genes affected included *TSC2* (25%) and *PTEN* (13%). *TP53* mutations were seen in 13% and were associated with higher-grade tumors. This genomic landscape from metastatic cases parallels prior exome/genome studies of localized panNETs but demonstrates a higher *SETD2* frequency (18% vs. prior reports), reflecting aggressive post-treatment disease biology.

### F2: LOH spanning ≥50% of genome is highly prevalent and associates with survival benefit

**Finding:** Somatic loss of heterozygosity (LOH) across ≥50% of the genome was identified in 58% (55/95) of samples and was significantly associated with mutations in *MEN1*, *DAXX*, *PTEN*, and *TSC2* (q < 0.05). Presence of LOH trended toward improved overall survival (P = .06).

**Detail:** LOH was detected genome-wide by FACETS analysis and was recurrently observed on chromosomes 1, 2, 3, 6, 8, 10, 11, 15, 16, 21, and 22. The association between LOH and improved survival may reflect a more differentiated tumor phenotype characterized by classic CRF/HMT hits rather than aggressive TP53 pathway alterations. This contrasts with the poor-prognosis context of LOH in many other cancers.

### F3: MEN1/DAXX/ATRX alterations predict improved survival; BRAF alterations predict poor survival

**Finding:** Significantly longer 1-year overall survival was observed in *MEN1*-altered (98% vs. 82%, P = .01) and *DAXX*/*ATRX*-altered (97% vs. 83%, P < .01) tumors vs. wild-type. Significantly inferior 1-year OS was seen in *BRAF*-altered tumors (75% vs. 95%, P < .01).

**Detail:** With median follow-up of 27.6 months, 14 deaths occurred. *BRAF* alterations (8% of patients, 6/80) included the canonical V600E as well as non-V600 variants (K601E, T599K, T310I), and were predominantly identified in post-treatment biopsy specimens — suggesting selection during therapy or an aggressive metastatic phenotype. *SETD2* alterations also trended toward aggressive histopathology (predominantly G2–G3 tumors, mean Ki-67 20%). No survival differences were observed for mTOR pathway or *TP53* alterations.

### F4: Serial sequencing reveals clonal evolution, tumor grade progression, and resistance mechanisms

**Finding:** Among 12 patients with pre- and post-treatment sequencing, 8 (67%) showed increased tumor grade. Specific molecular resistance mechanisms were captured: everolimus resistance via *PTEN* (Q298*), *RHEB* (Y35S), and *TSC2*+*AKT2* acquisition; BRAF V600E inhibitor (vemurafenib) resistance via *NRAS* Q61R.

**Detail:** Three patients treated with everolimus each developed distinct mTOR pathway alterations at progression (*PTEN*, *RHEB*, *TSC2*/*AKT2*), confirming on-target resistance evolution. One patient on vemurafenib acquired an *NRAS* Q61R hotspot mutation — a classical RAS-mediated BRAF inhibitor resistance mechanism, here reported for the first time in panNET. Separately, three patients treated with alkylating agents (dacarbazine, temozolomide, oxaliplatin) developed profound hypermutation (>100 new mutations), with C>T transition signatures consistent with alkylating agent mutagenesis and acquisition of mismatch repair gene mutations, mirroring patterns seen in malignant glioma.

### F5: Germline pathogenic variants in 16% of panNET patients, including non-syndromic TSC2 carriers

**Finding:** Germline analysis of 76 cancer susceptibility genes in 88 panNET patients identified pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants in 16% (14/88): 3% in high-penetrance genes (*MEN1*, *VHL*, *TSC2*), 5% *CHEK2* (4 patients), and 8% in low-penetrance genes (monoallelic *MUTYH*, *APC* I1307K).

**Detail:** One notable case involved a 32-year-old patient with a germline *TSC2* mutation who did not meet clinical criteria for tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC) testing — the diagnosis was incidental; subsequent brain MRI identified tubers and radial bands confirming TSC. This highlights underdiagnosis of TSC in panNET patients. Given that *MEN1* and *TSC2* are among the most common somatic drivers of panNETs, germline mutations in these same genes likely represent an underappreciated hereditary contribution. The data support germline testing for all panNET patients regardless of clinical syndromic features.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MEN1`, `VHL`, `TSC2`, `CHEK2`, `MUTYH`, `APC` · panNET

## Open Questions

- Do *BRAF* non-V600 mutations in panNETs (K601E, T599K, T310I) confer sensitivity to MEK inhibitors or class II BRAF inhibitors, distinct from V600E-directed agents?
- Can serial cfDNA monitoring replace invasive repeat biopsies for tracking clonal evolution and grade progression in metastatic panNETs?
- Is the frequency of *SETD2* mutations at 18% in this metastatic cohort (vs. lower in localized disease) a true biology of progression, or selection artifact from post-treatment biopsies?
- Does the presence of alkylating agent–induced hypermutation and mismatch repair gene acquisition create MSI-high tumors that would respond to immune checkpoint inhibition?
- Do germline *CHEK2* or monoallelic *MUTYH* variants in panNET patients confer meaningful cancer risk beyond panNET, and do they require altered surveillance?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n = 80) limits statistical power for subgroup analyses; survival findings based on 14 deaths.
- Only 12 patients had serial sequencing; clonal evolution patterns are descriptive and not systematically powered.
- No matched control population of localized/non-metastatic panNETs for direct comparison of genomic landscape.
- mTOR pathway alteration did not predict everolimus response, but patient numbers per subgroup were too small to be conclusive.
- Germline data not prospectively returned to all patients; cascade testing and long-term outcome of incidental germline findings not captured.
- MSK-IMPACT panel size varied across accrual (275–468 genes), creating potential for version-dependent ascertainment bias in less common genes.
