## Summary

Landmark TCGA multi-platform analysis of 200 adult de novo AML cases using WGS (n=50), WES (n=150), RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, and Illumina 450K DNA methylation arrays. Key findings: AML genomes carry far fewer mutations than other adult cancers (~13 coding mutations/sample, ~5 recurrently mutated); 23 significantly mutated genes organize into nine functional categories covering >99% of samples; co-occurring FLT3/NPM1/DNMT3A mutations define a novel AML subtype; IDH1/IDH2 mutations drive extensive DNA hypermethylation; and strong methylation signatures occur in CpG-sparse rather than CpG-island regions. RNA-seq and miRNA-seq groups align with FAB subtypes and cytogenetic risk. Full dataset available in `laml_tcga_pub` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: AML has exceptionally low mutation burden; 23 significantly mutated genes identified

**Finding:** AML genomes carry an average of only 13 coding (tier 1) mutations per sample — far fewer than most other adult solid cancers — with a mean of 5 recurrently mutated per genome. Using MuSiC, 23 significantly mutated genes (FDR < 0.05) were identified, including established drivers (FLT3, NPM1, DNMT3A, IDH1/2, CEBPA) and recently implicated genes (U2AF1, EZH2, SMC1A, SMC3).

**Detail:** Of 2,315 coding SNVs and 270 indels, 66% were missense and 71% of indels caused frameshifts. Three samples had no recurring coding mutations but harbored well-recognized fusion events (NUP98-NSD1, MLL-MLLT3, MLL-MLLT10) sufficient to initiate AML. MLL fusion samples had the fewest cooperating mutations (mean 2.09 vs. 5.24 overall), suggesting they require fewer co-drivers. Samples with RUNX1-RUNX1T1 fusions had significantly more co-mutations (mean 7.85).

**Genes / cancer types:** FLT3, NPM1, DNMT3A, IDH1, IDH2, CEBPA, U2AF1, EZH2, SMC1A, SMC3, RUNX1, TET2, TP53, WT1, PTPN11, KIT · AML

### F2: Nine functional gene categories cover >99% of AML samples; strong mutual exclusivity within categories

**Finding:** Mutations were grouped into nine biologically defined categories: transcription-factor fusions (18%), NPM1 (27%), tumor-suppressor genes (16%), DNA-methylation–related genes (44%), activated signaling genes (59%), chromatin-modifying genes (30%), myeloid transcription-factor genes (22%), cohesin-complex genes (13%), and spliceosome-complex genes (14%). Of 200 samples, 199 (>99%) had at least one mutation in one of these categories.

**Detail:** Dendrix++ identified three mutually exclusive gene sets. Group A: TF fusions, NPM1, RUNX1, TP53, CEBPA (functionally alternative AML initiators). Group B: FLT3 vs. other kinases/RAS family (one signaling driver per tumor). Group C: ASXL1, cohesin genes, myeloid TF genes, epigenetic modifiers. Spliceosome genes (U2AF1, SF3B1, SRSF2) also show strong intra-category exclusivity, suggesting single pathway saturation is sufficient.

**Genes / cancer types:** NPM1, FLT3, DNMT3A, IDH1, IDH2, TET2, ASXL1, EZH2, RUNX1, CEBPA, TP53, U2AF1, SMC1A, SMC3, MLL-fusions, PML-RARA · AML

### F3: FLT3/NPM1/DNMT3A co-occurrence defines a novel epigenetic AML subtype

**Finding:** Co-occurrence of mutations in NPM1, FLT3, and DNMT3A was far more frequent than expected by chance (P < 6.3×10⁻⁷ for NPM1/DNMT3A; P < 1.9×10⁻⁶ for NPM1/FLT3). Samples with all three mutations form a distinct cluster in mRNA, miRNA, and DNA methylation analyses and show extensive loss of methylation at CpG-sparse loci relative to normal CD34+CD38− hematopoietic stem cells.

**Detail:** The NPM1/FLT3/DNMT3A triple-mutant group exhibits characteristic miRNA expression: high miR-10a (consistent with published NPM1-associated signature), low miR-424, and high miR-196b. Methylation losses affect 328 of 382 differentially methylated regions >1 kb, predominantly in gene body and intergenic CpG-sparse regions. These data suggest this co-mutation combination represents a unique epigenetic AML subtype with specific molecular vulnerabilities beyond its cytogenetic classification.

### F4: IDH1/IDH2 mutations drive extensive DNA methylation gain; distinct from TF-fusion and NPM1 patterns

**Finding:** Samples with IDH1 or IDH2 mutations (20% of cases, mutually exclusive) showed extensive gains in DNA methylation relative to normal CD34+CD38− cells, particularly at CpG-sparse regions — a methylation phenotype distinct from that of TF-fusion or NPM1/DNMT3A/FLT3 samples, which instead show widespread methylation loss. IDH mutations thus drive a hypermethylator phenotype in AML analogous to but distinct from CIMP in colorectal cancer.

**Detail:** IDH1/IDH2 mutations produce the oncometabolite 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG), which competitively inhibits α-KG–dependent dioxygenases including TET2, explaining the gain-of-methylation phenotype. Consistent with this, IDH1/2 and TET2 mutations are mutually exclusive (both disrupt the same methylation regulatory axis). 42% of all CpG sites tested (160,519 loci) showed significant differential methylation across AML samples; 67% were gains and 33% losses. IDH mutations drove the majority of gains.

**Genes / cancer types:** IDH1, IDH2, TET2, DNMT3A · AML

### F5: Clonal architecture: most AML samples contain a founding clone plus ≥1 subclone

**Finding:** Deep digital sequencing of WGS samples enabled VAF-based cluster analysis to define clonal composition. More than half of tumors contained a founding clone plus at least one subclone; up to three independent subclones were identified in a single tumor. All samples had exactly one founding clone, confirming the clonal origin of AML. MLL-fusion AMLs had fewer cooperating co-driver mutations, suggesting a lower threshold for clonal expansion.

**Detail:** WGS mean coverage was 30.54× (limiting detection of subclones with VAF < 10%), while exome coverage was 167.50×. The number of tier 1 mutations was not significantly different between WGS (14.5/sample) and exome (12.7/sample). Most mutations in AML are present in nearly all cells of each sample (high VAF), consistent with a pre-leukemic stem cell origin where the founding clone captures a history of mutations from normal hematopoiesis. RUNX1-RUNX1T1 samples had unusually high co-driver counts (mean 7.85), suggesting their founding event may require more collaboration.

### F6: Seven RNA-seq expression groups align with FAB subtypes; concordant with miRNA and methylation data

**Finding:** Unsupervised NMF consensus clustering of RNA-seq data identified 7 optimal groups, with miRNA-seq yielding 5 groups. RNA group 3 aligned with FAB M3 (PML-RARA/APL), group 5 with M4 (myelomonocytic), group 7 with M5 (monocytic), and group 4 with M1. The APL (PML-RARA) group was the most coherent across mRNA, miRNA, and methylation data, with a striking imprinted small RNA cluster on chromosome 14 uniquely dysregulated in this group.

**Detail:** Association between expression groups and overall survival showed PML-RARA group had the best outcomes, consistent with known all-trans retinoic acid sensitivity. Concordance between RNA-seq groups and Affymetrix microarray data from the same 178 samples was high, validating the expression-based classification. Associations in CpG-sparse methylation were stronger with miRNA groups than mRNA groups, suggesting epigenetic regulation of miRNA is a key layer of AML biology. miR-10a was highly expressed in the NPM1/DNMT3A/FLT3 group.

## Open Questions

- Does the NPM1/FLT3/DNMT3A triple-mutant epigenetic AML subtype predict differential response to targeted therapy (e.g., FLT3 inhibitors, DNMT3A-targeting approaches)?
- What is the functional significance of methylation changes in CpG-sparse versus CpG-island regions — do they drive or reflect transcriptional reprogramming?
- Can the nine-category framework be used prospectively for treatment stratification beyond current cytogenetic and NPM1/FLT3/CEBPA testing?
- Do the clonal substructures identified (founding clone + subclones) predict relapse trajectory and the specific mutations that dominate at relapse?
- How does acquired DNMT3A mutation in clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) progress to leukemia, and does this cohort data help model that evolution?

## Limitations

- WGS performed on only 50 of 200 samples; clonal architecture analysis is incomplete for the exome-only cases
- Methylation arrays (HM450) cover ~450K CpG sites but leave the majority of the genome unprobed
- No prospective survival data at time of publication; relapse and treatment outcome associations were limited
- Cohort enriched for favorable/intermediate cytogenetic risk; unfavorable (complex karyotype) cases underrepresented relative to clinical incidence
- Study predates modern targeted therapies (FLT3i, IDH1/2i, venetoclax), limiting direct therapeutic translation
