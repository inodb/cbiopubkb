## Summary

Landmark Broad Institute WES/WGS analysis of 183 lung adenocarcinoma tumor/normal pairs. Identified a mean exonic somatic mutation rate of 12.0 mutations/Mb — among the highest for epithelial tumors — with a strong smoking-associated C→A transversion signature. Confirmed known drivers (KRAS 27%, TP53 50%, EGFR 17%, STK11 15%) and discovered novel recurrently mutated genes: splicing factor U2AF1 (S34F hotspot, 3%), RNA-binding protein RBM10 (7%), and chromatin remodeler ARID1A (8%). WGS analysis of 24 cases revealed 2,349 structural rearrangements including novel in-frame fusions in EGFR and kinase SIK2. Mutation spectrum clustering into 5 groups correlated with smoking history and driver gene status. U2AF1 and TP53 mutations were independently associated with reduced progression-free survival. Full dataset in `luad_broad` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Smoking drives a C→A transversion signature; five mutation clusters correlate with clinical features

**Finding:** Smokers had a median mutation rate of 9.8/Mb vs. 1.7/Mb in never-smokers (P=3.0×10⁻⁹). Unsupervised clustering of 96-context mutation spectra yielded five clusters: Cluster 1 (CpG→T, low rate) was enriched in never/light smokers; Cluster 4 (additional C→T and TpC transversions) was enriched in advanced-stage tumors; Cluster 5 comprised hypermutators.

**Detail:** The dominant signatures are C→A transversions (tobacco carcinogen adducts) and CpG→T transitions (endogenous deamination). The ratio of these two signatures was used to impute smoking status with >75% accuracy. Cluster 1 strongly anti-correlated with KRAS, STK11, SMARCA4, and KEAP1 mutations and positively correlated with EGFR mutations, consistent with clinical associations of EGFR with never-smokers. KRAS driver alterations associated specifically with Cluster 3.

**Genes / cancer types:** KRAS, EGFR, STK11, KEAP1, SMARCA4 · Lung adenocarcinoma

### F2: Twenty-five significantly mutated genes; novel candidates U2AF1, RBM10, and ARID1A

**Finding:** The InVEx algorithm (exploiting abundant non-coding mutations as a within-gene background) nominated 25 significantly mutated genes, including 13 previously reported (TP53, KRAS, EGFR, STK11, KEAP1, NF1, BRAF, SMAD4, SMARCA4, RB1, CTNNB1, PIK3CA, ATM) and novel candidates U2AF1, RBM10, ARID1A, BRD3, FGFR3, SETD2, FBXW7, PTEN, CBL, and ARID1B.

**Detail:** U2AF1 encodes a spliceosome U2-auxiliary factor; the recurrent S34F hotspot (identical to the mutation in myelodysplastic syndrome) was found in 4 of 5 mutant cases (3% overall). RBM10, an RNA-binding protein, had 7% mutation frequency with 4% carrying truncating events. ARID1A (SWI/SNF complex) had 8% frequency enriched for nonsense/frameshift mutations. Co-occurrence with canonical oncogenes (KRAS, EGFR, PIK3CA) for both RBM10 and U2AF1 suggests these are secondary hits rather than single-driver events.

### F3: U2AF1 and TP53 mutations independently predict reduced progression-free survival

**Finding:** Among 135 patients with PFS data, U2AF1 mutations (P=0.00011, log-rank) and TP53 mutations (P=0.0014) were significantly associated with shorter PFS. No other significantly mutated gene showed a survival association. U2AF1 mutation was also anti-correlated with spectrum Cluster 1 and co-occurred with NF1 mutations.

**Detail:** Five U2AF1-mutant patients with survival data all showed markedly reduced PFS. The co-occurrence of U2AF1 with KRAS mutations in at least one case suggests that spliceosomal disruption may amplify oncogenic signaling. TP53's survival impact is consistent with prior reports in LUAD. These associations provide potential biomarker value: U2AF1 S34F may identify a molecularly distinct high-risk subset regardless of smoking or EGFR/KRAS status.

**Genes / cancer types:** U2AF1, TP53 · Lung adenocarcinoma

### F4: WGS identifies 2,349 structural rearrangements including novel kinase fusions in EGFR and SIK2

**Finding:** WGS of 24 cases revealed 2,349 somatic rearrangements (median 98 per tumor). Frame-preserving rearrangements (71 total) included novel in-frame exonic alterations within EGFR and SIK2 kinases. Known tumor suppressors disrupted by rearrangement include CDKN2A, STK11, and APC.

**Detail:** CDKN2A had the highest rearrangement rate for its size (4.3/sequenced Mb), with antisense fusions and in-frame deletions providing an alternative inactivation mechanism beyond mutation and homozygous deletion. EGFR harbored in-frame exonic deletions representing novel variant classes distinct from classical L858R/exon 19 deletions. SIK2 kinase fusions were novel findings implicating this salt-inducible kinase in LUAD pathogenesis. ALK, RET, and ROS1 fusions — established oncogenic drivers — were also detected. ROCK1 and MAST2 fusions were identified as additional novel candidates.

**Genes / cancer types:** EGFR, SIK2, CDKN2A, STK11, APC, ROCK1, MAST2, ALK, RET, ROS1 · Lung adenocarcinoma

### F5: Copy number landscape includes frequent TERT, MYC, EGFR gains and CDKN2A losses

**Finding:** High-frequency somatic copy number alterations (SNP arrays, n=183): gains in TERT (42%, 15% focal), MYC (31%), EGFR (22%), NKX2-1 (18%, 10% focal); losses in CDKN2A (24%, 10% homozygous), TP53 (18%), SMAD4, KEAP1, SMARCA4.

**Detail:** TERT amplification is the most frequent alteration overall, consistent with its role in enabling replicative immortality. NKX2-1 (TTF-1) focal amplification confirms this lineage oncogene as a recurrent target in LUAD specifically. EGFR amplification (22%) substantially exceeds EGFR mutation frequency (17%), suggesting that additional EGFR-amplified tumors without classic mutations may exist. CDKN2A homozygous deletion (10%) co-occurs with rearrangements (WGS data), establishing multiple parallel inactivation routes.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, MYC, EGFR, NKX2-1, CDKN2A, TP53, SMAD4, KEAP1, SMARCA4 · Lung adenocarcinoma

## Open Questions

1. Do U2AF1 S34F mutations produce specific splicing alterations that could be exploited therapeutically, as has been shown in myeloid malignancies?
2. Can the 5-cluster mutation spectrum signature be validated prospectively as a biomarker of response to platinum chemotherapy, immunotherapy, or targeted agents?
3. What are the oncogenic mechanisms of the novel SIK2 and MAST2 kinase fusions, and are they actionable?
4. How do RBM10 truncating mutations interact with EGFR and KRAS to modulate tumor biology?
5. Does the distinct biology of never-smoker LUAD (Cluster 1, EGFR-enriched) warrant separate therapeutic strategies beyond EGFR TKIs?

## Limitations

- WGS available for only 24 of 183 cases, limiting structural variant discovery power.
- Cohort is enriched for early-stage (90 stage I) primary resection specimens; findings may not fully represent advanced/metastatic LUAD.
- PFS analysis is limited to 135 patients with follow-up; survival associations should be validated in larger cohorts.
- InVEx algorithm calibrated on this cohort's background rates; novel genes at low frequency may require larger samples for validation.
- Functional validation of most novel candidate genes (U2AF1 excepted) was not performed in this study.
