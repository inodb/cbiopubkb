## Summary

This landmark pan-cancer study from MSK analyzes the clinical and genomic data of 1,662 advanced cancer patients treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) and 5,371 non-ICI-treated controls, all profiled by MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing. Tumor mutational burden (TMB), defined as somatic non-synonymous mutations per megabase, was associated with improved overall survival (OS) in ICI-treated patients across a wide variety of cancer histologies (HR=0.52 for top 20% TMB; p=1.6×10⁻⁶), but had no prognostic effect in non-ICI-treated controls. The TMB thresholds defining "high TMB" varied markedly across cancer types, arguing against a universal cutpoint. Glioma was a notable exception — higher TMB was not associated with improved ICI survival, possibly reflecting temozolomide-induced hypermutation rather than immunogenic neoantigen burden. This study provided key evidence for FDA approval of the tissue-agnostic pembrolizumab indication for TMB-high tumors (≥10 mut/Mb) in 2020 and established the tmb_mskcc_2018 cBioPortal dataset as a widely used public resource.

## Key Findings

### F1: Higher TMB (top 20% within each histology) is associated with improved overall survival in ICI-treated patients (HR=0.52, p=1.6×10⁻⁶)

**Finding:** Across 1,662 ICI-treated patients, tumors in the highest TMB decile within each histology had the strongest survival benefit (HR for top 10%: 0.52; 95% CI: 0.42–0.64). A clear dose-response relationship was observed: increasing the TMB cutoff threshold progressively lowered the hazard ratio for death, with the top 10% group showing the largest survival benefit. This relationship was consistent across a wide variety of cancer types and ICI agent classes.

**Detail:** Survival analysis was performed stratifying by TMB decile within each histology (to account for the vastly different TMB distributions across tumor types). Kaplan-Meier curves and Cox regression confirmed the significant OS benefit for high TMB patients. Multivariable Cox analysis adjusting for cancer type, age, drug class, and year of ICI initiation showed TMB remained significant both as a continuous variable (HR=0.985 per unit increase, p=3.4×10⁻⁷) and as a binary variable (top 20% vs. bottom 80%: HR=0.61, p=1.3×10⁻⁷). Importantly, combination ICI therapy (e.g., nivolumab + ipilimumab) was associated with better OS than monotherapy (HR=0.67 vs. PD-1/PD-L1 reference), while CTLA4 monotherapy showed no significant difference.

### F2: TMB cutpoints for ICI benefit vary markedly across cancer types — no universal threshold exists

**Finding:** The absolute TMB cutpoint corresponding to the top 20% (associated with improved ICI survival) varied markedly across cancer types — from relatively low values in some cancers (e.g., RCC, where TMB is generally low) to high values in others (e.g., colorectal cancer at 52.2 mut/Mb, likely due to enrichment of MSI-high tumors). This variation argues strongly against a single universal "high TMB" definition applicable across all histologies.

**Detail:** Cancer-type-specific TMB distributions reflect distinct underlying mutational processes (UV damage in melanoma, APOBEC in bladder, tobacco in NSCLC, MMR deficiency in colorectal/endometrial, aging in various tumors). Using histology-specific TMB percentiles equalizes the comparison by ensuring 20% of patients in each cancer type are classified as "high TMB" regardless of absolute mutation count, making the survival comparison within histology meaningful. The colorectal top-20% cutpoint of 52.2 mut/Mb is dramatically higher than most other cancer types and likely captures the MSI-high/dMMR subgroup that is known to respond to pembrolizumab. Despite these differences, the directional trend of higher TMB → better ICI survival was observed across nearly all cancers, with glioma as the primary exception.

### F3: TMB has no prognostic value in non-ICI-treated patients — confirming it is predictive (not prognostic) of ICI benefit

**Finding:** In 5,371 metastatic cancer patients who did not receive ICI therapy, higher TMB was not associated with improved OS (HR=1.12, p=0.11), and this lack of prognostic effect was observed consistently within each histology. This critical comparison confirms that the survival benefit of high TMB is specifically predictive of ICI response rather than a general indicator of tumor indolence or favorable biology.

**Detail:** This non-ICI control cohort is essential for establishing predictive (ICI-specific) vs. prognostic (disease-wide) biomarker status. If high TMB tumors simply had better outcomes regardless of treatment, the OS benefit in ICI patients could be confounded by favorable tumor biology. The null result in non-ICI patients rules this out, supporting TMB as a true predictive ICI biomarker. The comparison also controls for potential selection bias (high-TMB patients more likely to receive ICI): analyses using TMB percentiles calculated from the combined ICI + non-ICI cohort gave similar results to histology-specific ICI-only percentiles.

### F4: Glioma is an exception — higher TMB does not predict ICI survival benefit, likely due to temozolomide-induced hypermutation

**Finding:** Glioma was the clearest exception to the pan-cancer TMB–ICI survival association: in glioma patients, higher TMB trended toward *worse* survival after ICI. This paradox is attributed to the fact that high TMB in many glioma patients reflects prior temozolomide (alkylator) treatment, which generates subclonal non-immunogenic mutations rather than the clonal immunogenic neoantigens that drive ICI response.

**Detail:** Temozolomide causes G:C-to-A:T transitions at non-CpG sites (characteristic alkylation signature), leading to a hypermutator phenotype — particularly in MGMT-methylated GBMs that survive initial therapy but develop MMR loss. These treatment-induced hypermutations are typically subclonal (present in a subset of cells), highly heterogeneous across tumor regions, and generate neoantigens with lower immunogenicity than the clonal UV- or APOBEC-driven mutations that predict ICI response in melanoma and bladder cancer. The glioma exception highlights that "TMB" is not a monolithic biomarker: the biological source and clonality of the mutations contributing to TMB matter as much as the total count. This finding was consistent with limited evidence of ICI efficacy in unselected GBM clinical trials.

### F5: Combination ICI therapy shows additional OS benefit independent of TMB, suggesting complementary predictive biomarkers needed

**Finding:** In the multivariable Cox model, combination ICI (e.g., anti-PD-1 + anti-CTLA-4) was independently associated with better OS compared to PD-1/PD-L1 monotherapy (HR=0.67, p=6.6×10⁻⁴), while CTLA-4 monotherapy showed no significant difference. This independent effect of combination therapy, beyond what is explained by TMB, suggests that additional biomarkers (beyond TMB) are needed to identify patients most likely to benefit from combination ICI and to balance toxicity against efficacy.

**Detail:** The survival benefit of combination ICI was independent of TMB in the multivariable model, indicating that combination therapy provides benefit even in some lower-TMB patients. This is consistent with the known mechanism of anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab) — which activates T cells early in the priming phase and may expand immune responses even when neoantigen burden is modest. The year of ICI initiation was also independently significant (p=2.3×10⁻⁸), reflecting improving outcomes over time due to advances in patient selection, supportive care, and combination strategies. These findings support a multi-biomarker approach to ICI patient selection beyond TMB alone.

## Open Questions

- Can histology-specific TMB percentile cutpoints (rather than universal 10 mut/Mb) improve the positive predictive value of TMB for ICI benefit and reduce overtreatment of TMB-high but ICI-unresponsive patients?
- Does clonal TMB (fraction from clonal mutations only) outperform total TMB in predicting ICI response, as suggested by the glioma exception (subclonal hypermutation)?
- Can the combination of TMB with other biomarkers (PD-L1 IHC, MSI status, tumor infiltrating lymphocyte score, immune gene expression signature) create a composite ICI response predictor superior to any single biomarker?
- What molecular features (other than MMR deficiency and alkylator exposure) explain why some cancer types (e.g., prostate, pancreatic) with high TMB tumors still do not benefit from ICI?
- As MSK-IMPACT panel sizes evolve (341 → 410 → 468 genes), do TMB estimates remain comparable across panel versions, and how should TMB be harmonized across clinical labs?
- Does the tissue-agnostic FDA pembrolizumab approval for TMB ≥10 mut/Mb (2020) — derived partly from this study — hold up in prospective validation across the cancer types excluded from this analysis?

## Limitations

- Retrospective observational study with heterogeneous patient population and variable prior treatment history; selection bias toward patients who received ICI at MSK
- TMB measured by targeted panel (MSK-IMPACT) rather than WES; panel-based TMB may not fully capture genome-wide mutation load, particularly in poorly covered regions or non-coding drivers
- Different MSK-IMPACT panel versions (341, 410, 468 genes) used across patients and years; TMB estimates normalized per megabase but residual variability exists
- Timing of MSK-IMPACT testing relative to ICI start was variable; TMB measured at different disease stages may not reflect TMB at time of ICI treatment
- OS as the primary endpoint can be confounded by post-ICI treatments; progression-free survival analysis limited to subgroups with available response data
- TMB as a continuous variable shows gradual HR improvement; the binary "top 20%" cutpoint is arbitrary and may differ from optimal clinical decision threshold
- Glioma exception highlights that TMB is not a universal biomarker, but the specific mechanism (subclonal vs. clonal mutations, immunogenicity) is not directly measured here
- No independent external validation cohort; all patients from a single institution (MSK)
