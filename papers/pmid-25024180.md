## Summary

Two independent MSK prostate cancer cohorts (initial n=168, median follow-up 8 years; contemporary n=104, median follow-up 6 years) were profiled for genome-wide copy number alterations (CNA). CNA burden — defined as the percentage of the autosomal tumour genome bearing copy number gains or losses — is significantly associated with biochemical recurrence (BCR) and metastasis, independent of PSA and Gleason score. CNA burden also stratifies outcomes within the clinically challenging Gleason 7 intermediate-risk group. Importantly, CNA burden can be measured from FFPE diagnostic needle biopsies using low-input whole-genome sequencing, providing a pre-treatment prognostic tool. Data are available in cBioPortal as `prad_mskcc_2014`.

## Key Findings

### F1: CNA burden is associated with biochemical recurrence in two independent prostate cancer cohorts

**Finding:** CNA burden (% autosomal genome bearing CNAs) is significantly associated with BCR in both the initial (HR = 1.09 per 1% CNA change; P < 0.001) and contemporary (HR = 1.05; P = 0.008) cohorts as a continuous variable; stratification at the 5.41% intermediate CNA threshold yields HRs of 1.99 and 3.85 respectively.

**Detail:** CNA burden in primary prostate cancer ranges from <0.1% to >50%, with cohort means of 5% and 4% — substantially lower than metastatic prostate cancer (~32%) or bladder/renal carcinomas. Stratification at the independently defined intermediate CNA cluster mean (5.41%) separates BCR risk: 13–15% at 5 years (low CNA) vs. 29–38% (high CNA). Results hold using cohort-internal quartile thresholds as well.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PTEN`, `RB1`, `TP53`, `MYC` (focal CNAs context) · Primary prostate adenocarcinoma

**Data availability:** `available`
> CNA data (segment-level and gene-level) available in `prad_mskcc_2014` on cBioPortal. Clinical follow-up including BCR is available.

**Tool support:** `native`
> CNA profiles viewable in Plots and OncoPrint. Survival analysis by CNA burden requires computing the burden metric; native BCR endpoint available. CNA burden as a continuous variable requires API extraction of segment data.

### F2: CNA burden independently predicts metastasis as a definitive clinical endpoint

**Finding:** In the initial cohort (now with sufficient metastatic events, n=19), CNA burden is significantly associated with metastasis as a continuous variable (HR = 1.10; P = 0.0004) and approaches significance as a stratified variable (log-rank P = 0.044), independent of PSA and Gleason score.

**Detail:** Metastasis rates of 11% (initial) and 3% (contemporary) reflect long follow-up required for this endpoint. Contemporary cohort had too few events (n=3) for metastasis analysis. CNA burden adds information beyond focal CNAs: PTEN loss and CNA burden are each independently associated with BCR when combined in multivariate models (PTEN HR = 3.04, P = 0.001; CNA burden HR = 1.09, P = 0.001).

**Genes / cancer types:** `PTEN`, `MYC`, `TP53`, `RB1` · Primary prostate adenocarcinoma

**Data availability:** `available`
> Metastasis and BCR clinical endpoints available in `prad_mskcc_2014`. PTEN, MYC, TP53, RB1 focal CNA status available.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Survival curves by PTEN loss or other focal CNAs native in cBioPortal. Combined CNA burden + focal CNA multivariate analysis requires API export and external Cox regression.

### F3: CNA burden stratifies outcome within the intermediate-risk Gleason 7 population

**Finding:** Within Gleason 7 (intermediate-risk) prostate cancers — clinically the most ambiguous group — CNA burden is significantly associated with BCR in both cohorts (HR = 1.08 and 1.06; P = 0.011 and 0.017), independent of pretreatment PSA and nomogram score.

**Detail:** CNA burden in Gleason 7 tumours ranges from 0.05–25% (initial) and 0.003–50% (contemporary). After correcting for Stephenson 5-year nomogram, CNA burden remains associated with BCR in the contemporary cohort (P = 0.014) and approaches significance in the initial cohort (P = 0.062). Too few Gleason 7 metastases available for analysis. The concordance index does not improve above ~0.7 from the nomogram alone, suggesting CNA burden complements but does not replace clinical risk factors.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide CNA · Primary prostate adenocarcinoma (Gleason 7 / intermediate risk)

**Data availability:** `available`
> Gleason score and BCR data available in `prad_mskcc_2014` for subgroup analysis. CNA segment data enables CNA burden computation.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Gleason subgroup filtering in Study View. CNA burden computation from segment data requires API extraction and external calculation. Survival analysis of the resulting subgroups then performable in cBioPortal or external tools.

**Concepts:** #Gleason7 #intermediate-risk #active-surveillance #CNA-burden #risk-stratification

### F4: CNA burden can be measured from FFPE diagnostic needle biopsies using low-input WGS

**Finding:** CNA burden is measurable in routine FFPE prostate needle biopsy specimens using low-input whole-genome sequencing, establishing feasibility for pre-treatment prognostic assessment without requiring prostatectomy tissue.

**Detail:** This is a proof-of-concept demonstration using a subset of biopsy specimens. Low-input WGS of FFPE material generates sufficient CNA signal to compute genome-wide burden. This is clinically important because biopsies are the standard diagnostic material; prostatectomy specimens are only available post-surgery, limiting pre-operative risk stratification.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide CNA · Prostate cancer (diagnostic biopsy setting)

**Data availability:** `partial`
> The prostatectomy CNA data are in `prad_mskcc_2014`; the biopsy feasibility dataset is described as a subset and may not be fully represented in the public cBioPortal study.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> Low-input WGS biopsy data analysis requires external bioinformatics pipelines (CNA calling from low-coverage WGS). Not directly reproducible within cBioPortal; results would need to be uploaded as custom clinical attributes.

**Concepts:** #FFPE-biopsy #low-input-WGS #pre-treatment #clinical-deployment #prostate

### F5: CNA landscape of primary prostate cancer: recurrent focal alterations at PTEN, RB1, TP53

**Finding:** The contemporary cohort recapitulates the canonical prostate CNA landscape: frequent losses on chromosomes 6p, 8p, 13q, 16p; gains on chromosomes 7 and 8q; focal deletions at PTEN, RB1, TP53 and amplification at MYC — concordant with prior MSK, Barbieri et al., and TCGA prostate cohorts.

**Detail:** Average CNA burden in primary prostate cancer (~4–5%) is markedly lower than in metastatic prostate cancer (~32%) and other urogenital cancers (bladder, RCC), reflecting genomic evolution with disease progression. CNA burden is highest in tumours that subsequently metastasise. This landscape is reproduced across independent cohorts, validating the metric's robustness.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PTEN`, `RB1`, `TP53`, `MYC` · Primary prostate adenocarcinoma; comparison with bladder and RCC

**Data availability:** `available`
> Full CNA landscape in `prad_mskcc_2014`. Comparable TCGA prostate data available as `prad_tcga` in cBioPortal for cross-cohort comparison.

**Tool support:** `native`
> CNA heatmaps, focal alteration frequencies, and cross-study comparison available natively. OncoPrint for PTEN/RB1/TP53/MYC focal CNA status available.

## Open Questions

- Does CNA burden improve pre-operative risk prediction when combined with RNA-based signatures (e.g., Oncotype DX Genomic Prostate Score) that are already in clinical use?
- Can CNA burden from ctDNA (cell-free DNA) in liquid biopsies replace or complement tissue-based measurement for pre-treatment or longitudinal monitoring?
- In the Gleason 7 group, does CNA burden better distinguish 3+4 from 4+3 disease, which have different clinical trajectories?
- What specific focal CNAs within the burden metric drive the BCR/metastasis association — is it dominated by PTEN loss, or are there additive contributions from multiple regions?
- Does CNA burden remain prognostic in the context of modern active surveillance protocols and evolving PSA screening guidelines?

## Limitations

- Two cohorts from a single referral centre (MSK); patient selection may skew toward higher-risk disease
- Contemporary cohort had too few metastatic events for metastasis endpoint analysis
- CNA burden computed from SNP array / WGS; clinical implementation requires standardised low-input WGS workflows in FFPE
- CNA burden does not improve concordance index substantially above the Stephenson postoperative nomogram
- No transcriptomic or epigenomic data; CNA burden is a DNA-only metric
- Gleason 7 subgroup analysis may be underpowered for definitive metastasis endpoint conclusions
