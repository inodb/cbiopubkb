## Summary

MSK-IMPACT genomic profiling of 469 gastric and small bowel GISTs (592 samples including longitudinal specimens), with development of a novel 3-tier genomic risk stratification model for recurrence-free survival (RFS) in 232 primary localized GISTs. Using elastic-net penalized Cox regression (OncoCast machine learning), the model incorporates chromosomal arm-level alterations and gene-level copy number/mutation events. For gastric GISTs: high risk = chr1p deletion or SDHB loss; intermediate = chr14q deletion or absence of KIT exon 11 mutation. For small bowel GISTs: high risk = MAX/MGA/MYC, CDKN2A, or RB1 alterations; intermediate = chr1p deletion or chr5q amplification. The genomic model both upgrades and downgrades patients relative to conventional Miettinen/NIH criteria, with potential implications for adjuvant imatinib selection. Full data in `gist_msk_2023` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: 3-tier genomic risk model for gastric GIST outperforms conventional clinicopathologic schemes

**Finding:** Gastric GISTs are stratified by: High risk — chr1p deletion OR SDHB loss; Intermediate risk — chr14q deletion OR absence of KIT exon 11 mutation; Low risk — none of the above. Log-rank P<0.0001. 3-year RFS: High 64%, Intermediate 99%, Low 100%; 5-year RFS: High 53%, Intermediate 88%, Low 100%.

**Detail:** Among 152 primary localized gastric GISTs, 27% of the traditional high-risk group (by Miettinen criteria) was downgraded to low genomic risk, while 10–28% of traditional low/very-low-risk patients were upgraded to high genomic risk. Genomic risk groups were not significantly correlated with tumor size or mitotic rate — demonstrating that the genomic model captures independent prognostic information. Patients in the low genomic risk group who received adjuvant imatinib were all reclassified from traditional moderate or high risk, suggesting potential overtreatment by conventional criteria.

**Genes / cancer types:** KIT, SDHB, SDHA · Gastric GIST

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `native`

### F2: 3-tier genomic risk model for small bowel GIST identifies MAX/MGA/MYC, CDKN2A, and RB1 as high-risk drivers

**Finding:** Small bowel GISTs are stratified by: High risk — MAX/MGA inactivating mutations or MYC amplification, CDKN2A deletion, or RB1 deletion/inactivating mutation; Intermediate risk — chr1p deletion or chr5q amplification; Low risk — none of the above. Log-rank P<0.0001. High-risk group had 53% metastasis rate; low-risk had 0% at 5 years.

**Detail:** Among 80 primary localized small bowel GISTs, the model stratified even within the KIT exon 11 mutation subgroup (N=46, P=0.0044). Small bowel high-risk genomic groups correlated positively with tumor size (P=0.001) and mitotic rate (P=0.018), in contrast to gastric GISTs. MAX/MGA/MYC axis alterations (15% vs. 2% in gastric), RB1 (13% vs. 2%), chr1p deletion (87% vs. 26%), and chr5q amplification (35% vs. 19%) were all significantly enriched in small bowel vs. gastric GISTs.

**Genes / cancer types:** MAX, MGA, MYC, CDKN2A, RB1 · Small bowel GIST

### F3: Conventional risk stratification misclassifies a substantial fraction of GIST patients in the TKI era

**Finding:** Comparing traditional Miettinen/NIH/Joensuu criteria to the genomic model: among gastric GISTs, 54–57% of traditional high-risk cases were downgraded to intermediate genomic risk, and 10–28% of low/very-low-risk cases were upgraded to high genomic risk. Among small bowel GISTs, 54–58% of traditional high-risk cases were downgraded.

**Detail:** The mismatch between traditional and genomic risk has direct adjuvant therapy implications: patients in the low genomic risk group who received adjuvant imatinib were all classified as moderate or high risk by traditional criteria (suggesting overtreatment). Conversely, genomically high-risk patients not identified by traditional criteria may be undertreated. The traditional models are further limited by not accounting for imatinib sensitivity of the primary genotype (KIT/PDGFRA mutation type), which is incorporated in the genomic model.

**Genes / cancer types:** KIT, PDGFRA · Gastric and small bowel GIST

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `api`

### F4: Longitudinal sequencing shows most secondary genomic events are present at baseline

**Finding:** In 56 patients with longitudinal MSK-IMPACT profiling, chromosomal arm-level CNAs (chr1p, chr14q, chr22q deletions; chr1q, chr5p, chr5q amplifications) and co-occurring secondary gene-level alterations were detected at baseline and remained stable during disease progression — unlike acquired secondary KIT mutations (exons 13/14, 17/18) which emerged after imatinib treatment.

**Detail:** In small bowel GISTs, CDKN2A deletions, RB1 deletions/mutations, TP53 mutations, and SETD2 mutations were mutually exclusive (except one case). TP53 and PTEN mutations were exclusively found in imatinib-resistant GISTs with secondary KIT mutations. Chr1p deletions in small bowel GISTs were present at baseline in all cases where identified. This supports using baseline biopsy genomic profiling for primary risk stratification without requiring longitudinal sampling.

**Genes / cancer types:** KIT, TP53, PTEN, CDKN2A, RB1, SETD2 · Gastric and small bowel GIST

**Data availability:** `available`

### F5: SDH-deficient GISTs have high metastatic rate but excellent long-term survival; TP53 and chr1q amplification predict worse outcomes

**Finding:** Among 26 SDH-deficient GISTs (all gastric; median age 37.5 years), 73% experienced metastasis (median RFS 24 months), but 88% and 82% survived at 10 and 15 years respectively — reflecting the characteristically indolent course of SDH-deficient GIST despite metastasis. Within this group, TP53 mutations (15%) or chr1q amplifications (23%) predicted significantly worse RFS and disease-specific survival.

**Detail:** SDH-deficient GISTs included SDHA (n=14), SDHB (n=11), SDHC (n=1) alterations (germline/somatic inactivating mutations, deletions, or IHC-defined epimutations). These tumors are otherwise genomically quiet (few CNAs) and KIT/PDGFRA wild-type. The Carney triad and Carney-Stratakis syndrome cases are included in this group. The excellent overall survival despite metastatic behavior distinguishes this subgroup and supports conservative management approaches.

**Genes / cancer types:** SDHA, SDHB, SDHC, TP53 · Gastric GIST (SDH-deficient)

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `native`

## Open Questions

- Can the genomic risk stratification model be prospectively validated in an independent, multi-institutional GIST cohort to confirm clinical utility?
- Should adjuvant imatinib eligibility criteria be revised to incorporate chromosomal arm-level CNA (chr1p, chr14q) and MAX/MGA/MYC status rather than relying solely on size and mitotic rate?
- What is the mechanism by which chr1p deletion drives aggressive behavior in gastric GIST — are there specific tumor suppressor genes in this region relevant to GIST?
- For SDH-deficient GIST patients with TP53 mutations or chr1q amplification, are there alternative therapeutic strategies beyond imatinib that may improve outcomes?
- Do KIT exon 11 codon 557/558 deletions retain independent prognostic value in the genomic era, or are they captured by other genomic variables?

## Limitations

- Retrospective cohort from a single tertiary cancer center (MSKCC); referral bias may affect generalizability of risk frequencies and outcomes
- OncoCast machine learning model was trained and tested on the same overall cohort using repeated random splits (100 runs); external validation has not yet been performed
- Adjuvant imatinib decisions were made based on traditional criteria, not randomized — this limits causal inference about genomic risk group and treatment benefit
- Small sample sizes in some genomic risk subgroups (e.g., gastric low risk n=14) limit survival estimate precision
- Variants of unknown significance were not specifically excluded; VAF was not incorporated in the model
- Rectal GIST excluded due to small numbers; applicability to this anatomic site is unknown
