## Summary

This landmark study performs whole-exome sequencing (WES) of 160 matched CLL tumor/normal pairs and integrates somatic copy-number alterations (sCNAs) from 111 SNP6 arrays to study intratumoral heterogeneity and clonal evolution in 149 cases. Using the ABSOLUTE algorithm to estimate cancer cell fractions (CCF), the study identifies 20 putative CLL driver genes across seven core signaling pathways and defines the temporal ordering of CLL mutations: early/clonal drivers (MYD88, trisomy 12, del(13q)) versus late/subclonal drivers (SF3B1, TP53, ATM). Longitudinal analysis of 18 paired samples shows that chemotherapy-treated CLL (10/12 cases) undergoes clonal evolution driven by pre-existing subclones. Crucially, the presence of a subclonal driver mutation at baseline is an independent risk factor for rapid disease progression (adjusted HR=3.61, p=0.007), establishing subclonal architecture as a prognostic biomarker.

## Key Findings

### F1: 20 putative CLL driver genes identified across seven core signaling pathways, including 12 novel genes

**Finding:** WES of 160 CLL cases with MutSig analysis identifies 20 significantly mutated genes (q<0.1), expanding the known CLL driver compendium from 9 to 20 and revealing two new pathways: B cell receptor signaling and chromatin modification.

**Detail:** Using MuTect with COSMIC hotspot analysis, recurrence analysis detected 20 candidate driver genes. Previously reported CLL genes confirmed: TP53, ATM, MYD88, SF3B1, NOTCH1, DDX3X, ZMYM3, FBXW7 (8 of 9 from the prior Wang 2011 study; MAPK1 fell below significance with larger cohort). Newly identified in prior CLL sequencing: XPO1, CHD2, POT1. Nine novel candidate drivers: NRAS, KRAS, BCOR, EGR2, MED12, RIPK1, SAMHD1, ITPKB, HIST1H1E. Seven pathway groupings: DNA repair/cell cycle, Notch signaling, inflammatory, Wnt signaling, RNA splicing/processing, B cell receptor signaling, chromatin modification. Copy number analysis (GISTIC2.0) identified del(8p), del(13q), del(11q), del(17p), and trisomy 12 as significantly recurrent SCNAs.

**Genes / features:** `TP53`, `ATM`, `MYD88`, `SF3B1`, `NOTCH1`, `DDX3X`, `ZMYM3`, `FBXW7`, `XPO1`, `CHD2`, `POT1`, `NRAS`, `KRAS`, `BCOR`, `EGR2`, `MED12`, `RIPK1`, `SAMHD1`, `ITPKB`, `HIST1H1E`; del(13q), del(11q), del(17p), del(8p), trisomy 12

### F2: CLL mutations are temporally stratified as early/clonal (MYD88, trisomy 12, del(13q)) or late/subclonal (SF3B1, TP53, ATM)

**Finding:** Integration of CCF estimates from ABSOLUTE with WES mutation data reveals distinct temporal ordering: three drivers (MYD88, trisomy 12, del(13q)) are clonal in 80–100% of affected cases (significantly higher clonal fraction than all other drivers, q<0.1), whereas SF3B1, TP53, and ATM are predominantly subclonal, suggesting they arise and are selected for later in CLL progression.

**Detail:** In 149 samples with ABSOLUTE-resolved CCF data: 1,543 clonal mutations (54%, average 10.3±5.5/sample) and 1,266 subclonal mutations (46%, average 8.5±5.8/sample). Subclonal mutations showed median CCF of 0.49 (range 0.11–0.89). MYD88 (n=12), trisomy 12 (n=24), and del(13q) (n=70) were clonal in ≥80% of affected samples (Fisher exact, q<0.1). HIST1H1E was clonal in 5/5 cases but did not reach significance. SF3B1 (n=19 samples, 19 mutations), TP53 (n=17 samples, 19 mutations), ATM (n=6 samples, 9 mutations) were more often subclonal. In samples with one "early" driver plus additional drivers, early events had equal or higher CCF than later events, confirming the temporal model.

**Genes / features:** `MYD88`, `SF3B1`, `TP53`, `ATM`, `NOTCH1`; trisomy 12, del(13q), del(11q), del(17p)

**Data availability:** `available`
> Mutation data with allelic fractions available in `lcll_broad_2013`. CCF estimates from ABSOLUTE may be available as supplementary clinical attributes. Clonal vs. subclonal classification requires CCF data from the supplementary tables.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Raw mutation data with VAF accessible via cBioPortal REST API for ABSOLUTE-based CCF re-estimation. Clonal/subclonal labels, if deposited as mutation annotations, may be queryable. Native cBioPortal cancer cell fraction views require CCF data in the mutation file.

### F3: Chemotherapy-treated CLL shows increased subclonal mutations and clonal evolution (10/12 treated vs 1/6 untreated)

**Finding:** Prior chemotherapy is significantly associated with increased subclonal mutation counts at time of sampling. Longitudinal analysis shows clonal evolution in 10 of 12 treated CLL cases but only 1 of 6 untreated cases (Fisher exact, p=0.012), driven by outgrowth of pre-existing subclones.

**Detail:** Cross-sectional analysis: 29 pre-treated CLL cases showed significantly more subclonal (but not clonal) sSNVs per sample than 120 chemotherapy-naïve cases (ANCOVA, p=0.048). The effect was confirmed when restricting comparison to 42 patients eventually treated after sampling (p=0.02). The number of subclonal mutations increased with number of prior therapies (p=0.011). Longitudinal analysis (18 pairs, median 3.5 years between samples): 11 of 18 showed clonal evolution. In 12 treated patients: 10 showed clonal evolution (5 with branched evolution patterns, indicating co-existing sibling subclones). Only 1 of 6 untreated patients showed evolution; 5 maintained stable clonal equilibrium over years. Expanding sSNVs were enriched in Cancer Gene Census genes (p=0.021) and CLL drivers (p=0.028), confirming positive selection pressure from therapy.

**Genes / features:** `SF3B1`, `TP53`, `ATM`, `NRAS`; del(11q), del(17p)

**Data availability:** `available`

### F4: Subclonal driver mutation presence is an independent risk factor for rapid disease progression (adjusted HR=3.61, p=0.007)

**Finding:** The presence of any subclonal driver mutation in a CLL sample is an independent prognostic factor for shorter failure-free survival (FFS), both from time of sample (FFS_Sample) and from time of next treatment (FFS_Rx), with a multivariable-adjusted HR of 3.61 (95% CI 1.42–9.18, Cox p=0.007).

**Detail:** Subclonal driver mutations detected in 68 of 149 patients (46%). CLL samples with subclonal drivers had significantly shorter FFS_Sample vs. those without (p<0.001, log-rank). Among 67 patients treated after sample collection: 39 with subclonal drivers had shorter FFS_Rx than 28 without (p=0.006). Cox regression adjusting for IGHV status, prior therapy, and high-risk cytogenetics (del(11q), del(17p)) confirmed subclonal driver presence as independent predictor (HR=3.61, p=0.007; unadjusted HR=3.20). Additional subclonal drivers per sample (but not clonal drivers) further increased HR for shorter FFS_Rx. The association retained significance after adjusting for presence of ATM, TP53, SF3B1 mutations (Cox p=0.033), indicating that subclonal *status* — not just driver identity — independently affects outcome.

**Genes / features:** `SF3B1`, `TP53`, `ATM`, `NRAS`; del(11q), del(17p); `IGHV` mutation status

**Data availability:** `available`
> Clinical outcome data (failure-free survival, treatment dates) and IGHV mutation status available as clinical attributes in `lcll_broad_2013`. Mutation data and CCF values needed to define subclonal driver status; may require combining cBioPortal data with supplementary tables.

**Tool support:** `native`
> KM survival analysis by mutation status (SF3B1, TP53, ATM) and IGHV available natively in cBioPortal Survival tab. Subclonal driver classification (requiring CCF thresholds) would need custom pre-processing before uploading as a clinical grouping attribute.

### F5: Two periods of CLL evolution identified — pre-transformation mutagenesis (passenger accumulation) linked to age and IGHV status

**Finding:** Clonal (but not subclonal) mutation counts are significantly associated with patient age at diagnosis (p<0.001) and IGHV mutation status (mutated vs. unmutated IGHV, p=0.05), consistent with pre-transformation mutagenesis before clonal expansion.

**Detail:** Among 149 samples, older patients accumulated more clonal somatic mutations but not more subclonal mutations — consistent with neutral somatic mutagenesis accumulating before transformation. IGHV-mutated CLL (which originates from post-germinal center B cells that experienced somatic hypermutation) also showed more clonal (but not subclonal) sSNVs, likely reflecting aberrant SHM-induced mutagenesis prior to transformation. ZAP70 expression was not associated with mutation burden. This two-period model proposes: (1) pre-transformation: neutral passenger accumulation in proportion to age/mutagenesis history; (2) post-transformation founding driver appears; (3) subclonal drivers expand under selection. B cell-specific early drivers (MYD88, del(13q), trisomy 12) initiate disease; ubiquitous cancer drivers (TP53, ATM, RAS) promote progression.

**Genes / features:** `MYD88`, `IGHV` (mutation status), `ZAP70`; del(13q), trisomy 12

**Data availability:** `available`
> IGHV mutation status, ZAP70 expression, and age at diagnosis available as clinical attributes in `lcll_broad_2013`. Clonal mutation counts derivable from mutation data if CCF values are included.

**Tool support:** `native`

## Open Questions

- Can liquid biopsy (cell-free DNA) reliably detect subclonal driver mutations (at CCF ~0.1–0.5) to enable prospective subclonal architecture profiling at diagnosis and during treatment in CLL?
- Do the 9 novel driver genes (NRAS, KRAS, BCOR, EGR2, MED12, RIPK1, SAMHD1, ITPKB, HIST1H1E) have distinct clonal/subclonal patterns and clinical associations in larger CLL cohorts?
- The branched evolution pattern (co-existing sibling subclones) in 5/12 treated CLL cases — does the identity of the sibling subclone predict which targeted agent (e.g., venetoclax vs. ibrutinib) is most likely to eliminate the emerging dominant clone?
- Given that subclonal driver status was prognostic independent of SF3B1/TP53/ATM identity, could a clinical CCF-based test (combining WES + ABSOLUTE) be incorporated into standard CLL staging?
- Do the chromatin modification pathway genes (HIST1H1E, BCOR) show any therapeutic vulnerability analogous to EZH2 inhibition in other B cell malignancies?

## Limitations

- WES at ~130× depth detects subclones at CCF ≥0.1; clones below this threshold (genetic "dark matter") are not observable, underestimating true heterogeneity
- Cross-sectional cohort samples at a single timepoint; temporal ordering of driver events inferred statistically (not directly observed) in 131 of 149 patients
- Longitudinal cohort limited to 18 patients (12 treated, 6 untreated); small sample size limits power to distinguish branched from linear evolution patterns
- Chemotherapy regimens in the treated group were heterogeneous (fludarabine/rituxan-based); different agents may create different selection pressures
- ABSOLUTE analysis requires near-diploid tumors; 11 of 160 samples could not be resolved, potentially excluding outlier cases
- Clinical outcome endpoint (failure-free survival via retreatment) may be affected by physician treatment thresholds, introducing non-random censoring
