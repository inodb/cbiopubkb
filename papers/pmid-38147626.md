## Summary

Retrospective analysis of 42,714 patients with nonhematologic cancer who underwent blood-based MSK-IMPACT sequencing as a normal comparator for tumor testing. Hematologic malignancy diagnoses were catalogued by NLP-assisted and manually curated medical record review. The study identifies specific CH genotypes associated with high hematologic malignancy risk (JAK2, RUNX1, XPO1, ASXL1), demonstrates that apparently neutral "silent" CH variants (synonymous/noncoding mutations) predict hematologic malignancy risk when occurring at high VAF or in multiple copies, and tracks CH clone dynamics at malignancy onset showing gene-specific expansion or contraction patterns. DNMT3A and PPM1D, despite high prevalence, were not associated with increased hematologic malignancy risk in this cohort.

## Key Findings

### F1: CH is prevalent (29.7%) in solid cancer patients and independently associated with hematologic malignancy and death

**Finding:** Of 42,714 nonhematologic cancer patients, 11,735 (29.7%) had at least one conventional CH variant; CH was independently associated with hematologic malignancy (HR=1.93; 95% CI: 1.45–2.57; p<0.001) and death (HR=1.11; 95% CI: 1.07–1.15; p≤0.001) in multivariate competing-risk regression.

**Detail:** CH prevalence correlated strongly with age across all cancer diagnoses, with little variation by tumor type. The most common CH genes were DNMT3A, TET2, ASXL1, PPM1D, CHEK2, and TP53. Among 39,510 evaluable patients, 216 hematologic malignancy diagnoses occurred ≥15 days after blood draw. High follow-up mortality limited long-term assessment: only 8,413 patients were observed beyond 3 years. CH incidence was similar across patients exposed to different anticancer therapeutic drug mechanisms, suggesting genotoxic therapy is not the primary driver of CH prevalence at the time of sequencing.

**Genes / cancer types:** DNMT3A, TET2, ASXL1, PPM1D, CHEK2, TP53 · All solid tumor types

### F2: JAK2, RUNX1, and XPO1 mutations confer high hematologic malignancy risk; DNMT3A and PPM1D do not

**Finding:** Among 166 candidate CH genes evaluated in cause-specific Cox models adjusted for age, sex, and cancer type, nine genotypes were significantly associated with hematologic malignancy (FDR<0.1), with JAK2, RUNX1, and XPO1 showing the highest risk; notably, DNMT3A and PPM1D — the most prevalent CH genes — were not significantly associated.

**Detail:** JAK2, RUNX1, and ASXL1 mutations predominantly preceded myeloid malignancies (20/26 total cases); XPO1 and KMT2D exclusively preceded lymphoid malignancies (7/7 cases); TP53 and TET2 preceded both lineages approximately equally. Mosaic chromosome alterations involving chromosomes 5, 7, and 17 (5/7/17) were strongly associated with subsequent hematologic malignancy and were classified as high-risk, consistent with their association with therapy-related myeloid neoplasms. The lack of DNMT3A risk association despite high prevalence may reflect length bias toward chronic/indolent diseases or the need for longer follow-up.

**Genes / cancer types:** JAK2, RUNX1, XPO1, ASXL1, KMT2D, TP53, TET2, SUZ12, ERBB4 · All solid tumor types

### F3: Silent (synonymous/noncoding) CH variants predict hematologic malignancy risk, especially when multiple or at high VAF

**Finding:** Silent CH variants — synonymous coding and noncoding mutations without predicted functional impact — were significantly associated with subsequent hematologic malignancy when present as multiple variants or at high variant allele frequency.

**Detail:** Silent CH provides a measure of hematopoietic clonality independent of mutational function, analogous to the CH with unknown drivers (CH-UD) concept from whole-genome studies. The association with hematologic malignancy risk was strongest for patients with multiple silent variants or high-VAF silent variants, suggesting that clonal expansion itself — even of apparently neutral variants — is a meaningful predictor. This finding has practical implications because silent variants may be under-filtered in standard CH pipelines that rely on functional annotation, potentially missing clonality information. The study used dN/dS analysis to confirm neutrality of the silent variant set.

**Genes / cancer types:** (genome-wide synonymous/noncoding variants across hematopoietic genes) · All solid tumor types

### F4: CH predicts MPNs most reliably; therapy-related AML with translocations largely unrelated to CH

**Finding:** All 11 patients who developed myeloproliferative neoplasms had CH at assessment (including 9 with JAK2 V617F); in contrast, most therapy-related myeloid neoplasms and high-grade B-cell lymphomas arose without prior detectable CH, suggesting distinct leukemogenic mechanisms.

**Detail:** Only 1 of 8 AML cases with an AML-defining chromosomal translocation had detectable CH at assessment, suggesting that translocation-driven therapy-related leukemia may arise de novo rather than from pre-existing CH clones. Approximately half of patients who developed myeloid malignancies (36/70) and lymphoid malignancies (70/133) had CH detected at assessment. The HR for myeloid malignancy posed by CH (HR=2.99; 95% CI: 1.82–4.91) trended higher than for lymphoid malignancies (HR=1.66; 95% CI: 1.17–2.36), though absolute myeloid risk was lower given higher lymphoid malignancy incidence.

### F5: Clone dynamics at hematologic malignancy onset: JAK2 and TP53 expand, DNMT3A and silent CH mostly contract

**Finding:** Tracking 26 paired blood samples (CH assessment → hematologic malignancy onset) revealed that JAK2 and TP53 VAFs consistently expanded at malignancy onset, whereas DNMT3A and silent CH VAFs mostly decreased, suggesting that DNMT3A-CH commonly represents a bystander rather than a malignancy driver.

**Detail:** VAF expansion of JAK2 and TP53 at malignancy diagnosis is consistent with their driver roles in myeloproliferative neoplasms and myeloid malignancies respectively. The VAF decrease observed for DNMT3A clones at malignancy diagnosis may reflect outcompetition of DNMT3A-bearing hematopoietic stem cells by the dominant malignant clone, rather than DNMT3A mutations being in the founding clone of the malignancy. Similarly, silent CH VAF contraction at malignancy onset further supports these variants as markers of clonal expansion rather than direct leukemogenic contributors.

**Genes / cancer types:** JAK2, TP53, DNMT3A · Myeloproliferative neoplasms, AML, MDS, lymphoma

## Open Questions

- Why are DNMT3A and PPM1D CH not associated with hematologic malignancy risk in this solid tumor cohort despite being validated leukemia drivers in experimental models?
- Can silent CH VAF or multiplicity be incorporated into clinical risk models alongside functional CH variants to improve hematologic malignancy prediction?
- What is the minimum follow-up required to detect DNMT3A-associated hematologic malignancy risk, given its expected long latency?
- Do specific co-mutation combinations (e.g., JAK2 + TET2) confer synergistic risk beyond what is captured by individual gene models?
- How does prior chemotherapy exposure modify the risk associated with specific CH genotypes identified at the time of blood collection?

## Limitations

- High mortality in the solid cancer cohort limits follow-up time: only 8,413 of 39,510 patients observed beyond 3 years, potentially underestimating risk for slowly progressing diseases (DNMT3A, PPM1D)
- MSK-IMPACT was designed for tumor profiling, not optimized CH calling; lower-VAF CH clones may be missed relative to dedicated CH sequencing panels
- NLP-based hematologic malignancy ascertainment may miss diagnoses made at other institutions
- Lack of standardized treatment exposure data; pharmacy records may undercount agents prescribed by non-MSK oncologists
- Competing risk of death from solid cancer limits observation of long-latency hematologic malignancy events
