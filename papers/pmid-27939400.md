## Summary

IMvigor210 cohort 1 is the first phase II trial of a PD-L1/PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor in untreated, cisplatin-ineligible metastatic urothelial carcinoma (mUC). 119 patients received atezolizumab 1200 mg IV q3w until progression. At 17.2-month median follow-up, ORR was 23% with a complete response rate of 9%; 70% of responses were ongoing. Median OS was 15.9 months and median DOR was not reached. Tumor mutation load (TMB) was a significant correlate of both response and OS across all PD-L1 and TCGA molecular subtype strata. Responses occurred across all PD-L1 immune cell (IC) subgroups, though IC2/3 patients had the highest ORR (28%). Upper-tract primaries responded particularly well (39% ORR). Data are in cBioPortal study `blca_iatlas_imvigor210_2017`. This is the primary analysis; the final long-term analysis is in [pmid-39642637](pmid-39642637.md).

## Key Findings

### F1: ORR 23% with durable responses in first-line cisplatin-ineligible mUC

**Finding:** Atezolizumab achieved a 23% ORR (95% CI 16–31%) in all patients, with a 9% complete response rate and median DOR not reached; 70% (19/27) of responses were ongoing at data cutoff.

**Detail:** Median time to first response was 2.1 months; late responses were seen in 2 patients after 6 months. Median PFS was 2.7 months overall, 4.1 months in IC2/3 patients. Clinical benefit rate was 30%. The primary endpoint of hierarchical testing in IC2/3 did not reach significance at the primary analysis (8.5-month follow-up), but all-comer ORR lower bound exceeded the 10% historical control at updated 17.2-month follow-up.

**Genes / cancer types:** PD-L1 (IC scoring), urothelial carcinoma (bladder, upper-tract)

### F2: PD-L1 IC expression correlates with ORR but responses occur in all subgroups

**Finding:** IC2/3 patients had the highest ORR (28%, 95% CI 14–47%), but objective responses were observed in IC0 (21%), IC1 (21%), and IC2/3 (28%) subgroups—without a statistically significant differential by PD-L1 status.

**Detail:** Among cisplatin-ineligibility sub-criteria: renal impairment (70% of patients) had ORR 25%; ECOG PS 2 patients (20%) had ORR 25%; patients ≥80 years (21%) had ORR 28%. Liver metastases (21% of patients) had only 8% ORR and 5.5-month median OS. Bajorin risk factors remained prognostically important: 0-factor patients had 34% ORR vs 11% with 2 factors.

**Genes / cancer types:** PD-L1 (VENTANA SP142, IC0/IC1/IC2/3 scoring), urothelial carcinoma

### F3: Tumor mutation burden correlates with response and OS independently of PD-L1 status

**Finding:** TMB was significantly higher in responders than non-responders (Wilcoxon p=0.018) and was associated with OS (log-rank p=0.004); the highest TMB quartile had significantly longer survival than quartiles 1–3.

**Detail:** The TMB–response relationship was consistent across all TCGA molecular subtypes and PD-L1 IC subgroups, suggesting TMB and PD-L1 are partially independent biomarkers. Microsatellite instability was detected in only 4 patients (2 upper-tract, 2 lower-tract), indicating MSI is not a major driver of the TMB-response correlation in this cohort. Luminal II subtype (TCGA) had numerically higher response rates.

**Genes / cancer types:** TMB (FoundationOne panel), TCGA molecular subtypes (luminal I/II, basal III/IV), urothelial carcinoma

### F4: Upper-tract primaries have strikingly higher ORR (39%) vs bladder (17%)

**Finding:** Patients with renal pelvis/ureter primary tumors (n=33) had an ORR of 39% (95% CI 23–58%) compared to 17% (95% CI 9–26%) for bladder/urethra primaries; median OS in upper-tract patients was not reached.

**Detail:** Exploratory analysis found no significant differences in TMB, T-effector gene expression, TCGA subtype, or metastatic site distribution between upper- and lower-tract patients. MSI was rare in both (2 patients each) and unlikely to explain the difference. This ORR differential persisted as a largely unexplained biological observation.

**Genes / cancer types:** Anatomic subtype (upper tract vs bladder/urethra), urothelial carcinoma

### F5: Atezolizumab is well-tolerated with manageable immune-mediated toxicity

**Finding:** Grade 3/4 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 16% of patients; only 8% discontinued due to AEs; one treatment-related death (sepsis). No major safety differences across PD-L1 subgroups.

**Detail:** Treatment-related AEs in ≥10% of patients: fatigue (any grade), diarrhea, pruritus. Grade 3/4 events: fatigue (3.4%), elevated ALT (3.4%), elevated AST (2.5%). Immune-mediated events occurred in 14 patients (12%). This safety profile supports atezolizumab use in elderly and comorbid cisplatin-ineligible patients, including those ≥80 years and those with renal dysfunction.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Why do upper-tract urothelial carcinomas respond better to atezolizumab than lower-tract tumors, given no difference in TMB, T-effector genes, or TCGA subtype?
- Does the combination of high TMB plus high PD-L1 IC2/3 status identify patients with exceptionally durable responses that would justify monotherapy even as combinations become available?
- Would maintenance atezolizumab after chemotherapy (as in IMvigor010) differ in biomarker requirements from first-line monotherapy?
- Can the late responders (after 6 months) be prospectively identified by clinical or genomic features?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Single-arm trial without a comparator arm; ORR benchmark of 10% is a historical composite estimate
- Primary hierarchical statistical test not met (IC2/3 subgroup did not reach significance at primary analysis)
- Small subgroup sizes (IC2/3: n=32) limit statistical power for biomarker analyses
- Most biomarker samples (61%) were from primary tumors, not metastatic sites; may not reflect current tumor biology
- Median follow-up was 17.2 months — not sufficient to characterize tail of survival distribution; addressed in [pmid-39642637](pmid-39642637.md)
- Single institution's assessment of cisplatin-ineligibility criteria; definitions may vary
