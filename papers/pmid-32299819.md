## Summary

Prospective MSK-IMPACT clinical sequencing study of 107 uterine sarcoma patients, enriching for high-risk subtypes. Uterine LMS (uLMS, n=80) dominated; 89% of patients had recurrent/metastatic disease at sequencing. Core uLMS alterations: *TP53* (56%), *RB1* (51%), *ATRX* (31%). Homozygous *BRCA2* deletions occurred in 5% and *PDCD1* (PD-1) deletions at the highest rate of any cancer type in the MSK series. WGD found in 50.5% of high-grade sarcomas. Actionable alterations identified in 45% of patients; six uLMS patients with *BRCA2* alterations treated with PARP inhibitors all achieved partial responses. Genomic profiling prompted diagnostic reclassification in three cases. Data available in `usarc_msk_2020` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Core uLMS alterations are *TP53*, *RB1*, and *ATRX* — distinct from high-grade non-LMS

**Finding:** The three most common oncogenic alterations in uterine LMS are loss-of-function mutations or homozygous deletions in *TP53* (56%), *RB1* (51%), and *ATRX* (31%). These are significantly less frequent in high-grade non-LMS (14%, 14%, and 5%, respectively; p<0.01 for all). Whole-genome duplication (WGD) occurred in 50.5% of high-grade uterine sarcomas but in none of the low-grade cases (p=0.04).

**Detail:** The combination of *TP53* inactivation, *RB1* inactivation, and *ATRX* loss defines a molecular signature of uterine LMS that distinguishes it from other uterine sarcoma subtypes. *MED12* mutations, present in ~70% of benign uterine leiomyomas, were found in only 11% of uLMS, supporting the hypothesis that uLMS arises *de novo* rather than from antecedent benign leiomyomas. Overall survival was significantly worse for high-grade non-LMS vs. uLMS (3.4 vs. 6.3 years; HR=0.48; p=0.03).

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, RB1, ATRX, MED12 · Uterine leiomyosarcoma, uterine sarcoma

### F2: Homozygous *BRCA2* deletions exclusive to uLMS; PARP inhibitor responses observed

**Finding:** Homozygous deletions of *BRCA2* were found exclusively in uLMS (not in other uterine sarcoma subtypes) and occurred in 5% of uLMS patients. Six uLMS patients with somatic *BRCA2* alterations (including deletions and truncating mutations) treated with PARP inhibitor-containing regimens all achieved at least partial radiographic regression, with one complete response and treatment durations ranging from 6 to 28 months.

**Detail:** *BRCA2* alteration frequency in uterine sarcomas is among the highest of any cancer type in the MSK-IMPACT series. BRCA1 promoter hypermethylation has been separately described in uLMS, and HRD signatures have been identified, supporting a broader "BRCAness" phenotype. Two patients in the prospective cohort plus five additional post-freeze patients all showed clinical benefit to PARP inhibitors, establishing *BRCA2* as an actionable target in uLMS. This is in contrast to most BRCA-related tumor types where *BRCA1* alterations are more common.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRCA2, BRCA1 · Uterine leiomyosarcoma

### F3: *PDCD1* homozygous deletions are most frequent in uLMS among all MSK cancer types

**Finding:** Homozygous deletions of *PDCD1* (encoding PD-1) were found in uLMS at the highest frequency of any cancer type among 15,816 prospectively sequenced cases in the MSK-IMPACT cohort. IHC confirmed loss of PD-1 protein in all tested cases. *PDCD1* deletions were more common in uLMS than non-uterine LMS, potentially explaining the poor objective response rates seen in phase II immunotherapy trials in uLMS.

**Detail:** Loss of PD-1 expression by tumor cells (rather than immune cells) may impair immune checkpoint signaling in uLMS, potentially diminishing the efficacy of PD-1/PD-L1 blockade. The homozygous deletion mechanism is distinct from epigenetic silencing, suggesting an irreversible loss. The relative rarity of MSI-H uLMS (2 of 80 cases, both with prolonged disease-free survival) alongside *PDCD1* deletion may partly explain the limited benefit of checkpoint inhibitors in unselected uLMS patients.

### F4: Fusion detection reclassified diagnoses; *ALK* and *BCOR* fusions have therapeutic and diagnostic relevance

**Finding:** Genomic profiling contributed to diagnostic reclassification in three cases. An *LBH-ALK* fusion initially missed by DNA sequencing was identified by RNA-based MSK-Fusion and prompted reclassification from uLMS to inflammatory myofibroblastic tumor (IMT); the patient achieved 30+ months of disease stabilization on crizotinib. A *BCOR-ZC3H7B* fusion reclassified a case from myxoid LMS to *BCOR*-mutated high-grade ESS. *YWHAE-NUTM2* rearrangements confirmed two HG-ESS diagnoses.

**Detail:** The *ALK* fusion breakpoint was located in an intron not commonly targeted for lung cancer *ALK* fusions, underscoring the diagnostic limits of DNA-only sequencing panels and the complementary value of RNA fusion panels. A second *ALK* fusion uLMS was identified post-freeze and also reclassified as IMT; crizotinib achieved brief response followed by progression, with further progression on ceritinib. An *ESR1* Y537S mutation in a LG-ESS patient treated >20 years with anti-estrogen therapy responded to fulvestrant, demonstrating acquired endocrine resistance.

**Genes / cancer types:** ALK, BCOR, YWHAE, NUTM2A/B, JAZF1, ESR1 · Uterine sarcoma, inflammatory myofibroblastic tumor

### F5: *PTEN* alterations enriched in metastatic vs. primary uLMS samples; actionable alterations in 45% of all patients

**Finding:** *PTEN* alteration frequency was significantly higher in metastatic samples compared to primary tumor samples (p=0.046), consistent with enrichment during metastatic progression. Overall, 45% of all patients (48/107) harbored at least one potentially actionable alteration (per OncoKB). Among those, 17% (8/48) received matched therapy; two achieved objective radiographic responses and one (the *ALK*-fusion patient) achieved prolonged stable disease.

**Detail:** Additional actionable alterations included two *BRAF* mutations (V600E and K601E), two MSI-H tumors, and one *ESR1* Y537S mutation. *RIT1* amplifications were most frequent in high-grade non-LMS (24% vs. 2.5% in uLMS; p=0.004) and *MAP2K4* amplifications were enriched in uterine sarcomas versus other cancer types. None of the common genomic alterations or WGD were significantly associated with OS in this cohort, likely due to selection bias toward patients with relatively favorable outcomes.

**Genes / cancer types:** PTEN, BRAF, RIT1, MAP2K4, ESR1 · Uterine sarcoma, uterine leiomyosarcoma

## Open Questions

- Does *BRCA1* promoter hypermethylation (as opposed to somatic mutation/deletion) confer an HRD phenotype and PARP inhibitor sensitivity comparable to *BRCA2* alterations in uLMS?
- Why do uLMS almost exclusively harbor *BRCA2* (not *BRCA1*) alterations, unlike breast and ovarian BRCA-related tumors?
- Can the high frequency of *PDCD1* homozygous deletions in uLMS be used to predict or explain resistance to PD-1 checkpoint inhibitors prospectively?
- What is the functional significance of *RIT1* and *MAP2K4* amplifications, which do not associate with OS in this dataset?
- Does *PTEN* enrichment in metastases represent a driver of metastatic progression or a passenger event in genomically unstable tumors?

## Limitations

- Predominantly recurrent/metastatic cohort (89%) at time of sequencing; likely over-represents patients with favorable survival who could seek care at a major cancer center, potentially masking poor-prognosis genomic patterns
- All patients alive at time of enrollment, introducing survival bias; patients dying shortly after diagnosis are under-represented
- No matched primary-metastasis pairs for most patients; PTEN enrichment in metastases cannot be definitively attributed to clonal selection
- Only 17% of patients with actionable mutations received matched therapy, limiting clinical outcome conclusions
- DNA-only sequencing missed the *ALK* fusion in one patient, underscoring panel limitations; RNA sequencing performed for only a subset
