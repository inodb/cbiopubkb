## Summary

First microscaled proteogenomics study of neoadjuvant carboplatin/docetaxel chemotherapy response in TNBC (59 patients from two clinical trials, NCT02547987 and NCT02124902). Integrated whole-exome sequencing, RNA-seq, and TMT-based proteomics and phosphoproteomics from pretreatment core needle biopsies identified three independent classes of chemotherapy response biomarkers: (1) metabolic pathway proteins (oxidative phosphorylation, fatty acid metabolism, adipogenesis) uniquely elevated at the protein level in non-responders; (2) immune microenvironment markers elevated in responders; and (3) a 19q13.31–33 somatic deletion encompassing *LIG1*, *POLD1*, and *XRCC1* — lagging-strand DNA synthesis genes — as a carboplatin-selective resistance marker. *LIG1* deletion was validated across multiple independent datasets, PDX models, and TCGA pan-cancer analyses, showing broader clinical implications including poor progression-free survival across multiple cancer types.

## Key Findings

### F1: Metabolic pathways uniquely elevated at protein level in non-pCR TNBC — oxidative phosphorylation, adipogenesis, fatty acid metabolism

**Finding:** Pre-treatment TNBC tumors that fail to achieve pathologic complete response (non-pCR) show significantly elevated protein-level enrichment of metabolic Hallmark pathways — oxidative phosphorylation, fatty acid metabolism, adipogenesis, and glycolysis — relative to pCR tumors. Critically, these associations are detected only at the protein level, not by RNA-seq, demonstrating a post-transcriptional regulatory layer invisible to transcriptomics alone.

**Detail:** GSEA of proteomic data identified differential metabolic pathway enrichment in non-pCR vs. pCR at FDR < 0.05, while the same analysis at the RNA level was not significant. Individual resistance-associated metabolic proteins include TCA cycle enzymes (ACO2, FH, MDH2, SUCLG1/2, PDP1, DLAT), electron transport chain components (SDHC, UQCR10), fatty acid metabolism enzymes (CRAT, ACADS, ACAT1, DECR1, ECHS1, HADHB), and amino acid catabolism proteins (ALDH6A1, HMGCL, DBT, BCKDHB). A 29-gene metabolic signature with high mRNA-protein correlation was validated in the BrighTNess trial (n=359; Wilcoxon P=0.003): higher metabolic scores correlated with increasing RCB category, supporting these as pan-carboplatin resistance markers. Fatty acid synthase inhibition (omeprazole) is under investigation in NCT02595372.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ACO2`, `FH`, `MDH2`, `SUCLG1`, `SUCLG2`, `SDHC`, `CRAT`, `ACADS`, `HADHB` · Triple-Negative Breast Cancer

### F2: Immune pathway elevation (interferon signaling, PD-L1) marks pCR and inversely correlates with chromosomal instability

**Finding:** Tumors achieving pCR show significantly higher protein-derived immune stimulatory scores, elevated PD-L1 protein and phosphoprotein levels, and enrichment of interferon-alpha/gamma response pathways at both RNA and protein levels. Immune stimulatory score is strongly negatively correlated with chromosomal instability index (CIN; Spearman rho = −0.61, P = 6.2e−6), and PD-L1 protein and phosphoprotein levels correlate significantly with PD-L1 IHC.

**Detail:** Immune checkpoint gene expression (PD-L1/CD274, PD-1/PDCD1, TIM-3/HAVCR2) and immune cell infiltration scores were significantly higher in pCR samples (Wilcoxon P=0.01 for immune stimulatory score). Nonsynonymous mutation load did not associate with either pCR (P=0.57) or immune scores (rho=−0.17), distinguishing this pattern from TMB-driven immunogenicity observed in other cancer types. Instead, CIN is the key negative driver of immune exclusion: high genomic instability correlates with low immune infiltration. This finding suggests proteogenomics-based immune profiling could complement standard PD-L1 IHC and potentially identify patients who may benefit from combined chemotherapy-immunotherapy regimens.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CD274`, `PDCD1`, `HAVCR2`, `CD8A` · Triple-Negative Breast Cancer

### F3: 19q13.31–33 somatic deletion encompassing LIG1/POLD1/XRCC1 is a carboplatin-selective resistance biomarker

**Finding:** Unbiased cytoband-level GSEA identified the 19q13.31–33 region as significantly depleted in non-pCR tumors at both mRNA and protein levels. The deletion spans *LIG1*, *POLD1*, *XRCC1*, and *ERCC2* — genes encoding lagging-strand DNA synthesis components. Hallmark DNA repair pathway enrichment analysis identified these as the leading-edge genes. *LIG1* hemizygous loss (GISTIC = −1) was present in ~26% of non-pCR tumors and was the most consistently resistance-associated across all validation datasets.

**Detail:** LIG1 (DNA ligase I) seals Okazaki fragments during lagging-strand synthesis and participates in long-patch base-excision repair, nucleotide-excision repair, and alternative NHEJ. LIG1/POLD1/XRCC1 co-deletion on 19q13.31–33 may produce a hemizygous compound lagging-strand synthesis deficiency, explaining carboplatin (but not docetaxel) resistance phenotype. Validation: (1) BrighTNess trial — LIG1 and POLD1 significantly lower in residual disease (P<0.05, carboplatin arms); (2) Hatzis dataset — low LIG1/XRCC1 associated with poor metastasis-free survival; (3) cisplatin trial — LIG1 lower in SD+PD vs. CR+PR; (4) NCI PDXnet — LIG1 lower in non-CR PDXs; (5) BCaPE database — LIG1 copy-number loss uniquely associated with carboplatin resistance across >100 drugs tested. LIG1-loss tumors also showed higher CIN, higher proliferation scores, and suppressed EGFR/PI3K signaling, suggesting insensitivity to targeted PI3K/AKT inhibitors.

**Genes / cancer types:** `LIG1`, `POLD1`, `XRCC1`, `ERCC2` · Triple-Negative Breast Cancer

### F4: Progressive LIG1 loss during TNBC treatment and metastatic evolution confirmed in PDX models

**Finding:** Three orthotopic PDX models from the same patient at baseline (WHIM68), post-neoadjuvant treatment (WHIM74), and hepatic metastasis (WHIM75) demonstrate progressive loss of LIG1 at copy number, mRNA, and protein levels as the tumor evolved through carboplatin treatment, with concordant progressive acquisition of carboplatin resistance. A BRCA2 loss-of-function mutation present in WHIM68 became undetectable in WHIM74/75, suggesting treatment-induced clonal selection.

**Detail:** WHIM68 (highest LIG1) was carboplatin sensitive; WHIM74 and WHIM75 (progressively lower LIG1) were markedly less sensitive (treatment-induced clonal selection of LIG1-deleted subclone). The BRCA2-mutant subclone regressed while the LIG1-deleted subclone expanded under carboplatin pressure, illustrating how a DNA repair-deficient clone can paradoxically confer chemotherapy resistance when the relevant repair pathway (LIG1-mediated lagging-strand ligation) is required for processing platinum-induced DNA damage. Docetaxel response was not affected in parallel experiments, confirming carboplatin selectivity.

### F5: Pan-cancer LIG1 hemizygous loss associates with poor progression-free survival and higher chromosomal instability

**Finding:** TCGA pan-cancer analysis demonstrates that *LIG1* hemizygous loss (GISTIC ≤ −1) is associated with significantly reduced progression-free survival (log-rank P < 0.0001), higher fraction genome altered (FGA), and lower COSMIC signature 3 scores (HRD signature) across multiple cancer types. Five cancer types show significant survival detriment: endometrial (HR=2.23), head and neck squamous (HR=1.46), prostate (HR=2.07), colon (HR=1.75), and renal papillary (HR=4.0, P=0.0001).

**Detail:** The pan-cancer analysis extends the carboplatin-resistance finding from a TNBC-specific to a broadly relevant genomic vulnerability. LIG1-loss tumors across cancer types show higher CIN (consistent with compromised replication fidelity), lower HRD signature (suggesting a different genomic instability mechanism than BRCA-related HRD), and worse clinical outcomes. Seminoma, which shows exquisite carboplatin sensitivity, had zero LIG1 loss cases — supporting the mechanistic link between LIG1 sufficiency and platinum sensitivity.

## Open Questions

- Does LIG1 protein level (rather than copy number alone) predict carboplatin resistance more precisely, and can TMT proteomics be translated to a clinical-grade assay?
- Is the compound 19q13.31–33 deletion (LIG1+POLD1+XRCC1) required for the resistance phenotype, or is LIG1 alone sufficient?
- Would TNBC patients with LIG1 loss benefit from substitution of carboplatin with PARP inhibitors or other DNA repair-targeted agents?
- Is immune exclusion in LIG1-loss tumors (lower immune stimulatory score) reversible with VEGF/angiogenesis inhibitors that may reduce CIN-driven immune suppression?
- Can the metabolic resistance signature (oxidative phosphorylation, fatty acid metabolism) identify patients for prospective trials of fatty acid synthase or OXPHOS inhibitors combined with chemotherapy?
- Does the proteomics-based immune signature outperform PD-L1 IHC in predicting pembrolizumab benefit in the neoadjuvant setting (KEYNOTE-522 context)?

## Limitations

- Small discovery cohort (n=59; 22 pCR, 37 non-pCR) limits power for rare molecular subtype analyses and multivariable modeling
- Microscaled proteomics from OCT-embedded core biopsies requires >45% tumor content — 9 low-purity samples excluded, potentially introducing selection bias
- No BRCA-stratified analysis possible due to insufficient cases; expected associations with germline BRCA1/2 and HRD signature 3 not detected (likely underpowered)
- Carboplatin and docetaxel administered in combination; PDX experiments needed to attribute findings to individual drugs
- Proteomic data not in cBioPortal; requires CPTAC Data Portal for protein-level re-analysis
- Median RNA-protein correlation of 0.37 means many protein-level findings cannot be confirmed by transcriptomics alone
- External validation BrighTNess used paclitaxel+carboplatin (not docetaxel+carboplatin), introducing regimen heterogeneity
