## Summary

Largest NGS study of NMIBC at the time, profiling 105 primary treatment-naïve index tumors (23 LGTa, 12 HGTis, 32 HGTa, 38 HGT1) from an IRB-approved prospective protocol using MSK-IMPACT (341 or 410 gene panel) with matched germline DNA. The study reveals that TERT promoter mutations (73%) and chromatin-modifying gene alterations (69%) are pan-NMIBC events suggesting early carcinogenetic roles. In high-grade NMIBC, ERBB2 and FGFR3 alterations occur in 57% of tumors in a mutually exclusive pattern, both representing druggable targets. DNA damage repair (DDR) gene alterations occur in 30% of high-grade NMIBC — a rate matching MIBC — and are associated with significantly higher TMB, with implications for BCG immunotherapy and checkpoint inhibitor trials. Critically, ARID1A mutations were the only genetic alteration significantly associated with BCG recurrence (HR 3.14, P=0.002), surviving multiple comparison correction. All genomic and clinical data publicly available in `blca_nmibc_2017` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: TERT promoter mutations (73%) and chromatin-modifying alterations (69%) are early, pan-NMIBC events

**Finding:** TERT promoter mutations were identified across all grade/stage groups: 61% LGTa, 88% HGTa, 79% HGT1 (no significant difference by grade P=0.15 or stage P=0.2). TERT promoter was not sequenced by TCGA WES. Chromatin-modifying gene alterations were found in 69% (72/105) of all NMIBC tumors; the most common were KDM6A (38%) and ARID1A (21%). KDM6A was highest in LGTa (52%) and decreased with stage; ARID1A showed no significant grade/stage correlation (P=0.23 and P=0.44).

**Detail:** The near-ubiquity of TERT promoter mutations and chromatin modifications across all NMIBC stages and grades strongly suggests they are among the earliest events in urothelial carcinogenesis — before grade/stage divergence. This supports development of non-invasive urine-based detection assays targeting TERT and chromatin gene mutations for NMIBC surveillance as an alternative to cystoscopy. MSK-MIBC also showed 85% TERT promoter mutation rate, confirming its persistence across the disease spectrum.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, KDM6A, ARID1A, KMT2D, EP300, CREBBP · Bladder/urothelial carcinoma (NMIBC)

### F2: ERBB2 and FGFR3 alterations affect 57% of high-grade NMIBC in mutually exclusive pattern

**Finding:** In 82 high-grade NMIBC tumors, FGFR3 alterations were present in 39% (32/82) and ERBB2 alterations in 28% (23/82), with 57% (47/82) having at least one alteration in ERBB2 or FGFR3. These two genes showed a highly mutually exclusive pattern (P-value significant per supplementary analysis). ERBB2 alterations were exclusively in high-grade tumors (except one LGTa R103Q missense of unknown significance). HGTis was particularly enriched for ERBB2: 6/12 (50%) harbored ERBB2 alterations including hotspot S310F missense in 3 tumors.

**Detail:** Four FGFR3 fusions were identified in the NMIBC cohort, including FGFR3-TNIP2 (predicted activating, in-frame kinase domain) and FGFR3-TACC3 (in one LGTa, a known low-grade fusion). FGFR3 mutations were strongly enriched in LGTa (83%) and declined with grade (59% HGTa, 34% HGT1). ERBB2 mutations were of known hotspot or likely functional types. ERBB2 and FGFR3 not associated with BCG recurrence (P=0.3), supporting their evaluation as targets for intravesical or systemic therapeutic trials rather than BCG response prediction.

**Genes / cancer types:** ERBB2, FGFR3, FGFR3-TACC3, FGFR3-TNIP2 · Bladder/urothelial carcinoma (NMIBC)

### F3: DDR gene alterations in 30% of high-grade NMIBC — matching MIBC — with elevated TMB

**Finding:** Deleterious DDR gene alterations were found in 30% (25/82) of high-grade NMIBC tumors vs. only 4% (1/23) of low-grade (P=0.012). The rate in high-grade NMIBC was similar to MSK-MIBC (33%, 13/40). ERCC2 missense mutations were the most common DDR alteration (17% of high-grade NMIBC, 20% of MIBC), clustering around conserved helicase domains. Additional DDR alterations: ATM, BRCA1, BRCA2, ERCC4, PALB2, CHEK2, FANCC, MSH6.

**Detail:** Tumors with deleterious DDR gene alterations had significantly higher TMB (median 26 mutations/Mb, IQR 15–36) vs. DDR-intact tumors (8, IQR 5–12, P<0.001). ERCC2-mutated tumors had especially elevated TMB (median 29 vs. 8, P<0.001). High-grade NMIBC had higher TMB than low-grade (median 9 vs. 7, P=0.032) and comparable TMB to MIBC (10, P=0.84). The high DDR alteration rate in high-grade NMIBC has implications for platinum chemosensitivity (as demonstrated in MIBC) and potentially for checkpoint inhibitor response (higher TMB → more neoantigens). Mutational count was not associated with BCG recurrence on MSK-IMPACT (HR 0.96, P=0.3).

### F4: ARID1A mutations associate with BCG recurrence (HR 3.14) — the only significant genomic predictor

**Finding:** Among 62 high-grade NMIBC patients treated with a 6-week induction BCG course (without maintenance), 52% recurred at median follow-up of 24 months. Cox regression across all genes altered in ≥5 tumors identified only ARID1A mutations as significantly associated with BCG recurrence (HR 3.14, 95% CI 1.51–6.51, P=0.002, FDR-corrected P=0.04). This association held when ARID1A missense variants of unknown significance were included (HR 3.08, P=0.002) and in the full 100-patient TUR-treated cohort (HR 2.07, P=0.024). No clinical variables (age, sex, stage, tumor number/size, concomitant CIS, smoking) were associated with BCG recurrence.

**Detail:** ARID1A encodes a subunit of the SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex. ARID1A loss-of-function mutations are associated with worse prognosis in multiple cancers and with EZH2 synthetic lethality (suggesting EZH2 inhibitors may rescue ARID1A-deficient tumors). ARID1A mutations did not correlate with grade (P=0.23) or stage (P=0.44), suggesting they do not merely identify higher-risk tumors. The mechanism of BCG resistance with ARID1A loss is unknown but may involve impaired SWI/SNF-mediated immune recognition or NLRC5 regulation of MHC-I expression.

**Genes / cancer types:** ARID1A · Bladder/urothelial carcinoma (NMIBC)

### F5: TP53/MDM2 alterations show stepwise increase with grade and stage; cell cycle gene alterations also increase

**Finding:** TP53/MDM2 alteration rates showed a stepwise increase with grade and stage: 9% LGTa → 19% HGTa → 45% HGT1 → 75% MSK-MIBC (P<0.001 for both grade and stage). Cell cycle regulation gene alterations (RB1, CCND1, CDKN1A/p21, CDKN2A) showed parallel increases: 13% LGTa → 41% HGTa → 42% HGT1 → 53% MSK-MIBC (P=0.028 stage, P=0.009 grade). STAG2 truncating mutations were enriched in LGTa (P=0.046), contrary to some prior reports suggesting aggressive associations.

**Detail:** This stepwise increase in TP53/MDM2 and cell cycle alterations with disease stage mirrors the two-pathway model of bladder cancer: the low-grade papillary pathway (driven by FGFR3/RAS activation) and the high-grade/invasive pathway (driven by TP53/RB loss). The STAG2 data resolves a prior controversy: truncating STAG2 mutations are associated with lower-stage disease in this cohort, consistent with their role as early but not progression-driving events. TP53 alteration was not associated with BCG recurrence in this cohort (HR 1.09, P=0.819).

## Open Questions

- Does ARID1A mutation status represent a true predictive biomarker of BCG response or a general prognostic marker, and can it be validated in an independent prospective NMIBC BCG cohort?
- Can EZH2 inhibitors rescue ARID1A-deficient NMIBC tumors and improve BCG outcomes, given the synthetic lethality relationship?
- Are TERT promoter mutations and KDM6A alterations detectable in urine cell-free DNA or exosomes at sufficient sensitivity to replace cystoscopic surveillance?
- Given that DDR-altered NMIBC has TMB comparable to MIBC and a rate of DDR mutations matching MIBC, should intravesical or systemic checkpoint inhibitors be tested preferentially in DDR-altered high-grade NMIBC?
- Should FGFR3 inhibitors (erdafitinib) and ERBB2-targeting agents be evaluated as alternatives or adjuncts to BCG in a prospectively selected NMIBC population with known FGFR3 or ERBB2 alterations?

## Limitations

- Relatively small cohort (n=105 total; n=62 evaluable for BCG recurrence), limiting statistical power, especially for subgroup analyses by grade/stage
- Tumor purity for carcinoma in situ (Tis) specimens was particularly challenging — nearly two-thirds of Tis samples had insufficient purity for analysis, leading to Tis under-representation
- MSK-IMPACT captures only 341–410 genes; whole-exome sequencing would provide broader mutational landscape
- TERT promoter not captured by TCGA WES (standard); comparison with TCGA MIBC is limited
- Median follow-up of 24 months may be insufficient to capture late BCG failures
- Single-institution cohort; generalizability to non-MSK centers and diverse patient populations needs validation
- No available RNA-seq, proteomics, or immune profiling to link genomic alterations to immune microenvironment features relevant to BCG mechanism
