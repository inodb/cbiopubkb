## Summary

This study performs whole-genome sequencing (WGS) of FACS-isolated Hodgkin and Reed Sternberg (HRS) cells from 25 classic Hodgkin lymphoma (cHL) patients, combined with WES from 36 additional cases (61 total), to provide the first comprehensive genomic landscape of cHL including noncoding alterations, structural variants (SVs), and mutational timing. Key findings include: 26 driver genes identified (8 high-confidence), novel drivers not previously described in cHL (CISH, MSL2, NONO), APOBEC mutagenesis in 64% of cases, chromothripsis, and temporal reconstruction showing structural variants and driver mutations precede chromosomal gains. High-level amplification of 9p24.1 (*JAK2/CD274/PDCD1LG2*) and 2p16.1 (*REL/XPO1*) occur in ~67–68% of patients. Data are in cBioPortal as `chl_sccc_2023`.

## Key Findings

### F1: WGS of FACS-isolated HRS cells reveals 26 driver genes including 8 novel cHL drivers

**Finding:** Driver discovery using MutSigCV, OncodriveFML, and dndscv across 61 cHL cases identified 26 significantly mutated driver genes; 8 were "high-confidence" (called by ≥2 tools). Most frequent: SOCS1 (62%), TNFAIP3 (36%), B2M (32%). Novel cHL drivers include CISH, FAM230A, MSL2, TMSB4X, MAL2, NONO, RAB19, SPDYE1. 95% of cases had ≥1 driver gene mutated (median 4 per sample).

**Detail:** WGS identified 15% additional drivers not found by WES alone, confirming WGS superiority for comprehensive cHL profiling. CISH (SOCS family, JAK/STAT regulator), MSL2 (H4K16 acetyltransferase involved in DNA damage repair), and IL4R (JAK/STAT pathway) are functionally relevant new targets. EZH2 hotspot mutations were rare (n=1) but statistically enriched (dndscv), representing a therapeutic target (tazemetostat is FDA-approved in follicular lymphoma).

**Genes / cancer types:** `SOCS1`, `TNFAIP3`, `B2M`, `GNA13`, `PTPN1`, `BCL7A`, `CISH`, `IL4R`, `EZH2`, `MSL2` · Classic Hodgkin lymphoma

### F2: APOBEC mutagenesis present in 64% of cHL — uniquely high among hematologic malignancies

**Finding:** 64% of cHL patients showed APOBEC mutational activity (SBS2/SBS13), a proportion significantly higher than non-Hodgkin lymphoma and CLL (P<0.00001) and similar to multiple myeloma. A subset (16%, n=4/25 WGS cases) showed hyper-APOBEC with predominant APOBEC3A activity. SBS84-AID activity localizes to specific driver genes (BCL7A, SOCS1, IL4R, TMSB4X, ITPKB) via somatic hypermutation.

**Detail:** Five mutational signatures were identified: SBS1/SBS5 (clock-like), SBS2/SBS13 (APOBEC), SBS84 (AID/somatic hypermutation). Clock-like signatures (SBS1/SBS5) are elevated up to 6-fold in pediatric/AYA patients vs. older adults (P=0.003), driving their higher overall mutation burden. SBS25, linked to procarbazine/dacarbazine exposure, was detected in 2 relapsed cases. APOBEC activity is an intermediate/late event in cHL — detected both before and after chromosomal gains.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BCL7A`, `SOCS1`, `IL4R`, `TMSB4X`, `ITPKB` (SHM targets) · Classic Hodgkin lymphoma

### F3: High ploidy in cHL acquired through independent multi-step chromosomal gains — not always whole-genome duplication

**Finding:** Median tumor ploidy was 2.95 (range 1.66–5.33); whole-genome duplication (WGD) was present in only 59% of cases. In 60% of patients, the final clonal copy-number profile was acquired through ≥2 independent chromosomal gain events at different molecular times — indicating progressive gain acquisition rather than single WGD events.

**Detail:** Most recurrent CNA peaks (19 total; 5 gains, 14 losses) are caused by large (>10 Mb) or whole chromosome/arm events (95.6%). Most frequently deleted: 6p21.33 (HLA locus, 52%) and 6q23.3/TNFAIP3 (44%). Most frequently amplified: 9p24.1 (*JAK2/CD274/PDCD1LG2*, 67–68%) and 2p16.1 (*REL/XPO1*, 67–68%). High-level gains (>6 copies) at 9p24.1 and 2p16.1 observed in 16–18% of cases.

**Genes / cancer types:** `JAK2`, `CD274` (PD-L1), `PDCD1LG2` (PD-L2), `REL`, `XPO1`, `TNFAIP3`, `HLA locus` · Classic Hodgkin lymphoma

### F4: Temporal reconstruction shows driver mutations and SVs precede chromosomal gains in cHL evolution

**Finding:** Across 313 nonsynonymous driver mutations, 42.5% were "early clonal" (acquired before chromosomal duplications). Mutations in *PTPN1*, *GNA13*, and *HIST1H1E* were identified as earliest drivers using the Bradley–Terry temporal model; *SOCS1*, *NFKBIE*, and *STAT6* were acquired later. The proportion of duplicated driver clonal mutations in cHL (61%) is significantly higher than multiple myeloma (38%, P=0.001).

**Detail:** Structural variants enriched for RAG recombination motifs, driver mutations in B2M, BCL7A, GNA13, and PTPN1, and onset of AID-driven mutagenesis typically precede large chromosomal gains. AID activity is detected throughout cancer evolution (not restricted to early phases), unlike in multiple myeloma. APOBEC activity is intermediate/late. Loss-of-function mutations in LOH regions were acquired before the duplications in 88% of cases.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PTPN1`, `GNA13`, `HIST1H1E`, `B2M`, `BCL7A`, `SOCS1`, `NFKBIE`, `STAT6` · Classic Hodgkin lymphoma

### F5: 9p24.1 amplification (JAK2/PD-L1/PD-L2) in ~67% and biallelic TNFAIP3 inactivation in 21% define major immune escape mechanisms

**Finding:** The most frequently amplified locus is 9p24.1 (JAK2/CD274/PDCD1LG2) in 67% of patients, with high-level gains (>6 copies) in 18% — providing both JAK/STAT pathway activation and PD-L1/PD-L2 overexpression. TNFAIP3 (A20, NF-κB negative regulator) was biallelically inactivated in 21% of cases. B2M biallelic inactivation occurred in 16% — potentially predicting resistance to PD-1 blockade.

**Detail:** 6p21.33 deletions (HLA locus, 52%) extend to cover the HLA region in most patients — the combination of HLA loss and B2M inactivation (immune evasion) alongside PD-L1 amplification (immune checkpoint exploitation) reveals a comprehensive genomic strategy for immune escape in HRS cells. TNFAIP3 deletion plus mutation represents the most common biallelic inactivation event (21%). NF-κB dysregulation through TNFAIP3, REL amplification, and BCL7A mutations converges on constitutive NF-κB activation in cHL.

## Open Questions

- Do B2M biallelic inactivation and HLA deletion in HRS cells predict resistance to PD-1 blockade (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) in relapsed/refractory cHL?
- Does early PTPN1 or GNA13 driver status represent a targetable vulnerability before chromosomal gain complexity accumulates?
- Is APOBEC activity in cHL a driver of chromosomal instability and SV formation, or a passenger process?
- Can the pediatric/AYA vs. older adult mutational burden difference (clock-like vs. APOBEC) be exploited therapeutically?
- Does SBS25 (procarbazine/dacarbazine signature) predict secondary mutation burden and late relapse risk after ABVD-based therapy?

## Limitations

- Small WGS cohort (n=25) with low DNA input requiring amplification — palindromic sequencing artifacts removed but may not be complete
- FACS isolation of HRS cells requires fresh tumor material — limits applicability to routine FFPE samples
- Only 3 EBV-positive cases — insufficient for EBV-positive vs. EBV-negative genomic comparisons
- No clinical outcome data directly linked to genomic subgroups in this study
- Temporal ordering (Bradley–Terry) is based on pairwise precedence — requires ≥2 patients sharing events; rare drivers are excluded
- Intratumoral T cells used as germline control — potential for T-cell somatic mutations confounding germline calling
