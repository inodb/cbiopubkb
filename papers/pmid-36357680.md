## Summary

This prospective international cohort study (MSK + GenesisCare Sydney) enrolled 1,127 patients with advanced NSCLC to evaluate the clinical utility of ctDNA sequencing for therapy guidance and its association with overall survival. Using the MSK-ACCESS liquid biopsy panel, ctDNA detection was independently associated with shorter OS (HR 2.05; P<0.001) even after adjusting for metabolic tumor volume and clinical factors. Among the 722 patients with detectable ctDNA, 255 matched to targeted therapy had significantly longer survival than those not matched (HR 0.63; P<0.001). A critical finding was that 25% of patients harbored ctDNA-only alterations absent from concurrent tissue sequencing, which disproportionately included subclonal resistance drivers such as *RICTOR* and *PIK3CA* — and were associated with short survival. This study provides the largest prospective evidence base confirming that ctDNA-guided therapy improves survival in NSCLC, and that liquid biopsy captures clinically relevant tumor heterogeneity invisible to tissue profiling.

## Key Findings

### F1: ctDNA detection independently predicts shorter OS in NSCLC regardless of tumor volume or clinical features

**Finding:** ctDNA positivity (any detectable ctDNA in plasma) was associated with a more than two-fold increase in mortality risk (HR 2.05; 95% CI 1.74–2.42; P<0.001), and this prognostic significance was independent of clinicopathologic features and metabolic tumor volume measured by PET.

**Detail:** 722 of 1,127 patients (64%) had detectable ctDNA. Multivariable analyses adjusting for histology, performance status, smoking, prior treatment, and PET-derived metabolic tumor volume all confirmed the independent prognostic value of ctDNA detection. This supports ctDNA as a bona fide biomarker of disease burden that captures tumor biology beyond what anatomic or metabolic imaging reveals.

### F2: ctDNA-matched targeted therapy confers significant OS benefit over non-targeted therapy in ctDNA-positive patients

**Finding:** Among the 722 patients with detectable ctDNA, 255 (23% of total cohort) who received targeted therapy guided by ctDNA sequencing had substantially longer survival than those not receiving targeted therapy (HR 0.63; 95% CI 0.52–0.76; P<0.001).

**Detail:** This is one of the first large-scale prospective demonstrations that ctDNA-matched targeted therapy translates into an OS benefit rather than only radiographic response. The effect size (37% reduction in mortality risk) is clinically meaningful and validates the use of ctDNA sequencing as an actionable diagnostic tool. Short-term studies had previously only shown equivalent radiographic response rates between ctDNA- and tissue-matched therapy, without long-term OS data.

### F3: ctDNA-only alterations (absent in tissue) capture subclonal resistance drivers in 25% of patients

**Finding:** In 25% of patients with paired ctDNA and tissue sequencing, genomic alterations were detected in ctDNA but not in time-matched tissue. These ctDNA-only alterations were disproportionately subclonal resistance drivers — particularly *RICTOR* amplifications and *PIK3CA* mutations — and were associated with significantly shorter survival.

**Detail:** ctDNA-only alterations likely reflect spatial tumor heterogeneity: subclonal populations at metastatic sites not sampled by the tissue biopsy. The enrichment for known resistance mechanisms (*RICTOR* amplification activating mTORC2, *PIK3CA* mutations bypassing targeted EGFR/ALK blockade) among these ctDNA-exclusive variants suggests they represent clinically actionable subclones. These findings underscore the complementary role of liquid biopsy alongside tissue profiling, particularly in the resistance setting.

**Genes / cancer types:** `RICTOR`, `PIK3CA`, `EGFR`, `KRAS` · Non-small-cell lung cancer

### F4: MSK-ACCESS dual-calling approach enables sensitive detection including de novo and hotspot calling

**Finding:** MSK-ACCESS uses hybrid-capture duplex barcoded sequencing with two bioinformatic calling methods: de novo base calling (requiring mutations in ≥3 duplex consensus reads for hotspots) and additional calling modes for non-hotspot variants. This enables highly sensitive and specific ctDNA detection from plasma.

**Detail:** The dual calling methodology is designed to maximize sensitivity for low-allele-fraction variants while controlling error rates from sequencing artifacts. This technical advance underpins the clinical utility demonstrated in the study, enabling reliable detection of the actionable alterations at allele fractions typical of plasma ctDNA. The methodology supports both primary diagnosis and monitoring in the community oncology setting (as demonstrated by the GenesisCare cohort).

### F5: ctDNA expands community access to life-prolonging targeted therapy

**Finding:** 20% of MSK patients had no tissue sequencing at all (due to inability to obtain tissue, insufficient material, or sequencing failure). ctDNA profiling identified actionable alterations in many of these tissue-inaccessible patients, providing a pathway to receive targeted therapy that would otherwise not be available.

**Detail:** The inclusion of the GenesisCare (community oncology) cohort demonstrates the feasibility of ctDNA-guided therapy outside major academic centers. This access dimension is a critical public health implication: liquid biopsy has the potential to democratize genomically guided cancer care by circumventing tissue sampling barriers. In resource-limited or community settings, ctDNA-only profiling may be the only viable route to matched targeted therapy.

## Open Questions

- Do ctDNA-only *RICTOR* or *PIK3CA* resistance alterations predict benefit from mTOR or PI3K inhibitor combinations with primary targeted therapy?
- Can serial ctDNA monitoring after ctDNA-matched targeted therapy initiation predict early progression and guide adaptive therapy switches?
- Does the prognostic effect of ctDNA detection vary by driver alteration type (e.g., EGFR-mutant vs. KRAS-mutant vs. driver-negative NSCLC)?
- In patients with discordant ctDNA/tissue results, does biopsy of an alternative metastatic site resolve the discordance?
- What fraction of ctDNA-only subclonal resistance alterations are biologically clonal at any single tumor site vs. truly pan-tumoral?

## Limitations

- Retrospective assignment of "targeted therapy" — not a randomized trial; confounding by indication may influence OS comparisons
- Not all patients received MSK-ACCESS (MSK-specific); Sydney cohort used diverse commercial ctDNA platforms with variable sensitivity/specificity
- ctDNA-tissue discordance analyzed only in the subset with concurrent draws (n=163 at MSK); generalizability limited
- Metabolic tumor volume estimated by PET only in 457/1,127 patients; covariable adjustment for tumor volume incomplete for the full cohort
- Sydney community cohort had different prior treatment profiles (59% previously treated vs. 32% at MSK), limiting direct comparison
- No germline or clonal hematopoiesis (CH) filtering described in detail — some ctDNA-only variants may be CH artifacts
- Single time-point ctDNA; longitudinal dynamics not reported in this primary analysis
