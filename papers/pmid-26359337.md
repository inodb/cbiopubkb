## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing (WES) of 110 pretreatment metastatic melanoma tumors and matched germline from patients treated with ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4) monotherapy, plus RNA-seq in 40 patients, establishes the first large-cohort genomic analysis of immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) response. Key findings: total nonsynonymous mutational load (TMB) is significantly associated with clinical benefit (P=0.0076), as is predicted neoantigen load (P=0.027). Cytolytic activity in the tumor microenvironment — measured by geometric mean expression of GZMA and PRF1 — is significantly elevated in responders (P=0.042). CTLA-4 and PD-L2 expression are also elevated in the tumor microenvironment of responders. No recurrent neoantigen sequences predict response — the vast majority of benefit-associated neoantigens are private events. No specific genes (including BRAF and NRAS) are enriched for mutations in responders vs. non-responders. Patient-matched RNA-seq outperforms TCGA-based expression filtering for identifying expressed neoantigens. A subset of patients show long-term survival despite early progression — a pattern consistent with delayed immunotherapy response. Data available in `skcm_dfci_2015` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Total mutational burden and neoantigen load are significantly associated with ipilimumab clinical benefit

**Finding:** Nonsynonymous mutational load (TMB) was significantly higher in patients with clinical benefit vs. no clinical benefit (P=0.0076, Mann-Whitney). Predicted neoantigen load (<500 nM HLA binding affinity) was also significantly higher in the clinical benefit group (P=0.027). Multivariate models controlling for prior RAF inhibitor treatment and M class confirmed that patients with high neoantigen load (>100 neoantigens) or high mutational load (>100 mutations) were significantly more likely to achieve clinical benefit (P=0.037 and P=0.017, respectively). Median nonsynonymous mutational load in the full cohort was 197 per sample (range 7–5,854).

**Detail:** Neoantigen load was highly correlated with mutational load (Spearman rho=0.97, P<0.0001), as expected since neoantigens largely arise from nonsynonymous mutations. Applying increasingly stringent HLA binding affinity thresholds (250 nM, 100 nM, 50 nM) progressively diminished the strength of the association between neoantigen load and clinical benefit, suggesting that the bulk of the signal derives from the total number of predicted neoantigens rather than high-affinity binders specifically. These results validate and extend prior smaller cohort findings and confirm TMB as a genomic biomarker of CTLA-4 blockade benefit in melanoma.

### F2: No recurrent neoantigens or neoantigen epitopes predict ipilimumab response

**Finding:** Of 77,803 unique neoantigens identified across the cohort, only 28 (~0.04%) were found in more than one patient with clinical benefit and absent in all patients with no clinical benefit or long-term survival. These 28 recurrent benefit-associated neoantigens showed no shared features or consistent motifs. A previously described tetrapeptide signature linked to ipilimumab response was not enriched in the clinical benefit cohort. Previously validated immunogenic nonamers from other cohorts were also not observed.

**Detail:** Patient-matched RNA-seq filtering identified 198 expressed neoantigens per patient (median), compared to 395 by DNA alone — a 50% reduction in candidates. TCGA-based unmatched expression filtering identified 347 neoantigens per patient, but only partially overlapped with patient-matched RNA filtering: TCGA overestimated expressed neoantigens by 6,320 in this cohort, and 166 patient-specific neoantigens would have been missed using TCGA data alone. This highlights the importance of patient-matched RNA-seq for neoantigen prioritization. The absence of recurrent neoantigens implies that identifying shared tumor rejection antigens for melanoma immunotherapy will require much larger cohorts (>100s of patients).

### F3: Cytolytic activity in the tumor microenvironment (GZMA, PRF1) predicts ipilimumab benefit

**Finding:** The geometric mean expression of granzyme A (GZMA) and perforin 1 (PRF1) — a validated surrogate for tumor-infiltrating cytolytic T cell activity — was significantly elevated in clinical benefit vs. no-clinical-benefit patients (P=0.042, Mann-Whitney, n=42 with RNA-seq). The long-term survival group showed cytolytic gene expression similar to the clinical benefit group. CTLA-4 mRNA expression was also significantly elevated in the clinical benefit group (P=0.033), as was PD-L2 (P=0.041).

**Detail:** These findings suggest that a pre-existing active anti-tumor immune response (as reflected by cytolytic effector gene expression) is required for ipilimumab to confer clinical benefit — consistent with the model that CTLA-4 blockade reinvigorates existing (but suppressed) immune responses rather than generating them de novo. Elevated CTLA-4 and PD-L2 expression in responding tumors is consistent with these molecules being markers of an ongoing immune response. HLA class I gene expression was similar across response groups (P>0.05), and HLA class I somatic mutations were rare and did not segregate by response. The small RNA-seq cohort (n=42) limits statistical power for these transcriptomic associations.

### F4: No specific somatic mutations (BRAF, NRAS, or others) associate with ipilimumab response

**Finding:** No individual gene showed significant enrichment of nonsynonymous mutations in the clinical benefit vs. no-clinical-benefit subgroups (P>0.05 for all genes, including BRAF and NRAS). Clinical metadata — age, gender, histology, primary tumor site, number of prior therapies, and LDH at initiation of ipilimumab — also showed no correlation with clinical response. Thus, response to CTLA-4 blockade in melanoma is primarily driven by the quantity (burden) rather than the identity of mutations.

**Detail:** BRAF mutation status (V600E and others) did not correlate with ipilimumab response, consistent with subsequent clinical studies showing similar ipilimumab efficacy regardless of BRAF mutation status. This finding distinguishes genomic correlates of CTLA-4 blockade from those of targeted BRAF inhibition. The lack of gene-specific associations suggests that ipilimumab response is an emergent property of total immunogenic burden rather than specific oncogenic pathway activation. This is mechanistically distinct from anti-PD-1 response in tumors like MMR-deficient colorectal cancer, where specific pathways (MMR) drive the hypermutation phenotype.

### F5: A distinct subgroup of patients experience long-term survival despite early progression on ipilimumab

**Finding:** Ten patients (9% of cohort) showed early tumor progression by RECIST (PFS <6 months) but had overall survival exceeding 2 years ("long-term survivors with early progression"). This group's TMB and neoantigen load tracked with the "no clinical benefit" subgroup, and their cytolytic activity expression was similar to the "clinical benefit" group, though the small subgroup size precluded definitive conclusions.

**Detail:** This pattern — early radiographic progression followed by long-term survival — may represent pseudoprogression (immune-cell infiltration mimicking progression) or a delayed response mechanism unique to immunotherapy. The observation is clinically relevant because standard RECIST-based early discontinuation criteria used for cytotoxic chemotherapy may be inappropriate for immune checkpoint inhibitors. The molecular basis for this survival pattern remains unclear; cytolytic gene expression similarity to responders raises the possibility that immune infiltration is present but not yet productive at the time of early imaging assessment.

## Open Questions

- Can GZMA/PRF1 cytolytic score and TMB be combined into a composite predictive model for ipilimumab response with higher precision than either biomarker alone?
- Why do some patients with high neoantigen loads fail to respond to ipilimumab, and can RNA-level expressed neoantigen filtering improve identification of these "bystander" vs "driver" neoantigens?
- What determines whether a patient is a "long-term survivor with early progression" — is it pseudoprogression (immune infiltration), a delayed response mechanism, or a distinct biology?
- Can the cytolytic microenvironment signature (GZMA, PRF1) predict response to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade as well as CTLA-4 blockade, enabling shared immune profiling platforms across ICI settings?
- Would larger cohorts (>500 patients) reveal recurrent neoantigens associated with ipilimumab benefit, or is neoantigen landscape in melanoma too private to discover population-level shared antigens?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=110 for WES, n=40 with matched RNA-seq) limits statistical power, particularly for transcriptomic and expressed neoantigen analyses
- Response definitions use a composite endpoint combining RECIST and OS-based criteria; different definitions in future studies could affect generalizability
- All patients received ipilimumab monotherapy — findings may not generalize to combination ICI (e.g., ipilimumab + nivolumab) settings
- FFPE-derived RNA-seq (n=42) may introduce technical noise in expression measurements
- HLA typing performed from germline WES data; HLA imputation accuracy limits neoantigen binding predictions for rare alleles
- 40 patients excluded post-WES due to QC issues; exclusion may introduce selection bias if germline contamination or purity issues correlate with tumor biology
- No functional validation that identified neoantigens actually trigger T cell responses in responding patients
