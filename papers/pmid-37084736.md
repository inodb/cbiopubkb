## Summary

Large-scale study of 2532 LUAD specimens (2309 patients) using MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing and WES to map genomic and clinicopathologic features of metastasis, organotropism, and metastasis-free survival. Ever-metastatic tumors are enriched for TP53, SMARCA4, and CDKN2A alterations and show higher chromosomal instability (FGA), increased whole-genome duplication, and higher APOBEC mutagenesis. Organ-site analyses reveal that SMARCA4 associates with bone metastasis and Hippo pathway alterations with CNS metastasis. In matched primary–metastasis pairs, driver mutations are predominantly shared (51.5% shared vs. 24.8% CNA sharing), with only 4% of metastases harboring a new level-1 actionable alteration not in the primary. APOBEC signatures are enriched in metastases, particularly liver lesions. Data available in `luad_mskcc_2023_met_organotropism` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Clinicopathologic features distinguish ever-metastatic from nonmetastatic LUAD

**Finding:** Patients with ever-metastatic LUAD are younger (median 67.3 vs. 68.6 years, q=0.032), more often male (37% vs. 27%, q=0.008), and have micropapillary or solid-predominant histology (18% vs. 8%, q=0.002) compared to patients who never develop metastasis.

**Detail:** Ever-metastatic tumors also have higher TMB (median 5.6 vs. 4.9 mut/Mb, p=0.003), higher FGA (0.47 vs. 0.26, p<0.001), and higher WGD rate (40% vs. 23%, p<0.001). APOBEC signatures (SBS2, SBS13) are enriched in metastatic tumors. Among 10 canonical pathways, p53, PI3K, cell cycle, Nrf2, and TGFβ are enriched; EGFR and KRAS proportions are similar between groups. Externally validated in Dana-Farber/AACR GENIE cohort (n=318).

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, SMARCA4, CDKN2A, MDM2, PIK3CA, MYC, RB1, NKX2-1, FOXA1, RBM10 · Lung adenocarcinoma

### F2: Gene-specific organotropism — SMARCA4 bone, CDKN2A bone timing, Hippo CNS

**Finding:** SMARCA4 alterations are associated with higher probability of bone-specific metastasis, and TP53 alterations with lymph node metastases. CDKN2A alterations correlate with shorter time to bone metastasis; Hippo pathway alterations associate with shorter time to CNS metastasis. SMARCA4 is associated with worse metastasis-free survival (MFS) across all sites.

**Detail:** Adrenal and liver metastases rarely occurred as solitary events, suggesting these sites are part of a broader metastatic phenotype. Younger age, higher chromosomal instability, TP53 alteration, and ERBB2 alteration are associated with higher metastatic burden (more sites). Prior to sequencing, only 10% of early- and late-stage metastatic primaries had received treatment, making treatment bias less likely.

**Genes / cancer types:** SMARCA4, TP53, CDKN2A, ERBB2, Hippo pathway (YAP1, LATS1, LATS2) · Lung adenocarcinoma

### F3: Driver mutations are predominantly shared between primary and matched metastases; only 4% of mets gain new actionable alterations

**Finding:** Among matched primary–metastatic pairs (n=152 primaries, 184 metastases), mutations are shared more often than CNAs or fusions (51.5% vs. 24.8% vs. 28.9%). Shared mutations and CNAs are enriched for oncogenic variants. Only 4% (7/184) of metastatic lesions developed a level-1 actionable alteration absent from the primary, nearly all after TKI treatment (EGFR T790M or ALK resistance mutations).

**Detail:** Clonal mutations account for 73.8% of all mutations and are more often shared between primary and metastasis than subclonal mutations (66.5% vs. 44.0%, p<0.001). FGA is higher in metastatic samples (median 0.085, p<0.001). WGD is most often shared (47.2%) rather than private to the metastasis (32.0%) or primary (20.8%). CNA alterations are predominantly private to metastases (59.1%). For patients with ≥2 sequenced metastases, 82.4% of driver alterations are shared across all matched samples.

**Genes / cancer types:** EGFR (T790M), ALK (I1171T), KRAS (G12C), MET, CDKN2A · Lung adenocarcinoma

### F4: APOBEC mutational signature enriched in metastases, especially liver

**Finding:** APOBEC-related signatures (SBS2 and SBS13) are significantly more prevalent in metastatic versus primary LUAD specimens. Among metastatic sites, liver metastases are most enriched for SBS13 (67% vs. 27%, q=0.034) and least often SBS4-positive (smoking signature: 27% vs. 65%, q=0.034).

**Detail:** Analysis was performed using WES on 231 specimens (120 primaries, 111 metastases). SBS4 (smoking) is the dominant signature in primaries; SBS1 (aging) and SBS2/13 (APOBEC) are co-detected. Liver metastases have a distinct mutational signature profile relative to metastases at other sites, which may reflect differential immune selection or microenvironmental influences in the liver.

### F5: Genomic findings externally validated; clinical stage I–III MFS analysis identifies prognostic genes

**Finding:** Key genomic and demographic associations (TP53, MYC, RBM10 enrichments; younger age and male sex in ever-metastatic) were confirmed in an independent Dana-Farber/AACR GENIE LUAD cohort (n=318). Among clinical stage I–III patients (n=576), TP53, SMARCA4, CDKN2A, and ERBB2 alterations are independently associated with worse MFS.

**Detail:** RBM10 alterations occur more often in nonmetastatic tumors (16% vs. 7%, q=0.001), consistent with its known association with early-stage and favorable outcomes. In the multivariable model incorporating clinicopathologic factors (pathologic stage, smoking, histology, sex), genomic features retained independent prognostic significance, supporting their biological relevance beyond stage.

## Open Questions

- Why are liver metastases specifically enriched for APOBEC (SBS13) and depleted for smoking (SBS4) signature, and does this reflect differential immune editing or a distinct metastatic clone?
- Does the rare acquisition of new actionable alterations in metastases (4%) mean that re-biopsy at progression has limited clinical utility in LUAD, or are specific scenarios (e.g., TKI resistance) clinically informative?
- Can the organotropism-associated gene signatures (SMARCA4 → bone, Hippo → CNS) be prospectively used to guide surveillance imaging protocols?
- What is the functional basis of the association between micropapillary/solid histology and increased metastatic proclivity?
- Does the MFS advantage of RBM10-altered tumors represent a direct tumor-suppressive effect or a surrogate for favorable biology?

## Limitations

- Retrospective, single-institution (MSK) cohort; external validation was limited to an independent targeted sequencing dataset (GENIE), not a full replication cohort
- Metastatic site annotation relied on imaging and pathology records; lesions without biopsy confirmation may introduce misclassification
- The matched primary–metastasis cohort (MP-M) required sequencing of the primary before metastasis, introducing possible selection bias toward biologically active tumors
- WES performed on only 231 of 2532 specimens; mutational signature analysis may be underpowered for rare sites
- Treatment-induced genomic changes in the 79% of metastatic specimens with prior therapy may confound primary–metastasis comparisons
