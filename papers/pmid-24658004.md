## Summary

This 2014 Nature Genetics study performed targeted deep sequencing (MSK-IMPACT, 279 cancer-related genes) on 12 paired tumor-normal samples from patients with small cell carcinoma of the ovary, hypercalcemic type (SCCOHT) — a rare, highly aggressive cancer predominantly affecting young women. The study identified inactivating biallelic *SMARCA4* mutations in 100% (12/12) of SCCOHT tumors, with only 4 additional non-recurrent mutations across all other 278 genes. IHC and immunoblot confirmed loss of SMARCA4 protein in 7/9 evaluable cases. One case (case 111) harbored a germline *SMARCA4* nonsense mutation. Functional studies showed SMARCA4 re-expression suppresses cell growth. SMARCA2 represents a synthetic lethal target in SMARCA4-deficient tumors. This study is the founding genomic characterization of the `scco_mskcc` cBioPortal study and established *SMARCA4* as the defining molecular driver of SCCOHT.

## Key Findings

### F1: Biallelic inactivating SMARCA4 mutations in 100% of SCCOHT tumors — an exceptionally clean single-gene driver

**Finding:** Every one of 12 SCCOHT tumors harbored biallelic inactivating *SMARCA4* mutations (probability < 2.22 × 10⁻¹⁶ by chance). Mutation types included nonsense, frameshift, splice-site mutations, and a homozygous in-frame deletion of two exons. No *SMARCA4* missense mutations were found.

**Detail:** 4 cases harbored two distinct inactivating *SMARCA4* mutations; the remaining 8 had a single inactivating mutation plus loss of heterozygosity (mutant allele frequency ≥0.75 in all cases). No missense mutations were observed — distinguishing SCCOHT *SMARCA4* alterations from the predominantly missense *SMARCA4* mutations seen in TCGA solid tumors (84% missense in 128 TCGA *SMARCA4*-mutant samples). In contrast, across 4,784 non-hypermutated TCGA tumors, the mean number of mutations in the 279 sequenced genes was 4.3 (SD 4.4). SCCOHT had essentially no other driver mutations — a striking contrast to virtually all other solid tumor types. This near-complete absence of passenger mutations suggests an unusually short mutational history and/or a cell of origin with distinct mutational tolerance.

### F2: Loss of SMARCA4 protein expression confirmed by IHC and immunoblot; splice-site mutations cause protein loss despite continued transcription

**Finding:** Immunohistochemistry demonstrated loss of SMARCA4 nuclear staining in all 4 nonsense-mutation cases with available tissue. Immunoblot of 2 cases with donor-site splice-site mutations showed complete protein loss, despite RNA-seq evidence that transcription continued downstream of the splice-site.

**Detail:** Seven of 9 cases with available tissue showed protein loss by IHC (using polyclonal anti-SMARCA4, Upstate Cell Signaling 07-478), with retained staining in internal control cells (blood vessels, stroma). Case 111 with a germline nonsense mutation plus somatic LOH also showed protein loss. For splice-site mutations (cases 101, 102, 112), cDNA sequencing confirmed intron retention — yet downstream transcription continued, suggesting mRNA truncation is not the dominant mechanism of protein loss. Immunoblot (cases 101 and 102) demonstrated complete SMARCA4 protein absence, confirming splice-site mutations functionally inactivate protein expression. Case 103 had a homozygous in-frame deletion of exons 25–26 (102 amino acids of the helicase domain), with retained protein expression by IHC — but the truncated product is catalytically inactive. These data confirm that the mutations create a functionally null SMARCA4 state through diverse molecular mechanisms.

### F3: SMARCA4 inactivation defines a SWI/SNF complex disruption with SMARCA2 as a synthetic lethal therapeutic target

**Finding:** SMARCA4 and SMARCA2 are mutually exclusive ATPase subunits of the BAF (SWI/SNF) complex. In SCCOHT, *SMARCA4* is uniformly lost while *SMARCA2* is retained — creating a selective vulnerability: silencing of *SMARCA2* by RNA interference suppresses growth of SMARCA4-deficient tumor cells (synthetic lethality).

**Detail:** SWI/SNF complexes remodel nucleosome conformation to regulate gene expression. SMARCA4 (BRG1) and SMARCA2 (BRM) are mutually exclusive catalytic subunits: tumors deficient in SMARCA4 depend on SMARCA2 for residual BAF complex activity. Prior work in SMARCA4-deficient lung cancer cell lines showed growth suppression upon SMARCA2 shRNA depletion — and this study identifies the same dependency architecture in SCCOHT. Analysis of TCGA data confirmed that inactivating co-mutations in both *SMARCA4* and *SMARCA2* are extremely rare (only one case with *SMARCA4* inactivating + *SMARCA2* missense across 4,784 tumors), supporting mutual exclusivity and the potential synthetic lethality. SMARCA4 re-expression in SMARCA4-null H1299 NSCLC cells produced dose-dependent growth suppression and increased p21, confirming SMARCA4 as a tumor suppressor with cell cycle regulatory roles. Inhibitors of the SMARCA2 ATPase represent an emerging therapeutic strategy.

### F4: One of 12 SCCOHT cases carried a germline SMARCA4 nonsense mutation, supporting a hereditary component

**Finding:** Case 111 harbored a germline *SMARCA4* nonsense mutation with somatic loss of the wild-type allele (LOH), and showed loss of protein expression by IHC. This case had unusual histology (paucity of pseudofollicular spaces). Clinical data were limited (age and year of diagnosis only, per HIPAA), but prior reports and this finding support hereditary SCCOHT in some patients.

**Detail:** SCCOHT has been reported in familial clusters and is now understood to be part of the SMARCA4 tumor predisposition syndrome — a rare autosomal dominant condition. This 2014 study represents an early report documenting a germline *SMARCA4* mutation as the cause of SCCOHT in an individual case. Subsequent studies (Witkowski et al., Ramos et al., 2014) published concurrently or shortly after identified germline *SMARCA4* mutations in 43–100% of familial/young-onset SCCOHT patients. The implication is that young women (<30 years) diagnosed with SCCOHT should receive germline genetic counseling, and first-degree relatives may be at risk for SMARCA4-related tumors (including rhabdoid tumors of other sites and possibly lung cancer).

### F5: SCCOHT has a near-silent mutational landscape — only 4 non-recurrent mutations in all other genes across 12 tumors

**Finding:** Across all 12 SCCOHT tumors, only 4 additional non-recurrent somatic mutations were found in any of the other 278 sequenced genes — an extraordinarily sparse mutational landscape compared to TCGA tumors (mean 4.3 mutations in the same 279 genes for any given tumor type).

**Detail:** This finding implies that *SMARCA4* biallelic inactivation alone is sufficient to drive SCCOHT — without the need for additional co-driver mutations commonly observed in adult solid tumors (e.g., *TP53*, *KRAS*, *PIK3CA*). This may reflect the young age of onset, the cell of origin (possibly embryonic/stem-like cells where a single SWI/SNF hit is catastrophic for differentiation programs), and/or a very short latency from initiating event to frank malignancy. The absence of additional drivers also has therapeutic implications: SMARCA4 loss (and its consequences for SWI/SNF complex function, EZH2 activity, and differentiation arrest) is the dominant — and essentially sole — molecular vulnerability in SCCOHT, simplifying the therapeutic target landscape.

## Open Questions

- Does EZH2 inhibition (tazemetostat) reverse the differentiation block in SMARCA4-deficient SCCOHT, and does it synergize with SMARCA2 inhibition or CDK4/6 inhibition in vivo?
- What is the full spectrum of *SMARCA4* germline mutation penetrance — do carriers have elevated risk for malignancies beyond SCCOHT (e.g., lung small cell, rhabdoid tumor)?
- Can SMARCA4 protein expression by IHC (loss vs. retained) serve as a reliable surrogate for functional SMARCA4 inactivation to guide clinical trials, given one case (103) with retained IHC but catalytically dead protein?
- Is there a cell of origin for SCCOHT — embryonic ovarian surface epithelium, granulosa cell precursors, or germ cells — and does the cell-of-origin context determine which SMARCA4 mutation types lead to oncogenesis vs. developmental arrest?
- Given mutual exclusivity of SMARCA2/SMARCA4 in the BAF complex, do SMARCA4-deficient SCCOHTs universally retain SMARCA2, making all cases theoretically sensitive to SMARCA2 ATPase inhibitors (e.g., AU-24118, BD98)?

## Limitations

- Very small cohort (n=12) due to extreme rarity of SCCOHT; statistical power is limited despite 100% mutation rate in a single gene.
- Clinical data collection restricted to age and year of diagnosis (HIPAA); no survival, treatment response, or long-term outcome data available.
- Three of 12 cases sourced from MSKCC; 9 from heterogeneous external sources with varying tissue quality, potentially affecting sequencing uniformity.
- IHC and immunoblot protein studies available in only 9 and 2 cases, respectively; conclusions about protein loss rest on a subset.
- Targeted panel sequencing (279 genes) may have missed driver alterations outside the panel, including non-coding regulatory mutations, structural rearrangements, or epigenetic changes.
- 2014 study predates EZH2 inhibitor clinical development and SMARCA2-targeted therapy; therapeutic implication discussion is speculative without clinical validation data.
