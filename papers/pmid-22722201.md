## Summary

Landmark exome and copy-number sequencing of 100 primary breast tumors (79 ER+, 21 ER−) identifies driver mutations in at least 40 cancer genes occurring in 73 distinct combinations — underscoring profound genetic diversity. Nine new breast cancer genes are established: *AKT2*, *ARID1B*, *CASP8*, *CDKN1B*, *MAP3K1*, *MAP3K13*, *NCOR1*, *SMARCD1*, and *TBX3*. The study reveals multiple distinct somatic mutational processes, including a novel TpC signature (C→T/G/A at cytosine preceded by thymine) enriched in ~10% of tumors that implicates transcription-coupled nucleotide excision repair. Only 7 genes are mutated in >10% of cases, while 33 genes collectively account for 42% of driver events — a "long-tail" distribution previewing the diversity recognized across cancer types.

## Key Findings

### F1: Nine new breast cancer driver genes established, expanding the known landscape

**Finding:** Systematic non-random mutation clustering analysis across 21,416 genes, validated in 250 additional breast cancers, establishes nine previously unrecognized breast cancer driver genes. These include putative recessive cancer genes (*ARID1B*, *CASP8*, *MAP3K1*, *MAP3K13*, *NCOR1*, *SMARCD1*, *CDKN1B*), an activated kinase gene (*AKT2*), and *TBX3* with ambiguous mechanism but recurrent in-frame deletions suggesting gain-of-function.

**Detail:** *MAP3K1* (6% of breast cancers, predominantly ER+) encodes a kinase upstream of MAP2K4 → JNK signaling; truncating mutations abrogate this apoptosis-promoting axis. *NCOR1* and *SMARCD1*/*ARID1B* further implicate chromatin dysregulation (SWI/SNF complex). *AKT2* p.E17K is identical to the known oncogenic *AKT1* mutation. *CASP8* truncations predict abrogated apoptosis. *TBX3* mutations (including recurrent in-frame deletions at Thr210/Asn212) are heterozygous and may act in a gain-of-function manner via estrogen-FGF-TBX3 stem cell expansion. Three new genes (*MAP3K1*, *CASP8*, *TBX3*) overlap with GWAS susceptibility loci, linking somatic drivers to germline risk architecture.

### F2: Forty cancer genes, 73 distinct mutational combinations — extreme genetic diversity

**Finding:** Driver mutations in at least 40 cancer genes are identified across 100 tumors, occurring in 73 distinct gene-combination patterns. Only 7 genes (*TP53*, *PIK3CA*, *ERBB2*, *MYC*, *FGFR1/ZNF703*, *GATA3*, *CCND1*) are mutated or amplified in >10% of cases (contributing 58% of driver events). The remaining 33 genes collectively account for 42% of drivers, each altered in <10% of cases. Maximum driver genes per tumor is 6; 28 tumors have only a single driver.

**Detail:** This long-tail distribution means most individual breast cancers are genotypically unique. ER+ and ER− subtypes have distinct driver landscapes: *TP53* and *ERBB2* are enriched in ER−, while *PIK3CA*, *GATA3*, *CDH1*, and *MAP3K1* are predominantly ER+. The 73 combinations observed suggest that convergent pathway-level disruption rather than gene-specific driver identity is the operative oncogenic principle. Multiple subclonal drivers were identified in some cases, consistent with branched evolution; in others, multiple drivers co-occurred in the founding clone.

### F3: Multiple distinct mutational processes operate in breast cancer; TpC signature identified

**Finding:** Substantial case-to-case variation in both mutation count and mutation spectrum reveals that multiple distinct mutational processes are operative across 100 breast cancers. A novel signature — elevated C→T, C→G, and C→A mutations at cytosines preceded by thymine (TpC dinucleotides) — is present in ~10% of tumors and shows transcription-strand bias, implicating transcription-coupled nucleotide excision repair (TC-NER) of an endogenous or exogenous bulky DNA adduct.

**Detail:** Mutation counts vary widely across cases (range not specified, but high variance). C•G → T•A transitions at CpG sites correlate with age at diagnosis in ER− but not ER+ cancers, suggesting a fundamental divergence in mutation accumulation dynamics between the two subtypes. The TpC signature (later formalized as COSMIC Signature 2/13, attributed to APOBEC deamination) was at the time unexplained; the strand bias in case PD4120 and ≥8 similar cases is consistent with exogenous genotoxin or endogenous enzymatic activity. No correlation between total mutation count and age at diagnosis was detected for most mutation classes, suggesting most substitutions accrue post-transformation.

### F4: JNK kinase signaling axis broadly disrupted through multiple convergent mechanisms in ~50% of breast cancers

**Finding:** Mutations in *MAP3K1* (6%), *MAP2K4* (known driver), *MAP3K13*, and upstream activation via *AKT2*/*PIK3CA*/*PTEN* collectively abrogate JUN kinase (JNK) signaling in approximately half of breast cancers. AKT phosphorylates and inhibits MAP2K4; thus PIK3CA/PTEN alterations (present in ~40% of cases) plus MAP3K1/MAP2K4 mutations converge on reduced JNK activity.

**Detail:** MAP3K1 → MAP2K4 → MAPK8/9 (JNK1/2) → JUN/TP53: truncating mutations in MAP3K1 and MAP3K13 sever this signaling axis at the apex. AKT inhibition of MAP2K4 means PIK3CA-activating and PTEN-loss alterations exert the same downstream effect. Consequences include reduced TP53 phosphorylation and disrupted pro-apoptotic stress responses. This pathway convergence is clinically relevant as it may explain why isolated PIK3CA targeting leaves significant residual pathway activity.

### F5: Chromatin remodeling genes broadly mutated; SWI/SNF complex disrupted in multiple ways

**Finding:** Beyond *ARID1A* (known in other cancers), the study identifies *NCOR1*, *SMARCD1*, *ARID1B*, and *ARID2* as somatically mutated chromatin regulators in breast cancer, joining established genes *PBRM1*, *SMARCB1*, and *SMARCA4* in the SWI/SNF complex. Additional chromatin-associated candidate genes include *ASXL2*, *ARID5B*, *KDM3A*, *SETD1A*, *CHD1*, *NCOR2*, *HDAC9*, and *CTCF*.

**Detail:** NCOR1 participates in both thyroid/retinoic acid receptor repression and estrogen receptor alpha transcriptional repression; its inactivation in ER+ cancer is biologically plausible as a driver of ER-mediated transcription dysregulation. SMARCD1 and ARID1B are SWI/SNF subunits; their inactivation complements known PBRM1/ARID1A/SMARCB1/SMARCA4 losses across cancer types. This finding establishes chromatin remodeling as a pan-cancer recurrently disrupted process, with breast cancer contributing unique entry points.

## Open Questions

- What is the identity and mechanism of the bulky DNA-adduct mutational process generating the TpC/strand-bias signature in ~10% of ER+ tumors? (Later attributed to APOBEC3A/3B — does this align with the strand bias observed here?)
- Do the 73 distinct driver gene combinations map onto functionally convergent pathway states, enabling classification by pathway rather than gene for therapeutic targeting?
- How do the multiple subclonal driver mutations identified in some cases influence treatment response and resistance evolution in clinical settings?
- Which of the 33 infrequently mutated cancer genes represent actionable vulnerabilities, particularly for ER+ tumors that lack standard targeted therapy beyond endocrine treatment?
- Does JNK pathway abrogation (via MAP3K1/MAP2K4 vs PIK3CA/PTEN) define a functionally coherent ER+ subtype responsive to specific therapeutic combinations?
- Can *TBX3* somatic mutations (especially in-frame deletions) serve as therapeutic vulnerabilities given TBX3's role in estrogen-dependent stem cell maintenance?

## Limitations

- Cohort of 100 tumors; statistical power limits discovery of genes mutated in <5–6% of cases without the follow-up 250-tumor validation series (which used targeted sequencing, not WES)
- Exome-only sequencing: non-coding drivers (TERT promoter, regulatory elements) and structural variants other than copy number changes not assessed
- Analysis focused on coding mutations; RNA-seq not performed — fusion transcripts not characterized
- Tumor purity and clonality analysis performed post hoc on sequencing reads; subclonal architecture is estimated, not definitively resolved
- ER status is the only molecular subtype stratification; HER2, luminal A/B, basal, claudin-low classification not applied
- SNP6.0 array for copy number provides lower resolution than WGS; some focal events may be missed
- Transcriptome-level validation of mutational impact (expression, splicing) not systematically performed
