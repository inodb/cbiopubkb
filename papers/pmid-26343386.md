## Summary

Genome and exome sequencing of 62 desmoplastic melanomas revealed the highest mutation burden of any melanoma subtype (median 62 mutations/Mb), dominated by UV-signature C>T transitions consistent with a superficially located epidermal cell of origin. Novel recurrent promoter mutations in NFKBIE (IκBε) were found in 14.5% of samples, strongly implicating NF-κB pathway suppression as a driver mechanism. Crucially, the common melanoma hotspot mutations BRAF V600E and NRAS Q61K/R were completely absent; instead, diverse alternative MAPK/PI3K-activating alterations (NF1, ERBB2, CBL, MAP2K1, EGFR, MET, RAC1, PIK3CA, and others) were present in 73% of tumors — several of which are directly targetable by approved drugs.

## Key Findings

### F1: NFKBIE promoter mutations in 14.5% of desmoplastic melanomas — novel NF-κB suppressor mechanism

**Finding:** The single most recurrently mutated genomic position (observed 9 times, plus a secondary hotspot 15 bp away in 2 additional samples) affects the promoter of the short isoform of NFKBIE. Twenty clustered mutations across 15 tumors (14.5% frequency) were identified; 5/15 tumors had biallelic mutations on opposing alleles and 4/10 single-mutation tumors showed LOH — strongly implicating selection for complete NFKBIE inactivation.

**Detail:** NFKBIE encodes IκBε, which sequesters NF-κB transcription factors in the cytoplasm. The relevant short isoform is ubiquitously expressed (unlike the brain-restricted long isoform). Promoter mutations affect binding sites for 32 transcription factors including GABPA and ELF1 consensus motifs. Two NFKBIE-mutant cell lines (M257, M375) show absent NF-κB nuclear translocation, consistent with gain-of-function IκBε suppressing NF-κB rather than activating it — a counterintuitive tumor suppressor-like role in melanoma. NFKBIE mutations were absent from COSMIC and TCGA melanoma studies (mostly intermittently sun-damaged skin).

**Genes / cancer types:** NFKBIE, NF-κB pathway · Desmoplastic melanoma

### F2: Extremely high UV-driven mutation burden (median 62 mut/Mb) — highest among melanoma subtypes

**Finding:** Desmoplastic melanoma has a median mutation burden of 62 mutations/Mb — >4× higher than conventional cutaneous melanoma (~15 mut/Mb) and ~30× higher than most solid tumors (~2 mut/Mb). 88% of mutations are C>T transitions in dipyrimidines, unequivocally implicating UV radiation as the dominant mutagen, indicating an epidermal or near-epidermal cell of origin despite the clinically deep-seated presentation.

**Detail:** Younger patients have significantly lower mutation burdens (P = 2×10⁻³). Tumors from sun-shielded sites had the lowest mutation burdens. TERT promoter mutations were found in 85% (17/20) of discovery cases, and overall TERT was activated in 90% of samples (via promoter mutation or amplification). Two cases arose from sun-shielded sites with low mutation burden; one patient had a germline CDKN2A mutation. Pure and mixed desmoplastic melanoma subtypes were genetically indistinguishable.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, CDKN2A (germline) · Desmoplastic melanoma

### F3: Absence of BRAF V600E and NRAS Q61K/R; diverse alternative MAPK/PI3K activators in 73% of tumors

**Finding:** BRAF V600E and NRAS Q61K/R — present in ~50% and ~20% of conventional melanoma respectively — were completely absent in this cohort. Instead, alternative MAPK/PI3K-activating alterations were found in 73% of samples: NF1 loss, CBL truncations, ERBB2 S310F (n=4), MAP2K1 P124S/L (n=2), PTPN11 E76A/K (n=2), RAC1 P29S (n=2), EGFR amplification (3 cases), MET amplification (2 cases), MAP3K1 amplification (3 cases), BRAF G466E/G469E/D594N kinase-dead variants (activating CRAF), and PIK3CA E542K.

**Detail:** Phospho-ERK immunohistochemistry was universally positive in desmoplastic melanomas, confirming ubiquitous MAPK activation despite heterogeneous genetic mechanisms. Multiple MAPK/PI3K alterations were not mutually exclusive in some tumors, suggesting cooperativity. BRAF kinase-dead mutations (G466E, G469E, D594N) paradoxically activate MAPK signaling via CRAF, a mechanistically distinct route from V600E.

**Genes / cancer types:** NF1, CBL, ERBB2, MAP2K1, PTPN11, RAC1, EGFR, MET, MAP3K1, BRAF (non-V600E), PIK3CA · Desmoplastic melanoma

### F4: CBL and FBXW7 as enriched tumor suppressor genes unique/elevated in desmoplastic melanoma

**Finding:** CBL (E3 ubiquitin ligase targeting receptor tyrosine kinases for degradation) harbors frequent truncating and damaging missense mutations with zero synonymous mutations — suggesting strong selection for loss-of-function. FBXW7 (WD-domain E3 ligase for MYC/CCNE1 degradation) has nonsense/damaging missense mutations in 11% of desmoplastic melanomas (enriched vs. conventional melanoma frequency). Both are inactivated in the context of absent BRAF V600E, positioning them as alternative RTK/MAPK suppressors.

**Detail:** CBL germline zinc-finger domain mutations cause Noonan syndrome, which shares pathway overlap with PTPN11/NRAS/KRAS/BRAF mutations implicated in melanocytic neoplasia. Loss of CBL increases RTK signaling, potentially substituting for BRAF/NRAS hotspot mutations in this subtype. FBXW7 co-occurrence with desmoplastic melanoma (vs. conventional) suggests its loss is particularly selected for in the desmoplastic genomic background.

**Genes / cancer types:** CBL, FBXW7, MYC, CCNE1 · Desmoplastic melanoma

### F5: Multiple directly targetable alterations in 73% of tumors — EGFR, ERBB2, MET, CDK4, MAP2K1, IDH1, PIK3CA

**Finding:** The diverse genetic landscape of desmoplastic melanoma encompasses FDA-approved targetable alterations: EGFR amplification (EGFR inhibitors), ERBB2 S310F mutations (HER2 inhibitors), MET amplification (MET inhibitors), MAP2K1 activating mutations (MEK inhibitors), PIK3CA E542K (PI3K inhibitors), CDK4 amplification (CDK4/6 inhibitors), and IDH1 R132C (ivosidenib). Combined with the extremely high TMB, this tumor type is a strong candidate for immune checkpoint blockade.

**Detail:** The high mutation burden (62 mut/Mb) places desmoplastic melanoma among the highest TMB cancers, theoretically generating the most tumor-associated neoantigens. Several non-desmoplastic melanomas with NFKBIE mutations showed similar features (BRAF/NRAS-WT, very high TMB, UV signature), suggesting this immunotherapy hypothesis extends beyond the desmoplastic subtype.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Do NFKBIE promoter mutations suppress NF-κB signaling as a cell-autonomous mechanism (gain-of-function IκBε), or do they affect the tumor microenvironment immune response? Does NFKBIE loss sensitize tumors to immunotherapy?
- Can the high TMB of desmoplastic melanoma predict response to immune checkpoint blockade (anti-PD-1/CTLA-4)? Are neoantigen loads correspondingly elevated?
- Does the absence of BRAF V600E necessitate a completely distinct treatment algorithm for desmoplastic melanoma, and do kinase-dead BRAF variants respond differently to BRAF/MEK inhibitor combinations?
- Does CBL loss create vulnerability to specific RTK inhibitors (EGFR, MET, or HER2 inhibitors) even in the absence of the corresponding gene amplification?
- Are the pure and mixed desmoplastic melanoma subtypes truly genomically equivalent despite different clinical behavior (lower nodal metastasis rate in pure subtype)?

## Limitations

- Validation cohort used FFPE material with no matched germline for 4/42 cases; somatic calling required population-level filtering, potentially missing private germline SNPs
- Low tumor/stroma ratio in desmoplastic melanoma may reduce variant allele fractions, biasing detection toward higher-clonality events
- NFKBIE functional studies limited to two cell lines; in vivo NF-κB signaling impact and therapeutic relevance not demonstrated
- No survival analysis presented; clinical outcome implications of specific genomic alterations not assessed
- TERT promoter region was only sequenced in a discovery subset (20 cases); validation cohort data may be incomplete for this key alteration
