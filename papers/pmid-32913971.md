## Summary

This retrospective study analyzed pretreatment tumors from 66 patients with metastatic BRAF V600E/K melanoma treated with BRAF inhibitors (BRAFi) — primarily vemurafenib (n=49) and dabrafenib (n=5), with 11 receiving BRAFi+MEKi combinations — using capture-based next-generation sequencing of >250 cancer-associated genes. Among BRAFi monotherapy patients, objective response rate was 55% (30/55) with 9% complete responses. The primary finding is that PTEN loss-of-function alterations (deep deletions, truncating mutations, and functionally characterized missense variants) are significantly enriched in patients with poor response grade — a composite measure of tumor regression depth and duration. PTEN-altered patients had markedly shorter PFS (HR 3.46; P<0.001) and OS (HR 3.10; P<0.001) that remained significant in multivariable analysis. Additionally, elevated BRAF-mutant allele fraction (indicating gain of the mutant allele) was associated with excellent response. These findings provide a molecular rationale for combining BRAF and PI3K/AKT pathway inhibitors in PTEN-altered BRAF-mutant melanoma.

## Key Findings

### F1: PTEN loss-of-function is significantly associated with poor BRAFi response grade and shorter survival

**Finding:** Among 55 BRAFi monotherapy patients, 11 patients with poor response grade harbored PTEN alterations (vs. 3 intermediate, 2 excellent). Patients with PTEN alterations had significantly shorter PFS (HR 3.46; 95% CI 1.79–6.71; P<0.001) and OS (HR 3.10; 95% CI 1.59–6.05; P<0.001). These associations remained statistically significant after controlling for disease stage and ECOG performance status.

**Detail:** PTEN alterations associated with poor response were predominantly likely loss-of-function: homozygous deep deletions (n=5), truncating mutations (n=4), and one functionally characterized missense mutation (P204S, affecting the C2 domain and known to reduce catalytic activity and stability). One variant of unknown significance (P38S, phosphatase domain) was also observed. Notably, PTEN alterations were not exclusive to poor responders — two excellent and three intermediate responders also harbored PTEN deep deletions or missense variants — indicating that PTEN loss is associated with but does not absolutely predict poor BRAFi response, and that cooperating molecular events likely modulate the phenotypic consequence.

### F2: PTEN loss activates PI3K/AKT signaling as a parallel resistance axis to RAF pathway inhibition

**Finding:** PTEN loss-of-function activates the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway in parallel to the BRAF/MAPK pathway, providing a BRAF-independent survival and proliferation signal that abrogates complete BRAFi response. PIK3CA activating hotspot mutations (V344G, E545K, H1047R) were also identified in 4 patients but were not exclusive to poor responders — two excellent responders harbored PIK3CA mutations, indicating that PTEN loss (constitutive PI3K activation) has a stronger predictive effect than PIK3CA mutation for BRAFi resistance.

**Detail:** No AKT hotspot mutations were observed in BRAFi monotherapy patients. TSC1/2 and MTOR non-recurrent variants were rare and distributed across responder classes. The pattern suggests that the PI3K/AKT axis is most relevant to BRAFi resistance when activated through PTEN deletion (complete loss of negative PI3K regulation) rather than through PIK3CA mutation (partial PI3K activation). The results support combining BRAF ± MEK inhibition with PI3K or AKT inhibitors specifically in PTEN-altered patients as a rational first-line strategy.

### F3: Elevated BRAF-mutant allele fraction is enriched in excellent BRAFi responders, suggesting allelic imbalance as a sensitizing event

**Finding:** Significantly elevated BRAF V600–mutant allele fractions (more than twice the median of all other variants in the sample) were enriched in excellent responders (P<0.001) vs. poor responders (P=0.02 for depletion). FACETS copy-number analysis confirmed allelic imbalance in two excellent responders, consistent with selective gain of the mutant BRAF allele or loss of the wild-type allele.

**Detail:** The biological rationale is that increased wild-type BRAF expression promotes RAF dimer formation (BRAF:CRAF heterodimers), which is paradoxically resistant to vemurafenib (which causes paradoxical MAPK activation in RAS-active contexts). Loss of the wild-type allele would reduce RAF heterodimer formation, increasing tumor dependency on mutant BRAF monomers that are potently inhibited by vemurafenib. BRAF amplification was observed in 3 tumors with intermediate responses, indicating amplification of the mutant allele (as distinct from allelic imbalance) does not preclude BRAFi response. This finding supports broader WES/WGS to evaluate BRAF allelic status as a predictive marker.

### F4: CDKN2A alteration does not predict BRAFi response, contrary to prior reports

**Finding:** *CDKN2A* was altered (mutation or homozygous deletion) in 41% (21/51) of BRAFi monotherapy patients, making it the most commonly co-altered gene. However, CDKN2A alteration did not correlate with response grade or PFS, contradicting a prior report suggesting a negative predictive role.

**Detail:** CDKN2A loss is known to abrogate G1/S cell cycle arrest and can cooperate with BRAF-driven proliferation. Its prevalence in both responders and non-responders in this cohort suggests it is a non-predictive background alteration in advanced *BRAF*-mutant melanoma. This has important implications for biomarker development: CDKN2A should not be used alone as a negative predictive biomarker for BRAFi. The distinction from PTEN — where functional loss clearly predicts poor outcome — highlights that not all tumor suppressor losses are equivalent in their impact on targeted therapy response.

### F5: Pre-existing RAS/MAPK pathway alterations are absent in pretreatment tumors, confirming they arise as acquired resistance

**Finding:** None of the 66 pretreatment tumor samples harbored *NRAS* mutations. Only one patient had an *NF1* loss-of-function alteration (X2441_splice), and this patient had a poor response grade — consistent with NF1 loss conferring BRAFi resistance. *MAP2K1* (MEK1) mutations were found in only 2 patients (both intermediate responders in different cohorts).

**Detail:** This finding confirms that the canonical mechanisms of acquired BRAFi resistance (NRAS mutation, NF1 loss, MEK1 mutations, BRAF splice variants) are rare as pre-existing intrinsic events — they predominantly arise de novo under treatment pressure. The exception is NF1 loss (1 patient, poor responder), consistent with preclinical data showing that NF1 loss causes RAF-independent RAS activation that bypasses vemurafenib. The absence of pretreatment NRAS mutations across 66 patients also implies that the predictive model for intrinsic BRAFi resistance is dominated by PI3K pathway activation (PTEN loss) rather than MAPK pathway re-activation through acquired alterations.

## Open Questions

- Do PTEN-altered BRAF-mutant melanoma patients benefit from upfront BRAFi+MEKi+PI3Ki triple combination, and can a prospective trial confirm the rationale established by this study?
- Does co-loss of PTEN and gain of BRAF-mutant allele have additive, antagonistic, or independent effects on BRAFi response?
- Can PTEN protein expression by IHC (as a surrogate for functional loss) reliably predict BRAFi resistance in the clinical setting without genomic sequencing?
- Do acquired PTEN losses (arising during BRAFi therapy) represent a distinct clinical problem from pre-existing PTEN loss?
- Would ctDNA monitoring of PTEN allele fraction during BRAFi therapy enable earlier detection of PI3K pathway-mediated progression?
- In the 5 excellent responders and 3 intermediate responders with PTEN alterations, what molecular events protect against BRAFi resistance despite PTEN loss?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=55 BRAFi monotherapy) limits power to detect associations with infrequently mutated genes and to perform robust multivariable modeling
- Predominantly vemurafenib-treated (n=49/55); modern standard of care is BRAFi+MEKi combination, which may modify the predictive role of PTEN loss
- Single pretreatment biopsy per patient — intratumoral heterogeneity may cause PTEN alteration to be missed in a subset of tumors; spatial sampling bias
- Capture-based sequencing does not detect promoter methylation-based PTEN silencing — epigenetic PTEN loss may explain some cases of "PTEN wild-type" poor responders
- No matched post-progression biopsies; acquired resistance mechanisms in this cohort are not characterized
- Variable interval between pretreatment biopsy collection and BRAFi initiation — tumor genomic evolution during this interval is unaccounted for
- Three patients with BRAF amplification (vs. point mutation) included; BRAF amplification may have different biology than V600E/K and could confound analysis
- Response grade classification (excellent/intermediate/poor) is a non-validated composite endpoint not universally adopted across studies, limiting comparability
