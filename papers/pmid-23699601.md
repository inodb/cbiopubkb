## Summary

Whole-genome sequencing of 40 de novo DLBCL tumor-normal pairs, plus 13 DLBCL cell lines, integrated with copy number array data and RNA-seq from an extended cohort of 96 cases. The study identifies 74 genes under significant positive selection (41 novel), uncovers widespread genomic rearrangements including chromothripsis, and characterizes 130 fusion transcripts. A novel and GCB-restricted convergent axis is described: inactivating mutations in GNA13 (14.3% of DLBCLs; 32.6% of GCB), GNAI2, and S1PR2 together disrupt Rho-mediated B-cell homing. Well-characterized drivers (EZH2, MYD88, CARD11, CD79B) are found in subclonal populations in some tumors, challenging the early-driver assumption. Timing analysis reveals BCL2 gains typically arise early, while chromosome 7/21 gains are late events. Data available in `nhl_bcgsc_2013` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: GCB-restricted convergent mutations in GNA13, GNAI2, and S1PR2 disrupt B-cell homing

**Finding:** Three genes encoding components of the Rho-GTPase/S1P signaling axis — GNA13 (~20% of DLBCL, 32.6% of GCB cases; inactivating), S1PR2 (targeted by aberrant somatic hypermutation and focal deletions; GCB-enriched), and GNAI2 (missense enrichment, GCB-enriched) — are each independently mutated in GCB-subtype DLBCL, collectively implicating disruption of S1P2-mediated B-cell germinal-center retention as a recurrent GCB-specific oncogenic mechanism.

**Detail:** GNA13 mutations were confirmed in 40/279 cases (14.3%); 29/89 GCB (32.6%) vs. 2/91 non-GCB. Mutations include frameshift indels, stop codons, and missense changes near the GTP-binding catalytic site. The AID-driven mutation motif (WRCY/RGYW enrichment) overlaps, but the distribution of truncating events argues for tumor suppressor function. S1PR2 was targeted by both focal small deletions (detected by assembly, below CNA resolution) and somatic SNVs. GNAI2 missense mutations at positions orthologous to gain-of-function GNAI3 mutations may act as gain-of-function events distinct from the loss-of-function pattern in GNA13. GNA13 mutation was not prognostic in 178 R-CHOP-treated cases.

**Genes / cancer types:** GNA13, GNAI2, S1PR2 · DLBCL (GCB subtype)

### F2: 74 genes identified under positive selection, including 41 novel DLBCL targets

**Finding:** Selective pressure analysis of SNVs across 40 WGS genomes (supplemented by validation in cell lines and literature) identified 74 significantly mutated genes (FDR = 0.08). 41 had not been reported as DLBCL mutation targets. Novel candidates include genes with tumor-suppressor signatures (splice site: EBF1, RB1; truncating: DNAH5, SGK1), oncogene hot spots (MEF2B, TBL1XR1), and histone genes. Known dominant-acting oncogenes (MYD88, CARD11, CD79B, EZH2) and classic tumor suppressors (TP53, CDKN2A/B, CREBBP, MLL2) were also confirmed.

**Detail:** Seventeen of 40 genomes had ≥1 nonsilent mutation in linker histone H1 or core histone H2 gene family members, confirmed in cell lines. Functional significance unclear but consistent with the general pattern of chromatin deregulation in DLBCL. MEF2B and TBL1XR1 hot spots were identified in multiple NHL types. TBL1XR1 is implicated in fusions with TP63, focal deletions, and SNVs. CDK11A was commonly lost by focal or large deletions at 1p36. FHIT was frequently targeted by large deletions. Overall average was 4.21 nonsilent mutations/Mb (range 35–400 coding mutations per genome).

### F3: Widespread structural rearrangements including chromothripsis, TP63 fusions, and focal single-gene deletions

**Finding:** De novo whole-genome assembly detected 0–41 individual structural rearrangements per genome, including events below the resolution of CNA arrays (e.g., 45–363 kb deletions affecting TP63 isoform-specific exons). Chromothripsis was confirmed in at least one case affecting the CDKN2A/B locus. 130 fusion transcripts were detected across 96 cases; the only recurrent fusion was TP63-TBL1XR1, which elevates TP63 expression. REL locus amplification can arise via complex, non-canonical mechanisms.

**Detail:** TP63-TBL1XR1 fusions correlated with significantly higher TP63 mRNA abundance (P=0.00378). Two separate deletions in one case selectively removed TA-TP63 isoform exons, implicating isoform-specific regulation. Chromothripsis case shows coordinated loss of multiple segments with focal rearrangements — consistent with a single catastrophic event. FHIT was a common target of large deletion. UTX deletions are notable: UTX is a H3K27 demethylase (opposing EZH2), and EZH2 inhibitors showed efficacy in DLBCL cell lines with UTX mutations.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP63, TBL1XR1, CDKN2A, CDKN2B, FHIT, UTX, REL · DLBCL

### F4: Driver mutations including EZH2, MYD88, CARD11, and CD79B can arise as subclonal events

**Finding:** Deep resequencing with correction for CNAs and LOH revealed that well-characterized DLBCL driver mutations — including hot spot mutations in EZH2, MYD88, CARD11, and CD79B — were present in subclonal (not clonal) populations in multiple cases. This challenges the assumption that important driver mutations are universally acquired early and are present in all tumor cells.

**Detail:** Cellular frequency estimates (via PyClone, using amplicon deep-seq + CNA/LOH data) showed subclonal drivers in several genomes. BCL2 gains and +6p were among the earliest CNA events; chr 7 and chr 21 gains tended to be latest. REL amplifications could be either early or late. BCL2 (+18) and MYC (+8q) gains, though typically early, were found as late events in some tumors. Ongoing acquisition of driver mutations after an early founding event suggests a more complex and protracted evolutionary history in DLBCL than previously appreciated.

**Genes / cancer types:** EZH2, MYD88, CARD11, CD79B, BCL2, MYC, REL · DLBCL

### F5: Aberrant somatic hypermutation (aSHM) broadly sculpts the DLBCL mutation landscape beyond immunoglobulin loci

**Finding:** Nonrandom spatial enrichment of SNVs near transcription start sites — a hallmark of AID-mediated aberrant somatic hypermutation — was detected genome-wide, affecting canonical targets (BCL6, PIM1, BCL2, IRF4, IRF8, CD83, DUSP2, P2RY8) as well as many newly identified genes. The mutation spectrum showed TA>CG transitions as the dominant class on average, though individual outliers showed distinct spectra linked to MMR defects (MSH3 truncation in the highest-mutation case).

**Detail:** AID-mediated aSHM can complicate driver identification: hot spots in aSHM targets (IRF4, BCL6) may reflect motif preference rather than positive selection. Distinguishing aSHM-driven mutations from selected oncogenic events requires integration of motif analysis and functional evidence. The case with the highest mutation load (~5× the cohort average) had a truncating MSH3 mutation, linking MMR deficiency to ultra-hypermutation in DLBCL as in colorectal cancer. The strong positive correlation between mutation load and spectrum deviation (r=0.69) suggests that unique mutational processes underlie outlier cases.

**Genes / cancer types:** BCL6, PIM1, BCL2, IRF4, IRF8, MSH3, AID (AICDA) · DLBCL

## Open Questions

- Do GNA13, GNAI2, and S1PR2 mutations functionally cooperate — and would simultaneous co-targeting of all three arms of the B-cell homing axis sensitize GCB-DLBCL to R-CHOP or novel agents?
- What is the clinical significance of subclonal driver mutations (EZH2, MYD88, CARD11, CD79B) — do they become clonal at relapse, and do they predict therapy resistance?
- Given that UTX deletion and EZH2 activating mutations both deregulate H3K27 methylation, do UTX-deleted DLBCLs respond to EZH2 inhibitors similarly to EZH2-mutant cases?
- Do chromothripsis events preferentially target tumor suppressor loci (CDKN2A/B), and are they associated with inferior outcomes in DLBCL?
- Can TP63-TBL1XR1 fusions be targeted therapeutically given their association with elevated TP63 expression?

## Limitations

- WGS cohort limited to 40 cases; selective pressure analysis is underpowered for rare drivers compared to larger exome studies
- Extended CNA cohort (96 cases) lacks matched WGS; structural rearrangements not characterized genome-wide in the full cohort
- Subclonal frequency estimates rely on pathology-derived purity estimates, which can be inaccurate
- No survival analysis for most newly identified mutations; only GNA13 was tested against R-CHOP outcomes (n=178; no significant association)
- Cell of origin (COO) determined by IHC rather than gene expression profiling in many cases, potentially misclassifying GCB vs. ABC
- Raw sequencing data gated behind dbGaP access (phs000532.v2.p1), limiting community reanalysis
