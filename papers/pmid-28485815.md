## Summary

Whole exome sequencing of 16 clear cell endometrial carcinomas (CCECs) combined with targeted Sanger sequencing of 22 candidate genes across 47 additional CCECs characterizes the mutational landscape of this rare, high-grade, and poorly understood endometrial cancer subtype. The study identifies *TAF1* as a novel candidate CCEC driver gene (9.5% of cases, with HAT domain clustering) and implicates *MLL3* as an additional candidate (7.9%). Frequently mutated genes include TP53 (39.7%), PIK3CA (23.8%), PIK3R1 (15.9%), ARID1A (15.9%), PPP2R1A (15.9%), and SPOP (14.3%). Molecular comparison to serous and endometrioid EC subtypes reveals that CCECs are a heterogeneous disease — a substantial fraction molecularly resembles either serous EC (SEC) or endometrioid EC (EEC) — while one-third shows no detectable alteration in the examined panel. This work provides the largest CCEC genomic characterization to date and has implications for treatment selection via PI3K pathway targeting and MSI-based immunotherapy.

## Key Findings

### F1: TAF1 is a novel candidate driver gene in clear cell endometrial carcinoma, mutated in 9.5% of cases with HAT domain clustering

**Finding:** *TAF1* — encoding the largest subunit of the TFIID basal transcription factor complex — is somatically mutated in 6/63 (9.5%) of CCECs, with five of eight mutations localizing to the putative histone acetyltransferase (HAT) domain, including two recurrently mutated residues (Arg-843 and Ala-850). This is the first report of somatic TAF1 mutations in CCEC and establishes TAF1 as a novel candidate driver.

**Detail:** All eight TAF1 mutations were missense mutations, predicted to impact function by at least two of three in silico algorithms (SIFT, Mutation Assessor, PolyPhen-2). The clustering of five mutations in the HAT domain (including two recurrently mutated residues) strongly suggests functional impact on histone acetylation activity. Three TAF1 mutations outside the HAT domain occurred within a single hypermutated MSH6-mutant tumor, suggesting they are likely passenger mutations in that case. TAF1 was previously nominated as a candidate driver in serous endometrial cancer but not previously confirmed in CCEC.

### F2: TP53, PIK3CA, PIK3R1, ARID1A, PPP2R1A, and SPOP are the most frequently mutated genes in CCEC

**Finding:** The most frequently mutated genes in 63 CCECs are TP53 (39.7%), PIK3CA (23.8%), PIK3R1 (15.9%), ARID1A (15.9%), PPP2R1A (15.9%), SPOP (14.3%), TAF1 (9.5%), FBXW7 (7.9%), and MLL3 (7.9%). MSI was detected in 11.3% of cases. This profile defines CCEC as a TP53-driven, PI3K-pathway–enriched, and chromatin-remodeling–disrupted malignancy.

**Detail:** TP53 mutations (39.7%) are high relative to endometrioid EC but lower than serous EC (~80–90%), consistent with CCEC's intermediate position between these subtypes. The PIK3CA-PIK3R1-PTEN PI3K axis was mutated in 34.9% of cases — a therapeutically actionable finding given the availability of PI3K pathway inhibitors. ARID1A and SPOP mutations implicate SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling as a key pathway. PPP2R1A (encoding the PP2A scaffolding subunit) mutations are characteristic of serous EC and gynaecologic clear cell tumors. MSI in 11.3% is clinically noteworthy given pembrolizumab responses observed in MSI endometrial cancer patients.

### F3: Clear cell endometrial carcinomas molecularly resemble either serous or endometrioid EC — heterogeneous disease with intermediate mutation profile

**Finding:** Comparative analysis of CCEC vs. serous EC (n=45) and endometrioid EC (n=40) across seven discriminating genes (TP53, PPP2R1A, PTEN, ARID1A, KRAS, PIK3R1, PIK3CA) and MSI reveals that 27.0% of CCECs are SEC-like (Group 1), 20.6% are EEC-like (Group 3), 19.1% have mixed features (Group 2), and 33.3% have no detectable alteration in this panel — an intermediate distribution unlike either pure SEC or pure EEC.

**Detail:** TP53 and PPP2R1A mutations are enriched in SECs; PTEN, ARID1A, KRAS, PIK3R1, PIK3CA mutations and MSI are enriched in EECs. CCECs show an intermediate distribution: 66.7% carry at least one alteration in these seven markers, vs. 82.2% of SECs and 97.5% of EECs. The SEC-like CCEC subset may share underlying biology with high-grade serous tumors, potentially responding to similar therapies (platinum, PARP inhibitors for BRCA-like states). The EEC-like CCEC subset may benefit from PI3K-targeted approaches. The large no-alteration group (33.3%) suggests additional undiscovered drivers.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PPP2R1A`, `PTEN`, `ARID1A`, `KRAS`, `PIK3R1`, `PIK3CA` · Clear cell, serous, and endometrioid endometrial carcinoma

### F4: MSI in 11.3% of CCECs and absence of POLE hotspot mutations distinguish CCEC from endometrioid EC

**Finding:** Microsatellite instability (MSI) is present in 11.3% (7/63) of CCECs, a frequency intermediate between EEC (~25–30%) and SEC (~2–5%). Notably, POLE exonuclease domain hotspot mutations — associated with favorable prognosis in EEC — were absent from all tested CCECs, defining a clinically important difference from endometrioid EC.

**Detail:** Two of the 16 exome-sequenced CCECs were MSI (T61 and T77) with high mutation loads (662 and 472 somatic variant calls), consistent with mismatch repair (MMR) deficiency. MSI is clinically significant given pembrolizumab responses in MSI EC patients (phase 2 trial data cited). POLE hotspot mutations (associated with ultramutation and favorable prognosis in EEC) were not found among 63 CCECs screened; one non-hotspot POLE exonuclease domain mutation (Phe367Leufs*15) was found in T24 but without an ultramutated phenotype. This absence of POLE-driven ultramutation means CCECs do not benefit from the favorable POLE-high prognostic category established in TCGA's EEC molecular classification.

### F5: MLL3 frameshift mutations implicated as loss-of-function driver events in a subset of CCECs

**Finding:** *MLL3* (also known as *KMT2C*), encoding a histone H3K4 methyltransferase, is mutated in 7.9% (5/63) of CCECs. Three of six MLL3 mutations are frameshift indels occurring N-terminal to the catalytic SET domain, predicted to be pathogenic loss-of-function events by analogy with truncating mutations in other solid tumors.

**Detail:** MLL3 frameshift mutations (three cases) and missense mutations (three cases across five tumors) were identified. The frameshift mutations — amino-terminal to the SET methyltransferase domain — are predicted to produce truncated proteins lacking the catalytic domain, consistent with loss of function. Truncating mutations in the Drosophila MLL3/MLL4 ortholog (trr) cause tissue overgrowth, supporting a tumor suppressor role. MLL3 had not previously been reported mutated in CCEC. Together with ARID1A and SPOP, MLL3 mutations reinforce histone modification and chromatin remodeling as key pathogenic mechanisms in CCEC.

## Open Questions

- Can the CCEC molecular subtypes (SEC-like, EEC-like, CCEC-unclassified) predict differential response to platinum-based chemotherapy vs. PI3K inhibitors vs. immunotherapy?
- What is the functional mechanism of TAF1 HAT domain mutations in CCEC — do they disrupt H3K18/H4K16 acetylation and thereby alter TFIID-dependent gene transcription?
- Does the one-third of CCECs with no detectable alteration in the seven-gene/MSI panel harbor driver events in unmeasured pathways (e.g., chromatin remodeling, splicing, epigenetic)?
- Can POLE exonuclease domain hotspot mutations occasionally occur in CCEC in larger series, or is their absence a fundamental biological distinction from EEC?
- Do MSI-positive CCECs respond to PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibitors with similar efficacy as MSI-positive EECs?
- Do MLL3 and ARID1A co-mutations cooperate mechanistically, and could EZH2 inhibitors be effective in MLL3-lost CCEC (synthetic lethality analogous to ARID1A-mutant ovarian cancer)?

## Limitations

- Relatively small discovery exome cohort (n=16); statistical power for rare variant discovery is limited
- Targeted gene Sanger sequencing in the prevalence screen (n=47) surveys only 22 selected genes; whole-exome coverage of the full cohort would likely reveal additional drivers
- No RNA-seq performed; fusion transcripts, splice variants, and expression-based subtypes not characterized
- Tumor cellularity varies across specimens; low-cellularity tumors may have reduced sensitivity for somatic mutation calling
- Reference genome hg18 used for alignment — some variant calls may differ using updated genome builds
- Clinical outcome data (recurrence, survival) not analyzed in relation to mutation profiles
- POLE analysis for hotspot mutations may have missed non-hotspot mutations driving hypermutation in some cases
- No copy-number analysis beyond what is captured in exome off-target reads; focal amplifications/deletions may be missed
