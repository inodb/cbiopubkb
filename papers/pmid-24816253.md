## Summary

This landmark paired exome-sequencing study characterised the somatic mutational landscape of Barrett's oesophagus (BE) — a premalignant metaplastic condition — and its progression to oesophageal adenocarcinoma (EAC) by sequencing matched BE and EAC specimens from the same patients. The study revealed that many driver mutations — most prominently in *TP53* — arise early in BE and are present clonally before malignant transformation, while other alterations (e.g., *SMAD4* loss, *ERBB2* amplification) emerge later in the transition to invasive adenocarcinoma. This clonal evolution model has important implications for endoscopic surveillance strategies and early intervention. The data inform our understanding of EAC carcinogenesis and provide a catalogue of genomic drivers that are targetable in advanced disease.

## Key Findings

### F1: TP53 mutations are the earliest and most prevalent driver events in Barrett's oesophagus, predating adenocarcinoma

**Finding:** *TP53* mutations are the most common somatic driver events in EAC and are frequently detectable at the stage of Barrett's oesophagus before malignant transformation — establishing TP53 inactivation as an early, clonal event in EAC carcinogenesis.

**Detail:** In paired BE–EAC exome analysis, TP53 mutations found in EAC were often traceable to the matched BE sample, demonstrating that TP53 inactivation is an early, clonal event that precedes invasive cancer by years. TP53 mutations in BE — detected endoscopically — thus carry significant prognostic and predictive value for cancer progression risk. The high frequency of TP53 mutations in EAC (>70%) is well established; the paired exome approach newly demonstrates that this is an early rather than late event. This has direct clinical implications for endoscopic surveillance: detection of TP53 mutation in BE biopsies may warrant more intensive surveillance or prophylactic intervention.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` · Barrett's oesophagus (BE) → Oesophageal adenocarcinoma (EAC)

### F2: CDKN2A loss is frequent in Barrett's oesophagus and EAC, occurring through multiple mechanisms including promoter methylation

**Finding:** *CDKN2A* (p16/INK4A) is inactivated in a high proportion of BE and EAC cases through a combination of homozygous deletion, point mutation, and promoter hypermethylation — making it one of the two most frequently inactivated tumour suppressors in the BE→EAC progression sequence, alongside TP53.

**Detail:** CDKN2A encodes two distinct tumour suppressors from the same locus: p16/INK4A (CDK4/6 inhibitor, G1 cell cycle checkpoint) and p14/ARF (MDM2 inhibitor, p53 stabiliser). Inactivation through promoter methylation — a mechanism not detected by exome sequencing alone — is particularly frequent in BE. The co-inactivation of CDKN2A and TP53 in BE creates a permissive state for replicative stress, genome instability, and clonal expansion. In the context of cBioPortal, CNA data captures homozygous deletions, while methylation-based silencing requires separate data types.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDKN2A` · Barrett's oesophagus (BE) → Oesophageal adenocarcinoma (EAC)

### F3: SMAD4 and ARID1A mutations emerge later in EAC progression and mark invasive adenocarcinoma

**Finding:** Somatic mutations in *SMAD4* (TGF-β pathway) and *ARID1A* (SWI/SNF chromatin remodelling) occur at higher frequency in EAC compared to matched BE samples, indicating they are later events in the progression sequence — acquired during or concurrent with malignant transformation.

**Detail:** SMAD4 inactivation (by deletion or mutation) disrupts TGF-β tumour suppressive signalling, which normally restrains epithelial cell proliferation. In EAC, SMAD4 loss is found in ~25–35% of tumours. ARID1A, a component of the SWI/SNF chromatin remodelling complex, is mutated in a significant fraction of EAC (~10–20%) and broadly across gastrointestinal cancers. The emergence of these mutations at the EAC stage rather than in BE suggests they may be associated with invasion or local progression rather than initiation — making them potential markers of advanced-stage risk stratification and potential targets for combination strategies (e.g., SMAD4 loss and platinum sensitivity, ARID1A loss and EZH2 dependence).

**Genes / cancer types:** `SMAD4`, `ARID1A` · Oesophageal adenocarcinoma (EAC) — later events vs. BE

**Data availability:** `available`
> SMAD4 and ARID1A mutation and CNA data available in cBioPortal EAC studies (`esca_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018`, `egc_bcm_2014`). Comparative analysis between BE and EAC stages not directly available.

### F4: ERBB2 amplification is a recurrent actionable alteration in EAC, arising as a focal amplification event

**Finding:** *ERBB2* (HER2) amplification is present in ~15–30% of EAC cases and represents a focal somatic copy-number amplification event that arises during EAC development, making it one of the most clinically important actionable alterations in oesophageal and gastro-oesophageal junction cancers.

**Detail:** ERBB2 amplification in EAC provides the molecular basis for trastuzumab benefit in the ToGA trial (first approved targeted therapy in gastro-oesophageal cancer). The paired exome data from BE–EAC pairs helps establish when ERBB2 amplification arises in the progression sequence — likely as a later focal amplification event amplifying a pre-existing chromosomal instability background. ERBB2 amplification can be assessed clinically by IHC, FISH, or next-generation sequencing, and its presence is a standard-of-care biomarker for trastuzumab or trastuzumab-based combination therapy in HER2-positive EAC. Co-occurring PIK3CA mutations may modulate response to HER2-directed therapy.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ERBB2`, `PIK3CA` · Oesophageal adenocarcinoma (EAC), gastro-oesophageal junction cancer

### F5: Clonal evolution model of Barrett's oesophagus reveals that genomic complexity precedes morphological dysplasia

**Finding:** Paired exome analysis demonstrates that BE harbours a complex clonal architecture with multiple independently arising somatic alterations prior to histological high-grade dysplasia — supporting a model in which genomic instability and clonal expansion precede and drive morphological transformation rather than following it.

**Detail:** The clonal structure of BE — where multiple clones with distinct somatic mutation profiles coexist within spatially separated biopsies — implies that current endoscopic surveillance (sampling a limited number of biopsy sites) may fail to detect high-risk clones. The identification of "field cancerisation" — where TP53 mutations and chromosomal instability are present across wide areas of BE mucosa — suggests that molecular analysis of BE biopsies could improve risk stratification beyond histological grading alone. Models of clonal competition and selection in BE suggest that the earliest driver mutations (TP53, CDKN2A) create a permissive field from which more aggressive clones with additional drivers (SMAD4, ERBB2) emerge stochastically.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `CDKN2A`, `SMAD4`, `ERBB2`, `ARID1A` · Barrett's oesophagus → Oesophageal adenocarcinoma (clonal evolution)

## Open Questions

- Can TP53 mutation detection in BE biopsies (by ultra-sensitive sequencing) serve as a clinical biomarker to guide endoscopic surveillance intensity and timing of ablation?
- At what stage in the clonal evolution of BE is intervention most effective — is there a genomic threshold (e.g., biallelic TP53 inactivation + chromosomal instability) that predicts imminent malignant transformation?
- Do the same clonal evolution dynamics described here apply across different geographic and ethnic populations with BE, given known differences in BE prevalence and EAC incidence?
- Can liquid biopsy (ctDNA in blood or oesophageal washing fluid) detect early clonal TP53 and CDKN2A alterations in BE, enabling non-endoscopic surveillance?
- How do SMAD4 loss and ERBB2 amplification in EAC correlate with response to standard-of-care (FOLFOX/FLOT, trastuzumab) and emerging therapies (T-DXd, immunotherapy)?
- Does clonal diversity within BE (measured by multi-region exome sampling) predict the risk of progression to EAC beyond standard pathological risk stratification?

## Limitations

- Sample sizes are small for the paired exome component (25 pairs); gene-level statistical power is limited for rare alterations
- Paired exome requires matched BE and EAC tissue from the same patient — feasible in surgical resection series but challenging to obtain in endoscopic surveillance cohorts
- Single time-point sampling does not capture the temporal dynamics of clonal evolution; longitudinal studies are needed to track BE clone dynamics over time
- Promoter methylation (important for CDKN2A silencing in BE) is not captured by exome sequencing; integrated methylation data would strengthen the clonal evolution model
- Clinical outcomes (EAC-specific survival, response to therapy) not reported in the original paired exome cohort
