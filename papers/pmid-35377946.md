## Summary

Retrospective MSK-IMPACT study of 237 locally advanced esophageal/GEJ adenocarcinoma (EAC) patients treated with multimodality neoadjuvant therapy and curative-intent surgery (2014–2020). Using OncoKB-filtered somatic alteration data and a structured TP53 pathway severity classification, the study identifies MDM2 amplification as an independent predictor of poor pathologic response and shorter PFS, and demonstrates that bi-allelic truncating TP53 mutations confer a clinically equivalent poor prognosis. Severity of TP53 dysfunction — categorized as MDM2 amplification, bi-allelic truncating TP53 mutation, other TP53 alteration, or TP53 wild-type — is significantly associated with neoadjuvant therapy response (p=0.004, q=0.07). The study proposes MDM2 inhibition combined with chemotherapy as a candidate therapeutic strategy.

## Key Findings

### F1: MDM2 amplification independently predicts poor response to neoadjuvant therapy in EAC

**Finding:** Among 8 recurrent oncogenic driver alterations (≥8% prevalence) — TP53 (79%), ERBB2 (16%), KRAS (19%), CDKN2A (22%), ARID1A (14%), SMAD4 (10%), MDM2 (9%), CCNE1 (8%) — only MDM2 amplification was independently associated with failure to achieve major pathologic response on multivariable logistic regression (OR 0.10, 95% CI 0.01–0.55; p=0.032), after controlling for clinical stage, tumor grade, and chemotherapy regimen.

**Detail:** MDM2 amplification was present in 9% of the MSS cohort. No other individual driver alteration, genomic feature (TMB, WGD, FGA, LST score), or oncogenic pathway (aside from TP53 pathway as a structured category) reached statistical significance for response prediction. The independent significance of MDM2 underscores that, beyond TP53 mutation per se, negative regulation of TP53 via MDM2 amplification produces an equivalent resistance phenotype — likely through suppression of apoptotic signaling in response to cytotoxic therapy.

### F2: Severity of TP53 pathway dysfunction correlates with neoadjuvant therapy response — a hierarchical model

**Finding:** Stratifying TP53 pathway alterations by severity of TP53 dysfunction (MDM2 amplification > bi-allelic truncating TP53 > other TP53 mutation > WT) reveals a significant dose-response relationship with neoadjuvant therapy response (p=0.004, q=0.07 on univariable analysis). On multivariable analysis, MDM2 amplification was associated with worse response vs. WT (OR 0.06, 95% CI 0.00–0.39; p=0.01), and bi-allelic truncating TP53 trended similarly (OR 0.31, 95% CI 0.08–1.03; p=0.06).

**Detail:** MDM2 amplification and bi-allelic truncating TP53 mutations were nearly mutually exclusive (q<0.05), consistent with prior literature. Both groups exhibited a substantially lower median pathologic treatment effect in the primary tumor bed compared with TP53 WT tumors. The 24-month cumulative incidence of progression was 61% (MDM2 amp), 67% (TP53 bi-allelic truncating), and 42% (WT). This functional equivalence between MDM2 amplification and bi-allelic TP53 loss suggests a common mechanistic pathway — abrogation of p53-dependent apoptosis — as the driver of resistance.

### F3: FOLFOX-based chemotherapy is associated with superior pathologic response versus carboplatin-paclitaxel in EAC

**Finding:** On multivariable logistic regression (controlling for clinical stage and tumor grade), FOLFOX regimen was significantly associated with better neoadjuvant response compared with carboplatin-paclitaxel (Carbo-Taxol; OR 2.38, 95% CI 1.20–4.76; p=0.013). This association was independent of genomic alteration status.

**Detail:** FOLFOX-only patients constituted 41% of nT-Rs vs. 22% of nT-NRs. The PET-directed strategy (administered to 64% of patients) allowed chemotherapy switching but did not independently predict pathologic response in multivariate analysis. These regimen differences highlight the clinical importance of chemotherapy backbone choice, which may interact with tumor genomics (e.g., MDM2 status) in ways not yet fully characterized. The overall pCR rate in the cohort was only 12%, underscoring the unmet clinical need.

### F4: No non-TP53 pathway genomic feature predicts treatment response — TMB, WGD, FGA, and LST are not associated

**Finding:** Among all tested genomic features — including TMB, whole-genome doubling (WGD), fraction of genome altered (FGA), large-scale transition (LST) scores, and 11 oncogenic pathways (p53, Cell Cycle, Hippo, Myc, Notch, NRF2, PI3K, RTK/RAS/MAPK, TGFβ, Wnt, DDR) — none except the TP53 pathway category reached statistical significance for neoadjuvant response after multiple testing correction. Individual pathways outside TP53 were also not significant.

**Detail:** This negative result is clinically important: it indicates that the response landscape in EAC is not broadly shaped by genomic complexity metrics (CIN, TMB, WGD), in contrast to findings in some other GI cancers. MSI-high patients (n=14; 6%) were excluded from analysis, as they exhibit distinct clinical and genomic behavior. This supports the specificity of the TP53/MDM2 pathway as the primary genomic determinant of neoadjuvant response in MSS EAC.

### F5: MDM2 inhibition proposed as a therapeutic strategy to overcome TP53 pathway–mediated resistance in EAC

**Finding:** Given the high prevalence of TP53 pathway dysfunction (MDM2 amp or TP53 truncating bi-allelic mutations) in EAC, and the dearth of established actionable targets in this cancer type, the authors propose that MDM2 inhibition (MDM2i) combined with cytotoxic chemotherapy may represent an important therapeutic strategy, particularly in MDM2-amplified or TP53 WT cases where p53 function can be restored.

**Detail:** MDM2 amplification constitutes 9% of the cohort, with an additional fraction harboring bi-allelic TP53 truncating mutations (conferring equivalent poor prognosis). For MDM2-amplified tumors retaining functional TP53 (expected in MDM2-amplified cases given mutual exclusivity), MDM2i agents (e.g., idasanutlin, milademetan) could restore p53-dependent apoptotic signaling during chemotherapy. This represents an actionable therapeutic hypothesis directly supported by the clinical genomic findings. The remaining TP53-pathway WT subgroup (minority of EAC) also represents a candidate population for MDM2i.

## Open Questions

- Does the severity of TP53 dysfunction (MDM2 amp vs. bi-allelic TP53 truncating vs. missense) differentially impact response to specific chemotherapy regimens (FOLFOX vs. Carbo-Taxol)?
- Would prospective clinical trials of MDM2 inhibitors combined with FOLFOX in MDM2-amplified EAC demonstrate improved pathologic response rates?
- Are TP53 pathway alterations predictive of response to neoadjuvant immune checkpoint blockade (included in 33 patients as exploratory)?
- What is the functional biology linking TP53 pathway abrogation to chemotherapy resistance in EAC (apoptosis suppression, cell cycle dysregulation, or both)?
- Can bi-allelic TP53 status be reliably detected on pre-treatment biopsies with sufficient tumor purity to serve as a prospective biomarker?
- Is the poor prognostic impact of TP53 bi-allelic truncating mutations generalizable to gastric adenocarcinoma and other GI cancers treated with perioperative chemotherapy?

## Limitations

- Retrospective, single-institution study — selection biases from MSK-IMPACT sequencing eligibility and surgical candidacy may affect generalizability
- Only 46% of patients had pre-treatment tissue available for sequencing; the remainder were sequenced on resection specimens post-neoadjuvant therapy, potentially reflecting treatment-induced clonal selection
- MSI-high patients (n=14) were excluded, limiting generalizability to that subgroup
- Multiple testing remains a concern: q-value threshold of 0.07 for TP53 pathway association is marginal; genome-wide replication needed
- TMB assessed from targeted panel (MSK-IMPACT); structural variants, mutational signatures, and transcriptomic data not integrated
- No proteomics or functional p53 activity data to directly confirm degree of TP53 pathway dysfunction
- Short median follow-up (20.9 months among survivors) limits OS analysis; PFS used as primary survival endpoint
- FLOT and combination regimen patient numbers are small, limiting regimen-specific genomic analyses
