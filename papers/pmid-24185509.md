## Summary

This study performs whole-exome sequencing of 32 intrahepatic cholangiocarcinomas (ICC) and 9 gallbladder carcinomas (GBC) in a discovery screen, followed by targeted sequencing of 17 candidate driver genes in an independent prevalence screen (32 ICC + 8 GBC). The key discovery is the frequent inactivating mutation of three chromatin-remodeling genes — *BAP1* (25%), *ARID1A* (19%), and *PBRM1* (17%) — in ICC, with at least one of these genes mutated in 47% of ICCs. The study also confirms recurrent IDH1/IDH2 hotspot mutations (19% of ICCs) and identifies FGFR2 mutations (13%) as therapeutic targets. Combined across discovery and prevalence screens (n=64 ICCs), chromatin-remodeling gene mutations occur in 41% of tumors. IDH1/IDH2 mutations are significantly associated with worse prognosis (HR=7.37, p=0.037). By contrast, gallbladder carcinomas are dominated by TP53 mutations (63%), highlighting distinct genetic architectures across biliary tract cancer subtypes.

## Key Findings

### F1: BAP1, ARID1A, and PBRM1 are frequently inactivated in intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma — a novel finding with chromatin remodeling as the dominant pathway

**Finding:** Three chromatin-remodeling genes are significantly and frequently inactivated in ICC: BAP1 (25%), ARID1A (19%), and PBRM1 (17%) in the discovery screen. At least one of these three genes is mutated in 47% of ICCs. Combined across discovery + prevalence screens (n=64), frequencies are BAP1 20%, ARID1A 14%, PBRM1 13%, with 41% of ICCs having at least one alteration.

**Detail:** Discovery screen: 32 ICCs; mean 39 somatic mutations/sample (range 13–300); all but one had <60 non-synonymous somatic mutations. BAP1 mutations in 8/32 (25%); ARID1A in 6/32 (19%); PBRM1 in 5/32 (17%). Significance by Bonferroni-corrected combined p-values: BAP1 p=7.4×10⁻¹², ARID1A p=4.3×10⁻³, PBRM1 p=1.8×10⁻⁵. Mutations are strongly enriched for inactivating types: frameshift (FS), nonsense (NS), and splice-site (SS) mutations account for the majority in all three genes. Three tumors harbored mutations in multiple chromatin-remodeling genes (not mutually exclusive). Prevalence screen confirmed: BAP1 5/32 (16%), ARID1A 3/32 (9%), PBRM1 3/32 (9%); mutations mutually exclusive in prevalence screen. BAP1 previously reported in renal cell carcinoma, uveal melanoma, mesothelioma — first report in any gastrointestinal cancer. ARID1A previously in ovarian, colorectal, gastric but not cholangiocarcinoma. PBRM1 previously in ~40% of clear-cell RCC.

**Genes / features:** `BAP1`, `ARID1A`, `PBRM1`

**Data availability:** `available`
> BAP1, ARID1A, and PBRM1 somatic mutations from both discovery and prevalence screens available in `chol_jhu_2013` on cBioPortal. Mutation types (frameshift, nonsense, splice site, missense) annotated.

### F2: IDH1/IDH2 hotspot mutations in 19% of ICCs are significantly associated with worse prognosis (HR=7.37, p=0.037)

**Finding:** IDH1 (codon 132) and IDH2 (codon 172) hotspot mutations occur in 19% of ICCs in the discovery screen and 22% in the prevalence screen (combined: 20%). IDH1/IDH2 mutation status is independently associated with significantly worse overall survival (HR=7.37, 95% CI 1.13–48.29, p=0.037) after adjusting for stage, age, and sex.

**Detail:** Discovery screen: IDH1 in 4/32 (hotspot R132) and IDH2 in 2/32 (hotspot R172) ICCs. These mutations clustered exclusively in known hotspot codons, consistent with gain-of-function neomorphic enzyme activity producing 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG). Prevalence screen: IDH1/IDH2 in 7/32 (22%). Combined: 13/64 ICCs (20%) with IDH mutation. IDH-mutant ICC: 3-year survival 33% vs. 81% for IDH wild-type (p=0.0034 by log-rank). Cox regression adjusting for stage, age, sex: HR=7.37 (95% CI 1.13–48.29, p=0.037). Patients with IDH mutations were somewhat older and had higher tumor stage (both non-significant). These findings contrast with a prior larger study reporting improved survival for IDH-mutant cholangiocarcinoma — the discrepancy warrants validation in a larger independent cohort. IDH hotspot mutations are detectable in plasma cell-free DNA, enabling potential screening strategies for high-risk populations.

**Genes / features:** `IDH1` (R132), `IDH2` (R172)

**Data availability:** `available`
> IDH1 and IDH2 mutations with hotspot annotations available in `chol_jhu_2013`. Overall survival data may be available as clinical attributes for Kaplan-Meier analysis.

**Tool support:** `native`

### F3: FGFR2 mutations in 13% of ICCs and previously reported FGFR2 fusions highlight FGFR2 as a therapeutic target

**Finding:** Somatic FGFR2 mutations were identified in 4/32 ICCs (13%) in the discovery screen, including two mutations at residues previously reported in endometrial carcinoma. Combined with the previously described FGFR2 gene fusions in cholangiocarcinoma, FGFR2 alterations represent an important therapeutic target with FGFR inhibitors in clinical trials.

**Detail:** 4 somatic FGFR2 mutations identified in 32 ICCs (significance p=4.8×10⁻², near significance threshold). Two mutations were at residues previously mutated in endometrial carcinoma. FGFR2 fusions had already been reported separately in cholangiocarcinoma at the time of this paper, establishing FGFR2 as a recurrently altered gene via both point mutation and structural rearrangement. Multiple FGFR inhibitors (pemigatinib, infigratinib, futibatinib) have since received FDA approval for FGFR2 fusion-positive cholangiocarcinoma. In the prevalence screen, FGFR2 mutation frequency was not separately reported.

**Genes / features:** `FGFR2`

**Data availability:** `available`
> FGFR2 somatic mutations available in `chol_jhu_2013`. FGFR2 fusion data, if deposited from RNA-seq or structural variant analysis, would be in the fusions tab; however, this study did not perform RNA-seq — fusions referenced are from external reports.

### F4: PI3K pathway mutations (PIK3CA, PTEN, PIK3C2G, PIK3C2A) in 22% of ICCs; additional RAS/KRAS/NRAS mutations identified

**Finding:** Components of the PI3K signaling pathway are collectively mutated in 22% of ICCs. RAS pathway genes (KRAS, NRAS) also harbor somatic mutations, confirming broad oncogenic signaling dysregulation in ICC.

**Detail:** In 32 discovery screen ICCs: PIK3CA mutations (n=2), PTEN mutations (n=2), PIK3C2G mutations (n=2), PIK3C2A mutation (n=1) — totaling 22% with PI3K pathway mutation. TP53 mutated in 2/32 ICCs (6%), much lower than in gallbladder carcinoma. Single inactivating CDKN2A mutation and single oncogenic KRAS hotspot mutation identified. Single NRAS mutation identified. No SMAD4/DPC4 mutations in ICCs (present in 1 tumor — TGFBR2). KRAS mutation prevalence in ICC (≤3%) contrasts sharply with pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (>90%), supporting a distinct pathogenesis. Prevalence screen additionally confirmed KRAS and NRAS mutations and PI3K pathway components.

**Genes / features:** `PIK3CA`, `PTEN`, `PIK3C2G`, `PIK3C2A`, `KRAS`, `NRAS`, `TP53`, `CDKN2A`, `SMAD4`, `TGFBR2`

**Data availability:** `available`

### F5: Gallbladder carcinoma is genetically distinct from intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma — TP53 dominant (63%), different chromatin gene profile

**Finding:** Gallbladder carcinoma (GBC) has a distinct genetic profile from ICC: TP53 is the most frequently mutated gene (5/8 tumors = 63% in discovery screen; 44% combined), with PBRM1 mutations in 25% and KMT2C/MLL3 in 25%. BAP1, ARID1A, IDH1, and IDH2 mutations were absent in the discovery screen GBC cohort.

**Detail:** Discovery screen (8 GBC after excluding 1 with hypermutator phenotype; the excluded GBC had ~3,000 non-synonymous mutations): TP53 in 5/8 (63%); PBRM1 in 2/8 (25%); KMT2C (MLL3) in 2/8 (25%) — the latter not mutated in ICC in this study. PIK3CA (1/8), SMAD4 (1/8) also identified. Prevalence screen (8 additional GBC): TP53 in 2/8 (25%, combined 44%); PBRM1 in 2/8 (25%, combined 25%); BAP1 in 1/8 (13%), ARID1A in 1/8 (13%) in prevalence screen only. No IDH1/IDH2 mutations in any GBC. The genetic distinction between ICC and GBC — despite both arising from biliary epithelium — suggests distinct oncogenic mechanisms, different cells of origin, or different etiological exposures, and argues against treating them as equivalent diseases clinically.

**Genes / features:** `TP53`, `PBRM1`, `KMT2C` (MLL3), `PIK3CA`, `SMAD4`, `BAP1`, `ARID1A`

**Data availability:** `partial`

## Open Questions

- Do the BAP1/ARID1A/PBRM1-mutant ICCs respond differently to HDAC inhibitors, EZH2 inhibitors, or other chromatin-targeted agents, as suggested by their potential sensitization to epigenetic therapy?
- IDH-mutant ICC is associated with worse survival in this study (HR=7.37) but better survival in a prior larger study — can a harmonized analysis reconciling these discrepancies define whether IDH inhibitors (ivosidenib) should be tested in IDH1-mutant ICC selected by prognosis?
- Can circulating tumor DNA detection of IDH1 R132 and IDH2 R172 hotspot mutations serve as a liquid biopsy screening strategy for ICC in high-risk populations (primary sclerosing cholangitis, liver fluke), as the authors suggest?
- Do FGFR2 point mutations (13% of ICCs) confer sensitivity to FGFR inhibitors (pemigatinib, futibatinib) comparable to FGFR2 fusions, for which FDA approval now exists?
- Are BAP1 mutations mutually exclusive with IDH mutations in ICC (as seen in other BAP1-mutant tumor types such as uveal melanoma and mesothelioma), suggesting discrete molecular subtypes?

## Limitations

- Small discovery cohort (n=32 ICC, 8 GBC); prevalence screen targets only 17 pre-selected candidate genes, potentially missing additional drivers in ICC
- No RNA-seq; FGFR2 fusions, splice variants, and gene expression programs cannot be characterized — limits therapeutic biomarker potential
- No liver fluke–associated cholangiocarcinoma samples; prior WES of liver fluke–ICC did not identify BAP1/ARID1A/PBRM1, highlighting etiological genetic heterogeneity that this cohort cannot fully address
- Survival analysis underpowered (n=32 discovery screen); IDH survival finding (HR=7.37) has wide confidence interval (1.13–48.29) reflecting small sample size
- Gallbladder carcinoma sample sizes too small (n=8 discovery, 8 prevalence) for definitive conclusions about driver gene landscape or cross-tumor comparisons
- No functional validation of chromatin-remodeling gene mutations in ICC-specific model systems; therapeutic implications (HDAC inhibitor sensitivity) remain speculative
