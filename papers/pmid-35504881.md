## Summary

Comprehensive whole-exome sequencing of 168 tissue samples from 51 patients with prostate cancer brain metastases (PCBM) reveals that PCBM harbors significantly higher mutational burden and is enriched for HRD mutational signature (SBS3) compared to non-brain prostate cancer metastases. Critically, 19.6% (10/51) of PCBM patients harbor qualifying pathogenic alterations in the 15 PROfound trial HRR genes, and 9.8% (5/51) have *BRCA1/2* alterations specifically—a population excluded from the PROfound PARP inhibitor trial. Clonal evolution analysis of 20 matched primary-PCBM pairs demonstrates subclonal expansion of driver alterations during metastasis, supporting a model of continued clonal selection in brain metastases.

## Key Findings

### F1: HRD mutational signature is enriched in prostate cancer brain metastases

**Finding:** The HRD mutational signature (SBS3) is significantly enriched in PCBM compared to non-brain metastases from the CRPC500 cohort (q = 0.041, Wilcoxon test) and in primaries from the PCBM cohort compared to TCGA (q = 0.0003).

**Detail:** Four patients had high SBS3 representation (>10% mutations): P7, P9, P19, and P30. HRR alteration (HRD signature and/or PROfound-gene copy number loss/mutation) was detected in 100% of PCBM patients vs. 87.26% of CRPC500 (P = 0.0025, Fisher's exact). The enrichment persisted after controlling for sequencing depth differences (downsampling analysis) and when restricting to cases with matched normal tissue.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRCA1` · `BRCA2` · `ATM` · Prostate cancer · Brain metastases

### F2: 19.6% of PCBM patients qualify for PARP inhibitor therapy under PROfound criteria

**Finding:** 10/51 (19.6%) PCBM patients harbor qualifying pathogenic HRR gene alterations meeting PROfound trial inclusion criteria; 8/51 (15.7%) show biallelic loss of a PROfound gene; 5/51 (9.8%) have *BRCA1/2* pathogenic alterations.

**Detail:** The PROfound genes interrogated: *BRCA1*, *BRCA2*, *ATM*, *BRIP1*, *BARD1*, *CDK12*, *CHEK1*, *CHEK2*, *FANCL*, *PALB2*, *PPP2R2A*, *RAD51B*, *RAD51C*, *RAD51D*, *RAD54L*. Every patient in the PCBM cohort harbored some genetic alteration (mutation or somatic copy number loss/deletion) in at least one PROfound gene. Patients with PCBM were explicitly excluded from the PROfound trial despite being a population with clinically relevant HRR deficiency.

### F3: PCBM has higher mutational burden than matched primaries and non-brain metastases

**Finding:** PCBM samples have significantly more SNVs, insertions, and deletions than matched primary tumors (SNVs q = 6.75×10⁻⁶; insertions q = 3.06×10⁻⁵; deletions q = 9.30×10⁻³, Wilcoxon) and non-brain metastases (CRPC500; SNVs q = 2.38×10⁻¹⁹).

**Detail:** Average mutation rate: 4.43 coding alterations/Mb across all 168 samples (range 0.28–50.45). Most frequently mutated genes in PCBM: *TP53* (25%, 13/51), *APC* (16%, 8/51), *SPOP* (16%, 8/51). *PTEN* loss or deletion detected in 64.71% (33/51) of patients. *AR* amplifications in 63% (32/51); *AR* coding alterations in 14% (7/51). *TMPRSS2-ERG* fusion in 25% (13/51), correlating with ERG IHC overexpression (Pearson r = 0.70). Four patients were hypermutators (>15 mut/Mb), including two MSI-H cases with *MSH2* or *POLD1* mutations.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` · `APC` · `SPOP` · `PTEN` · `AR` · Prostate cancer brain metastases

### F4: Clonal evolution shows subclonal expansion from primary to brain metastases in 18/20 matched cases

**Finding:** In 18/20 patients with matched primary-metastatic pairs, subclonal mutations in primary tumors expanded to clonal levels (CCF > 0.9) in PCBM, demonstrating active clonal selection during metastasis.

**Detail:** Clonal evolution trees were generated for 14/20 patients using PhylogicNDT. Genes with mutations expanding clonally in metastases were enriched for calcium signaling and FAT10 cancer signaling pathways (IPA enrichment analysis). Parenchymal metastases showed enrichment for calcium signaling and EMT regulation (q = 0.013, q = 0.02); dural metastases were enriched for aldosterone signaling (q = 0.047) and trended toward EZH2-regulated genes (q = 0.07). The primary tumors in PCBM patients were predominantly high-grade (ISUP Grade Group 5 in 75% of cases).

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDK12` · `TP53` · `ZFHX3` · Prostate cancer brain metastases

### F5: Aging (SBS1, SBS5) and HRD (SBS3) are the dominant mutational signatures

**Finding:** Age-related signatures SBS1 and SBS5 are the most frequently observed signatures (detected in 19 [37%] and 22 [43%] patients, respectively), followed by SBS16 (strand bias/NER, 37%); HRD signature SBS3 is present in 4 patients with >10% contribution.

**Detail:** Two patients with SBS44 (mismatch repair deficiency) and high MSI: P39 with *MSH2* missense mutation and MSI-H, P48 with *MSH2* frameshift and MSI-H. Patient P58 had *POLD1* frameshift with SBS44, and patient P55 was a hypermutator with SBS10b. Average patient age at PCBM diagnosis was 71 years, consistent with predominance of aging signatures.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MSH2` · `POLD1` · Prostate cancer brain metastases

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Would PARP inhibitor therapy (olaparib, niraparib) benefit the 19.6% of PCBM patients meeting PROfound criteria—a clinical trial is warranted since they were excluded from PROfound?
- Does the HRD mutational signature in primary PCa serve as a risk stratification biomarker to predict subsequent PCBM development?
- Do parenchymal vs. dural brain metastases have distinct therapeutic vulnerabilities related to their enriched calcium signaling vs. aldosterone signaling pathways?
- Can the enrichment of EZH2-regulated genes in dural metastases be exploited with EZH2 inhibitors?
- Is the high prevalence of PTEN loss (65%) in PCBM targetable with PI3K/AKT pathway inhibitors?

## Limitations (from paper)

- 24/51 patients had no matched normal tissue; mutations called against pooled normal, potentially inflating somatic calls (sensitivity analysis showed consistent results).
- Small cohort (n=51) limits statistical power for subgroup analyses (dural vs. parenchymal, n<20 each).
- FFPE tissue used for WES; lower quality than fresh-frozen may affect sensitivity for low-frequency mutations.
- Longitudinal sampling limited; most patients have a single metastatic sample, limiting clonal dynamics analysis.
- No treatment response data available; the therapeutic implications of HRR alterations cannot be validated in this retrospective dataset.
