## Summary

WGS/WES of 28 uveal melanoma (UM) tumors and cell lines identifies a recurrent hotspot mutation in *PLCB4* (p.D630Y), which encodes the canonical downstream effector of GNAQ/GNA11. This mutation is mutually exclusive with *GNAQ* and *GNA11* mutations, occurring in ~7% of tumors across datasets (3/84 total), and is predicted to be a gain-of-function variant activating the same Gαq signaling pathway. The study also characterizes the UM genomic landscape: low overall mutation burden (~0.5/Mb), a predominant BRCA mutational signature (not UV), frequent monosomy 3, BAP1 loss, and chr8q gain as a late event. Data are in cBioPortal study `um_qimr_2016`.

## Key Findings

### F1: PLCB4 p.D630Y is a novel recurrent gain-of-function hotspot in uveal melanoma

**Finding:** A recurrent *PLCB4* hotspot mutation (c.G1888T, p.D630Y) was identified in 2/28 discovery samples and 1/56 validation samples, and is predicted deleterious by SIFT/PolyPhen2, located at a highly conserved site (GERP++ score 5.69) in the Y-domain of the catalytic core.

**Detail:** The identical mutation was also found in 2/80 TCGA UM samples (noted in proof). The mutation lies in the Y-domain of PLCB4, which is critical for GNAQ-mediated signal transduction in the retina. PLCB4 is the direct downstream effector of GNAQ/GNA11; the hotspot p.D630Y is proposed to be a gain-of-function mimicking constitutive GNAQ/GNA11 activation. In contrast, *PLCB4* mutations in cutaneous melanoma are scattered (loss-of-function), not hotspot, suggesting context-specific oncogenic vs. tumor-suppressive roles.

### F2: GNAQ/GNA11 mutual exclusivity with PLCB4 confirms pathway convergence

**Finding:** PLCB4 p.D630Y mutations are strictly mutually exclusive with *GNAQ* and *GNA11* mutations across both the discovery cohort and four external datasets, consistent with convergent activation of the same downstream signaling pathway.

**Detail:** Known driver frequencies in this cohort: GNA11 p.Q209 mutations (n=14, 50%), GNAQ p.Q209/p.G48 (n=8, 29%), BAP1 loss-of-function (n=11, 39%), EIF1AX (n=4), SF3B1 (n=3). PLCB4 p.D630Y (n=2, 7%) occurred only in tumors lacking GNAQ/GNA11 mutations. A second PLCβ family member, *PLCB3*, harbored a mutation (p.K898N) in 1 sample in the CTD linker region, also relevant to GNAQ activation.

### F3: UM has a BRCA-like (not UV) mutational signature and low overall mutation burden

**Finding:** UM has a mean mutation rate of 0.50/Mb — one of the lowest among solid tumors — with a predominant BRCA-associated signature (Signature 3; 79% of samples) and complete absence of the UV signature (Signature 7), consistent with sun-shielded origin.

**Detail:** Signatures 1 and 5 (ubiquitous aging) also contributed. Signatures 12/16 were present in nearly every sample. No UV signature was detected, distinguishing UM from cutaneous melanoma. The BRCA signature association was not explained by BAP1 mutation status in this dataset.

### F4: Chromosome 8q gain is a late event; monosomy 3 co-occurs with BAP1 loss

**Finding:** Chr8q gain (6/14 WGS samples) is a late event in UM development — VAF analysis shows ~93% of chr8q mutations occurred before duplication, confirming duplication follows initial mutation accumulation. Monosomy 3 (57%) always co-occurred with BAP1 mutation.

**Detail:** Other frequent CNVs: 8p loss (n=3), 16q loss (n=2), X loss (n=3). All BAP1-mutant samples (n=6) were hemizygous for chr3. Samples with diploid chr3 had fewer large chromosomal aberrations. 297 total structural variants detected in WGS samples (mean 21/sample); two in-frame fusions identified (GSPT1-HSD17B3, FAM135B-PDSS2).

### F5: LLGL1 and other recurrently mutated genes are candidate drivers

**Finding:** Beyond established UM drivers (GNAQ, GNA11, BAP1, EIF1AX, SF3B1, PLCB4), four genes had mutations in >1 sample: MUC3A, TCHH, TTN, and LLGL1. LLGL1 is the most compelling candidate, being a known tumor suppressor in glioblastoma, oesophageal SCC, and hepatocellular carcinoma.

**Detail:** Each of these four genes had exactly 2 missense mutations. LLGL1 reduced expression has been linked to progression in cutaneous melanoma. Functional studies to assess LLGL1's role in UM are warranted.

## Open Questions

- Do PLCB4 p.D630Y mutations respond differently to MEK/PKC inhibitors compared to tumors driven by GNAQ/GNA11 hotspots?
- Is PLCB3 p.K898N a recurrent second-hit in the same pathway, and does it co-occur with GNAQ/GNA11 mutations?
- What is the functional consequence of LLGL1 loss in UM — is it epistatic with monosomy 3 / BAP1 loss?
- Does the BRCA mutational signature imply HRD and susceptibility to PARP inhibitors in BAP1-mutant UM?
- Do chr8q and monosomy 3 alterations emerge simultaneously or sequentially in UM development?

## Limitations

- Small discovery cohort (n=28); single study, not independently replicated at scale
- Validation set (n=56) drawn from 4 external publications with heterogeneous methods
- No RNA-seq; transcriptomic consequences of PLCB4 p.D630Y not characterized
- Functional studies confirming gain-of-function activity of PLCB4 p.D630Y are absent from this paper
- Structural variant analysis limited to WGS subset (n=14); no SV data for WES samples
- No clinical outcome data; prognostic significance of PLCB4 mutation not assessed
