## Summary

Hypodiploid ALL (<44 chromosomes) is a rare, high-risk subtype of acute lymphoblastic leukemia characterized by whole-chromosome losses and poor outcome. This study performed the first comprehensive genomic analysis of 124 pediatric hypodiploid ALL cases, identifying two molecularly distinct subtypes: near haploid ALL (24–31 chromosomes), driven primarily by RTK/Ras signaling alterations (70.6%), and low hypodiploid ALL (32–39 chromosomes), hallmarked by near-universal TP53 mutations (91.2%) — nearly half of which are germline — and frequent IKZF2 and RB1 alterations. Both subtypes show activated Ras/PI3K signaling and sensitivity to PI3K inhibitors in ex vivo models, pointing to a potential new therapeutic strategy for this aggressive disease.

## Key Findings

### F1: Near haploid and low hypodiploid ALL are two distinct genetic subtypes

**Finding:** Genomic profiling resolves hypodiploid ALL into two subtypes with non-overlapping molecular features. Near haploid ALL (24–31 chromosomes) is dominated by RTK/Ras signaling alterations in 70.6% of cases, whereas low hypodiploid ALL (32–39 chromosomes) is defined by TP53 mutations (91.2%), IKZF2 deletions (52.9%), and RB1 alterations (41.2%).

**Detail:** Gene expression profiling by PCA and unsupervised hierarchical clustering clearly separates near haploid, low hypodiploid, and near diploid cases into distinct clusters, independent of the effect of aneuploidy. Over 15,000 probesets were differentially expressed between near haploid and low hypodiploid ALL. The near diploid subgroup (44–45 chromosomes) has a distinct profile from both subtypes and commonly harbors dicentric chromosomes. Masked hypodiploid cases (doubled hypodiploid clone) cluster with their respective non-masked counterparts, indicating that the transcriptional differences reflect biology rather than ploidy alone. Both pediatric and adult low hypodiploid ALL share the TP53 mutation signature, with 90.9% of adult low hypodiploid cases also mutated.

### F2: RTK/Ras signaling alterations dominate near haploid ALL

**Finding:** More than 70% of near haploid ALL cases harbor activating alterations in RTK- and Ras signaling genes including NF1, NRAS, KRAS, FLT3, MAPK1, and PTPN11 — a frequency far exceeding that seen in low hypodiploid (8.8%) or near diploid ALL (31.8%).

**Detail:** NF1 was the most commonly altered gene, with focal intragenic deletions (always involving exons 15–35) or sequence mutations in 44.1% of near haploid cases. These deletions are unique to hypodiploid ALL and remove the GAP domain, causing loss of NF1 expression. Breakpoint sequencing indicates that aberrant RAG recombinase activity drives these deletions. NRAS mutations occurred in 14.7% of near haploid cases, and FLT3 in 8.8%. Due to chromosomal aneuploidy, the majority of these alterations are biallelic in near haploid cells (76.7% for NF1). RTK/Ras and IKZF2 alterations are mutually exclusive in low hypodiploid ALL, suggesting IKZF2 loss activates these pathways by an alternative mechanism.

### F3: TP53 mutations in low hypodiploid ALL are frequently germline — linking to Li-Fraumeni syndrome

**Finding:** TP53 alterations are present in 91.2% of pediatric low hypodiploid ALL cases, and 43.3% of these mutations were detected in non-tumor hematopoietic cells, indicating germline origin and suggesting that low hypodiploid ALL is a manifestation of Li-Fraumeni syndrome (LFS).

**Detail:** The TP53 mutations included missense, nonsense, and indel mutations, the majority rendering the protein non-functional; homozygosity arises from aneuploidy. Many mutations match known LFS-associated variants (e.g., p.Arg248Trp, p.Arg306*). In one family, a p.Gly302fs mutation was confirmed as germline (heterozygous in skin biopsy, homozygous in leukemia), and the proband's father had glioblastoma with the same mutation — a classic LFS cancer. Adult low hypodiploid ALL similarly shows 90.9% TP53 mutation frequency, though adult cases had exclusively somatic mutations in available remission DNAs. No chromothripsis was identified in TP53-mutated hypodiploid ALL, distinguishing it from TP53-associated pediatric brain tumors.

### F4: IKZF2 (HELIOS) and IKZF3 (AIOLOS) are subtype-specific Ikaros family alterations

**Finding:** While IKZF1 (Ikaros) alterations are rare in hypodiploid ALL, IKZF2 deletions are highly recurrent in low hypodiploid ALL (52.9%) and IKZF3 deletions in near haploid ALL (13.2%), representing the first description of these genes as recurrent leukemia drivers.

**Detail:** IKZF2 deletions were biallelic (due to aneuploidy) and led to loss of HELIOS protein expression by immunoblot. IKZF2 and IKZF3 deletions are mutually exclusive with Ras pathway mutations, and shRNA knockdown of Ikzf2 and Ikzf3 in murine pre-B cells increased pERK and pS6 levels, suggesting HELIOS and AIOLOS normally suppress Ras/PI3K signaling. The differential expression of Ikzf2 (highest in common lymphoid progenitors and pre-pro-B cells) versus Ikzf3 (higher in more mature B-cell precursors) supports different cells of origin for the two hypodiploid subtypes. Low hypodiploid ALL shows lower antigen receptor rearrangement frequency and CD19 expression, and its gene expression resembles immature murine Hardy stage B progenitors — consistent with transformation of a less mature lymphoid progenitor.

### F5: RB1 pathway inactivation is a hallmark of low hypodiploid ALL

**Finding:** RB1 deletions or mutations are present in 41.2% of low hypodiploid ALL versus 8.8% of near haploid ALL and 0% of near diploid ALL, making RB1 pathway inactivation a second molecular hallmark of the low hypodiploid subtype alongside TP53.

**Detail:** RB1 and CDKN2A/B alterations are largely mutually exclusive (only 1 of 14 RB1-altered low hypodiploid cases had concomitant CDKN2A/B deletion), indicating they are alternative mechanisms for inactivating the RB1 tumor suppressor pathway. In total, 61.8% of low hypodiploid ALL harbored either RB1 or CDKN2A/B alterations. In contrast, TP53 mutations co-occurred with CDKN2A/B deletions, suggesting that p53 loss confers a survival advantage that is not compensated by CDKN2A loss alone, while RB1 and CDKN2A/B are functionally redundant. Adult low hypodiploid ALL showed a similar, though lower, frequency of RB1 deletion (18.9%).

### F6: PI3K pathway is activated in both subtypes and sensitive to PI3K inhibitors

**Finding:** Both near haploid and low hypodiploid ALL exhibit activated Ras- and PI3K/mTOR signaling (elevated pERK, pS6, pmTOR, Ras-GTP) in xenograft models, and ex vivo treatment with PI3K inhibitor GDC-0941 and dual PI3K/mTOR inhibitor BEZ235 substantially inhibited proliferation of all tumor samples tested, while MEK inhibitors had no effect.

**Detail:** Flow cytometric analysis of xenograft cells demonstrated PI3K pathway activation even in low hypodiploid tumors lacking canonical Ras pathway mutations, suggesting alternative activation mechanisms (potentially via IKZF2 loss). The PI3K/mTOR inhibitors showed potent activity at IC50 concentrations (GDC-0941: 0.7 µM, BEZ235: 0.3 µM) on NALM-16 (near haploid cell line) and primary xenograft cells. MEK inhibitors PD0325901 and MEK162 did not significantly affect proliferation despite upstream Ras activation. This unexpected dissociation of MEK and PI3K sensitivity suggests that PI3K/mTOR — not MAPK — is the critical downstream effector in hypodiploid ALL.

### F7: High frequency of histone modifier gene mutations, including CREBBP, at diagnosis

**Finding:** Whole-genome and exome sequencing revealed a striking frequency of mutations in histone modifier genes — 64% of near haploid and 60% of low hypodiploid sequenced cases — with CREBBP being the most common target (32% of near haploid cases), a frequency not previously reported at diagnosis in any ALL subtype.

**Detail:** CREBBP mutations included deletions and missense mutations in the histone acetyltransferase (HAT) domain (e.g., p.Arg1446Cys, p.Gln1500Pro), residues critical for acetylase activity. Previously, CREBBP mutations were primarily associated with relapsed ALL; finding them at this frequency at diagnosis in near haploid ALL suggests they are early oncogenic events in this subtype. Focal deletions of a histone gene cluster at chromosome 6p22 (involving HIST1H2BE, HIST1H4D, HIST1H3D, HIST1H2AD, HIST1H2BF) were also recurrent in near haploid ALL (19.1%). EZH2 missense mutations were identified in 3 cases. These histone modifier alterations highlight epigenetic dysregulation as a co-driver in hypodiploid ALL pathogenesis.

## Open Questions

- What is the mechanism driving near haploidy itself — no recurrent somatic driver (beyond Ras pathway) has been identified that explains the catastrophic chromosomal loss leading to a near-haploid genome.
- Are inherited TP53 mutations in low hypodiploid ALL uniformly penetrant for leukemia, or do additional hits (e.g., RB1, IKZF2, aneuploidy) dictate cancer development — and should LFS families be screened for this specific leukemia type?
- Do IKZF2 and IKZF3 directly regulate Ras/PI3K signaling in B-cell progenitors, and are they actionable targets — can their loss be exploited therapeutically or used as synthetic lethality context?
- Why are PI3K inhibitors effective while MEK inhibitors are not, despite Ras pathway activation — does signaling flux preferentially route through PI3K in this leukemia subtype?
- Can PI3K/mTOR inhibitors be incorporated into frontline or salvage regimens for hypodiploid ALL, and will resistance mechanisms emerge through MAPK reactivation?
- What is the full spectrum of germline predisposition in hypodiploid ALL beyond TP53 — are other cancer predisposition genes (PTPN11, NRAS) inherited at meaningful frequencies?

## Limitations

- The near haploid and low hypodiploid cohort sizes are small (50 and 26 pediatric cases, respectively), limiting statistical power for rare co-occurring mutations and outcome analyses.
- Whole-genome or exome sequencing was performed on only 40 of 124 cases, so the full somatic mutation landscape (including long-tail mutations) may not be fully captured for the remaining cases profiled by Sanger and arrays alone.
- Germline TP53 status was confirmed by non-hematopoietic tissue in only one family; for the majority of cases, germline inference relied on non-tumor hematopoietic cells (T-cells), which could theoretically harbor somatic mutations acquired in a hematopoietic progenitor rather than true germline mutations.
- Drug sensitivity data (PI3K/mTOR inhibitors) are from ex vivo xenograft studies only; no clinical validation in hypodiploid ALL patients is presented, and in vivo pharmacodynamics and resistance are unknown.
- The adult ALL cohort (117 cases, 11 low hypodiploid) is comparatively small and lacks WGS/WES, limiting comparison of the full genomic landscape between pediatric and adult hypodiploid ALL.
- The cBioPortal study (all_stjude_2013) may not include all 124 cases from this cohort or provide hypodiploid subtype annotations, limiting direct replication of subtype-specific analyses within cBioPortal.
