## Summary

Giannakis et al. performed whole-exome sequencing (WES) of 619 incident colorectal cancers (CRCs) from the Nurses' Health Study and Health Professionals Follow-up Study (NHS/HPFS) prospective cohorts, integrating genomic data with detailed pathologic immune infiltration scores and survival data. The study identified 90 significantly mutated genes in non-hypermutated CRC (73 new to CRC), demonstrated that higher neoantigen load strongly correlates with lymphocytic infiltration and TILs — even within microsatellite-stable (MSS) tumors — and showed that neoantigen load predicts improved CRC-specific survival. Additionally, HLA class I and antigen-processing machinery (APM) gene mutations were found to be positively selected in TIL-rich tumors, suggesting adaptive immune escape. The data are accessible via cBioPortal study `coadread_dfci_2016`.

## Key Findings

### F1: 90 significantly mutated genes in non-hypermutated CRC, including 73 novel driver candidates

**Finding:** MutSigCV analysis of 488 non-hypermutated CRCs identified 90 significantly mutated genes, including 73 not previously statistically designated as CRC drivers — expanding the known landscape of CRC pathogenesis.

**Detail:** Known drivers (APC, KRAS, TP53, SMAD4, PIK3CA, BRAF, RNF43) were confirmed. Newly identified significantly mutated genes include: *BCL9L* and *TCF7* (WNT pathway), *RBM10* and *RBM12* (RNA splicing), *CTCF* (chromatin organizer/cohesin), *KLF5* (intestinal stem-cell transcription factor), *TGIF1* (TGF-β co-repressor), *PTEN*, and *RB1*. The longer list was accessible because of the larger sample size (619 vs. prior ~200-sample studies), consistent with the predicted long tail of low-frequency cancer drivers.

**Genes / cancer types:** `APC`, `KRAS`, `TP53`, `BCL9L`, `TCF7`, `RBM10`, `CTCF`, `KLF5`, `PTEN`, `RB1`, `RNF43`, `TGIF1` · Colorectal cancer

### F2: Neoantigen load positively correlates with overall immune infiltration in CRC

**Finding:** Higher neoantigen load (predicted HLA-binding peptides from somatic mutations) was significantly associated with increased overall lymphocytic reaction score in CRC (Spearman's ρ = 0.29, p = 2.6×10⁻¹¹), with the strongest individual association for tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs; ρ = 0.36, p = 2.0×10⁻¹⁹).

**Detail:** Neoantigen predictions used NetMHCpan v2.4 with patient-specific HLA alleles inferred by POLYSOLVER; binders with affinity <500 nM were counted. The lymphocytic reaction was assessed on four components (Crohn's-like reaction, peritumoral reaction, intratumoral periglandular reaction, TILs), each scored 0–3 (total 0–12). Frameshift indels generated proportionally more neoantigens per mutation than SNVs. The Crohn's-like reaction was the second most correlated component (ρ = 0.27, p = 6.1×10⁻¹⁰), suggesting tertiary lymphoid structures are also neoantigen-driven.

**Genes / cancer types:** `RNF43`, `KRAS`, `NRAS` (recurrent neopeptide sources) · Colorectal cancer

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Somatic mutation data in `coadread_dfci_2016`. Neoantigen predictions and pathologic lymphocytic reaction scores are not directly available in cBioPortal; neoantigen load must be recomputed from mutation data using HLA typing + NetMHCpan.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> Mutation data exportable via cBioPortal API; neoantigen load recomputation requires POLYSOLVER + NetMHCpan externally. Immune infiltration scores (lymphocytic reaction) not included as clinical attributes in the cBioPortal study.

### F3: Neoantigen load predicts immune infiltration even within microsatellite-stable tumors

**Finding:** Restricting analysis to MSS *POLE*-wild-type tumors, higher neoantigen load was still significantly associated with increased TIL grade (p = 0.035 for TIL 2+ vs. TIL 1; p = 0.015 for TIL 2+ vs. TIL 0, Wilcoxon rank-sum test).

**Detail:** MSI-H and *POLE*-mutant tumors had significantly more neoantigens than MSS tumors as expected. However, within the MSS *POLE*-wild-type subset (the majority of CRCs), neoantigen load remained a genomic predictor of TIL density — indicating that even low-to-intermediate mutation burdens in MSS CRC generate meaningful immune responses. The authors suggest that MSS CRC patients with elevated neoantigen loads may represent a subset responsive to immune checkpoint inhibition, distinct from the MSI-H population already known to respond.

**Genes / cancer types:** `POLE` (excluded from MSS analysis), MSI markers · Colorectal cancer (MSS subset)

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `api`
> MSI status filterable in cBioPortal. POLE mutation status queryable in Mutations tab. Neoantigen load stratification within MSS tumors requires API export and external analysis.

### F4: Neoantigen load is associated with improved CRC-specific survival

**Finding:** Elevated neoantigen load (>450 neoantigens) was associated with significantly improved CRC-specific survival (log-rank p = 0.004; multivariate HR = 0.57, 95% CI 0.35–0.93, p = 0.03), after adjusting for stage, age, sex, tumor location, and differentiation.

**Detail:** Patients were classified as high neoantigen (>450 neoantigens, n = 149) vs. medium-low (n = 448). The 450-neoantigen cutoff was selected by sensitivity analysis ranging 200–4,000 in steps of 50 (optimal cutoff). No significant difference was observed for overall survival, attributed to the large number of non-cancer deaths in this population with 9.4-year median follow-up. Neoantigen load correlated specifically with CD45RO⁺ (memory) T cell density but not significantly with CD8⁺, CD3⁺, or FOXP3⁺ T cells, supporting neoantigen-driven immunological memory.

**Genes / cancer types:** General neoantigen burden · Colorectal cancer

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Survival data for NHS/HPFS participants are in `coadread_dfci_2016`. Neoantigen load values not available as a clinical attribute; must be recomputed from mutation + HLA data.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Survival analysis (Kaplan-Meier) available natively in cBioPortal using clinical attributes. Neoantigen stratification requires custom computation and API data export.

### F5: HLA class I mutations are enriched in TIL-high CRC tumors — evidence of positive selection for immune escape

**Finding:** HLA class I somatic mutations were found in 66 of 619 CRC samples (11%), and their frequency was significantly enriched in tumors with high TIL scores (chi-square test), even within MSS *POLE*-wild-type tumors — consistent with positive selection for HLA loss as an immune escape mechanism.

**Detail:** A total of 96 HLA mutations were identified using POLYSOLVER. Of affected samples, 27% (18/66) had mutations in more than one HLA allele; all 18 multi-allele cases were hypermutated. Mutations were most enriched in exon 4 (TCR-binding domain), even after normalizing for exon length. Of 37 mutations in peptide-binding domains (exons 2–3), 46% (17/37) occurred at positions in direct contact with the peptide, expected to impair HLA:peptide presentation. Mutated HLA alleles harbored more neoantigens than non-mutated alleles (Wilcoxon p = 0.006), indicating the alleles most likely to present neoantigens are selectively lost.

**Genes / cancer types:** `HLA-A`, `HLA-B`, `HLA-C`, `B2M` · Colorectal cancer

**Data availability:** `not-available`
> HLA mutation calls (POLYSOLVER-based) and allele-level neoantigen assignments are not available in cBioPortal. B2M somatic mutations may be queryable in the Mutations tab.

**Tool support:** `custom`

### F6: Antigen-processing machinery (APM) mutations are positively selected in immune-infiltrated CRC

**Finding:** Mutations in genes of the MHC class I antigen-processing and presentation pathway — including *B2M*, TAP genes, *TAPBP*, *CALR*, *PDIA3*, *CANX*, and *HSPA5* — were significantly enriched in TIL-rich CRC tumors, indicating positive selection for APM dysfunction as a route to immune evasion.

**Detail:** The APM pathway encompasses chaperones for MHC class I folding (CANX, HSPA5), the MHC class I complex itself (HLA class I, B2M), and the ER peptide-loading complex (TAP1/TAP2, TAPBP/tapasin, CALR, PDIA3). Enrichment of APM mutations in TIL-rich tumors was assessed by logistic regression adjusting for overall mutation rate. This finding suggests that while neoantigen load drives immune infiltration, subsequent APM pathway mutations represent an adaptive resistance mechanism — with implications for predicting resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors in CRC patients.

**Genes / cancer types:** `B2M`, `TAP1`, `TAP2`, `TAPBP`, `CALR`, `PDIA3`, `CANX`, `HSPA5` · Colorectal cancer

**Data availability:** `partial`
> B2M and some APM gene mutations may be queryable in `coadread_dfci_2016`. Pathway-level APM enrichment analysis requires exporting all mutation data and annotating against KEGG APM pathway.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Individual APM gene mutations queryable via cBioPortal Mutations tab or API. Pathway enrichment analysis requires export and custom annotation (KEGG Antigen Processing and Presentation pathway).

## Open Questions

- Do MSS CRC patients with high neoantigen load (>450 neoantigens) actually respond to PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibition, and can neoantigen load serve as a predictive biomarker independent of MSI status?
- Do APM mutations detected in TIL-rich tumors cause primary resistance to checkpoint inhibitors administered in the adjuvant or metastatic setting?
- Can the novel CRC driver genes identified here (BCL9L, TCF7, KLF5, CTCF, RBM10) be functionally stratified by their contribution to immune exclusion vs. immune evasion?
- Is *POLE*-mutant MSS CRC (4 cases here) a distinct immunogenic subtype that warrants checkpoint inhibitor trials independent of MSI-H designation?
- Do HLA allele-specific neoantigen burdens predict which patients will develop HLA loss-of-function mutations as an acquired resistance mechanism during immunotherapy?

## Limitations

- FFPE archival tissue (decades-old in some cases) introduces DNA quality constraints; coverage inversely correlated with years since fixation (ρ = 0.33), potentially reducing sensitivity for low-allele-fraction mutations.
- Immune infiltration scoring was pathologist-assessed from H&E sections (0–3 scale per component) and TMA IHC — semi-quantitative methods with inter-rater variability (κ = 0.56–0.68 for individual components).
- T cell IHC data (CD3/CD8/CD45RO/FOXP3) available for only 299 of 619 samples, limiting power for T cell subset analyses.
- Neoantigen predictions used NetMHCpan v2.4 without experimental validation; predicted binding affinity (<500 nM) does not confirm immunogenicity or T cell recognition.
- Only 4 POLE-mutant MSS cases identified; insufficient power to assess POLE-mutant CRC as a distinct immunogenic subtype.
- WES does not capture CNVs or structural variants (e.g., beta-catenin nuclear localization, HER2 amplification) that may also shape immune infiltration independently of mutations.
