## Summary

This study characterizes chromoplexy—large-scale chained chromosomal rearrangements—in a cohort of 173 Ewing sarcomas (ES) and 104 desmoplastic small round cell tumors (DSRCT), all *EWSR1*-rearranged, using the MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing panel. Chromoplexy was detected in 31% of ES and 19% of DSRCT cases, with *EWSR1* at the core of 76–93% of events. Breakpoint allele fraction analysis and longitudinal sequencing demonstrate that chromoplexy is an early clonal event arising simultaneously with the driver fusion. Chromoplexy is significantly associated with metastatic presentation at diagnosis in both ES and DSRCT.

## Key Findings

### F1: Chromoplexy is frequent in EWSR1-rearranged sarcomas and detectable by targeted NGS

**Finding:** Chromoplexy was detected in 46/173 (31%; 24% EWSR1::FLI1, 42% EWSR1::ERG) ES cases and 20/104 (19%) DSRCT cases using Delly SV calling within the MSK-IMPACT pipeline, demonstrating that chromoplexy is accessible without whole-genome sequencing.

**Detail:** The vast majority of chromoplectic events were interchromosomal translocations (91–94%), with inversions (4–7.5%) and deletions (<1%) as minor components. Validation in 50 TMPRSS2::ERG-positive prostate carcinoma cases found similar chromoplectic events in 34%, confirming the pipeline methodology. An external WES cohort (26 ES from dbGaP) validated chromoplectic events in 3 cases.

**Genes / cancer types:** EWSR1, FLI1, ERG, WT1 · Ewing sarcoma, Desmoplastic small round cell tumor

### F2: EWSR1 is the core of chromoplectic chains, enriched at exons 8–13

**Finding:** EWSR1 was involved in 76–93% of chromoplectic SV events across all fusion types; non-canonical EWSR1 rearrangements involved diverse gene partners across the genome, with breakpoints clustered at EWSR1 exons 8–13 regardless of fusion driver type (FLI1, ERG, or WT1).

**Detail:** Non-canonical partners included cancer-related genes ATR, EGFL7, ETS1, EP300, EZR, FOSB, NAB2, ZNF331. Open chain (3-way, 5-way) and closed loop (triangle) architectures were identified using gGnome genome graph. Despite rearrangement at the DNA level, none of the non-canonical EWSR1 fusions were expressed at the mRNA level by RNA-seq in 20 cases with concurrent data (30% of chromoplexy cohort).

**Genes / cancer types:** EWSR1, ATR, EP300, ETS1 · Ewing sarcoma, DSRCT

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `custom`
> Circos visualization and gGnome genome graph analysis required for chromoplexy chain reconstruction.

### F3: Chromoplexy is an early clonal event arising simultaneously with the driver fusion

**Finding:** Breakpoint allele fractions (BAF) of canonical and non-canonical EWSR1 SVs were similar in distribution (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test p not significant), implying simultaneous clonal origin. Longitudinal sequencing in 7 ES and 5 DSRCT showed chromoplexy present in primary tumors and conserved through disease progression, while secondary SNVs/CNAs were often acquired only in recurrent/metastatic samples.

**Detail:** BAF analysis of sequential samples (median interval 10.5 months in ES, 13 months in DSRCT) showed stable chromoplectic BAFs normalized by tumor purity. This contrasts with secondary alterations (TP53, STAG2, CDKN2A mutations; chr8 gains in ES) that were acquired later. The consistency of chromoplectic events across time-points supports their utility as stable disease markers.

**Genes / cancer types:** EWSR1, FLI1, ERG, WT1 · Ewing sarcoma, DSRCT

### F4: Chromoplectic deletion bridges disrupt tumor suppressor loci

**Finding:** A subset of chromoplectic events formed "deletion bridges" that disrupted chromosomal regions adjacent to breakpoints, including 11q12-q24 (adjacent to FLI1) containing ATM (8 cases), 22q11-q13 containing EWSR1 and CHEK2 (5 cases), 1p35-p36 (1 case), 3p13 (1 case), and 13q33-34 (2 cases).

**Detail:** Deletion bridge formation is a structural consequence of chromoplectic chained rearrangements where deletions span or flank two rearrangement breakpoints. These losses represent potential "second hits" beyond the driver fusion, expanding the functional impact of chromoplexy beyond transcriptional deregulation to include tumor suppressor gene loss (ATM, CHEK2).

**Genes / cancer types:** ATM, CHEK2, EWSR1, FLI1 · Ewing sarcoma

**Data availability:** `available`
> CNA data in `es_dsrct_msk_2023` can show focal deletions at ATM/CHEK2 loci.

**Tool support:** `native`

### F5: Chromoplexy is significantly associated with metastatic presentation

**Finding:** Chromoplexy was significantly more likely to be present in ES cases metastatic at presentation [14/45 (31%) vs. 13/126 (10%); chi-square p=0.004] and in DSRCT [3/20 (15%) vs. 2/74 (2.7%); p=0.03], but was not associated with TP53, STAG2, or CDKN2A alterations, chr8 gains, or FGA.

**Detail:** Over median follow-up of 8.9 years (EWSR1::FLI1) and 5.7 years (EWSR1::ERG), metastatic ES had significantly worse OS than localized ES (median 4.6 vs. 8.9 years; log-rank p=0.0047). However, among localized cases, chromoplexy presence did not predict significantly worse OS or PFS. Chr8 gains significantly predicted worse OS in localized ES (median 6.6 vs. 11.3 years; p=0.034).

**Genes / cancer types:** EWSR1, FLI1, ERG, WT1, TP53, STAG2, CDKN2A · Ewing sarcoma, DSRCT

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Do the non-canonical EWSR1 rearrangements—though not expressed at mRNA level—disrupt regulatory elements of adjacent genes and affect transcription of those genes in cis?
- Can chromoplexy status be used prospectively to risk-stratify localized ES patients for treatment intensification?
- What is the mechanism driving EWSR1 as a "fragile" locus susceptible to recurrent breakage in coordinated chromoplectic events?
- Is chromoplexy related to germline predisposition (e.g., TP53 germline mutations) in any subset of EWSR1-sarcoma patients?
- Does the mutually exclusive relationship between chromoplexy and STAG2 (in EWSR1::ERG ES) or ARID1A (in DSRCT) reflect convergent oncogenic pathways?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Targeted panel (MSK-IMPACT) captures only SVs where at least one partner is on the panel; many chromoplectic events are missed compared to WGS.
- FLI1 is not on MSK-IMPACT, so FLI1-involving SVs (beyond EWSR1::FLI1) are largely not captured.
- Non-expressed non-canonical fusions suggest they may not be functionally relevant—their oncogenic significance is unclear.
- Survival analysis limited by relatively short follow-up in some subgroups, particularly DSRCT (median 4.0 years).
- WES validation cohort is small (26 ES patients) and uses a custom chain detection algorithm that may differ from Delly-based pipeline.
