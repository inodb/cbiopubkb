## Summary

Quantitative TMT-based proteomic profiling of 375 CCLE cancer cell lines spanning 22 tissue lineages, quantifying >12,000 proteins at 1% protein-level FDR. This constitutes the first large-scale deep proteome characterization of a diverse cancer cell line collection and reveals fundamental organizing principles of the cancer proteome invisible to transcriptomics: (1) coordinated pathway-level coexpression (not tissue or mutation) dominates the primary principal component of proteome variation in 75% of expressed proteins; (2) MSI (microsatellite instability) causes surprisingly few protein-level changes (50 proteins) despite thousands of transcriptomic changes ‚Äî with coherent downregulation of specific protein complexes (mismatch repair, MRN, SKI, RQC, HMT); (3) protein complex membership predicts drug/knockdown sensitivity; and (4) KRAS and TP53 mutation have specific protein-level signatures beyond mRNA-level effects. Data publicly available at gygi.med.harvard.edu and depmap.org.

## Key Findings

### F1: Coordinated pathway-level coexpression ‚Äî not tissue lineage or mutation ‚Äî is the primary organizing principle of the cancer cell line proteome

**Finding:** PCA of protein expression across 375 cell lines reveals that PC1 captures coordinated expression of >200 pathways enriched across 75% of all expressed proteins. RNA-seq PC1 GSEA finds far fewer enriched pathways (and none from transcription factor binding data), demonstrating that this coordinated proteome organization is a fundamentally post-transcriptional phenomenon. The two major anticorrelated expression programs along PC1 represent Warburg effect vs. oxidative metabolism, and growth factor signaling vs. epithelial adhesion.

**Detail:** PC1 encompasses two anticorrelated protein expression clusters spanning many pathways: one program includes upregulation of MAPK/insulin receptor signaling, glycolysis, nucleotide metabolism, and cell division; the other includes upregulation of cell-cell/cell-matrix adhesion (epithelial hallmarks), KRAS and p53 signaling markers, and oxidative phosphorylation. Glycolysis and oxidative phosphorylation are anticorrelated ‚Äî characteristic of the Warburg effect. EPCAM has the highest loading in PC1; VIM is among the 50 most negative loadings ‚Äî confirming PC1 captures EMT axis. GSEA on protein PC1 loadings: >200 pathway enrichments using MSigDB c2+hallmark; corresponding RNA PC1 GSEA: far fewer pathways; transcription factor target enrichment (c3): 0 in protein data, >100 in RNA data ‚Äî demonstrating that transcription factors organize RNA but post-transcriptional mechanisms organize the proteome. Several dozen genes (CRISPR) and drugs (EGFR inhibitors, PIK3CB inhibitors, EIF4 inhibitors) show sensitivity correlated with PC1 projection.

**Genes / cancer types:** `EPCAM`, `VIM`, `KRAS`, `TP53`, `EGFR`, `PIK3CB` ¬∑ Pan-cancer (22 lineages)

### F2: MSI cell lines show only 50 protein-level changes despite thousands of mRNA changes ‚Äî coherent downregulation of surveillance protein complexes

**Finding:** Despite having an order of magnitude more mutations than MSS lines, MSI cancer cell lines across multiple tissue lineages show only 50 consistently altered proteins (vs. >1,000 significantly dysregulated mRNAs). The 50 proteins form a coherent network of surveillance complexes: MMR (MLH1, PMS2, MSH2, MSH6 downregulated), MRN complex (MRE11, RAD50, NBN), SKI ribosome-associated complex (SKIV2L, TTC37 downregulated), RQC complex (LTN1 downregulated), and HMT complex (KMT2B, KMT2D, SETD1B downregulated). This proteomic buffering of MSI transcriptomic effects suggests active proteome homeostasis.

**Detail:** The MutL-associated cluster (MLH1, PMS2, HMT complex) is best fit by MSI status alone as predictor; the MutS-associated cluster (MSH2/6, MRN, SKI, LTN1) is better fit by a combined MSI√ómutation-burden model. H3K4me1 and H3K4me2 (regulated by KMT2B, KMT2D, SETD1B) are significantly downregulated in MSI cell lines ‚Äî revealing an MSI-specific epigenetic state not previously described. The SKI complex (SKIV2L, TTC37) degrades problematic mRNAs from ribosomes; its downregulation in MSI lines may allow expression of mutant-bearing transcripts, alleviating negative selective pressure of high mutation burden. MSI sensitivity to WRN knockdown (4 independent studies confirmed) is associated at the protein level with both MMR complex members (expected) and SKI complex proteins SKIV2L and TTC37 (novel), suggesting SKI as a functional marker for WRN dependency.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MLH1`, `PMS2`, `MSH2`, `MSH6`, `MRE11`, `RAD50`, `NBN`, `SKIV2L`, `TTC37`, `LTN1`, `KMT2B`, `KMT2D`, `SETD1B`, `WRN` ¬∑ Pan-cancer MSI cell lines (colorectal, endometrial, gastric, and others)

### F3: RPL22 mutation uniquely associates with MSI-linked protein complex dysregulation; RPL22L1 sensitivity intersects SKI complex

**Finding:** Unbiased mutation-to-protein-expression analysis identifies RPL22 as the single gene whose mutation most specifically reproduces the MSI-associated protein complex expression pattern (MMR, SKI, RQC complexes), despite RPL22 mutation not causing MSI directly. RPL22 is a mutation hotspot in MSI endometrial cancer; its loss derepresses RPL22L1. RPL22L1 sensitivity (gene knockdown lethality) is highly specific to MSI cell lines, and SKIV2L is the only protein whose expression predicts both WRN sensitivity and RPL22L1 sensitivity simultaneously.

**Detail:** RPL22 expression in MSI cells clusters with the MutS complex (MutS-associated cluster). The best model for both RPL22 and RPL22L1 includes MSI√ómutation burden interaction. RPL22 interacts with MDM2 to suppress p53 degradation; upon RPL22 loss, RPL22L1 provides functional substitution and MDM4 splicing is affected (from CCLE second-generation analysis). Chan et al. independently identified RPL22L1 as the second-highest sensitivity hit after WRN in MSI lines (DRIVE screen). The SKIV2L connection ‚Äî being the only protein linking WRN sensitivity and RPL22L1 sensitivity ‚Äî suggests the ribosome surveillance axis (SKI, RQC) as a novel vulnerability in MSI cancers beyond the established WRN dependency.

**Genes / cancer types:** `RPL22`, `RPL22L1`, `WRN`, `SKIV2L`, `TTC37`, `LTN1`, `MDM2`, `MDM4` ¬∑ Pan-cancer MSI cell lines

### F4: Protein complex membership predicts gene knockdown sensitivity ‚Äî ATR/ATRIP, MCM, Chromosomal Passenger Complex, SUMO E1 associations

**Finding:** Systematic analysis of protein complex coexpression vs. DRIVE shRNA knockdown sensitivity identifies multiple protein complexes where ‚â•50% of complex members' expression predicts sensitivity to knockdown of specific genes: ATR/ATRIP complex ‚Üí ATR knockdown sensitivity; MCM complex ‚Üí MDM2 knockdown sensitivity; Chromosomal Passenger Complex (AURKB, INCENP) ‚Üí TP53 knockdown sensitivity; SUMO E1 heterodimer (SAE1, UBA2) ‚Üí WRN knockdown sensitivity and PCSK7 mutation. TEAD1 knockdown sensitivity associates with integrin complexes containing ITGB1, linking Hippo pathway to mechanosensing proteins.

**Detail:** ATR and ATRIP protein levels (ATR/ATRIP complex members) predict sensitivity to ATR knockdown ‚Äî demonstrating that complex member expression levels reflect complex activity and cellular dependency. MCM complex (MCM2, MCM4, MCM6) associations with MDM2 knockdown reveal TP53-independent MDM2 functions in DNA replication. The CPC (Chromosomal Passenger Complex: AURKB, INCENP, BOREALIN, SURVIVIN) association with TP53 knockdown aligns with known CPC roles in mitosis and cell division. SUMO E1 (SAE1/UBA2) association with WRN sensitivity extends the mechanistic understanding of WRN dependency: beyond MMR, WRN loss creates a SUMOylation vulnerability. PCSK7 mutation uniquely associates with SUMO E1 complex expression ‚Äî a novel connection requiring further mechanistic investigation.

### F5: KRAS and TP53 mutations have specific pan-lineage protein-level signatures; ERBB2 copy number predicts protein in breast lines

**Finding:** Pan-lineage mutation-to-protein association analysis across solid organ cell lines identifies KRAS mutation as significantly associated with protein levels of many cell motility and adhesion proteins, and TP53 mutation as associated with a handful of p53 pathway/DNA damage response proteins. ERBB2 (HER2) protein expression is strongly predicted by ERBB2 copy number across cell lines, particularly in breast-derived lines, though non-breast lines with high ERBB2 copy number also show high protein ‚Äî validating copy number as a predictive feature for protein level at this locus.

**Detail:** KRAS mutation associations extend beyond the canonical RAS/MAPK/PI3K pathway to include proteins involved in cell motility and ECM adhesion, reflecting the broad transcriptional effects of oncogenic KRAS on cell-matrix interactions. TP53 mutation shows more limited and pathway-specific effects, predominantly affecting p53-target proteins and DNA damage response components. ERBB2 protein-copy number correlation validates the general model that DNA copy number predicts protein levels for amplified oncogenes, and this is one of the few clear examples where RNA expression also accurately predicts protein. The median RNA-protein correlation across all genes is 0.48 (consistent with prior studies) ‚Äî demonstrating that while RNA is a reasonable proxy for many genes, systematic post-transcriptional regulation means protein measurements are essential for understanding cancer biology.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `TP53`, `ERBB2`, `EGFR`, `BRAF` ¬∑ Pan-cancer (22 lineages)

## Open Questions

- Can the PC1 proteome axis (Warburg/epithelial‚Äìmesenchymal program) serve as a predictive biomarker for drug sensitivity beyond the EGFR/PIK3CB associations reported here?
- Does the SKI complex downregulation in MSI cancers mechanistically explain WRN dependency ‚Äî by impairing ribosome quality control, creating synthetic lethality with WRN's role in resolving R-loops at replication forks?
- Is PCSK7 mutation upstream of SUMO E1 complex downregulation, and does PCSK7 inhibition sensitize MSI cells to WRN or RPL22L1 loss?
- Can H3K4me1/me2 reduction in MSI cells serve as an epigenetic biomarker for MSI in liquid biopsy (histone modification-based cfDNA)?
- How many of the ~50 buffered MSI proteins represent therapeutic dependencies (where re-expression confers drug sensitivity) vs. passenger downregulation?
- Do the protein complex‚Äìknockdown sensitivity associations generalize from CCLE cell lines to primary TCGA tumors, enabling tumor-derived proteomics to predict treatment vulnerabilities?

## Limitations

- 375 cell lines are ~40% of the full CCLE collection ‚Äî selected for lineage diversity but not exhaustive; rare lineages underrepresented
- Cell line biology differs from primary tumor biology ‚Äî tumor microenvironment, clonal heterogeneity, in vivo signaling not recapitulated
- Proteomics data not in cBioPortal ‚Äî major accessibility limitation; requires downloading raw data from Gygi lab or DepMap portal
- Median CV of 60% between biological replicates within a cell line indicates substantial biological variation across culture conditions
- Haematopoietic/lymphoid lines removed from pathway analyses due to transcriptome-level distinctness ‚Äî limits findings' applicability to liquid tumors
- RNA-protein correlation averages 0.48 ‚Äî stronger than often reported, but still means ~50% of protein variation is unexplained by RNA, making RNA-only analyses incomplete
- TMT10-plex limited to 9 samples + 1 bridge per batch; comparison across 42 batches relies on bridge normalization quality
- No phosphoproteomics, acetylomics, or metabolomics in this dataset ‚Äî limits mechanistic depth compared to CPTAC tumor studies
- Drug sensitivity data from orthogonal screens (DRIVE shRNA, Achilles CRISPR) ‚Äî not all sensitivity associations validated with drug treatments
