## Summary

This paper characterizes the molecular evolution of therapy-related myelodysplastic syndrome and acute leukemia (t-MDS/AL) in 52 pediatric neuroblastoma patients using 199 serial bone marrow (BM) samples and a secondary MSK-IMPACT cohort of 657 pediatric solid tumor patients. Using HemePACT targeted sequencing and ArcherDX RNA fusion capture, pre-leukemic mutations were detected in 13/17 t-MDS/AL patients at a median of 15 months before diagnosis. Key findings: TP53 mutations in pediatric patients emerged exclusively after chemotherapy onset (unlike adults with pre-existing CH); KMT2A rearrangements identified by RNA capture months to years before clinical presentation; relative risk of t-MDS/AL with oncogenic mutation RR=8.8. In a broader cohort of 657 pediatric solid tumor patients, CH-PD (clonal hematopoiesis with potential driver) was found in only 1%—far lower than adult rates (17.6% for patients >25 years). Data available in cBioPortal study `msk_ch_ped_2021`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Pre-leukemic mutations detectable median 15 months before t-MDS/AL in 13/17 patients

**Finding:** Combined DNA and RNA capture detected pre-leukemic alterations in 13/17 (76%) pediatric neuroblastoma patients who developed t-MDS/AL, at a median of 15 months before transformation (range 1.3–32.4 months), significantly earlier than conventional cytogenetics.

**Detail:** Disease-defining gene mutations were identified in 11/14 mutation-positive patients at median 11.8 months (range 1.3–32.4) before t-MDS/AL at a clinically detectable VAF of 0.02. KMT2A rearrangements were identified by RNA capture in 5/6 patients at median 28.5 months before transformation (range 2.3–61.8 months), compared to only 2/6 by routine cytogenetics. Median VAF at initial detection was 0.08 (range 0.03–0.33) and generally increased leading up to transformation. The median time between neuroblastoma diagnosis and pre-leukemic mutation detection was 26.9 months (range 10.8–78.1 months).

**Genes / cancer types:** `KMT2A`, `TP53`, `NPM1`, `IDH1`, `PTPN11`, `NRAS`, `PPM1D`, `STAG2` · Neuroblastoma (primary); t-MDS/AL (secondary)

### F2: TP53 mutations emerge exclusively post-therapy in pediatric patients—distinct from adult CH

**Finding:** In all 5 patients with TP53 mutations at t-MDS/AL diagnosis, ddPCR of serial BM samples confirmed that TP53 mutations were not detectable before initiation of cytotoxic therapy, suggesting therapy-induced DNA damage as the primary mechanism (unlike CH in adult cancer patients where TP53 mutations pre-exist before treatment).

**Detail:** For 4/5 patients with TP53 mutations, the earliest available BM sample (within 7 months of neuroblastoma diagnosis) was negative for the TP53 variant. The 5th patient's earliest sample (>4 years post-diagnosis) was already positive. TP53 VAF increased on- and off-therapy, with greater increases during chemotherapy/radiation vs. immunotherapy alone. LOH at TP53 locus was detected at t-MDS/AL diagnosis in multiple patients, consistent with TP53 pathway convergent evolution. Multiple independent TP53 mutations (different variants) and concurrent PPM1D mutations were observed in some patients, suggesting parallel clonal evolution.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PPM1D` · High-risk neuroblastoma treatment → t-MDS/AL

### F3: KMT2A rearrangements include two novel partners (PRDM10, DDX6) detectable years before clinical transformation by RNA capture

**Finding:** KMT2A rearrangements occurred in 6/17 t-MDS/AL patients with known (ELL, AFF1, FRYL, GAS7) and two novel partner genes (PRDM10, DDX6); RNA capture detected 5/6 rearrangements pre-transformation at median 28.5 months before diagnosis—conventional cytogenetics captured only 2/6.

**Detail:** KMT2A rearrangements were mutually exclusive with TP53 mutations (except one patient with clonal KMT2A and subclonal TP53). Both novel partners (PRDM10 and DDX6) are involved in histone methyltransferase activity. The PRDM10 fusion was detected by both RNA capture and cytogenetics; DDX6 fusion was detectable only by RNA capture. This highlights the superiority of RNA-based fusion detection for early surveillance versus cytogenetics alone.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KMT2A` (rearrangements with ELL, AFF1, FRYL, GAS7, PRDM10, DDX6) · t-MDS/AL (therapy-related) in neuroblastoma survivors

### F4: Oncogenic mutations confer RR=8.8 for t-MDS/AL vs transient cytogenetic changes; mutation count and VAF trajectory predict transformation

**Finding:** Relative risk of developing t-MDS/AL in the presence of an oncogenic mutation was 8.8 (95% CI 1.3–57.8, p<0.001) compared to patients with only transient cytogenetic abnormalities; transformation patients had significantly more oncogenic mutations per sample (p<0.001) and higher VAFs despite receiving less therapy.

**Detail:** Transient cohort (n=14): only 4/14 had any somatic mutations; median VAF 0.042 (range 0.025–0.085), 5-fold lower than at t-MDS/AL diagnosis. Transformation cohort had highest mutation burden per sample despite receiving significantly less total therapy at median time-to-transformation vs transient (p=0.04) and control (p=0.008) groups. High-risk cytogenetic events (monosomy 5/7, 17p abnormalities, KMT2A rearrangements) were predominantly in transformation patients (7 vs 1, p<0.05). Transient cohort had enrichment for 1q gain (5/14 vs 0/17, p=0.01)—a benign marker.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `KMT2A`, `NPM1`, `IDH1`, `PPM1D`, `DNMT3A`, `KRAS`, `TP53` · Neuroblastoma → t-MDS/AL

### F5: CH with potential driver (CH-PD) is rare in pediatric solid tumor patients (1%) vs adults (17.6%)

**Finding:** Among 657 pediatric solid tumor patients (≤25 years) with MSK-IMPACT profiling, only 1% (n=7) had CH-PD (oncogenic myeloid mutations at VAF ≥0.02), compared to 17.6% in patients >25 years—confirming that pediatric CH is rare and likely more often therapy-induced than age-related.

**Detail:** Median VAF for CH-PD variants: 0.027 (range 0.023–0.081). Of 7 CH-PD patients: 3 died of primary malignancy, 4 alive without hematologic malignancy at median 3.7-year follow-up. Among 657 patients, 4 later developed t-MDS/AL but none had evidence of CH-PD (VAF ≥0.02) at the time of MSK-IMPACT analysis (median 17.5 months prior to t-MDS/AL diagnosis). This suggests that for pediatric patients, a single time-point evaluation is insufficient—longitudinal serial BM surveillance is required.

**Genes / cancer types:** Myeloid CH genes (e.g., `TP53`, `PPM1D`, `DNMT3A`, `KRAS`, `BCOR`) · Pediatric solid tumors (neuroblastoma 144; other pediatric tumors 513)

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can error-corrected sequencing (duplex sequencing) or high-sensitivity SNP arrays detect sub-clinical TP53 variants (below VAF 0.02) before the first therapy window, definitively resolving whether they are pre-existing or truly therapy-induced?
- Would RNA-based KMT2A fusion surveillance in all pediatric patients receiving topoisomerase II inhibitors enable earlier intervention before overt t-MDS/AL?
- Do specific neuroblastoma therapy regimens (topoisomerase II inhibitors correlating with KMT2A, alkylating agents correlating with TP53) predict the molecular subtype of t-MDS/AL that emerges?
- Does the presence of CH-PD at VAF <0.02 in pediatric MSK-IMPACT samples (seen in 3 patients by pile-up analysis) predict later t-MDS/AL with longer follow-up?
- How do CH dynamics in peripheral blood (more clinically accessible than BM) compare to BM in predicting t-MDS/AL in pediatric patients?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Primary cohort is small (n=52 neuroblastoma patients; 17 transformation cases), limiting power for fully predictive models
- Single-institution MSK cohort with a high-referral, potentially higher-than-average risk patient mix; results may not generalize to community-treated patients
- Transformation cohort received less total therapy than controls (selection toward earlier transformation events); creates potential confounding
- BM samples archived in DMSO over decades; some sample quality variability; earlier samples used additional processing steps
- VAF threshold for reporting (≥0.02) is a clinical reporting standard; true sub-clinical CH may be missed, as suggested by ddPCR pile-up findings in 3 transformation patients
- Extended MSK-IMPACT cohort (n=657): CH detection used tumor sequencing blood controls, which may not capture CH as sensitively as dedicated bone marrow sampling
- No data on peripheral blood CH prevalence—BM required, which limits applicability of longitudinal surveillance recommendations to institutions with structured BM monitoring programs
