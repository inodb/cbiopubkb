## Summary

The largest integrative multi-omic analysis of cholangiocarcinoma (CCA) to date, combining WGS (71 cases), exome sequencing (200 cases), targeted sequencing (188 cases), SNP array copy-number profiling (175 cases), DNA methylation (138 cases), and gene expression (118 cases) across 489 CCAs from 10 countries. Integrative clustering (iClusterPlus) on 94 cases with all data types defines 4 molecular CCA clusters delineating etiology-specific biology: Clusters 1/2 (fluke-positive enriched) are characterized by *ERBB2* amplification and *TP53* mutations; Clusters 3/4 (fluke-negative enriched) by high CNAs with *PD-1/PD-L2* expression or epigenetic mutations (*IDH1/2*, *BAP1*) with *FGFR2/PRKACB* rearrangements. The study identifies 4 novel CCA driver genes (*RASA1*, *STK11*, *MAP2K4*, *SF3B1*), demonstrates FGFR2 3′UTR deletion as a mechanism of FGFR2 upregulation, characterizes two distinct DNA hypermethylation subtypes with different molecular drivers (EZH2/TET1 vs. IDH1/2 vs. BAP1), and introduces FIREFLY — a novel method for identifying gene sets dysregulated by non-coding promoter mutations targeting H3K27me3-associated sites.

## Key Findings

### F1: Four integrative CCA clusters define etiology-linked molecular subtypes — fluke-positive (C1/2: ERBB2/TP53) vs. fluke-negative (C3: high-CNA/immune; C4: epigenetic/FGFR2)

**Finding:** iClusterPlus integrative analysis reveals 4 distinct CCA molecular clusters. Clusters 1/2 are enriched in Fluke-Positive CCAs: Cluster 1 features promoter CpG island hypermethylation, ARID1A/BRCA1/2 mutations, high nonsynonymous mutation burden, and Cluster 2 shows upregulated *CTNNB1/WNT5B/AKT1* expression. Both Clusters 1/2 are significantly enriched for *TP53* mutations and *ERBB2* amplifications (p<0.001 and p<0.01 respectively) and elevated ERBB2 expression. Clusters 3/4 are predominantly Fluke-Negative: Cluster 3 has the highest CNAs (especially 2p/2q amplifications) and elevated *PD-1/PD-L2/BTLA* expression; Cluster 4 features *FGFR2/PRKACB* rearrangements, *IDH1/2* mutations (31.6% in C4 vs. 1% elsewhere), and CpG island shore hypermethylation.

**Detail:** The 4 clusters show significant survival differences: Clusters 3 and 4 have improved survival compared to Clusters 1 and 2, even after adjusting for fluke association, anatomical location, and clinical staging. Cluster 3's elevated PD-1/PD-L2/BTLA expression suggests potential susceptibility to immune checkpoint inhibitors. Cluster 4's IDH1/2 mutations and FGFR2 rearrangements represent the most actionable genomic targets — IDH1 inhibitors (ivosidenib) and FGFR2 inhibitors (pemigatinib, futibatinib) subsequently received FDA approval for CCA. These clusters are reproducible across anatomical CCA subtypes (≥88% concordance) and across individual data modalities.

### F2: Four novel CCA driver genes — RASA1, STK11, MAP2K4, SF3B1 — with RAS/MAPK and RNA splicing implications

**Finding:** Driver gene analysis across 459 CCAs identifies 32 significantly mutated genes (SMGs). Four novel driver genes not previously reported in CCA: *RASA1* (p120 Ras GTPase-activating protein; 4.1% of cases, 10 frameshifts, 4 nonsense — inactivating, with focal deletions; shRNA knockdown enhances CCA cell migration and invasion in vitro), *STK11* (5% of cases, predominantly inactivating — 7 nonsense, 9 frameshift), *MAP2K4* (2.2% with focal homozygous deletions in 2 cases; half inactivating), and *SF3B1* (4.6%; hotspot mutations at codons 625 and 700 — also seen in uveal melanoma and breast cancer).

**Detail:** *RASA1* inactivation and reduced expression correlate with enhanced CCA invasion, functionally validating a tumor-suppressor role. *SF3B1* mutations at known cancer hotspots suggest RNA splicing dysregulation as a CCA mechanism — implicating splicing inhibitors as potential therapeutic targets. *STK11* loss activates AMPK/mTOR signaling and is targetable in principle by mTOR inhibitors. The three RAS/MAPK genes (*RASA1*, *STK11*, *MAP2K4*) collectively expand the therapeutic rationale for MEK and MAPK pathway targeting in CCA. Known CCA drivers confirmed include *TP53* (28%), *KRAS* (17%), *SMAD4* (7%), *IDH1* (10%), *IDH2* (3%), *BAP1* (11%), *ARID1A* (8%), *FBXW7* (5%), and *PBRM1* (8%).

### F3: FGFR2 upregulation via multiple mechanisms — in-frame fusions (including novel FGFR3-TACC3), 3′UTR deletion, point mutations, and copy gain; all enriched in Cluster 4

**Finding:** WGS analysis reveals multiple mechanisms of FGFR2 upregulation beyond in-frame fusions: (1) four FGFR2 in-frame fusions (*FGFR2-STK26*, *FGFR2-TBC1D1*, *FGFR2-WAC*, *FGFR2-BICC1*) with intact tyrosine kinase domains; (2) one novel *FGFR3-TACC3* in-frame fusion (first in CCA; previously seen in bladder, GBM, lung); (3) FGFR2 3′UTR truncating rearrangements to intergenic regions (removing 3′UTR regulatory elements), confirmed by luciferase reporter to increase expression; (4) point mutations/indels (n=13) and copy gain (n=1). All FGFR2-altered CCAs are significantly enriched in Cluster 4 and express higher FGFR2 mRNA than wildtype. *PRKACB* rearrangements (*ATP1B1-PRKACB*, *LINC00261-PRKACB*) — retaining the PKA pseudokinase domain — also identified.

**Detail:** The 3′UTR deletion mechanism is analogous to PD-L1 3′UTR truncation described in other cancers — removing miRNA regulatory sites to stabilize mRNA and increase protein expression. This non-fusion mechanism of FGFR2 upregulation would be missed by RNA-seq fusion calling alone, requiring WGS for complete detection. FGFR2 fusions are now clinically validated targets: pemigatinib (FGFR1-3 inhibitor) received FDA approval for FGFR2-fusion CCA in 2020; futibatinib (irreversible FGFR1-4 inhibitor) approved in 2022. The identification of 3′UTR deletion as an additional activating mechanism expands the FGFR2-altered population potentially eligible for these therapies.

### F4: Two distinct CCA DNA hypermethylation subtypes — Cluster 1 (CpG island hypermethylation, EZH2/TET1, fluke-positive) vs. Cluster 4 (CpG shore hypermethylation, IDH1/2, BAP1, fluke-negative)

**Finding:** Unsupervised methylation clustering of 138 CCAs reproduces two hypermethylated subgroups (96.3% and 86.1% concordance with Clusters 1 and 4 respectively). Cluster 1 (Fluke-Pos) shows CpG island hypermethylation associated with TET1 downregulation and EZH2 upregulation, and elevated Signature 1 (CpG>TpG) mutations co-localizing with hypermethylated CpGs — suggesting deamination of 5-methylcytosine as a mutational mechanism. Cluster 4 (Fluke-Neg) shows CpG shore hypermethylation driven by IDH1/2 mutations (31.6%; oncometabolite 2-HG inhibiting TET enzymes) or BAP1 mutations (in IDH-wildtype Cluster 4 cases). Cluster 4 has more clonal (lower subclonality) mutation distribution vs. Cluster 1's heterogeneous VAF spread.

**Detail:** The Cluster 1 mechanism — hypermethylation → deamination → CpG>TpG signature accumulation — creates a self-perpetuating mutagenic cycle. Cluster 4 IDH mutations produce 2-HG, which competitively inhibits TET enzymes (involved in DNA demethylation), explaining the CpG shore hypermethylation. BAP1-mutant cases (in IDH-wildtype Cluster 4) also show increased CpG hypermethylation relative to BAP1-wildtype. Both clusters affect PRC2 target gene sets, suggesting convergence on Polycomb-regulated chromatin as a common downstream effect. These epigenetic findings directly informed the development of IDH1 inhibitors (ivosidenib) for CCA.

### F5: Somatic promoter mutations pervasively target H3K27me3-associated gene sets (FIREFLY analysis); L1 retrotransposition enriched in fluke-positive CCA

**Finding:** The novel FIREFLY method identifies 4 gene sets with significant enrichment of promoter mutations that alter TF binding and are transcriptionally dysregulated: two are subsets of H3K27me3-marked PRC2 target genes (MIKKELSEN\_MCV6/MEF\_HCP\_WITH\_H3K27me3 sets), with Cluster 1 enriched for mutations in 3/4 sets. Luciferase validation confirms 2/3 selected promoter mutations alter regulatory activity. Additionally, L1 (TTC28 intron 1) retrotransposition events occur in 28.2% of WGS cases (52 insertions in 20/71 tumors), predominantly in Fluke-Pos CCAs (p<0.01), and are associated with increased SV burden.

**Detail:** FIREFLY's systematic approach — combining TF binding prediction from protein binding microarray (PBM) data for 486 TFs, statistical enrichment in gene sets, and transcriptional validation — provides the first evidence that non-coding promoter mutations in CCA systematically target Polycomb-repressed chromatin. TERT promoter mutations, the only previously described non-coding driver in CCA, were found in only 2.8% of this WGS cohort. The L1 retrotransposition from TTC28 intron 1 is a remarkable recurrent genomic event that, while not directly disrupting cancer genes, increases genomic instability in fluke-associated CCA — potentially contributing to the higher mutation burden observed in Cluster 1.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TERT`, `TTC28` · Cholangiocarcinoma

## Open Questions

- Do patients with FGFR2 3′UTR deletion respond to pemigatinib/futibatinib similarly to those with canonical FGFR2 in-frame fusions, given equivalent levels of FGFR2 upregulation?
- Can Cluster 3 CCAs with elevated PD-1/PD-L2/BTLA expression be prospectively enriched for immune checkpoint inhibitor benefit, and what biomarker cutoffs define this enrichment?
- What are the downstream transcriptional targets of NFIB fusion overexpression (when applicable) vs. EZH2-mediated H3K27me3 remodeling in Cluster 1 — and do these converge on shared oncogenic programs?
- Does RASA1 inactivation predict sensitivity to RAS pathway inhibitors (e.g., MEK inhibitors, SHP2 inhibitors) in CCA?
- Why does fluke infection predominantly drive Clusters 1/2 biology — is it via specific carcinogens (e.g., nitrosamines), chronic inflammation, or epigenetic reprogramming?
- Can the two hypermethylation subtypes (CpG island vs. shore) be distinguished by accessible clinical assays (e.g., liquid biopsy methylation profiling) for non-invasive molecular subtyping?

## Limitations

- Multi-platform integration introduces batch effects that require careful normalization; analyses confirmed with subsampling but residual confounding possible
- WGS limited to 71 samples; larger WGS cohort would improve power for non-coding driver discovery and SV analysis
- DNA methylation array (not EPIC/WGBS) — covers a limited subset of CpG sites; genome-scale methylation patterns are inferred rather than directly measured
- FIREFLY is a novel computational method without extensive external validation at the time of publication
- No matched longitudinal samples — impossible to determine temporal order of genomic vs. epigenomic events
- No tumor microenvironment (immune cell deconvolution) analysis beyond immune gene expression in Cluster 3
- L1 retrotransposition analysis limited to WGS subset; prevalence in the broader cohort estimated but not directly measured
- The 4 clusters are defined on a mixed cohort from 10 countries — cultural/clinical variation in treatment and sample processing is a potential confounder
