## Summary

The AURORA US Metastasis Project performed multiplatform genomic profiling (RNA-seq, WES, low-pass WGS, DNA methylation arrays) of matched primary and metastatic tumor pairs from 55 females with metastatic breast cancer (51 primary tumors, 102 metastases). Key findings: PAM50 subtype switching occurs in ~30% of cases (luminal subtypes most unstable); HLA-A promoter methylation with coincident reduced expression occurs in 17% of metastases, especially brain and liver, constituting an immune-evasion mechanism; ER+/luminal metastases show lower fibroblast and endothelial content; TNBC/basal metastases show reduced B and T cell signatures; and liver and brain metastases have the lowest immune infiltrates across all sites. ESR1, TP53, and PIK3CA are the most frequently mutated genes. Full multiomics dataset available in `brca_aurora_2023` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: PAM50 molecular subtype switching in ~30% of primary-to-metastasis pairs, enriched in luminal subtypes

**Finding:** 13 of 39 RNAseq cases (33%) showed PAM50 subtype switching between primary tumor and metastasis. Basal-like tumors were the most stable (15/16 pairs maintained basal-like). Luminal subtypes (LumA, LumB, HER2E) experienced switching in 8 of 19 individuals. Subtype switching was coincident with DNA methylation and copy number clonality shifts, especially involving HER2.

**Detail:** Hierarchical clustering using 1,710-gene intrinsic list co-clustered 31 of 39 primary-metastasis pairs, indicating overall conservation. However, subtype instability was notable in luminal cases. HER2-enriched (HER2E) subtype switching was associated with clonal shifts and enrichment of HER2 amplification events. The immunomodulatory (IM) TNBC subtype frequency dropped from 13% in primaries to 2% in metastases, suggesting selection against immune-active phenotypes during metastasis. DNA methylation profiles were highly conserved within pairs (32/36 highest correlation to matched pair), confirming individuality of each patient's tumor biology.

**Genes / cancer types:** ERBB2, ESR1, PAM50 subtype genes (ESR1, FOXA1, GATA3, CDH1) · Metastatic breast cancer (all subtypes)

### F2: ESR1 mutations enriched in ER+ metastases, marking endocrine therapy resistance

**Finding:** ESR1 was identified as a metastasis-specific driver mutation: found in 4 individuals with ER+ metastases (all in the ligand-binding domain). ESR1 amplification was also detected in additional metastatic samples. TP53 and PIK3CA were the most frequently mutated genes overall, with TP53 more frequent in metastases than primaries (66% vs 33%, P=0.006 unadjusted).

**Detail:** Somatic mutation landscape identified TP53, KMT2C, FLG, and PIK3CA as the most frequently mutated genes. ESR1 mutations (ligand-binding domain hotspots) were found in 4 individuals from the ER+ subset, consistent with prior reports linking ESR1 hotspot mutations to acquired endocrine therapy resistance. TP53 frequency was significantly higher in metastases than primary tumors (uncorrected P=0.006), as was FLG (28% vs 3%, P=0.003). Of 11 significantly amplified DNA segments in metastases (q<0.05), all overlapped published MBC amplification regions, and 2 overlapped MYC and MDM4 amplification regions. ESR1 focal amplification was also identified in metastatic samples.

**Genes / cancer types:** ESR1, TP53, PIK3CA, KMT2C, FLG, ERBB2, MYC, MDM4, RB1, PTEN · Metastatic breast cancer (ER+)

### F3: HLA-A promoter methylation in 17% of metastases drives immune evasion, enriched in brain and liver

**Finding:** HLA-A promoter CpG island hypermethylation was detected in 23 tumors from 12 individuals (17% of metastases), significantly more frequent in metastases than primary tumors (P=0.035 unpaired). HLA-A methylation was associated with reduced HLA-A mRNA expression and lower immune cell infiltrates. Focal deletions near HLA-A were found in an additional subset (mutually exclusive with methylation events). HLA-B and HLA-C methylation were rare.

**Detail:** HLA-A methylation was preferentially found in brain and liver metastases, sites with the lowest overall immune signatures. HLA-A protein (quantified in 62 metastatic samples) positively correlated with HLA-A mRNA. Lower HLA-A mRNA was confirmed in HLA-A-methylated tumors. Focal deletions near HLA-A were identified in 13 samples from 3 participants; these appeared mutually exclusive with methylation events. TAPBP was the only other HLA-associated gene with notable methylation. B2M focal deletion was found in 3 tumors from 3 individuals. Together these events (methylation + focal deletion) affect MHC class I presentation in a substantial fraction of MBC metastases.

**Genes / cancer types:** HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C, B2M, TAPBP · Metastatic breast cancer (TNBC and ER+)

### F4: Subtype-specific TME changes — luminal metastases lose stroma, TNBC metastases lose adaptive immunity

**Finding:** When analyzed by molecular subtype, luminal/ER+ metastases show significantly lower fibroblast and endothelial cell content compared to primaries. Conversely, TNBC/basal metastases show a pronounced decrease in adaptive immune signatures (T cells, B cells, NK cells, HLA-related signatures) with no change in stromal content.

**Detail:** Supervised analysis of 749 RNAseq-based gene expression signatures identified 135 differentially expressed between all primaries and metastases (q<0.05). Subtype-stratified analysis revealed divergent TME remodeling: ER+/luminal metastases showed low fibroblast/endothelial signatures (q<0.01 by LMM), while basal-like/TNBC metastases showed significantly lower multiple T cell, B cell, NK cell, IgG, and HLA metagene signatures. These immune losses in TNBC metastases may explain inferior response to ICIs in the metastatic vs early-stage settings. Luminal adaptive immune features were largely unchanged. Immune signatures (GP2-immune-metagene, CIBERSORT) were prognostic for overall survival after adjusting for subtype.

**Genes / cancer types:** (Immune cell gene signatures: CD8A, CD19, NCAM1, HLA metagene; stromal: FAP, ACTA2, PECAM1) · Metastatic breast cancer

### F5: Liver and brain metastases have the lowest immune infiltrates across metastatic sites

**Finding:** Liver metastases consistently showed the lowest GP2-immune-metagene signature scores within individuals with multiple metastatic sites analyzed (9 of 14 individuals). In combined AURORA–RAP–GEICAM cohort analyses, liver metastases showed significantly lower adaptive immune signatures than lung or lymph node metastases. Brain metastases (particularly in basal-like/TNBC) showed upregulated differentiation signatures and lower immune and stromal content versus other sites.

**Detail:** Within-individual comparison of immune signatures across metastatic sites (n=14 individuals with ≥2 metastases) showed liver metastases had lowest immune metagene scores in 9/14 cases, often lower than the matched primary tumor. Statistical testing in AURORA–RAP–GEICAM combined cohort confirmed: liver vs lung (lower immune, significant), liver vs LN (lower immune, significant). Brain vs lung and brain vs LN also showed lower immune signatures in brain. Lung metastases showed no significant immune signature differences vs primary. Liver and brain immune depletion may partly explain reduced ICI efficacy at these sites.

**Genes / cancer types:** (Immune metagene signatures; HLA class I genes) · Metastatic breast cancer (all subtypes)

## Open Questions

- Does HLA-A methylation in brain/liver metastases predict primary resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors, and can it be detected in ctDNA to guide treatment selection?
- Can restoration of HLA-A expression (e.g., via DNMT inhibitors) sensitize HLA-A-methylated metastases to T cell-based immunotherapy?
- What drives the loss of the immunomodulatory (IM) TNBC subtype from 13% in primaries to 2% in metastases — immune selection, epigenetic reprogramming, or clonal dynamics?
- Do the immune-depleted liver and brain microenvironments represent intrinsic site-specific immunosuppression or tumor-intrinsic evolution favoring immune escape?
- Can HER2-targeting therapies be repurposed for HER2E subtype-switching events where HER2 amplification is acquired in the metastatic setting?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (55 individuals, 102 metastases); underpowered for rare subgroup analyses and survival endpoints
- Higher TNBC enrichment and younger median age than typical MBC population (autopsy samples and trial enrollment bias)
- Retrospective collection; matched primary tumors from different time points and treatment exposures than metastases (confounds primary vs metastasis comparisons)
- DNA methylation arrays (EPIC) probe coverage is incomplete for some HLA loci; allele-specific methylation not resolved
- Bone metastases not collected (technical difficulty with FFPE decalcified bone); a common metastatic site excluded
- Transcriptomic immune deconvolution cannot resolve spatial organization of immune cells within metastatic TME
- ER/HER2 status at primary diagnosis vs metastasis may diverge; receptor conversion not fully captured for all patients
