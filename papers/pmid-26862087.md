## Summary

A comprehensive WGS study of 25 fresh-frozen salivary gland adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC) tumors — the first to apply whole-genome sequencing at scale to this rare disease. Beyond the well-known MYB-NFIB fusion (found in 44% of this cohort), the study uncovers five novel *NFIB* rearrangements (with *MAP3K5*, *MYBL1*, *RPS6KA2*, *MYO6*, *RIMS1*), bringing the total NFIB translocation prevalence to 60% of cases. NFIB is also significantly overexpressed in ACC tumors vs. normal tissue regardless of fusion status, suggesting an independent oncogenic role. Novel somatic mutations implicate Rho family GTPase signaling (44% of tumors) and axonal guidance signaling (56%) as recurrently disrupted pathways. NOTCH1/2 mutations are confirmed in a subset, consistent with prior reports. The study provides a comprehensive WGS framework for a disease previously characterized only by exome or cytogenetic methods.

## Key Findings

### F1: NFIB rearrangements occur in 60% of ACC — five novel non-MYB fusion partners identified; NFIB overexpressed regardless of fusion status

**Finding:** WGS identified MYB-NFIB fusion in 11/25 tumors (44%) and five novel NFIB rearrangements — *MAP3K5-NFIB*, *MYBL1-NFIB* (two tumors each), *RPS6KA2-NFIB*, *MYO6-NFIB*, and *RIMS1-NFIB* — in 7 additional tumors. Combined, NFIB translocations occurred in 15/25 tumors (60%, 95%CI 41–77%). Three tumors had both MYB-NFIB and a novel NFIB fusion. Importantly, *NFIB* mRNA is significantly overexpressed in ACC tumors vs. normal tissues (p=0.002), and expression does not differ between tumors with vs. without NFIB fusions (p=0.91).

**Detail:** Two tumors with *MYBL1-NFIB* fusions had notably low *MYB* expression and high *MYBL1* expression, suggesting MYBL1 can substitute for MYB oncogenic activity. *NFIB* intrachromosomal rearrangements (deletions and inversions) were also found in 3 tumors without NFIB translocation. The ubiquitous NFIB overexpression in ACC — independent of fusion — points to NFIB as an autonomous oncogenic driver, not merely a passenger in MYB translocations. These data shift the conceptual framework from "MYB-NFIB fusion cancer" to "NFIB-dysregulated cancer," with MYB overexpression as a parallel but non-obligate event.

### F2: NOTCH1 is the most frequently mutated gene (3 tumors, 12%); chromatin regulation genes broadly disrupted

**Finding:** Among somatic point mutations, *NOTCH1* is the most frequently altered gene, with 4 mutations in 3 tumors (including 1 nonsense, 1 non-synonymous, 1 frameshift, 1 in-frame deletion). *NOTCH2* also harbors a non-synonymous substitution. Chromatin regulation genes are mutated in multiple tumors, consistent with prior exome-sequencing studies. Other recurrently mutated genes are listed in the paper's Table 2, including members of axonal guidance and Rho GTPase pathways.

**Detail:** Low somatic mutation burden in ACC (median 14 per tumor, range 2–36) is characteristic of this disease and contrasts sharply with high-TMB cancers. The low mutation rate makes identification of significantly mutated genes challenging, and WGS (vs. WES) was critical for recovering structural alterations that dominate the ACC mutational landscape. NOTCH signaling disruption in ACC is consistent with prior reports and with the known role of Notch in salivary gland differentiation; however, the therapeutic implication of NOTCH mutations in ACC remains unclear given the absence of approved targeted therapies.

### F3: Rho family GTPase signaling disrupted in 44% and axonal guidance signaling in 56% of ACC tumors — novel pathway findings

**Finding:** IPA pathway analysis reveals Rho family GTPase signaling as significantly disrupted (11/25 tumors, 44%; p=3.9×10⁻⁶), with mutations in *RHOA* (2 tumors), *CDH2*, *CDH13*, *ARHGEF3*, *ARHGEF5*, *ARHGEF18*, *ACTB*, and others. Axonal guidance signaling is disrupted in 14/25 tumors (56%; p=8.3×10⁻⁵), with mutations in *PLXNB1* (2 tumors), *PRKD1* (2 tumors), *SLIT1*, *PLXNA1*, *SRGAP2*, *SRGAP3*, *ADAM2*, *ADAMTS5*, *ADAMTS13*, *SEMA3G*, and others.

**Detail:** The enrichment of axonal guidance pathway mutations in ACC is biologically plausible given ACC's hallmark of perineural invasion (PNI) — the ability of ACC cells to spread along nerve sheaths. These pathway mutations could mechanistically explain PNI propensity and represent potential therapeutic targets. Rho GTPase signaling controls cytoskeletal remodeling and cell migration; its disruption could similarly promote invasion and perineural spread. Additional pathways highlighted include PKA signaling (21/25 tumors), FGF signaling (15/25), Wnt/β-catenin (13/25), PI3K/AKT (13/25), and Notch signaling (9/25).

### F4: Chromosomal breakpoints concentrated on 6q (MYB locus) and 9p (NFIB locus); complex rearrangement architecture

**Finding:** Among 253 chromosomal rearrangements (median 7/tumor), breakpoints are highly concentrated at chromosome 6q (144/506 breakpoints, 28% — the MYB locus) and 9p (71 breakpoints, 14% — the NFIB locus). Intrachromosomal rearrangements are predominantly deletions (50%), followed by inversions (35%) and duplications (16%). Median rearranged segment size is 4,074 kb. MYB rearranged with only one partner other than NFIB, while NFIB recombined with at least 6 different partners, indicating NFIB as the more promiscuous translocation partner.

**Detail:** The concentration of breakpoints at 6q23 and 9p22 makes the MYB-NFIB t(6;9) the dominant cytogenetic event in ACC, occurring via multiple different molecular mechanisms (translocation, deletion, inversion). The asymmetry — NFIB recombining with many partners but MYB predominantly with NFIB — suggests that NFIB overexpression (not NFIB fusion per se) is the critical oncogenic event, with multiple genomic mechanisms achieving this endpoint. T-cell receptor gene amplification and DUX4 deletion were observed in a subset of samples (Table 1), with uncertain functional significance.

### F5: Low somatic mutation burden (median 14/tumor) with recurrent chromatin, Notch, Rho, and axonal guidance alterations defines ACC molecular landscape

**Finding:** ACC has a markedly low somatic mutation burden (median 14 per tumor, range 2–36) compared to common epithelial malignancies, with mutations in 372 genes across 25 tumors. Only 21 genes are mutated in more than one tumor. Beyond NOTCH1/2, recurrently mutated genes include members of chromatin regulation (multiple tumors), Rho family GTPase pathway, and axonal guidance pathway. Comprehensive IPA analysis identifies corroboration of previously described ACC pathways (FGF, Wnt, PI3K/AKT, Notch, PKA) alongside novel Rho/axonal guidance findings.

**Detail:** The low mutation burden shifts analytical emphasis from point mutations to structural rearrangements — a lesson confirmed by the high prevalence of NFIB fusions. Whole-genome sequencing is essential for ACC genomics because WES misses intronic and regulatory breakpoints that create the functionally critical MYB-NFIB and novel NFIB fusions. The corroboration of PKA, FGF, Wnt, PI3K/AKT, and Notch pathway involvement using IPA provides a comprehensive catalog of potentially targetable pathways in ACC — a disease with no approved systemic therapies at the time of publication.

## Open Questions

- Does *NFIB* overexpression in the absence of a structural fusion (accounting for ~40% of cases) arise through epigenetic, transcriptional, or post-transcriptional mechanisms?
- Do *MYBL1-NFIB* fusion-bearing tumors (which lack *MYB* expression) have distinct clinical behavior compared to *MYB-NFIB* tumors?
- Do the axonal guidance pathway mutations directly contribute to perineural invasion — ACC's most clinically distinctive feature?
- Are Rho GTPase pathway mutations (RHOA, ARHGEF3/5/18) actionable targets in ACC using existing Rho pathway inhibitors?
- Can WGS-based fusion detection (vs. FISH or RT-PCR) improve diagnostic sensitivity for ACC in difficult cases?
- What is the clinical significance of *NOTCH1/2* mutations in ACC — does Notch inhibition show preclinical activity in ACC models?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=25) limits statistical power for identifying significantly mutated genes; mutation-based driver analysis is underpowered for a low-TMB tumor type
- No survival or clinical outcome data presented — cannot correlate genomic features with prognosis
- WGS breakpoint detection followed by RT-PCR confirmation showed that not all genomic fusions produce expressed transcripts (only 2/6 novel NFIB fusions confirmed at mRNA level)
- Fresh-frozen tissue requirement limits generalizability — FFPE-based clinical ACC samples were not studied
- No functional validation for most novel driver candidates (Rho GTPase, axonal guidance gene mutations)
- Predominantly major salivary gland tumors; minor salivary gland ACC (the most common site) may differ genomically
- Expression analysis limited to 17/25 tumors with available RNA
