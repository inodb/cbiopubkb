## Summary

Prospective study of a high-intensity cfDNA sequencing assay (508 genes, ~2 Mb, >60,000× raw depth) with matched white blood cell (WBC) DNA in 124 patients with metastatic breast (MBC), non-small cell lung (NSCLC), or castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC), plus 47 non-cancer controls. Simultaneous MSK-IMPACT tumor biopsy sequencing allowed categorization of every cfDNA variant by biological source: tumor-derived (biopsy-matched or subthreshold), clonal hematopoiesis (WBC-matched), or variants of unknown source (VUSo). The study demonstrated high de novo tumor mutation detection (84% of patients), showed that the vast majority of cfDNA variants in both controls and cancer patients originate from clonal hematopoiesis, and established that TMB, MSI status, and mutational signatures are inferable from cfDNA alone, with direct therapeutic implications.

## Key Findings

### F1: Clonal hematopoiesis dominates cfDNA — 81.6% of control variants and 53.2% of cancer patient variants are WBC-matched

**Finding:** The vast majority of cfDNA somatic variants detected in both non-cancer controls (81.6%) and cancer patients (53.2%) are also present in matched WBC DNA, indicating clonal hematopoiesis (CH) origin rather than tumor or technical artifact.

**Detail:** Among 47 non-cancer controls, 297 of 364 somatic cfDNA mutations were WBC-matched; among 114 non-hypermutated cancer patients, 918 of 1,727 mutations were WBC-matched. The number of WBC-matched variants correlated strongly with age (smoking-adjusted p=3.86e-27) but not with tumor-matched variant counts. Top WBC-matched genes were canonical CH genes: DNMT3A, TET2, PPM1D, TP53. This analysis estimated that 89.5% of cancer patients and 83% of non-cancer controls have evidence of CH in their cfDNA, underscoring the necessity of matched cfDNA–WBC sequencing for accurate tumor mutation calling.

**Genes / cancer types:** DNMT3A, TET2, PPM1D, TP53 · Breast, lung, prostate (metastatic)

### F2: De novo tumor detection in 84% of metastatic cancer patients across MBC, NSCLC, and CRPC

**Finding:** Without prior knowledge of tumor mutations, the high-intensity cfDNA assay detected at least one tumor-derived mutation in 104 of 124 evaluable patients (84%; 95% CI: 76%–90%), and recovered 72% of all MSK-IMPACT–defined tumor mutations in cfDNA.

**Detail:** Detection rate was highest in MBC (95%; 95% CI: 83%–99%), comparable in CRPC (82%), and lower in NSCLC (76%; p=0.0258 vs. MBC). Per-mutation detection correlated strongly with cancer cell fraction (CCF) in the tumor biopsy (p=5.33e-22), with clonal mutations detected far more frequently than subclonal ones. ctDNA fraction varied significantly by cancer type (p=0.0046) and correlated with disease volume on CT (MBC p=1.03e-4; NSCLC p=0.042) and bone scan index in CRPC (p=0.023).

### F3: TMB, mutational signatures, and MSI status are inferable from cfDNA alone

**Finding:** The cfDNA assay accurately detected hypermutated tumors (4 of 6 MSK-IMPACT high-TMB cases confirmed; 6 additional cfDNA-high-TMB cases identified), APOBEC and smoking mutational signatures, and one MSI-H CRPC that responded dramatically to anti-PD-L1 therapy.

**Detail:** Six cfDNA high-TMB cases (>22.7 mutations/Mb) not identified as high-TMB in tumor biopsies were detected, plausibly due to spatial intratumoral heterogeneity and sampling bias. Hypermutated MBC and one CRPC displayed dominant APOBEC mutational signatures; hypermutated NSCLCs showed smoking-related signatures plus APOBEC and HRD components. One CRPC patient with MSI-H and MMR signature by cfDNA received anti-PD-L1 therapy and demonstrated rapid, sustained tumor regression (RECIST and PSA). Hypermutated samples accounted for 75% of all cfDNA-only (VUSo) variants.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, POLE · Breast, lung, prostate (metastatic)

### F4: Variants of unknown source (VUSo) are enriched in hypermutated tumors and likely reflect spatial heterogeneity

**Finding:** VUSo — cfDNA mutations absent from both matched tumor biopsy and WBCs — account for 82.1% of non-WBC-matched mutations in hypermutated cases vs. 35.4% in non-hypermutated tumors (p=9.3e-103), and likely reflect unsampled metastatic clones.

**Detail:** VUSo rarely constituted the highest-VAF mutation in cancer patients (17.6–23.1%), suggesting they are not the dominant tumor clone. The 10 hypermutated samples accounted for 77.7% (994/1,280) of all VUSo across the 124-patient cohort. ddPCR orthogonal validation confirmed VUSo at concordant allele fractions, ruling out sequencing artifacts. These data suggest that a single biopsy may miss substantial portions of tumor mutational diversity, particularly in hypermutated cancers with high spatial heterogeneity.

### F5: Matched cfDNA–WBC sequencing is essential for accurate liquid biopsy interpretation

**Finding:** Without matched WBC sequencing, CH mutations would be misclassified as tumor-derived in the majority of cfDNA variants, fundamentally confounding clinical interpretation of liquid biopsy results.

**Detail:** At a per-base error rate of 1–3×10⁻⁵ — comparable to other high-fidelity cfDNA assays — the assay's UMI-based noise model and joint cfDNA–WBC variant calling distinguished CH from tumor-derived variants with high specificity (<1 error per million bases). WBC-matched variants at low VAF were highly reproducible across independent biological replicates and correlated with their WBC VAFs, confirming biological rather than artifactual origin. The study established a framework in which de novo cfDNA analysis can simultaneously resolve clonal hematopoiesis, tumor-derived mutations, and spatial tumor heterogeneity.

## Open Questions

- What fraction of VUSo in non-hypermutated cancer patients represent true tumor-derived mutations from unbiopsied metastatic sites vs. other forms of somatic mosaicism?
- Can longitudinal cfDNA monitoring using this high-intensity assay detect clonal evolution and emerging resistance mutations earlier than imaging?
- Does the 6-fold higher cfDNA TMB threshold relative to tumor biopsy TMB reflect a systematic biological phenomenon or technical bias that needs recalibration for clinical use?
- What proportion of the non-cancer control VUSo originate from somatic mosaicism in non-hematopoietic tissues (e.g., colon, liver) rather than CH?
- Can matched cfDNA–WBC sequencing be routinely incorporated into clinical liquid biopsy pipelines at sufficient throughput and cost for widespread clinical adoption?

## Limitations

- Prospective feasibility study with a modest sample size per cancer type (39–44 patients), limiting power for cancer-type–specific comparisons
- Tumor biopsies were single-site and collected within 6 weeks of cfDNA; spatial and temporal heterogeneity may cause underestimation of concordance
- High sequencing depth (>60,000×) and 2 Mb footprint have significant cost implications not addressed in the study
- VUSo classification is agnostic to biological source; ddPCR validation was performed in only a subset of cases
- ctDNA fraction estimation methods (volumetric CT for MBC/NSCLC; automated bone scan index for CRPC) are not directly comparable across cancer types
