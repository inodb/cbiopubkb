## Summary

Comprehensive whole-genome analysis of 212 gastric cancer (GC) genomes from four cohorts to identify non-coding mutation hotspots using a statistical framework incorporating epigenetic and sequence context covariates. Genome-wide scan identified 34 significant non-coding SNV hotspots, of which 11 overlap CTCF binding sites (CBSs). CBS hotspot mutations are enriched in chromosomal instability (CIN) GC tumors (71%), co-occur with neighboring chromosomal aberrations, and affect 25% of all GC and 19% of colorectal cancers. Three CBS hotspots were associated with altered expression of neighboring genes (CENPQ, KCNQ5, SPG20). CBS hotspot mutations are clonal and early events (comparable VAFs to coding driver mutations). Three gastric lineage-specific genes (LIPF, PGC, MUC6) show broad enrichment of non-coding indels. GC genomic data available in `stad_oncosg_2018` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: 11 of 34 non-coding SNV hotspots overlap CTCF binding sites — enriched in CIN gastric tumors

**Finding:** Genome-wide scan of somatic SNVs in 192 non-hypermutated GC genomes identified 34 significant non-coding mutation hotspots (Bonferroni-adjusted P<0.01). Strikingly, 11 of 34 (32%) overlapped CTCF binding sites (CBSs). CBS hotspot mutations occurred predominantly in CIN subtype tumors (71%, P=0.012 by Fisher's exact test). The CIN subtype is the most common GC subtype (~50% of all GC).

**Detail:** The statistical framework incorporated 33 general + 36 gastric-specific chromatin features, 133 transcription factor binding profiles, DNA replication timing, and expanded nucleotide sequence context (tri- and penta-nucleotide). LASSO logistic regression identified the most predictive epigenetic and sequence features. Despite a genome-wide 3-fold elevated mutation rate at CBSs in GC (11 vs 3.6 mutations/Mb in flanking regions), the 11 CBS hotspots remained significant after a CBS-specific background model controlling for subtype, chromatin loop boundaries, mutation spectrum, and mutation signatures 1 and 17. 9/11 CBS hotspots remained significant (Bonferroni P<0.01); 2 borderline (adjusted P=0.025 and 0.086). CBS hotspot mutations are biased toward A.T>C.G and A.T>G.C substitutions, consistent with but functionally distinct from background CBS mutations.

**Genes / cancer types:** CTCF (functional protein) · Gastric adenocarcinoma

### F2: CBS hotspot mutations are significantly associated with neighboring chromosomal copy number breakpoints

**Finding:** CBS hotspot mutations are spatially associated with somatic copy number alteration (SCNA) breakpoints: the median distance between a CBS hotspot mutation and its nearest SCNA breakpoint in the same tumor was ~1 Mb (vs ~2 Mb for non-CBS hotspots, P=0.0018). This coupling was strongest in CIN tumors and was also observed genome-wide for all mutated CBSs at chromatin loop boundaries (P=5.7×10⁻¹⁶).

**Detail:** SCNA breakpoint proximity analysis was performed for all 11 CBS hotspots vs non-CBS hotspots and for all mutated CBSs vs non-mutated CBSs. CBS mutations at chromatin loop boundaries showed stronger SCNA association than non-boundary CBSs. In GS tumors the association trended but was not significant (1.58-fold vs 2.17-fold distance difference in CIN). These findings support a model where CTCF disruption at loop boundaries contributes to chromosomal instability through altered topological domain organization. VAF analysis showed CBS hotspot mutations are predominantly clonal (comparable VAFs to coding driver gene mutations, paired Wilcoxon P=0.49), indicating these are early, truncal events.

**Genes / cancer types:** CTCF, (neighboring genes: CENPQ, KCNQ5, SPG20) · Gastric adenocarcinoma (CIN subtype)

### F3: CBS hotspot mutations associated with expression changes of neighboring cancer-relevant genes

**Finding:** Three of four tested CBS hotspots (with ≥3 mutated samples having RNA-seq data) showed nominally significant altered expression of neighboring genes. CBS hotspot on chr6 associated with elevated CENPQ and MUT expression (adj. P=0.026 and 0.042); another chr6 hotspot with reduced KCNQ5 expression (adj. P=0.047); chr13 hotspot with ~3-fold reduced SPG20 expression (P=0.045, not significant after correction).

**Detail:** Gene expression associations tested using Wilcoxon rank-sum test in TCGA (n=35 mutated) and validated in Singapore (SG) cohort (n=14 mutated). Hi-C chromatin contact data from IMR90 cells confirmed 3D chromatin contacts between KCNQ5/SPG20 loci and their corresponding CBS hotspots. CENPQ (centromere protein Q, mitotic progression/chromosomal segregation) was upregulated in GC vs normal gastric tissue (P=0.0028). KCNQ5 and SPG20 were both downregulated in GC vs normal (P=3.2×10⁻⁷ and P=0.00082). KCNQ5 knockdown of CTCF confirmed to downregulate KCNQ5 expression. A prior study confirmed chr6:73,122,103 A>G CBS mutation disrupts CTCF binding by EMSA. All expression associations were confirmed after correcting for copy number variation and tumor purity.

**Genes / cancer types:** CENPQ, KCNQ5, SPG20, MUT, AFDN, CTCF · Gastric adenocarcinoma

### F4: CBS hotspot mutations found in 25% of gastric cancers and 19% of colorectal cancers but rare in other cancer types

**Finding:** Collectively, 25% of all gastric tumors harbor at least one CBS hotspot mutation — the second most mutated functional region in GC after TP53 (50%). Among 826 non-hypermutated tumors from 18 other cancer types, colorectal cancer showed the highest CBS hotspot mutation rate (19%), consistent with the molecular and pathological similarities between GC and CRC. CBS hotspot mutations were rare or absent in melanoma and bladder carcinoma despite high genome-wide mutation rates at CBSs in those cancers.

**Detail:** Pan-cancer analysis examined 18 cancer types from ICGC/TCGA pan-cancer WGS datasets. CBS hotspot mutations were present at low frequencies in breast, liver, lung, pancreatic cancers and lymphoma. Melanoma and bladder carcinoma, despite high CBS mutation rates genome-wide, had zero CBS hotspot mutations — suggesting hotspot formation requires cancer-type-specific mechanisms beyond elevated CBS mutation burden. All 11 CBS hotspot positions were also found in COSMIC or published gastrointestinal tumor genome studies, further supporting gastrointestinal-specific selective pressure.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53 (reference driver), CTCF (binding site target) · Gastric adenocarcinoma, colorectal carcinoma

### F5: Lineage-specific non-coding indels enriched in LIPF, PGC, and MUC6 — gastric tissue-specific genes

**Finding:** Gene-based analysis of non-coding indel recurrence across the 192 GC genomes identified three gastric-lineage-specific genes with broad enrichment of non-coding indels in their promoter, UTR, and intronic regions: LIPF (gastric lipase), PGC (pepsinogen C), and MUC6 (mucin). These three genes account for the top three positions in the genome-wide non-coding indel analysis but none showed associated gene expression changes.

**Detail:** LIPF is expressed at extremely high levels in stomach (GTEx), PGC at 11,940 TPM in stomach vs ≤2 TPM in most other tissues, and MUC6 at 133 TPM in stomach. Non-coding indels in all three genes were not associated with expression change despite high recurrence. The top overall indel hotspot was ~100 kbp upstream of AFDN (frequently translocated in leukemia, downregulated in multiple cancers). The biological mechanism driving indel enrichment in lineage-specific genes remains unclear but may reflect regional mutational processes in actively transcribed tissue-specific loci.

**Genes / cancer types:** LIPF, PGC, MUC6, AFDN · Gastric adenocarcinoma

## Open Questions

- What is the mechanistic relationship between CBS mutations and nearby chromosomal breakpoints — do CBS mutations precede and facilitate CNA formation, or does CNA-mediated chromatin remodeling promote CBS mutations?
- Can CBS hotspot mutations be detected in cfDNA as early biomarkers of gastric or colorectal cancer?
- Why are CBS hotspot mutations absent in melanoma and bladder cancer despite high genome-wide CBS mutation rates — is there a tissue-specific CTCF binding site structure that creates the hotspot context?
- Do mutations at the same CBS hotspot at different positions within the CTCF motif have differential disruptive potentials for CTCF binding?
- Are the non-coding indels in LIPF, PGC, and MUC6 passenger events driven by transcription-coupled mutagenesis, or do they contribute to lineage specification in gastric carcinogenesis?

## Limitations

- WGS cohort from four sources with different sequencing depths; ICGC cohort had notably fewer mutations per tumor, potentially due to depth differences
- Functional validation limited to gene expression changes; direct CTCF binding disruption tested for only one hotspot (chr6:73,122,103) by an independent study using EMSA
- Hi-C data for 3D chromatin contact analysis derived from non-gastric cell lines (IMR90, GM12878); no gastric tissue Hi-C available
- Allele-specific expression could not be resolved for CBS hotspot target genes due to >5 kb distance between hotspot and target gene
- Expression validation in only 35 mutated samples (TCGA) and 14 (SG cohort); statistical power limited for some hotspots
- MSI tumors excluded from non-coding hotspot discovery, limiting applicability to MSI-H gastric cancers
- Pan-cancer analysis based on available ICGC/TCGA WGS samples; may miss cancer types not well represented
