## Summary

Exome sequencing of 147 melanomas (expanded to 364 for validation) characterized the mutational landscape across sun-exposed and sun-shielded subtypes and identified a highly recurrent UV-signature RAC1 P29S activating mutation in 9.2% of sun-exposed tumors — the third most frequent somatic mutation after BRAF and NRAS. Crystal structures and biochemical assays confirmed RAC1 P29S releases the switch I domain conformational constraint, mimicking activated HRAS conformation and increasing effector binding 4.5–9-fold. A novel phosphatase driver PPP6C was co-discovered in 12% of BRAF/RAS-mutant sun-exposed tumors.

## Key Findings

### F1: RAC1 P29S is a UV-signature activating mutation in 9.2% of sun-exposed melanomas

**Finding:** A recurrent C>T transition at the TTTCCT motif (155-fold enriched in sun-exposed vs. sun-shielded tumors) causes RAC1 P29S in 20/217 (9.2%) sun-exposed melanomas and 5.3% of Australian cell lines. RAC1 P29S is more prevalent in males (12.8% vs. 2.4%, P = 0.01), occurs equally in primary and metastatic tumors, and is absent from all germline databases.

**Detail:** Structural analysis shows P29S releases the proline conformational restraint in the switch I loop, enabling HRAS-like direct hydrogen bonding to GTP ribose — increasing GTPase activity and PAK1/MLK3 effector binding 4.5–9-fold. Functionally, RAC1 P29S promotes melanocyte proliferation and migration. Co-occurrence with MAPK-mutant tumors is enriched (26.3% in MAPK-mutant vs. 2.4% in MAPK-WT, P = 0.009).

**Genes / cancer types:** RAC1, PAK1, MLK3, BRAF, NRAS · Cutaneous melanoma (sun-exposed)

### F2: PPP6C catalytic-cleft mutations define a novel driver in BRAF/RAS-mutant sun-exposed melanoma

**Finding:** PPP6C (serine/threonine phosphatase 6) harbors active-site mutations in 12.4% of sun-exposed melanomas, all co-occurring with BRAF or RAS mutations (P = 0.007). Mutations cluster at conserved catalytic residues (R301C recurs 4×, H92Y recurs 2×); 6/12 tumors show concurrent LOH; 3/12 have biallelic mutations. These data indicate likely loss-of-function cooperating with MAPK activation.

**Detail:** PPP6C mutations represent probable loss-of-function (active-site clustering + LOH pattern) that may cooperate with BRAF/RAS oncoproteins without redundancy — suggesting PPP6C may modulate MAPK output or cell cycle checkpoint function. This was the first large-scale report of PPP6C as a cancer driver.

### F3: UV mutagenesis dominates sun-exposed melanomas; distinct mutational context from sun-shielded subtypes

**Finding:** Sun-exposed melanomas have a median 171 mutations/tumor vs. 9 in sun-shielded (P = 1.6×10⁻⁵). The excess is accounted for by C>T transitions in dipyrimidines (CC>TT signature) with a 2.93-fold strand asymmetry in expressed genes consistent with transcription-coupled repair — hallmarks of UV-induced cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers. Sun-shielded tumors (acral, mucosal, uveal) lack this enrichment.

**Detail:** Mutation rate increases with patient age (Pearson r = 0.4, P = 0.0015). Tumors with >500 mutations are enriched in head/neck (77%) and older patients (median 80.5 vs. 65 years), consistent with chronically sun-damaged melanoma. The TTTCGT motif has a 10.5-fold elevated mutation frequency — the canonical CPD hotspot sequence context.

**Genes / cancer types:** (genome-wide signature) · Cutaneous melanoma, acral melanoma, mucosal melanoma, uveal melanoma

### F4: Three molecular melanoma classes defined by mutation load, copy number, and BRAF/NRAS status

**Finding:** Supervised clustering identifies three major classes: (1) sun-shielded melanomas with wild-type BRAF/RAS, low mutation load, and copy gains at 5p13 (RICTOR), 11q13 (CCND1), 12q14 (CDK4) activating PI3K-mTOR; (2) sun-exposed BRAF/RAS-WT with high mutation load, NF1 loss (30%), TP53/ARID2/PTPRK inactivation; (3) sun-exposed BRAF/RAS-mutant with PTEN/CDKN2A deletions and PPP6C mutations.

**Detail:** NF1 loss in class 2 (BRAF/RAS-WT, high mutation burden) suggests an alternative MAPK activation mechanism amenable to MEK inhibition. Class 1 amplicons of RICTOR/CDK4/CCND1 suggest mTOR and CDK4/6 inhibitor sensitivity in acral/mucosal/uveal melanomas. This taxonomy provides an early framework for subtype-directed therapy.

**Genes / cancer types:** NF1, RICTOR, CDK4, CCND1, PTEN, CDKN2A, ARID2, TP53 · Melanoma (all subtypes)

### F5: DCC inactivation and multiple novel tumor suppressors enriched for nonsense mutations

**Finding:** Seven genes show significantly elevated nonsense/frameshift/splice-site mutation burden (FDR < 0.05): DCC, TP53, NF1, ARID2, ZNF560, FAM58A, and ME1. DCC mutations are unique: while commonly lost via LOH or epigenetic silencing in other cancers, here it accumulates a high burden of inactivating mutations — a novel mechanism for netrin receptor loss in melanoma.

**Detail:** DCC co-occurrence with RAC1 P29S is significantly enriched (28.6% in DCC-mutant vs. 0% in DCC-WT, P = 0.001). ARID2 loss recurs in the subset with high mutation load, consistent with SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling as a cooperating pathway. These inactivated tumor suppressors are not directly targetable but define synthetic lethal contexts.

**Genes / cancer types:** DCC, NF1, ARID2, TP53, ZNF560 · Cutaneous melanoma (sun-exposed)

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can pharmacological inhibition of RAC1 downstream effectors (PAK1, MLK3) provide clinical benefit in RAC1 P29S-mutant melanoma, either as monotherapy or in combination with MAPK inhibitors?
- Does RAC1 P29S confer resistance to BRAF/MEK inhibitors in tumors that co-harbor BRAF mutations?
- What is the functional mechanism by which PPP6C loss cooperates with BRAF/NRAS activation — does it regulate mitotic fidelity or MAPK feedback?
- Is DYNC1I1 R629C in acral melanoma a true driver, and do spindle checkpoint defects explain the distinct CNA landscape of this subtype?
- Does sex-specific UV exposure fully explain the male predominance of RAC1 P29S, or are there underlying germline modifier loci?

## Limitations

- Unmatched tumor samples (48/147) required somatic calling without a paired normal, potentially including rare germline variants
- Whole-exome sequencing depth averages not reported per-sample; low-purity tumors may have reduced sensitivity for subclonal mutations
- Functional studies (crystal structure, PAK1 binding, cell proliferation) performed with recombinant proteins and cell lines — in vivo melanocyte or tumor model validation not included
- Sun-shielded melanoma subtypes (acral n=17, mucosal, uveal) are small subgroups; statistical power for driver discovery is limited
- Study predates TERT promoter discovery; TERT promoter status not assessed in this cohort
