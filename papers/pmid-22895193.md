## Summary

This 2012 Nature study performed comprehensive multi-platform genomic characterization of 74 primary colon tumor-normal pairs using exome sequencing (72 pairs), RNA-seq (68 pairs), SNP arrays (74 pairs), and whole-genome sequencing (2 pairs). The study identified 36,303 protein-altering somatic mutations and 23 significantly mutated genes in microsatellite-stable (MSS) tumors, including known drivers (*KRAS*, *APC*, *TP53*, *SMAD4*, *FBXW7*, *PIK3CA*) and novel candidates (*ATM*, *TCF7L2*, *TET2*, *TET3*, *ERBB3*). The landmark discovery was recurrent RNA fusions involving R-spondin family members: *EIF3E–RSPO2* (3%) and *PTPRK–RSPO3* (8%), together occurring in ~10% of colon tumors. These fusions were mutually exclusive with *APC* mutations, functionally activate Wnt signaling, and represent potentially targetable driver events. This study is the primary data source for the `coadread_genentech` cBioPortal study.

## Key Findings

### F1: RSPO2 and RSPO3 gene fusions occur in ~10% of colon cancers and activate Wnt signaling

**Finding:** RNA-seq identified two recurrent fusion transcript classes: *EIF3E–RSPO2* (3%, 2/68) and *PTPRK–RSPO3* (8%, 5/68), together affecting ~10% of MSS colon tumors. Both fusion proteins were expressed, secreted, and functionally capable of potentiating canonical Wnt signaling in reporter assays.

**Detail:** The *EIF3E(e1)–RSPO2(e2)* fusion places the *RSPO2* coding sequence under the *EIF3E* promoter via a ~113–158 kb genomic deletion, producing a functional RSPO2 protein. The *PTPRK(e1)–RSPO3(e2)* fusion results from a chromosomal inversion on 6q, preserving the full RSPO3 coding sequence and replacing its signal peptide with PTPRK's. A second *PTPRK(e7)–RSPO3* variant encodes a ~70 kDa chimeric protein. In all fusion-positive cases, *RSPO2*/*RSPO3* expression was elevated compared to fusion-negative tumors. Fusion proteins were confirmed to activate a TOPbrite Wnt reporter in 293T cells and HT-29 colon cancer cells, amplified by exogenous Wnt. All *RSPO*-positive tumors expressed LGR4, LGR5, and LGR6 receptors.

### F2: RSPO fusions are mutually exclusive with APC mutations, functionally replacing APC loss in Wnt pathway activation

**Finding:** *RSPO2* and *RSPO3* fusions were mutually exclusive with *APC* mutations or copy loss (Fisher's exact test, P = 0.038), and also mutually exclusive with *CTNNB1* mutations. All *RSPO*-fusion-positive tumors showed upregulation of Wnt pathway target genes similar to *APC*-mutant tumors.

**Detail:** This mutual exclusivity indicates that RSPO fusions and APC mutations are functionally equivalent in driving Wnt pathway activation during CRC tumorigenesis. Supporting evidence from a transposon-based mouse CRC screen (*Sleeping Beauty*) independently identified *Rspo2* as a top candidate cancer gene, with transposon insertions mutually exclusive with *Apc* mutations in ~80% of mouse tumors. *Rspo2* transposon insertions near the transcription start site caused ~20-fold mRNA upregulation. However, all *RSPO*-fusion-positive samples also carried mutations in *KRAS* or *BRAF*, indicating that both Wnt and RAS/MAPK activation are required for tumorigenesis. R-spondin fusions represent attractive targets for antibody-based or LGR5-directed therapeutic strategies.

### F3: 23 significantly mutated cancer genes in MSS CRC include novel candidates ATM, TCF7L2, TET2/3, and ERBB3

**Finding:** Q-score analysis of 55 MSS CRC samples identified 23 significantly mutated genes (FDR ≤5%), including established drivers (*KRAS*, *APC*, *TP53*, *SMAD4*, *FBXW7*, *PIK3CA*) and new candidates: *ATM* (cell cycle checkpoint), *TCF7L2* (Wnt transcription factor), *TET2*/*TET3* (methylcytosine dioxygenases), and *ERBB3* (receptor tyrosine kinase, 8% of samples, multiple oncogenic hotspots).

**Detail:** *ERBB3* was mutated in 8% (6/72) of tumors with oncogenic hotspot mutations confirmed in a concurrent study. *TET1*, *TET2*, and *TET3* — encoding methylcytosine dioxygenases involved in DNA demethylation — were collectively mutated across multiple samples, representing the first description of TET family mutations in CRC. *TCF7L2* harbored a hotspot Arg603 mutation in 3/5 MSI-mutated cases, within the helix-loop-helix domain, likely abrogating DNA binding. *ATM* mutations implicate checkpoint kinase dysfunction as a novel CRC mechanism distinct from MMR deficiency. Among copy number alterations, *KRAS* amplification was found in 13%, *MYC* in 23%, and *IGF2* amplification co-occurring with *IGF2* overexpression in 12% of tumors.

### F4: IGF2 amplification and overexpression define a distinct CRC subset with therapeutic implications

**Finding:** *IGF2* was overexpressed (≥2-fold) in 12% (8/68) of tumors by RNA-seq, and 7/8 of these showed focal *IGF2* locus amplification by SNP array — identifying a genomically defined subset potentially amenable to IGF1R/IGF2 pathway inhibition.

**Detail:** *IGF2* encodes insulin-like growth factor 2, a ligand for IGF1R, which was previously identified as a target in CRC. The co-occurrence of amplification and overexpression confirms this is a genomic driver event rather than epigenetic dysregulation alone. Combined with mTOR pathway involvement, IGF2 amplification in CRC provides rationale for IGF1R inhibitor trials in genomically selected patients. Top differentially expressed genes by RNA-seq also included *FOXQ1* (Wnt/EMT regulation) and *CLDN1* (tight junction; associated with EMT and invasion in CRC), suggesting additional transcriptomic subsets.

### F5: Comprehensive mutational landscape reveals C>T transition dominance and defines MSS vs. MSI molecular subsets

**Finding:** In MSS tumors, C>T transitions at non-CpG sites dominated the mutational spectrum (mean 2.8/Mb); MSI tumors showed 47/Mb and a broader transversion spectrum. Two hypermutated MSS samples (~9,479 and 2,332 protein-altering mutations) were identified and excluded from significance analysis, illustrating rare MSS hypermutator phenotypes.

**Detail:** Exome sequencing of 72 pairs with deep average coverage (179×, 97.4% bases ≥10×) yielded 36,303 protein-altering somatic changes. ~80% of MSI samples had alterations in one or more MMR genes. ~98% of protein-altering SNVs were novel (not in COSMIC v58), underscoring the importance of large discovery cohorts. The MSS/MSI distinction is clinically fundamental: MSI-high tumors are responsive to immune checkpoint inhibition (pembrolizumab FDA-approved). 23 significantly mutated genes were identified in MSS CRC; MSI analysis was limited by small numbers. Pathway-level analysis implicated Wnt, TGF-β, RTK/RAS, and TP53 pathway alterations across most tumors.

## Open Questions

- Do patients with RSPO2 or RSPO3 fusions respond to anti-RSPO3 antibodies (e.g., OMP-131R10/rosmantuzumab) or other Wnt pathway inhibitors — and does the fusion type (RSPO2 vs. RSPO3) predict differential sensitivity?
- Can RSPO fusion detection from liquid biopsy (ctDNA in plasma or CSF) serve as a companion diagnostic for LGR5-directed therapies such as antibody-drug conjugates?
- What is the interaction between *RSPO* fusions and co-occurring *KRAS*/*BRAF* mutations — do they cooperate through distinct or overlapping downstream effectors?
- Does *TET2*/*TET3* mutation in CRC create a hypomethylated epigenome that is therapeutically exploitable or predictive of immune sensitivity?
- Is the hypermutated MSS CRC phenotype (as observed in 2 samples) driven by polymerase epsilon (POLE) mutations or a distinct mechanism, and does it carry an immune checkpoint inhibitor sensitivity similar to MSI-high disease?

## Limitations

- Cohort limited to 74 primary colon tumors from commercial biobanks; lack of rectal tumors, metastatic tissue, and clinical outcome data limits translational conclusions.
- RSPO fusion frequency (10%) based on RNA-seq in 68 samples; DNA-level confirmation by WGS was performed in only 7 of the fusion-positive tumors.
- Significant gene analysis powered for MSS (55 samples); MSI analysis underpowered (15 samples) — no MSI-specific significantly mutated genes reached FDR threshold.
- No functional validation of TET family mutations, TCF7L2 hotspot, or ATM loss in CRC-specific models.
- 2012 study predates comprehensive CRC molecular subtype classifications (CMS1–4 and CRIS); RSPO fusion distribution across CMS subtypes is not characterized.
- RNA-seq coverage (~30 million reads/sample) may miss low-abundance fusions or fusions in lowly expressed genes.
