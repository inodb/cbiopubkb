## Summary

This multi-institutional study compares the somatic mutational landscape of gallbladder cancer (GBCA) across 81 patients from Chile (n=21), Japan (n=11), and the United States (n=49) using MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing (341–410 genes). The study reveals marked regional differences in clinicopathologic features — Japanese patients present at earlier stage and with well-differentiated tumors, Chilean patients are younger and predominantly female with late-stage disease — and identifies region-specific gene mutation patterns: Japanese GBCA lacks *ARID1A* and *PIK3CA* mutations, while Chilean GBCA lacks *ERBB3* and *ARID2* mutations. *TP53* (58%) and *SMAD4* (31%) are the most prevalent and cross-regional drivers; *SMAD4* mutation is independently associated with worse overall survival. Eighty percent of all patients harbor at least one actionable gene alteration, supporting a precision medicine approach to GBCA treatment design.

## Key Findings

### F1: TP53 is the most commonly mutated gene across all regions (58%); Chilean patients show highest frequency (76%)

**Finding:** *TP53* is the dominant recurrent driver in GBCA across all three geographic cohorts, mutated in 47 of 81 patients (58%), with a numerically higher frequency in Chilean patients (76%, 16/21) versus Japan (55%, 6/11) and the United States (51%, 25/49), though this difference is not statistically significant after FDR correction.

**Detail:** TP53 mutations were distributed across the gene. The predominance of TP53 mutation across all cohorts suggests it is a universal early driver of GBCA pathogenesis regardless of geographic or etiologic context. Chilean tumors were also younger-onset (median age 59 years) and predominantly female (81%), implicating possible constitutional or environmental TP53-activating factors distinct from gallstone-associated inflammation. No survival impact of TP53 mutation alone reached statistical significance in this small cohort.

### F2: SMAD4 mutations occur in ~one-third of GBCA across all regions and are associated with significantly worse survival

**Finding:** *SMAD4* is mutated in 31% (25/81) of all patients — 38% in Chile, 36% in Japan, 27% in the US (P=0.68) — making it the most consistently cross-regional secondary driver. Patients with *SMAD4* mutations have significantly shorter overall survival (median 10 months vs. 25 months; log-rank P=0.039) in the surgically resected subset.

**Detail:** SMAD4, a key TGF-β pathway transcription factor, shows a strikingly similar mutation frequency across all three geographically and etiologically distinct GBCA populations, suggesting it may represent a convergent late event in GBCA progression. The survival impact of SMAD4 mutation held in univariate analysis, identifying it as a potential prognostic biomarker. Loss of SMAD4 abrogates TGF-β-mediated tumor suppression and may promote invasion and metastasis. Somatic alterations were analyzed jointly (point mutations + CNAs + fusions).

### F3: Region-specific mutation patterns distinguish Japanese GBCA from Chilean and American GBCA

**Finding:** Japanese GBCA is distinguished by the complete absence of *ARID1A* mutations (0% Japan vs. 38% Chile, 25% US; P-adj=0.35) and *PIK3CA* mutations (0% Japan vs. 14% Chile, 14% US; P-adj=0.68). Conversely, *ERBB3* and *ARID2* mutations are absent in Chilean patients but present in both Japanese (9% each) and American patients (12% and 10%, respectively). Japanese patients also present with earlier stage and more well-differentiated tumors.

**Detail:** These region-specific patterns suggest that distinct carcinogenic mechanisms are at play in different populations. Japanese GBCA may be driven more heavily by anomalous pancreatobiliary junction (APBJ)-related bile acid reflux and KRAS activation, while Chilean and American GBCA may rely more on chromatin remodeling (ARID1A) and PI3K (PIK3CA) pathway activation. The absence of ARID1A in Japanese GBCA is particularly striking, as ARID1A loss is among the most common events in biliary tract cancers globally. This has direct implications for the design of GBCA clinical trials, which should stratify by ethnicity/region.

### F4: 80% of GBCA patients harbor at least one actionable gene mutation; multiple pathway classes affected

**Finding:** Eighty percent of patients across all three centers carried at least one mutation in a potentially actionable gene (as defined by MSK OncoKB or prior clinical evidence). Major actionable pathway alterations include RTK-RAS (ERBB3, ERBB2, KRAS, FGFR), PI3K (PIK3CA), TGF-β (SMAD4), p53 (TP53), and chromatin remodeling (ARID1A, ARID2) pathways.

**Detail:** While GBCA has historically been treated as a homogeneous disease with limited systemic therapy options, this study demonstrates a high proportion of patients carry potentially drugable lesions. The breadth of affected pathways supports basket trial eligibility for a meaningful subset. Notably, at least one of PIK3CA, ERBB2/3, FGFR, or RAS pathway genes was altered in a substantial fraction of patients. The OncoKB-informed actionability assessment provides a foundation for future genomics-guided GBCA trial design.

### F5: SMAD4 pathway (TGF-β) and RTK-RAS pathway alterations show regional differences and potential mutual exclusivity patterns

**Finding:** Pathway-level analysis across all 81 patients shows RTK-RAS pathway alterations in a significant proportion of all cohorts. Mutual exclusivity analysis was performed across individual gene and pathway combinations. SMAD4 pathway (TGF-β) alterations, while individually most common in Chile and Japan, are the most cross-regionally consistent. RTK-RAS pathway alterations differ in their specific gene composition by region (ERBB3 absent in Chile; KRAS more prominent in Japan as associated with APBJ anomaly).

**Detail:** The study formally tested mutual exclusivity using a one-sided Fisher exact test with FDR correction across gene and pathway pairs. Though not all specific mutual exclusivities reached significance in this small cohort (n=81), the analytical framework established here is applicable to larger GBCA datasets. The pathway-level approach avoids sample size limitations of individual gene analysis. Survival analyses were restricted to genes/pathways mutated in ≥10 patients; only SMAD4 remained significant.

## Open Questions

- Why is ARID1A mutation completely absent in Japanese GBCA? Does APBJ-associated KRAS activation substitute for chromatin remodeling defects in driving Japanese GBCA?
- Do the regional mutation differences reflect distinct cell-of-origin or mutational exposures (bile acid composition, bacteria, inflammation type), and can these be dissected with mutational signature analysis?
- Would stratification by geography/ethnicity in GBCA clinical trials change the interpretation of targeted therapy outcomes?
- What is the clinical significance of SMAD4 mutation as a predictive (not just prognostic) biomarker — does it predict resistance to specific therapies?
- Can liquid biopsy (ctDNA) reliably detect the cross-regional GBCA drivers at diagnosis and recurrence?
- Do the ~20% of patients without actionable alterations have molecular events detectable by WGS (structural variants, mutational signatures) not captured by the targeted panel?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=81 total; Japan n=11) severely limits statistical power for regional comparisons; none of the regional gene differences survived FDR correction
- No matched-population controls; differences in mutation frequencies may reflect selection bias from the clinical centers rather than true population-level differences
- Targeted sequencing (MSK-IMPACT, 341–410 genes) excludes structural variants, non-coding mutations, and events outside the panel
- Retrospective tissue collection (1999–2016) spans two MSK-IMPACT panel versions, introducing heterogeneity in gene coverage
- No RNA sequencing; fusion events (e.g., FGFR fusions) and expression-based subtypes not captured
- Survival analysis restricted to surgical patients; Chilean patients had 76% late-stage disease, limiting surgical cohort size for that group
- Pathology review performed retrospectively; histologic grading may not be fully comparable across institutions using different classification systems
