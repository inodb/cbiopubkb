## Summary

MSK study characterizing mechanisms of acquired resistance to combined KRAS^G12C^ and EGFR inhibition (sotorasib/adagrasib + cetuximab/panitumumab) in KRAS^G12C^-mutant colorectal cancer, using cell lines, PDX models, and serial ctDNA from 12 patients. Resistance alterations converge on ERK reactivation and are highly heterogeneous — including *KRAS* mutations, *NRAS* Q61K/R, *BRAF* mutations/fusions, MEK1 mutations, RTK amplifications/fusions (MET, RET, EGFR), and *MYC* amplification. Most resistance alterations were low-VAF and subclonal, appearing and disappearing without clonal sweeps. *KRAS^G12C^* amplification was the exception: a recurrent dominant resistance mechanism that tracked with tumor markers and clinical progression. Crucially, drug withdrawal in KRAS^G12C^-amplified cells triggers oncogene-induced senescence (OIS) with elevated mTOR signaling, creating a senolytic vulnerability. Genomic data available in `coadread_mskresistance_2022` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Resistance to KRASG12C-EGFR inhibition is highly heterogeneous and primarily converges on ERK reactivation

**Finding:** At radiographic or clinical progression, ctDNA from 12 CRC patients revealed heterogeneous emergent alterations: *KRAS^G12C^* amplification, *KRAS* mutations (G12A/D/F/L/R/S/V, H95L/Q/R, Y96D/H/N), *NRAS* Q61K/R, *BRAF* mutations/fusions, *MEK1* mutations, RTK activation (*MET* amplification/fusion, *RET* fusion, *EGFR* mutations), and *MYC* amplification. Multiple resistance alterations were detected in individual patients simultaneously.

**Detail:** These mechanisms parallel those seen with KRAS^G12C^ inhibitor monotherapy resistance (which also showed *KRAS*/*NRAS*/*BRAF* mutations and RTK activations), suggesting that the EGFR antibody does not fundamentally alter the spectrum of resistance mechanisms. The common final pathway is ERK reactivation — preventing downstream ERK inhibition by the drug combination. In cell lines, resistant sublines maintained elevated RAS-GTP levels and showed incomplete suppression of ERK by drug treatment. The C106 resistant subline acquired a clonal *NRAS^G12D^* mutation co-occurring in the same cells as the *KRAS^G12C^* alteration (confirmed by single-cell sequencing).

**Genes / cancer types:** KRAS, NRAS, BRAF, MEK1, MET, RET, EGFR, MYC, APC · Colorectal carcinoma (KRAS^G12C^-mutant)

### F2: KRASG12C amplification is the dominant recurrent resistance mechanism — tracks with tumor markers

**Finding:** Unlike other resistance alterations (which appeared and disappeared at low VAF <1%), *KRAS^G12C^* amplification was the only resistance alteration that rose steadily and in step with tumor marker (CEA) elevation and clinical progression. In the RW7213 resistant cell line, >20 copies of *KRAS* were detected by FISH. In the CLR-113 PDX, acquired *KRAS^G12C^* amplification (VAF 1.00, CCF 100%) co-occurred with minor subclonal *BRAF^K601E^* and *RAF1^S259F^* alterations.

**Detail:** Serial ctDNA tracking in 4 patients with ~6-week sampling intervals showed that most resistance alterations emerged weeks before clinical progression yet remained at very low VAF, while only *KRAS^G12C^* amplification trended upward in tandem with CEA. In multiple patients, non-amplification resistance alterations (e.g., *KRAS^G13D^*, *NRAS^Q61K^*, *BRAF^V600E^*) actually decreased in VAF despite continued treatment, suggesting low fitness of those resistant subclones. This parallels *BRAF* amplification in RAF inhibitor–resistant melanoma, which can be modulated by intermittent treatment.

**Genes / cancer types:** KRAS (G12C amplification), BRAF, RAF1, NRAS · Colorectal carcinoma

### F3: Drug withdrawal in KRASG12C-amplified resistant cells triggers oncogene-induced senescence

**Finding:** Withdrawal of cetuximab-sotorasib from *KRAS^G12C^*-amplified RW7213 resistant cells triggered a senescent phenotype within 24–48 hours: large flat morphology, elevated β-galactosidase activity, reduced Ki-67, upregulated p16/p21/uPAR, downregulated apoptosis markers, and SASP cytokine secretion. mTOR pathway (p-S6K, p-S6) was markedly elevated. This OIS phenotype was specific to *KRAS^G12C^* amplification — C106 resistant cells (harboring *NRAS^G12D^* mutation) did not undergo senescence upon drug withdrawal.

**Detail:** The mechanistic explanation: *KRAS^G12C^* amplification drives sustained high-level KRAS signaling that is sufficient for drug resistance in the presence of EGFR+KRAS inhibition; upon drug withdrawal, this same hyperactivated signaling exceeds the threshold that triggers OIS. Tissue from patient 12 (who underwent biopsy 8 days after drug withdrawal at progression) confirmed elevated p-ERK, p-S6 (absent pretreatment, 2+ at 70% of cells post), and p16 (5% pretreatment → 65% at progression) — providing human validation of the OIS phenotype in vivo. After drug withdrawal, amplification signal in ctDNA declined ~2-fold in patients 1 and 5, consistent with senescent cells being protected from apoptosis and releasing less cell-free DNA.

**Genes / cancer types:** KRAS (amplified G12C), CDKN2A (p16), TP53 · Colorectal carcinoma

### F4: Senolytic mTOR inhibitor AZD8055 preferentially targets KRASG12C-amplified senescent cells

**Finding:** After drug withdrawal from *KRAS^G12C^*-amplified RW7213 resistant cells, mTOR inhibitor AZD8055 selectively inhibited S6K/S6 phosphorylation. Proliferation assays showed RW7213 cells in senescent conditions (off drug) were more sensitive to AZD8055 than cells maintained on continuous drug. Navitoclax (BCL2 inhibitor), a different senolytic agent, failed to block proliferation in either condition.

**Detail:** Rechallenge of *KRAS^G12C^*-amplified senescent cells with the same cetuximab-sotorasib combination, or with MEK inhibitor trametinib, was unable to suppress mTOR signaling or restore apoptotic potential (cleaved PARP), particularly at later time points off drug. This demonstrates that the senescent state creates a new irreversible steady-state resistant to re-treatment. The proposed therapeutic strategy is a sequential "one-two punch": (1) withdraw KRAS^G12C^+EGFR inhibitors to allow *KRAS^G12C^*-amplified cells to enter senescence, then (2) administer mTOR-targeting senolytic therapy to eliminate the senescent population. Patients 1 and 5 showed reduction of *KRAS* amplification ctDNA signal after 4 weeks of drug withdrawal, supporting feasibility.

**Genes / cancer types:** KRAS, MTOR · Colorectal carcinoma (KRAS^G12C^-amplified, acquired resistance)

### F5: Resistance alterations emerge early and are subclonal — most resist clonal sweeps

**Finding:** Longitudinal ctDNA tracking showed that most resistance alterations first appeared many weeks before clinical progression and remained at very low VAF (<1%), with no dominant clonal sweep observed across any patient. In several patients, putative resistance alterations (e.g., *KRAS^G13D^*, *NRAS^Q61K^*, *BRAF^V600E^*) became undetectable despite continued treatment, suggesting they are low-fitness subclones unable to effectively drive resistance.

**Detail:** This pattern — many low-frequency resistance alterations that appear and disappear — suggests two possibilities: drug-induced mutagenesis (induction of new mutations by treatment) and/or low fitness of most resistant clones. The implication is that tumor heterogeneity, rather than a single dominant resistance mechanism, governs clinical progression in most patients. Only *KRAS^G12C^* amplification breaks this pattern by rising with the tumor. This has clinical implications for ctDNA-guided re-treatment or combination strategies: the dominant resistance mechanism may vary between patients, and liquid biopsy sampling timing may influence which alterations are detected.

## Open Questions

- Can the "one-two punch" senolytic strategy (KRAS^G12C^+EGFR inhibition withdrawal → mTOR inhibition) be safely tested in patients with acquired *KRAS^G12C^* amplification at progression?
- What is the optimal duration of drug holiday required to achieve maximal senescence induction before senolytic therapy?
- Can ctDNA monitoring of *KRAS^G12C^* amplification dynamics serve as a clinical biomarker to guide timing of senolytic therapy or drug re-challenge?
- Do other *KRAS* amplification scenarios (e.g., *KRAS^G12D^* or *KRAS^G12V^* amplification in other contexts) also trigger OIS upon drug withdrawal?
- Can upfront triplet combinations (KRAS + EGFR + mTOR inhibition) prevent or delay the emergence of *KRAS^G12C^* amplification-driven resistance?

## Limitations

- Patient cohort limited to 12 cases; resistance mechanism analysis underpowered for all but the most recurrent events
- ctDNA analysis uses liquid biopsy, which may underrepresent spatially heterogeneous resistance clones not shed into blood
- Senolytic vulnerability demonstrated in vitro and with a single patient tissue biopsy; prospective clinical validation is lacking
- The specific senolytic agent AZD8055 is not clinically approved; translation requires identification of clinically available mTOR inhibitors with senolytic activity
- Preclinical resistance modeling used escalating drug concentrations over months, which may not perfectly recapitulate the kinetics of in vivo resistance emergence
- Only CRC patients analyzed; resistance landscape may differ from KRAS^G12C^-mutant NSCLC (which has higher baseline response rates to monotherapy)
