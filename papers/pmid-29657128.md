## Summary

Hellmann et al. performed whole-exome sequencing (WES) on 75 NSCLC patients treated with combination nivolumab plus ipilimumab (PD-1 + CTLA-4 blockade) on the CheckMate-012 trial. High tumor mutational burden (TMB) was the strongest predictor of objective response (ORR), durable clinical benefit (DCB), and progression-free survival in multivariable analysis. TMB and PD-L1 expression were independent predictors, and their combination further refined outcome prediction. Data are available in cBioPortal under study `nsclc_mskcc_2018`.

## Key Findings

### F1: High TMB strongly predicts response to combination ICI (nivolumab + ipilimumab)

**Finding:** High tumor mutational burden (above the cohort median of 158 nonsynonymous mutations) is significantly associated with improved objective response, durable clinical benefit, and progression-free survival in NSCLC patients treated with combined PD-1 and CTLA-4 blockade.

**Detail:** Responders had a median TMB of 273 vs. 114 in non-responders (Mann-Whitney p=0.0004). High TMB was associated with ORR 51% vs. 13% (Fisher's p=0.0005; OR 6.97), DCB 65% vs. 34% (p=0.011; OR 3.55), and improved PFS (median 17.1 vs. 3.7 months; HR=0.41, 95% CI 0.23–0.73; log-rank p=0.0024). ROC AUC for TMB predicting ORR was 0.75 (p=0.0006). Increasing TMB percentile rank relative to TCGA NSCLC tumors was associated with a monotonic improvement in PFS (log rank for trend p=0.01).

**Genes / cancer types:** TMB · NSCLC (adenocarcinoma and squamous)

### F2: TMB and PD-L1 are independent predictors; their combination improves accuracy

**Finding:** TMB and PD-L1 expression are not correlated with each other and are independently associated with efficacy in multivariable analysis, with the combination of both variables providing the strongest predictive accuracy.

**Detail:** No correlation was found between PD-L1 expression and TMB (Spearman ρ −0.087, p=0.48). In multivariable logistic/Cox regression adjusting for histology, smoking status, ECOG PS, and tumor burden, TMB remained independently associated with ORR (p=0.001) and PFS (p=0.002). Patients with both high TMB (>median) and PD-L1 ≥1% had the highest ORR (chi-square for trend p<0.0001). Notably, 4/5 responders with PD-L1-negative tumors had high TMB (absolute TMB range 307–1175 mutations; TCGA percentile rank 72–98th).

### F3: STK11 mutations exclusively associated with resistance to combination ICI

**Finding:** Mutations in *STK11* (LKB1) were found exclusively among non-responders, consistent with an immune-excluded tumor phenotype, though the association did not reach statistical significance due to small numbers.

**Detail:** Of 7 patients with *STK11* mutations, none had an objective response (0/7; 0% ORR). Similarly, *PTEN* mutations were exclusively in non-responders (0/4). *IFNGR1* mutations (n=3) were found only in responders. While individual gene associations did not reach statistical significance, *STK11* showed the most consistent pattern of resistance, aligning with pre-clinical reports of T-cell exclusion phenotype in STK11-mutant tumors.

### F4: TP53 mutations enriched in responders, confounded by co-elevation of TMB

**Finding:** *TP53* mutations were modestly enriched in responders, but this association is largely explained by the strong link between *TP53* alterations and increased TMB rather than an independent predictive effect.

**Detail:** *TP53* mutations were enriched in responders vs. non-responders (OR 2.9, Fisher's exact p=0.048). However, *TP53* mutations were also strongly associated with higher TMB in both this cohort and in TCGA NSCLC (consistent with known co-occurrence of TP53 mutations and smoking-associated hypermutation). This suggests that *TP53* is likely a surrogate for high TMB rather than an independent immunotherapy biomarker in NSCLC.

### F5: Clonal predicted neoantigen burden is more predictive than total neoantigen burden

**Finding:** Clonal predicted neoantigens (those present in the dominant tumor clone) are more predictive of improved PFS than total predicted neoantigen burden, though neither outperforms TMB in this dataset.

**Detail:** Clonal predicted neoantigens (>median) associated with improved PFS (log-rank p=0.04), while total predicted neoantigens did not reach significance (p=0.07). TMB and predicted neoantigen burden were highly correlated (Spearman ρ=0.92, p<0.0001), meaning predicted neoantigen burden offered no additional predictive power beyond TMB. No specific HLA alleles were associated with objective response. Nonsynonymous TMB was more predictive than mutation burden inclusive of silent variants, consistent with the neoantigen hypothesis.

**Genes / cancer types:** Neoantigen burden, clonal mutations · NSCLC

### F6: Targeted panel TMB (MSK-IMPACT or FoundationOne) is equivalent to WES TMB for predicting ICI response

**Finding:** TMB estimated from genes covered by clinically available targeted NGS panels (MSK-IMPACT 468-gene or FoundationOne 315-gene) has similar predictive fidelity for ICI efficacy as TMB measured by WES.

**Detail:** Restricting variant calls to the 468 genes in MSK-IMPACT or the 315 genes in FoundationOne maintained similar predictive performance for ORR and PFS as full WES-based TMB. This supports the clinical feasibility of panel-based TMB assessment using assays already routinely deployed in cancer care, without requiring WES. This finding informed the incorporation of panel-based TMB in subsequent Phase III CheckMate-227 trial design.

**Genes / cancer types:** Panel-based TMB · NSCLC

## Open Questions

- Does TMB retain predictive power for combination ICI (nivo+ipi) when measured prospectively with standardized panel-based assays (e.g., TMB ≥10 mut/Mb threshold from CheckMate-227)?
- What are the mechanisms by which high TMB specifically augments benefit from CTLA-4 blockade in addition to PD-1 blockade — i.e., what does ipilimumab uniquely add in TMB-high NSCLC?
- Can clonal neoantigen burden be refined beyond TMB as a predictor once HLA presentation, TCR binding, and neoantigen fitness are better modeled computationally?
- Do *STK11*-mutant NSCLC patients have any genomic co-alterations that could restore ICI sensitivity (e.g., co-occurring KRAS, high TMB, or immunomodulatory gene alterations)?
- How do these WES-derived findings translate to squamous vs. non-squamous histologies separately, given the small squamous subset (16/75) in this cohort?

## Limitations

- Retrospective correlative analysis; tissue was not available from all CheckMate-012 patients, and available tissue may be subject to selection bias.
- Small cohort (n=75) limits power to detect individual gene associations (e.g., STK11, PTEN did not reach statistical significance despite consistent directionality).
- Pre-treatment tissue only; acquired resistance mechanisms and TMB evolution on therapy not assessed.
- No RNA expression or immune cell infiltration data to complement genomic predictors (insufficient tissue for expression analysis).
- Neoantigen prediction algorithms based on peptide-HLA binding affinity alone are incomplete, limiting the mechanistic interpretation of TMB's role via neoantigens.
- Five patients (7%) had unknown PD-L1 status, slightly limiting the joint TMB + PD-L1 composite analysis.
