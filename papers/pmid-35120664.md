## Summary

MSK-MET is a pan-cancer clinico-genomic cohort of 25,775 patients (15,632 primary, 10,143 metastatic tumors) from 50 tumor types profiled with MSK-IMPACT (341–468 genes), with metastatic events extracted from electronic health records (EHR) and mapped to 21 organ sites. Three complementary analyses are presented: (1) Genomic differences between primary vs. metastatic tumors across tumor types — metastases are more chromosomally unstable (FGA higher in 16 types), have higher WGD frequency (7 types), and harbor more TP53 mutations (7 types); (2) Chromosomal instability (FGA) is positively correlated with metastatic burden in some types (strongest in prostate adenocarcinoma, rho=0.33) but not others (e.g., MSS colorectal); (3) Specific genomic alterations are associated with organotropism — e.g., AR amplification with bone/brain metastasis in prostate cancer, PTEN deletion with liver metastasis, WNT pathway alterations with lung metastasis. The most clinically significant enrichments in metastases are therapy-resistance alterations: AR amplification/mutations in prostate cancer, ESR1 mutations in ER+ breast cancer. Data available in `msk_met_2021` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Pan-cancer metastatic organotropism map across 50 tumor types and 21 organ sites

**Finding:** A comprehensive pan-cancer map of metastatic spread from 50 tumor types to 21 organ sites was constructed from 99,419 EHR-derived metastatic events. Metastatic patterns differ by histological type and molecular subtype. Lung, liver, and bone are the most common target organs overall. Organ-site frequencies replicate known clinical patterns (e.g., lobular breast cancer: higher ovarian [15%] and peritoneal [30%] vs ductal breast cancer; lung neuroendocrine: higher liver [42%] vs lung adenocarcinoma [22%]).

**Detail:** The median number of metastatic organs per patient was four (range 1–≥6); five-year survival ranged from 90% (testicular seminoma) to 10% (pancreatic adenocarcinoma). Within breast cancer, HR−/HER2+ ductal has higher CNS/Brain metastasis (38%) vs HR+/HER2− (20%), while HR+/HER2− has more bone metastasis (67% vs 49%). A Bradley-Terry model shows the order of metastatic colonization differs across cancer types, with liver and ovary tending to be colonized earlier and CNS/Brain later for most cancers.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer (50 tumor types)

### F2: Chromosomal instability is elevated in metastases and correlates with metastatic burden in a tumor-type-dependent manner

**Finding:** In 16 tumor types, metastatic tumors had significantly higher fraction of genome altered (FGA) than primary tumors; WGD frequency was higher in 7 tumor types in metastases. FGA was positively correlated with metastatic burden (number of distinct organ sites) in 11 tumor types, with the strongest association in prostate adenocarcinoma (Spearman rho=0.33, q<1×10⁻⁴⁵). However, this correlation was absent in MSS colorectal cancer, high-grade serous ovarian cancer, and other types where CNA patterns are established early.

**Detail:** Metastases from 12 tumor types had a higher fraction of clonal mutations (lower intratumor heterogeneity), consistent with the evolutionary bottleneck hypothesis — that metastasis-founding cells undergo clonal selection. Metastases from 10 tumor types had higher TMB, while hypermutated uterine cancer was the exception (lower TMB in metastases). Adjusted FGA (controlling for purity and ploidy) was confirmed significant in 11 types using FACETS. The type-specificity of FGA-metastatic burden correlations suggests that chromosomal instability is a driver of metastasis in some but not all cancers.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, CDKN2A, MYC, PTEN · Pan-cancer (prostate, lung, breast, colorectal, uterine, and others)

### F3: Therapy-resistance alterations are the most significantly enriched events in metastatic vs. primary samples

**Finding:** The most dramatic primary-to-metastasis genomic differences are therapy-driven resistance alterations. AR amplification (1% vs 30%, q<0.001) and AR mutations (0% vs 6%, q<0.001) are markedly enriched in prostate cancer metastases. ESR1 mutations are enriched in metastases from HR+/HER2− ductal breast cancer (2% vs 19%), lobular breast cancer (2% vs 13%), and endometrioid uterine cancer (3% vs 10%). TERT mutations are enriched in papillary thyroid (46% vs 69%) and melanoma (70% vs 81%) metastases.

**Detail:** Across all tumor types, 53 of 67 significant alterations were more frequent in metastases, while only 14 were more frequent in primary tumors. TP53 mutations were the most commonly enriched event across 7 tumor types in metastases. CDKN2A deletion was enriched in 5 tumor types; PTEN mutation/deletion in 4; MYC amplification in 4. The most enriched oncogenic pathways in metastases were p53, Cell Cycle, and DNA damage repair. These enrichments were largely replicated in an independent validation cohort of 9,215 patients sequenced after the data freeze.

### F4: Specific genomic alterations predict organotropism within tumor types

**Finding:** 57 significant recurrent oncogenic alterations were associated with specific metastatic organs across 10 tumor types. Key examples: prostate cancer bone metastasis — higher AR amplification (5% vs 21%) and PTEN deletion (9% vs 19%), lower ERG fusions (29% vs 24%); prostate liver metastasis — higher PTEN loss (11% vs 30%), RB1 loss (3% vs 10%), APC mutations (5% vs 11%); prostate lung metastasis — higher WNT pathway alterations (13% vs 26%); lung adenocarcinoma brain metastasis — higher TP53, TERT amplification, EGFR mutations; melanoma brain metastasis — higher PTEN mutations (7% vs 23%), PI3K pathway (19% vs 39%).

**Detail:** Organotropism-associated genomic alterations generally do not overlap across tumor types, indicating that the genomic determinants of organ preference are cancer-type specific rather than universal. Lobular breast cancer ovarian metastasis had enriched RHOA mutations (3% vs 36%) and liver metastasis had enriched FOXA1 mutations (3% vs 33%). In HR+/HER2− ductal breast cancer, ESR1 mutations were enriched in liver metastasis (5% vs 16%). These associations were identified from unpaired cross-sectional samples, meaning causal relationships require experimental validation.

### F5: Genomic features in primary tumors distinguish metastatic from non-metastatic patients

**Finding:** Comparing primary tumors from metastatic (n=11,942) vs. non-metastatic patients (n=3,690), FGA was significantly higher in 10 tumor types in metastatic patients. TP53 mutations were enriched in primary tumors from metastatic patients with lung adenocarcinoma (28% vs 45%), HR+/HER2− breast (17% vs 29%), bladder (31% vs 53%), prostate (16% vs 23%), and uterine endometrioid (9% vs 20%). TERT promoter mutations were enriched in metastatic papillary thyroid patients (20% vs 56%). SPOP mutations were depleted in primary tumors from metastatic prostate cancer patients (18% vs 12%), suggesting SPOP-mutant prostate cancer has lower metastatic potential.

**Detail:** MYC amplification was enriched in primary tumors from metastatic patients in prostate (1% vs 4%), MSS colorectal (1% vs 4%), and triple-negative breast cancer (3% vs 17%). PIK3CA mutations were depleted in primary tumors from metastatic HR+/HER2− breast cancer patients (49% vs 38%), suggesting PIK3CA-mutant luminal breast tumors may have lower metastatic risk. These primary tumor genomic features represent potential biomarkers of metastatic risk that could be assessed at the time of initial diagnosis.

## Open Questions

- Which organotropism-associated genomic alterations (e.g., PTEN in melanoma brain metastasis, WNT pathway in prostate lung metastasis) are causal drivers of organ preference vs. passengers reflecting disease progression?
- Can primary tumor genomic features (TP53 status, FGA, TERT mutations) be combined into a metastatic risk score to guide surveillance intensity after primary resection?
- Are the FGA-metastatic burden correlations absent in colorectal cancer because CNA patterns are established very early (precluding an association with later metastatic events), or because metastasis in CRC is driven by specific mutations rather than global instability?
- Does ESR1 mutation in primary tumors from metastatic ER+ breast cancer patients predict worse outcomes on adjuvant endocrine therapy before metastasis develops?
- Can the pan-cancer organotropism map be used to infer primary tumor type from biopsy of a metastatic lesion (cancer of unknown primary)?

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional unpaired design (one representative sample per patient); paired primary-to-metastasis evolution is not directly assessed
- EHR-based metastatic event extraction may miss events documented inconsistently or not captured in ICD billing codes; variability between treating physicians
- Targeted panel (MSK-IMPACT 341–468 genes) misses alterations outside the panel; whole-genome approaches would capture additional structural variants and regulatory changes
- Single representative sample per patient does not capture intratumor heterogeneity or organ-to-organ genomic diversity
- Confounding by treatment: metastatic samples are more pre-treated than primary samples, making some primary-to-metastasis differences attributable to therapy selection rather than intrinsic metastatic biology
- Single-center MSK cohort may not generalize globally; referral biases may affect cancer type composition and treatment exposure distributions
