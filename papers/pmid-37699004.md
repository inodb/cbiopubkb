## Summary

Largest single-institution study comparing clinical and molecular features of early-onset (<50 years, n=219) vs. average-onset (≥50 years, n=904) esophagogastric cancer at MSK (2005–2018), using MSK-IMPACT tumor sequencing and germline analysis. Early-onset disease is characterized by a higher proportion of women, gastric primary site (64% vs. 44%), signet ring cell/diffuse-type histology (31% vs. 9%), genomically stable (GS) molecular subtype, and low MSI-H rate (2% vs. 7%). Despite these clinical distinctions, overall survival from metastasis was statistically identical (22.7 vs. 22.1 months). CCNE1 and CDH1 alterations were enriched in early-onset tumors; CDKN2A in average-onset. Germline high/moderate-penetrance alteration rates were similar between groups (~10%). A critical finding: 35% of early-onset patients were first treated for an alternative diagnosis, indicating a clinician awareness gap.

## Key Findings

### F1: Early-onset EGC is enriched for gastric primary site and signet ring cell/diffuse histology

**Finding:** Early-onset patients were significantly more likely to have a gastric primary (64% vs. 44%, p<.0001), signet ring cell and/or diffuse type (31% vs. 9%, p<.0001), and less likely to have esophageal primary (16% vs. 28%) or ERBB2 positivity (14% vs. 22%, p=.02).

**Detail:** Female sex was more prevalent in early-onset (39% vs. 28%, p=.002), as was Asian race (16% vs. 9%, p=.002). Signet ring cell/diffuse-type enrichment persisted within the White race subgroup and when restricting to adenocarcinoma only, suggesting it is not solely driven by racial composition. Early-onset patients were less likely to smoke (22% vs. 39%, p<.0001) and had lower BMI (median 26.2 vs. 27.1 kg/m², p=.009). These features indicate a biologically distinct disease pathway not explained by traditional upper GI cancer risk factors.

**Genes / cancer types:** ERBB2, CDH1 · Gastric, esophageal, GEJ adenocarcinoma

### F2: Early-onset EGC is genomically stable with low MSI-H; average-onset shows chromosomal instability

**Finding:** Early-onset tumors were more frequently classified as genomically stable (31% vs. 18%, p=.0002) and less frequently MSI-high (2% vs. 7%, p=.003), while average-onset tumors were more commonly of the chromosomal instability (CIN) subtype.

**Detail:** TMB and fraction genome altered (FGA) were both significantly lower in early-onset tumors (TMB median 3.3 vs. 4.9 mutations/Mb, p<.001; FGA median 0.055 vs. 0.132, p<.001). These differences may partly reflect the higher proportion of gastric (vs. esophageal/GEJ) primary sites in early-onset disease, as the GS subtype is enriched in gastric cancers generally. The lower MSI-H rate in early-onset patients means standard anti-PD-1 monotherapy biomarker selection (MSI) is less likely to apply to this population.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1 (MSI), CDH1 (GS subtype) · Gastric adenocarcinoma

### F3: CCNE1 and CDH1 enriched in early-onset; CDKN2A enriched in average-onset EGC

**Finding:** After multiple-hypothesis correction, CCNE1 (16% vs. 7%, Q=0.011) and CDH1 (12% vs. 6%, Q=0.03) were significantly enriched in early-onset tumors, while CDKN2A (22% vs. 11%, Q=0.011) was enriched in average-onset tumors. ERBB2 and KRAS showed trends toward higher frequency in average-onset but did not reach significance after correction.

**Detail:** CCNE1 enrichment in early-onset tumors was driven by the CIN esophageal/GEJ subgroup. CDH1 enrichment is consistent with the higher proportion of diffuse/signet ring cell type (CDH1 loss drives the GS subtype). CDKN2A (p16) deletion is a hallmark of CIN-type and Barrett's-related esophageal adenocarcinoma, consistent with its enrichment in average-onset disease. In multivariable survival analysis, CDKN2A alteration was independently associated with worse OS (HR=1.55, 95% CI 1.21–1.98, p<.001), while ERBB2 was associated with improved OS (HR=0.65, p=.01).

### F4: Metastatic OS is equivalent between early-onset and average-onset EGC

**Finding:** Overall survival from the time of first metastasis did not differ significantly between early-onset and average-onset EGC (median 22.7 vs. 22.1 months, p=.78), arguing against a more aggressive biology in young patients.

**Detail:** Stage distribution at diagnosis was similar between groups (57% vs. 53% metastatic, p=.26), and resection rate was equivalent (44% in both). In subgroup analysis, early-onset gastric cancer had shorter OS than early-onset esophageal/GEJ cancer (19.8 vs. 32.0 months, p=.024), likely due to higher peritoneal metastasis rates in signet ring cell type (40.3% in early-onset gastric vs. 23.8% in average-onset, p<.001). Age and primary tumor site were not independently associated with outcome after multivariable adjustment.

### F5: Diagnostic delay and misdiagnosis are common in early-onset EGC

**Finding:** 35% of early-onset patients were initially treated for an alternative diagnosis (vs. 21% of average-onset, implied), and time from symptom onset to diagnosis was significantly longer in the early-onset group (median 90 vs. 64 days, p=.03), with clinicians more commonly mistaking symptoms for reflux, H. pylori infection, or genitourinary pathology.

**Detail:** Presenting symptoms differed significantly: pain (54% vs. 37%, p=.0003) and change in bowel habits (10% vs. 5%, p=.02) were more common in early-onset disease, while weight loss and dysphagia were more common in average-onset patients (reflecting esophageal predominance). The diagnostic delay was most pronounced for esophageal/GEJ early-onset cancers (median 100 vs. 64 days, p=.02), not gastric cancers. These findings highlight a critical need for clinician awareness of rising esophagogastric cancer incidence in young patients.

## Open Questions

1. What etiological factors drive the rise of signet ring cell/diffuse-type gastric cancer in young women specifically? Hormonal (estrogen receptor α) pathways are hypothesized—do organoid or clinical cohort studies support this?
2. Should early-onset EGC patients undergo distinct screening or surveillance protocols (e.g., peritoneal staging laparoscopy) given the high rates of signet ring cell type and peritoneal metastasis?
3. Can the CCNE1 amplification enrichment in early-onset CIN-type tumors inform CDK2 inhibitor trial designs in young EGC patients?
4. Why do early-onset patients present with pain and bowel habit change more than weight loss or dysphagia—does this reflect tumor biology (peritoneal involvement) or anatomy (gastric location)?
5. Given equivalent OS from metastasis, should systemic therapy regimens be the same for young and older EGC patients, or are there age-specific toxicity considerations that warrant de-escalation or intensification?

## Limitations

- Single-institution cohort with relatively homogeneous racial/ethnic composition (79% White), limiting generalizability especially to Asian populations where gastric cancer is more common.
- Retrospective design with selection bias toward patients who underwent molecular profiling—more advanced disease likely over-represented.
- Institution is a referral center with expertise in ERBB2-positive disease, potentially inflating ERBB2+ prevalence and median OS vs. population-level data.
- Germline analysis available for only 466/1123 patients (41%), limiting power to detect differences in hereditary cancer risk between groups.
- TCGA molecular subtype assignment uses histologically defined gastric criteria; subtype accuracy for esophageal/GEJ cancers may be lower.
