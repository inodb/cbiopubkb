## Summary
This study presents a comprehensive integrated proteogenomic analysis of 122 HCC biopsies from 114 patients spanning multiple etiologies (alcohol liver disease 59%, HCV 26%, HBV 11%, NAFLD 17%) and clinical stages (BCLC 0/A 53%, B/C/D 47%). WES and RNA-seq were performed on all 122 biopsies; global proteome and phosphoproteome profiling was completed on a subset of 51 tumors. Findings reveal candidate CNA-driven driver genes, novel significantly mutated genes (*GPAM*), targetable kinases (AURKA, CDK1/2/5/7), mutation-specific phosphoproteomic signatures for *CTNNB1* and *TP53*, and three integrative molecular clusters with distinct biology and survival.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: GPAM Identified as Novel Significantly Mutated Gene in HCC
- **Detail**: Using MutSigCV and OncodriveFML on 24,488 somatic mutations across 122 biopsies, 7 significantly mutated genes (SMGs) were identified. Six were previously known (*ALB*, *ARID1A*, *AXIN1*, *CDKN1A*, *CTNNB1*, *TP53*). *GPAM* (glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase, mitochondrial) was newly identified as an SMG, mutated in 7.4% of this cohort vs. 1.8% in prior meta-analyses (P=0.001). Seven of nine *GPAM* mutations were frameshift mutations, strongly suggesting a tumor suppressor role. Knockdown of *GPAM* in HepG2 and Huh7 HCC cell lines significantly increased cell proliferation, confirming functional relevance.
- **Genes/Alterations**: GPAM (7.4%, predominantly frameshift), CTNNB1 (~25%), TP53 (~25%), ARID1A, AXIN1, CDKN1A, ALB (all SMGs)
- **Data availability**: available — mutation data in hcc_meric_2021; GPAM mutation frequency queryable
- **Tool support**: native — mutation frequency, lollipop plots, OncoPrint all native
- **Concepts**: #mutation #driver-genes #liver #hepatocellular

### Finding 2: CTNNB1 and TP53 Mutations Are Mutually Exclusive with Distinct Clinicopathological Correlates
- **Detail**: *CTNNB1* and *TP53* mutations were mutually exclusive (P=0.012). CTNNB1-mutant HCCs were more frequently lower Edmondson grade (P=0.017), associated with the immune-desert phenotype (P=0.039), and had better prognosis (multivariate Cox, P=0.025 for good prognosis). TP53-mutant HCCs were higher grade (P=0.001), associated with HBV infection (P=0.010), and poor prognosis (P=0.034). CTNNB1 mutations were associated with altered phosphorylation of actin filament organization proteins (Par3-alpha, PDLIM2, MAP1B, ACF7), suggesting loss of epithelial phenotype. TP53 mutations associated with altered lipid metabolism phosphorylation and increased CDK1/2/5 activity by kinase-substrate enrichment analysis.
- **Genes/Alterations**: CTNNB1 (mut → low grade, immune desert, GLUL/AMACR/ACSS3 upregulated, Wnt activated), TP53 (mut → high grade, HBV, lipid metabolism phosphorylation, CDK1/2/5 up)
- **Data availability**: available — mutation and expression data in hcc_meric_2021; phosphoproteomics not in portal
- **Tool support**: native — mutation status, co-alteration analysis (mutual exclusivity), expression comparison; api for proteomics-linked queries
- **Concepts**: #mutation #driver-genes #liver #hepatocellular #molecular-subtypes #survival

### Finding 3: Candidate CNA-Driven Driver Genes Identified via Multi-Omic Correlation
- **Detail**: GISTIC2 identified 8 recurrently amplified and 11 deleted peaks. Integrating CNA-mRNA and mRNA-protein correlations (Spearman rho >0.5 in both), 136 candidate genes were prioritized and 29 within significant GISTIC2 regions (gained/lost in 27–61% of cohort) were filtered to those dysregulated at both mRNA and protein levels. Nine genes met all criteria: *ATP6V1C1*, *BYSL*, *CHD1L*, *NUDCD1*, *LRRC47*, *RRM2B*, *UBQLN4*, *XPO5*, *YWHAZ*. Known cancer genes include *CHD1L* (1q21.3, drives EMT) and *YWHAZ* (14-3-3 zeta, 8q22.2). Novel candidates: *NUDCD1* (8q, EMT via PI3K/AKT/ERK), *UBQLN4* (1q21.3, Wnt-β-catenin, PARP1 sensitivity), *BYSL* (6p21.1, MYC target, nucleologenesis).
- **Genes/Alterations**: CHD1L (1q21.3 gain), YWHAZ (8q22.2 gain), NUDCD1 (8q), UBQLN4 (1q21.3), BYSL (6p21.1), ATP6V1C1, RRM2B (8q22.3)
- **Data availability**: partial — CNA and RNA-seq in hcc_meric_2021; protein-level data not in cBioPortal
- **Tool support**: native — CNA and expression queries for individual genes; api for correlation across data types
- **Concepts**: #copy-number #driver-genes #liver #hepatocellular #targeted-therapy

### Finding 4: Targetable Kinases AURKA and CDKs Show Increased Activity in HCC
- **Detail**: Kinase-substrate enrichment analysis (KSEA) of the phosphoproteome revealed significantly increased activity of Aurora Kinase A (*AURKA*), CDK1/2/5/7, ERK1/2 (*MAPK1/3*), and PLK1 in HCC compared to normal livers. When normalizing phosphorylation by protein levels, AURKA activity was reduced while CDK1/2/5 and GSK3B remained elevated. AURKA, CDK1/2/5 inhibitors have shown efficacy in preclinical HCC models and potential synergy with sorafenib/regorafenib. The *UBQLN4* candidate driver represses homologous recombination repair, creating potential PARP1 inhibitor sensitivity. *RRM2B* (p53R2) may be targeted by gemcitabine.
- **Genes/Alterations**: AURKA (kinase activity up), CDK1, CDK2, CDK5, CDK7 (activity up), PLK1 (up), ERK1/2 (up); PRKACA/G, PRKCA/Z, SGK1 (reduced)
- **Data availability**: not-available — phosphoproteomics not in cBioPortal; AURKA/CDK gene-level data (mutation/CNA) partially available
- **Tool support**: custom — KSEA requires phosphoproteomics data not in portal; gene expression and CNA queries for AURKA/CDKs native
- **Concepts**: #targeted-therapy #proteomics #liver #hepatocellular #driver-genes

### Finding 5: Three Integrative Molecular Clusters with Distinct Biology and Survival
- **Detail**: Multi-omic iCluster integration of mutation, CNA, transcriptome, proteome, and phosphoproteome (n=51) identified three robust clusters. Cluster 1: ECM organization, angiogenesis, amino acid metabolism (mRNA/protein); intermediate prognosis. Cluster 2: fatty acid/amino acid metabolism enriched at mRNA and protein; enriched for *CTNNB1* mutations; best prognosis (P=0.015). Cluster 3: cell proliferation, chromatin assembly, RNA processing (mRNA/protein); elevated CDK1/2/6 activity (phosphoproteome); enriched for *TP53* mutations; associated with macrovascular invasion (P=0.01); worst prognosis (P=0.005). Integrative clusters recapitulated Hoshida et al. HCC molecular subtypes.
- **Genes/Alterations**: CTNNB1 (cluster 2 enriched), TP53 (cluster 3 enriched); cluster 3 associated with CDK1/2/6 activity and cell cycle signatures
- **Data availability**: partial — genomics and transcriptomics (clusters 1–3 replicable) in hcc_meric_2021; proteomics/phosphoproteomics needed for full clustering
- **Tool support**: api — gene expression and mutation data accessible; full iCluster requires proteomics and custom implementation
- **Concepts**: #molecular-subtypes #survival #liver #hepatocellular #proteomics

### Finding 6: Transcriptome-Proteome Discordance Reveals Post-Transcriptional Regulation
- **Detail**: Differential expression of HCC vs. normal liver showed only moderate transcriptome-proteome correlation (Spearman rho=0.33). Quadrant analysis revealed that 37.7% of genes were up-regulated on both levels, while 20.9% were up on mRNA but down at protein, and 16.6% were up at protein but down at mRNA. Translational control-related pathways were up at mRNA but not protein (possibly compensatory). Coagulation/complement pathways were up at protein but not mRNA. This suggests HCC biology cannot be fully inferred from transcriptomics alone; proteomics provides additional mechanistic insights not captured in mRNA data.
- **Genes/Alterations**: GLUL, AMACR, ACSS3 (CTNNB1-associated up at both levels); complement/coagulation proteins (protein-only up); translational control genes (mRNA-only up)
- **Data availability**: partial — transcriptomics available in hcc_meric_2021; proteomics not in cBioPortal
- **Tool support**: custom — proteomics comparison requires external data integration; transcriptomics queries native
- **Concepts**: #proteomics #liver #hepatocellular #RNA-seq #molecular-subtypes

## Open Questions
- Can the three integrative HCC clusters be approximated from genomic + transcriptomic data alone (available in cBioPortal) and used to stratify patients by prognosis and treatment-relevant biology?
- Do AURKA or CDK inhibitor sensitivities in HCC models correlate with the genomic/transcriptomic features accessible in hcc_meric_2021, enabling patient selection queries?
- How does the *GPAM* tumor suppressor function interact with common HCC drivers (*CTNNB1*, *TP53*) in co-mutation analyses?

## Limitations
- Proteome and phosphoproteome profiling performed on only 51 of 122 biopsies (though representative of full cohort)
- Predominantly Western (non-HBV) cohort; findings may differ from Asian HBV-dominant HCC cohorts previously studied
- Phosphoproteomics data are inherently noisy and represent a snapshot; dynamic phosphorylation states cannot be captured
- Not all proteins and phosphorylation sites detectable by MS; sparse phosphoproteomics limit comprehensive pathway analysis
- Long accrual period (outcomes data difficult to interpret due to evolving clinical practice); no survival data directly linked to proteomics subset
- Proteomics and phosphoproteomics data are not available in cBioPortal, limiting reproducibility of key findings
