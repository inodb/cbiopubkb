## Summary

Landmark targeted resequencing study of 111 cancer genes across 738 patients with MDS and related neoplasms (CMML, MDS-AML), characterizing the full landscape of oncogenic driver mutations in the absence of matched germline DNA. This work demonstrated that 78% of MDS patients carry at least one oncogenic driver (point mutation or cytogenetic lesion), identified complex networks of pairwise gene associations (46 significant pairs), and established a novel concept of "genetic predestination": early RNA splicing factor mutations (SF3B1, SRSF2) dictate distinct trajectories of clonal evolution and clinical phenotype. Crucially, subclonal driver mutations carried equivalent prognostic weight to clonal ones, and the total number of driver mutations—not individual genes alone—was the strongest predictor of leukemia-free survival.

## Key Findings

### F1: 78% of MDS patients carry at least one oncogenic driver mutation

**Finding:** Targeted sequencing identified oncogenic point mutations in 74% of patients; when combined with cytogenetic lesions, 78% had at least one driver alteration. Drivers were found in 43 distinct genes; the most frequently mutated were SF3B1 (24%), TET2 (22%), SRSF2 (14%), DNMT3A, RUNX1, and ASXL1 (all 5–10%).

**Detail:** Many driver genes are rare individually (<5% each), yet collectively account for ~33.5% of all driver mutations identified. Novel oncogenic mutations were identified in IRF1, CUX1, EP300, CREBBP, and PTEN—genes not previously implicated in MDS. Four genes were mutated in >10% of patients; 36 genes in <5%. The study also detected copy number aberrations (deletions 5q, 11q, 20q, 17p; monosomy 7; trisomies 8 and 21) in 13% by sequencing read-depth alone, including uniparental disomy invisible to cytogenetics.

**Genes / cancer types:** SF3B1, TET2, SRSF2, DNMT3A, RUNX1, ASXL1, EZH2, CBL, ZRSR2, U2AF1, IDH1, IDH2 · MDS, CMML

### F2: Complex gene-gene co-occurrence and mutual exclusivity define epistatic MDS networks

**Finding:** 46 pairwise gene associations reached statistical significance (FDR <10%), revealing both functional redundancy (mutual exclusivity within splicing machinery, within TET2/IDH2 DNA hydroxymethylation axis) and context-dependent interactions transcending pathway membership.

**Detail:** Splicing factor mutations (SF3B1, SRSF2, U2AF1, ZRSR2) were mutually exclusive with each other, consistent with functional redundancy—any single mutation is sufficient and no selective advantage accrues from a second. TET2 and IDH2 were mutually exclusive (both disrupt DNA hydroxymethylation). Notably, EZH2 and SRSF2 were mutually exclusive (q=0.04) despite operating in different pathways, suggesting context dependence. By contrast, IDH2 showed a striking proclivity to co-mutate with SRSF2 (OR=6.7, q=0.0004), while ASXL1+NRAS co-mutation drove more aggressive, penetrant disease in mouse models.

**Genes / cancer types:** SF3B1, SRSF2, TET2, IDH2, EZH2, ASXL1, NRAS, RUNX1 · MDS, CMML

### F3: Temporal ordering of mutations—splicing factors mutate early, chromatin/signaling genes late

**Finding:** Using variant allele fractions in patients with ≥2 driver mutations, the study reconstructed pairwise temporal precedences and showed that RNA splicing gene mutations (SF3B1, SRSF2) consistently arise early in clonal evolution, while chromatin modifiers and signaling genes (ASXL1, EZH2, CBL, RUNX1) accumulate later.

**Detail:** Among 313 patients with ≥2 driver mutations, 62% showed only clonal drivers (no subclonal heterogeneity detectable), while 34% showed statistically significant evidence of clonal plus subclonal drivers with resolvable phylogenetic relationships. A Bradley-Terry model ranking temporal acquisition order placed splicing factors and DNA methylation genes (TET2, DNMT3A) early, and chromatin/signaling genes later. This supports a hypothesis of "genetic predestination": early splicing factor identity constrains future cooperating mutation repertoire, producing distinct clinical phenotypes (SF3B1→ringed sideroblasts; SRSF2→CMML-enrichment).

### F4: Subclonal driver mutations are equally prognostic as clonal drivers

**Finding:** For six genes with established survival effects (TET2, ASXL1, SRSF2, EZH2, CBL, RUNX1), there was no significant difference in leukemia-free survival between patients with clonal vs. subclonal driver mutations—subclonal mutations carry the same adverse prognostic weight.

**Detail:** This finding has clinical implications: detection of a prognostically adverse mutation in even a minor subclone is sufficient to risk-stratify a patient, arguing for sensitive sequencing assays that detect low-VAF variants. Leukemia-free survival in patients with 1 driver mutation had a median of 49 months, dropping to 42, 27, 18, and 4 months for 2, 3, 4–5, and ≥6 mutations, respectively (all p<.0001). Rate of transformation to AML increased monotonically with driver count.

**Genes / cancer types:** TET2, ASXL1, SRSF2, EZH2, CBL, RUNX1 · MDS

### F5: Number of driver mutations is the dominant prognostic variable, independent of IPSS

**Finding:** Leukemia-free survival declined steadily and significantly with increasing count of oncogenic mutations/cytogenetic lesions (p<.0001), and this variable added independent prognostic information beyond the IPSS classification (p=.0004 after IPSS stratification).

**Detail:** Combining standard clinical variables (blood counts, morphology, cytogenetics, demographics) yielded an AUC of 0.80 for 90-month leukemia-free survival vs. IPSS AUC of 0.76; adding genomic mutation data marginally increased AUC to 0.82 (non-significant, reflecting redundancy between genomic and clinical variables). SF3B1 mutations predicted low blast fraction and ringed sideroblasts; WT1, IDH2, STAG2, and NRAS predicted high blast fraction. These genotype-phenotype relationships suggest genomic data could refine MDS diagnosis and prognosis beyond bone marrow morphology.

**Genes / cancer types:** SF3B1, TET2, WT1, IDH2, STAG2, NRAS, TP53 · MDS, CMML

## Open Questions

1. How do the clonal evolution trajectories defined here translate to serial blood-sample monitoring strategies—can annual cfDNA sequencing reliably detect emerging subclonal mutations before clinical progression?
2. Does combining molecular driver count with the revised IPSS-R (which expanded cytogenetic granularity) significantly improve prognostic stratification compared with either alone?
3. What determines the "predestination" constraint—why do early SF3B1 mutations preferentially cooperate with specific partner mutations vs. SRSF2? Mechanistic studies in iPSC or mouse models needed.
4. Several new myeloid genes discovered after this study (SMC1A, SMC3, SETBP1, BCOR) were not included; what fraction of the 22% mutation-negative patients would be reclassified with an updated panel?
5. Can knowledge of the cooperating mutation landscape be exploited therapeutically—e.g., using epigenetic inhibitors in SRSF2/IDH2 co-mutated CMML?

## Limitations

- No matched germline DNA; stringent filtering likely missed some somatic variants, and rare germline polymorphisms may have been included—though extensive controls showed high specificity.
- Whole-genome amplification (WGA) was used for most samples, introducing potential non-uniform amplification; validated against native DNA with minimal bias.
- Cross-sectional design: clonal hierarchy inferred from single-timepoint VAF ratios, not confirmed longitudinally—serial samples needed to validate "predestination" hypothesis.
- Missing outcome data for 143/738 patients, limiting survival analyses.
- cBioPortal study ID `mds_mskcc_2020` reflects a different (MSK) cohort assembled later; direct replication of ICGC findings requires access to EGA-deposited data.
