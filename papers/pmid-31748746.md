## Summary

The Glioma Longitudinal Analysis (GLASS) Consortium analyzed temporally separated DNA sequencing data (WES and WGS) from 222 adult diffuse glioma patients treated at 35 institutions worldwide, each with tumor specimens from at least two time points (initial and recurrence). Three molecular subtypes were analyzed: IDH-mutant with 1p/19q codeletion (IDHmut-codel, n=25), IDH-mutant without codeletion (IDHmut-noncodel, n=63), and IDH wild type (IDHwt, n=134). Key findings: driver mutations (IDH1, TERT promoter) are stable trunk events never acquired or lost at recurrence; alkylating agent-induced hypermutation is common (47% of IDHmut-noncodel) but does not worsen survival; CDKN2A deletions and aneuploidy are frequently acquired in recurrent IDHmut-noncodel glioma and are associated with poor outcomes; clonal architecture is largely preserved over time with neutral evolution predominating; and immunoediting is not significantly active in glioma. Data available in `difg_glass_2019` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: IDH1, TERT promoter mutations, and chr1p/19q co-deletion are stable trunk events never lost or newly acquired at recurrence

**Finding:** Driver events defining glioma molecular subtype — IDH1 mutations, TERT promoter mutations, and chromosome 1p/19q co-deletion — were invariably shared between initial and recurrent tumors and were never lost or newly acquired during the surgical interval in any patient. These are the earliest, most clonally dominant events in gliomagenesis.

**Detail:** IDH1 mutations showed lower cancer cell fractions (CCF) than TP53 in recurrent IDHmut-noncodel tumors (P=0.0018), suggesting IDH1 mutations may precede TP53 in some cases, or that TP53-mutant subclones expand preferentially at recurrence. TP53 mutation CCF significantly increased over time in IDHmut-noncodel (P=0.037), but not IDHwt gliomas. ATRX mutations in IDHmut-noncodel initial tumors had lower CCFs than TP53 (P=0.03) and IDH1 (P=0.10), indicating ATRX inactivation is a later event. Chromosome 7 gain and chromosome 10 loss — hallmarks of IDHwt glioma — were also stable between time points.

**Genes / cancer types:** IDH1, TERT, TP53, ATRX, EGFR, CDKN2A · Diffuse glioma (IDHmut-codel, IDHmut-noncodel, IDHwt)

### F2: Alkylating agent-induced hypermutation is common but does not worsen survival across subtypes

**Finding:** Treatment-associated hypermutation (>10 Mutations/Mb at recurrence, signature 11 enriched) occurred in 47% of IDHmut-noncodel recurrences, 25% of IDHmut-codels, and 16% of IDHwt gliomas. The difference between subtypes was significant (Fisher's exact test P=2.0e-03), establishing IDHmut-noncodel as the most susceptible subtype. Despite their high mutation burden, hypermutated tumors showed no significant difference in overall survival compared to non-hypermutated tumors within each subtype.

**Detail:** Among hypermutated tumors, 48% showed predominantly clonal recurrence-only mutations (suggestive of a selective sweep), but IDHmut-noncodel hypermutators with clonal vs. subclonal hypermutation did not differ in survival (log-rank P=0.38). Acquired hypermutation was linked to MGMT promoter methylation (enabling temozolomide activity), but MGMT methylation status was not available for all patients. The survival equivalence between hypermutators and non-hypermutators suggests that the noted benefit of alkylating agent therapy is preserved despite the hypermutation phenotype, possibly because increased neoantigen load does not trigger effective immune clearance in glioma.

**Genes / cancer types:** MGMT (methylation), MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, POLE (via signature 11) · Diffuse glioma (all subtypes)

### F3: CDKN2A deletions acquired at recurrence in IDHmut-noncodel drive aneuploidy and shorter survival

**Finding:** CDKN2A homozygous deletions were enriched specifically in recurrent IDHmut-noncodel gliomas (the only subtype showing significant acquisition of this alteration). Acquired cell cycle gene alterations (CDKN2A deletion, focal CCND2/CDK4/CDK6 gain, RB1 mutation or loss) drove increased aneuploidy levels at recurrence (P=1.4E-06 total aneuploidy; P=8.6E-03 arm-level). Critically, patients with either acquired cell cycle alterations or the largest aneuploidy increases had significantly shorter survival (log-rank P<0.0001).

**Detail:** CDKN2A deletion CCFs were higher than genome-wide average CNV CCFs in the same tumors (Wilcoxon P significant), establishing that CDKN2A deletion precedes the broader aneuploidy that accompanies it. This temporal ordering — CDKN2A loss → aneuploidy — positions CDKN2A deletion as a genomic instability driver in IDHmut-noncodel glioma, analogous to its role in other cancer types. The findings provide a rationale for CDK inhibitor trials in recurrent IDHmut-noncodel glioma — a translational implication that does not apply to IDHwt or IDHmut-codel subtypes.

**Genes / cancer types:** CDKN2A, CCND2, CDK4, CDK6, RB1 · IDHmut-noncodel glioma

### F4: Clonal architecture is largely stable over time; neutral-to-neutral is the most common evolutionary trajectory

**Finding:** 84% of tumors had a persistent mutational cluster with CCF > 50% from initial to recurrent tumor, representing a stable tumor trunk. Kendall rank correlation of cluster CCF ranks between time points was significantly positive (tau=0.20, P=3.76E-24), indicating overall preservation of clonal hierarchy. Using a Bayesian model (SubClonalSelection) in 104 paired patients, neutral-to-neutral evolution was the most common trajectory across all subtypes (52%). IDHwt gliomas showed the highest selection at any timepoint (64%), and IDHwt tumors with evidence of selection at recurrence had shorter OS than neutrally evolving IDHwt tumors (P=2.7E-02).

**Detail:** Selection at recurrence was significantly associated with shorter survival across subtypes in Cox regression (HR=1.53; 95% CI 1.00–2.41; P=4.8E-02). However, selection was not significantly associated with chemotherapy or radiation therapy (Fisher's exact P>0.05), suggesting that standard therapies do not systematically drive clonal selection — instead, tumor survival benefit from treatment may derive from non-genetic (stochastic) cell elimination rather than genetically-driven selective sweeps.

**Genes / cancer types:** IDH1, TP53, EGFR (and genome-wide) · Diffuse glioma (all subtypes)

### F5: No evidence of progressive immunoediting in glioma over time; IDH1 R132H neoantigen is stable and clonal

**Finding:** Neoantigen-causing mutations comprised 42% of nonsynonymous exonic mutations on average across the GLASS cohort, and this fraction did not differ by glioma subtype or between initial and recurrent tumors (P>0.05). The observed-to-expected neoantigen ratio did not significantly deviate from 1 in any subtype, and did not differ between initial and recurrent tumors, indicating no measurable immunoediting over time. The most prevalent clonal neoantigen arose from IDH1 R132H, present in 22/88 IDH-mutant tumor pairs.

**Detail:** The observed-to-expected neoantigen ratio was strongly correlated between initial and recurrent tumors of each patient (Pearson's R=0.73; P=5E-38), and did not differ between clonal and subclonal neoantigens, consistent with the absence of neoantigen-specific immune selection in glioma. IDHwt tumors showed lower observed-to-expected ratios than IDHmut-noncodels (t-test P=0.04), but neither differed from 1. The stability and clonality of the IDH1 R132H neoantigen across initial and recurrent gliomas justifies ongoing neoantigen vaccine trials targeting this mutation.

**Genes / cancer types:** IDH1 (R132H neoantigen) · Diffuse glioma (all subtypes)

## Open Questions

- Why does treatment-induced hypermutation not worsen survival, and can the increased neoantigen load in hypermutated gliomas be exploited with immune checkpoint inhibitors combined with alkylating agents?
- Do the acquired CDKN2A deletions in recurrent IDHmut-noncodel glioma represent a targetable vulnerability for CDK4/6 inhibitors in the recurrent setting?
- Can the stability of the IDH1 R132H clonal neoantigen justify personalized vaccine strategies, and what combination approaches (with temozolomide or checkpoint inhibitors) would be most effective?
- What drives the subtype-specific differences in hypermutation rates (47% IDHmut-noncodel vs. 16% IDHwt), and does MGMT methylation account for the full difference or are there additional mechanisms?
- Is the observed neutral evolution pattern truly stochastic, or does spatial sampling bias from single-biopsy recurrence specimens mask subclonal heterogeneity that would be visible with multi-region or liquid biopsy approaches?

## Limitations

- Data from 35 institutions with heterogeneous sequencing platforms, capture kits, and coverage depths; harmonized processing pipeline mitigates but cannot fully eliminate batch effects
- Spatial sampling limited to one or two biopsies per time point; intratumoral heterogeneity not captured across the tumor volume
- MGMT promoter methylation status not systematically available for all patients, preventing full analysis of hypermutation risk factors
- Neoantigen analysis used computational HLA typing, which has lower accuracy than serological typing; observed neoantigen ratios were significantly associated with number of unique HLA loci (Spearman's Rho=0.28)
- Expression data available for only a subset (n=84 from 42 pairs), limiting immune microenvironment analysis to this restricted group
- IDHmut-codel subtype was substantially underpowered (n=25) compared to IDHwt (n=134), limiting subtype-specific findings for oligodendroglioma
