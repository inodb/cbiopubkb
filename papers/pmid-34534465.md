## Summary

Comprehensive CPTAC proteogenomic characterization of 140 treatment-naïve pancreatic ductal adenocarcinomas (135 PDACs + 5 adenosquamous carcinomas), 67 paired normal adjacent tissues (NATs), and 9 normal macrodissected pancreatic ductal tissues. Eight omics platforms (WES, WGS, RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, methylation, TMT-proteomics, phosphoproteomics, glycoproteomics) generated one of the largest multi-omics PDAC datasets to date. Key innovations include: rigorous tumor cellularity assessment via multi-method deconvolution (KRAS VAF, methylation, histology); inclusion of normal ductal tissue controls to overcome acinar cell confounding in NAT comparisons; and first large-scale glycoproteomic atlas of PDAC identifying 75 N-linked glycoproteins upregulated in tumors, with sialylation/fucosylation enzyme upregulation (ST6GAL1, FUT3, B4GALT1) as novel therapeutic targets. Phosphoproteomic kinase-substrate analysis identifies PAK1/PAK2, AKT1, SRC, and CDK7 as KRAS-downstream therapeutic targets with FDA-approved or investigational inhibitors.

## Key Findings

### F1: KRAS hotspot mutations (97%) drive downstream kinase activation — PAK1, PAK2, AKT1, SRC, CDK7 as druggable targets

**Finding:** Phosphoproteomic kinase-substrate co-regulation analysis across 41 paired tumor/NAT tissues identifies five kinase-substrate pairs significantly elevated in PDACs relative to NATs: CDK7→MCM2-S27 (S-phase entry/progression), AKT1→BAD-S134 (apoptosis suppression), PAK1→BAD-S134 (apoptosis suppression), PAK2→MAPK6-S189 (atypical MAPK/cell motility), and SRC→STAT3 (proliferation/invasion). All five kinases have FDA-approved or investigational inhibitors, providing actionable targets downstream of the otherwise undruggable KRAS.

**Detail:** PAK1 is upregulated in >70% of tumors and activated via KRAS→RAC1→PAK1 signaling; PAK2 is elevated in ~90% of tumors. MET is concordantly upregulated with KRAS/RAC1/PAK1/PAK2 at transcriptomic and proteomic levels, linking MET/HGF axis to PAK signaling (MET/PAK1 axis drives proliferation, motility, cytoskeletal remodeling). CDK7 inhibition can cause cell cycle arrest and suppress tumor progression. AKT1 elevation is a consequence of universal KRAS mutation activating PI3K→AKT1. KRAS-mutation-specific kinase analysis identifies 19 kinases with differential expression across KRAS hotspot variants (G12D, G12V, G12R, Q61H), including 7 FDA-approved drug targets, suggesting variant-specific therapeutic strategies. Validated by DIA mass spectrometry for PAK1, SRC, AKT1, CDK7.

### F2: 75 N-linked glycoproteins upregulated in PDAC tumors; sialylation/fucosylation enzymes as novel therapeutic targets

**Finding:** Glycoproteomic analysis identifies 75 N-linked glycoproteins upregulated >2-fold in PDAC tumors vs. NATs, with 57 from Pancreatic Cancer Database and 18 novel (GSDMB, LECT2). Upregulated glycoproteins are predominantly secreted or membrane proteins enriched for EMT, collagen formation, and complement pathways. Glycosylation enzyme upregulation — particularly ST6GAL1 (sialylation), ST3GAL1, FUT3, FUT11 (fucosylation), B4GALT1, B4GALT4 — drives sialylated/fucosylated glycan conjugation in a pattern not captured by transcriptomics alone.

**Detail:** Mucin-type O-linked glycoproteins MUC1, MUC3A, MUC5AC, MUC5B, MUC13, MUC16 (CA19-9 antigen-related) are significantly upregulated in tumors including early-stage tumors vs. NATs/normal ducts. CEACAM5 and CEACAM6 are significantly upregulated in KRAS G12D, G12V, and Q61H (but not G12R) tumors — providing a mutation-specific glycoprotein pattern. CEACAM6 overexpression is associated with low cytolytic T-cell activity, making it both a resistance mechanism and immunotherapy target. LOXL2 protein abundance associates with shorter overall survival (KM P-value significant). ST6GAL1 and ST3GAL1 enzyme upregulation is not detected at the transcriptomic level, underscoring the necessity of glycoproteomic profiling. Inhibition of these sialylation/fucosylation enzymes could attenuate tumor-associated glycan branching as a therapeutic strategy.

### F3: TP53 missense mutations stabilize TP53 protein and upregulate DNA repair phosphosites — distinct from truncation effects

**Finding:** TP53 alterations (83% of PDAC) have extensive trans-effects at phosphosite levels, with TP53-S315 phosphorylation the strongest predictor of TP53 protein stabilization (Pearson r with TP53 protein expression). Missense TP53 mutations specifically (not truncating mutations) show significantly higher TP53 protein and TP53-S315 phosphosite levels in cis. Both missense and truncating TP53 alterations share common trans-effects: elevated phosphorylation of MKI67-S1376 (proliferation marker), MSH6-S219, and TP53BP1-S321 (DNA damage repair).

**Detail:** DNA repair gene phosphosites are upregulated in TP53-mutant tumors despite no increase in protein levels — indicating specific post-translational regulation of DNA damage response rather than transcriptional compensation. Higher MKI67-S1376 phosphorylation implies that TP53 mutations lead to increased proliferative activity independent of alteration type. MDM2 is decreased at both RNA and protein in TP53-mutant tumors, consistent with disrupted negative feedback. TP53BP1-S321 phosphorylation elevation is particularly notable as TP53BP1 is a key regulator of DSB repair pathway choice (NHEJ vs. HR). SMAD4 deletions (29% of cases) are associated with SERPINE1 downregulation (TGFβ target) and MAPK3 upregulation with E2F4 phosphorylation, providing functional evidence for pathway rewiring upon SMAD4 loss.

### F4: Immune-cold PDAC (majority) associated with endothelial cell remodeling, hypoxia/VEGF, and glycolytic pathway upregulation

**Finding:** Transcriptomics-based deconvolution of all 140 PDAC tumors identifies four cell-composition clusters. Only a small fraction (Cluster D, ~10%) are "immune-hot" with high CD8+ T-cell infiltration and immune checkpoint molecule expression; the majority are "immune-cold" (Clusters B, C). Immune-hot tumors are co-enriched for endothelial cells. Immune-cold tumors show: upregulated VEGF and hypoxia pathways, reduced endothelial cell adhesion proteins, increased glycolytic pathway components, and elevated phosphorylation of cell junction proteins.

**Detail:** Immune-cold tumors have higher glycolytic enzyme expression at both RNA and protein levels, with functional phosphosites identified on glycolytic components. Reduced endothelial adhesion proteins (cadherins, integrins) in immune-cold tumors suggests defective immune cell trafficking through tumor vasculature. Upregulated VEGF/hypoxia creates an immunosuppressive microenvironment that further restricts CD8+ T-cell infiltration. Cell junction phosphorylation enrichment in immune-cold tumors — proteins involved in tight junctions and adherens junctions — may create physical barriers to immune cell penetration. CD8+ T-cell infiltration (immune-hot) is associated with better clinical outcomes; VEGF/hypoxia pathway activity is associated with worse survival. This mechanistic framework suggests that combining anti-VEGF therapy with immune checkpoint inhibitors could convert immune-cold to immune-hot PDAC.

### F5: 27 proteins robustly upregulated in PDAC vs. normal ductal epithelium — 12 secreted as early detection candidates; LOXL2 survival predictor

**Finding:** Differential protein abundance analysis (high purity tumors vs. NATs, adjusted for stromal content) identifies 27 proteins >2-fold upregulated in PDAC, of which 21 are also upregulated vs. normal ductal tissues and in early-stage tumors. Twelve are secreted proteins with potential as serum/pancreatic juice biomarkers. LOXL2 protein abundance independently associates with shorter overall survival. Fourteen of 21 candidates validated by DIA mass spectrometry.

**Detail:** Known markers confirmed: THBS2, LAMC2 (previously elevated in PDAC sera). Novel markers reported for first time at protein level: GSDMB (gasdermin B) and LECT2. LOXL2 (lysyl oxidase-like 2), an enzyme involved in ECM crosslinking and metastasis promotion, shows robust protein upregulation only previously supported by RNA or cell line evidence in the Pancreatic Cancer Database — now validated at the protein level with survival correlation. PTM analysis reveals that N-linked glycosylation on APOD is associated with better overall survival while APOD protein abundance is not — illustrating the independent prognostic value of specific glycoforms. Phosphosites on PIGR (better prognosis) and ERRFI1 (worse survival) provide additional PTM-based prognostic markers independent of protein abundance. This 27-protein panel represents the most rigorously validated PDAC protein biomarker set to date.

**Genes / cancer types:** `LOXL2`, `THBS2`, `LAMC2`, `GSDMB`, `LECT2`, `HK2`, `COL12A1`, `TSPAN1`, `MDK`, `MUC5AC`, `APOD` · Pancreatic Ductal Adenocarcinoma

## Open Questions

- Can the PAK1/PAK2 inhibitor strategy (combined with KRAS downstream MAPK/PI3K inhibition) overcome KRAS-driven drug resistance in prospective PDAC trials?
- Do KRAS mutation-specific kinase profiles (G12D vs. G12V vs. G12R) predict differential responses to specific kinase inhibitors, enabling variant-stratified treatment?
- Can glycosylation enzyme inhibitors (anti-ST6GAL1, anti-FUT3) reduce CA19-9 and sialylated glycan levels to sensitize PDAC to immune recognition?
- Do the 12 secreted proteins upregulated in early-stage PDAC (including GSDMB, LECT2) perform as early detection markers in serum from at-risk populations?
- Can anti-VEGF therapy (bevacizumab) convert immune-cold PDAC to immune-hot to enable checkpoint inhibitor efficacy — as suggested by the endothelial cell/immune colocalization finding?
- What is the functional significance of CEACAM6 upregulation in KRAS G12D/G12V tumors specifically — does it mediate immune escape through direct T-cell suppression?

## Limitations

- 35 of 140 tumors categorized as "low purity" due to high acinar/stromal content; these were retained for TME analyses but excluded from neoplastic-cell-specific analyses
- Median RNA-protein correlation of 0.35 — lower than other CPTAC cancer types — reflects greater cell-type heterogeneity in PDAC
- Predominantly resectable (Stage I–III) tumors; metastatic PDAC underrepresented (n=9 Stage IV), limiting generalizability to advanced disease
- Glycoproteomic data not in cBioPortal — major limitation for the largest novel finding in this study
- No tumor purity-adjusted protein analysis for low-purity samples; stromal signals confound direct tumor protein comparisons in ~25% of cases
- KRAS G12C (the one druggable KRAS variant) is rare in PDAC (~1%); most kinase inhibitor targets are indirect (PAK, AKT) and require combination strategies
- Normal pancreatic duct tissues only 9 samples — limits statistical power for ductal-specific normal comparisons
- Survival data represent surgical cases only (good prognosis selected); true PDAC survival impact likely underestimated
