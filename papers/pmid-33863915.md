## Summary

This study characterized the frequency and genomic context of *TERT* somatic alterations in 60 metaplastic breast cancers (MBCs) with distinct predominant histologic components (squamous 23%, spindle 27%, osseous 8%, chondroid 42%), using whole-exome sequencing (n=27), MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing of 341–468 genes (n=17), and Sanger sequencing (n=16). *TERT* promoter hotspot mutations (predominantly C228T) were found in 15% (9/60) and *TERT* gene amplification in 2% (1/60), for an overall *TERT* alteration rate of 17%. *TERT* alterations were absent from all chondroid MBCs (0/25 vs. 10/35 non-chondroid, p=0.005), were significantly enriched for co-occurring *PIK3CA* mutations (83% vs. 13%, p=0.001), and were mutually exclusive with *TP53* mutations (p<0.001), highlighting the molecular heterogeneity of MBCs and identifying a clinically important *TERT*-driven subset.

## Key Findings

### F1: TERT promoter C228T hotspot mutation and gene amplification found in 17% of MBCs, enriched in non-chondroid subtypes

**Finding:** *TERT* somatic genetic alterations were identified in 17% (10/60) of MBCs: 9 cases harbored the C228T promoter hotspot mutation and 1 had *TERT* gene amplification. *TERT* alterations were completely absent from MBCs with predominant chondroid differentiation (0/25) compared to 10/35 (29%) non-chondroid MBCs (p=0.005, Fisher's exact test).

**Detail:** The C228T *TERT* promoter mutation creates a new ETS transcription factor binding motif, leading to upregulated telomerase activity and enhanced proliferative capacity. MBCs with chondroid predominance are also known to lack *PIK3CA* and RAS pathway mutations, suggesting they represent a genetically distinct entity from non-chondroid MBCs. Two of the 10 *TERT*-altered cases displayed minor areas of chondroid differentiation (one predominantly spindle, one predominantly osseous), indicating that strict histologic classification may miss some *TERT*-altered cases. Overall *TERT* alteration rate of 17% is consistent with a prior report (Krings and Chen, 25%), and both studies agree on enrichment in squamous/spindle subtypes.

### F2: TERT alterations are mutually exclusive with TP53 mutations in MBCs

**Finding:** In a formal mutual exclusivity analysis (CoMEt) on the full cohort of 60 MBCs, *TERT* somatic genetic alterations were significantly mutually exclusive with *TP53* mutations (p<0.001). Among the 44 MBCs subjected to WES or MSK-IMPACT sequencing, *TP53* mutations were present in 50% (3/6) of *TERT*-altered vs. 89% (34/38) of *TERT* wild-type MBCs (p=0.04, Fisher's exact test).

**Detail:** *TP53* mutations are the most common alterations in MBC, found in ~89% of *TERT* wild-type cases. The mutual exclusivity between *TERT* and *TP53* alterations has been observed in other cancer types and may reflect epistatic interactions: *TERT* promoter mutations may promote oncogenesis in the context of intact TP53 function, while *TP53*-mutant tumors utilize alternative mechanisms of telomere maintenance or immortalization. Alternatively, this mutual exclusivity may be partly driven by the differential prevalence of these alterations across histologic subtypes (e.g., chondroid MBCs have high *TP53* rates and no *TERT* alterations). The distinction has potential therapeutic implications since TP53-pathway status may modulate sensitivity to DNA-damaging agents and telomerase inhibitors.

### F3: TERT-altered MBCs are strongly enriched for co-occurring PIK3CA hotspot mutations

**Finding:** Among 44 MBCs with WES or MSK-IMPACT sequencing, *PIK3CA* clonal mutations were present in 83% (5/6) of *TERT*-altered MBCs vs. 13% (5/38) of *TERT* wild-type MBCs (p=0.001, Fisher's exact test). PIK3CA mutations predominantly affected canonical hotspots and were clonal in *TERT*-altered cases.

**Detail:** The co-occurrence of *TERT* promoter mutations and *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations defines a molecularly distinct subgroup of MBCs with therapeutic implications. PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway inhibitors have shown activity in PIK3CA-mutant triple-negative breast cancer, and the enrichment of this mutation in *TERT*-altered non-chondroid MBCs suggests this subgroup may benefit from mTOR inhibitor combinations. One prior study found that PI3K pathway-altered advanced triple-negative MBCs had significantly higher response rates to mTOR inhibitors (temsirolimus or everolimus) combined with liposomal doxorubicin and bevacizumab. *HRAS* hotspot mutations (Q61 codons) were also found in three *TERT*-altered MBCs, suggesting additional RAS pathway co-alterations in this subset.

### F4: Homologous recombination DNA repair defect (HRD) signatures dominant in 60% of MBCs

**Finding:** Among 38 MBCs with sufficient SNVs for mutational signature analysis, 60% (23/38) displayed dominant COSMIC signatures 3 and 8, associated with homologous recombination DNA repair deficiency (HRD). Aging signatures 1 and 5 were dominant in 34% (13/38), APOBEC signature 2 in 3% (1/38), and signature 17 of unknown etiology in 3% (1/38). No statistically significant difference in HRD signature frequency was found between *TERT*-altered and *TERT* wild-type MBCs.

**Detail:** The high frequency of HRD signatures in MBCs is clinically significant because HRD predicts sensitivity to platinum-based chemotherapy and PARP inhibitors. The SigMA algorithm used here was previously validated for FFPE samples analyzed by MSK-IMPACT, making these results clinically actionable. Notably, HRD signature prevalence in MBCs (60%) is substantially higher than in standard triple-negative breast cancer, suggesting that platinum-based regimens and PARP inhibitor trials should be explored specifically in MBC. The lack of difference in HRD frequency between *TERT*-altered and wild-type MBCs indicates that HRD is a pan-MBC feature independent of TERT status.

### F5: TERT-altered MBCs have lower fraction of genome altered than TERT wild-type MBCs

**Finding:** MBCs harboring *TERT* genetic alterations (n=5 with FGA data) had a significantly lower fraction of genome altered (FGA; median 22%, range 9–51%) compared to *TERT* wild-type MBCs (median 54%, range 20–86%; p=0.002, Mann-Whitney U test). Tumor mutation burden was similar between groups (median 3.1 vs. 3.5 mutations/Mb; p=0.72).

**Detail:** Lower genomic instability (as measured by FGA) in *TERT*-altered MBCs, despite comparable TMB, suggests these tumors may arise through a distinct oncogenic pathway with less chromosomal instability than typical MBC. This finding may have implications for the mechanism of *TERT* promoter mutation-driven oncogenesis — telomere elongation driven by *TERT* upregulation may stabilize chromosomes and reduce CNA accumulation relative to TP53-mutant tumors. The similar TMB between *TERT*-altered and wild-type MBCs argues against *TERT* alterations creating a hypermutator phenotype.

## Open Questions

- Do *TERT* promoter mutations in MBC predict sensitivity to telomerase inhibitors (e.g., imetelstat) or have other direct therapeutic implications?
- Is the mutual exclusivity of *TERT* and *TP53* in MBC reflective of true epistatic interaction or driven by histologic subtype confounding?
- Do *TERT*-altered, *PIK3CA*-co-mutated MBCs have better clinical outcomes or distinct responses to mTOR inhibitor combinations?
- Can *TERT* promoter mutation be used to distinguish MBC from malignant phyllodes tumor in diagnostically challenging cases?
- What is the evolutionary relationship between *TERT*-mutant MBCs and breast adenomyoepitheliomas, which share *TERT* and *PIK3CA* alterations?
- What are the survival and treatment response differences between *TERT*-altered and wild-type MBC patients?

## Limitations

- Small sample size (n=60); the study is underpowered to detect survival differences associated with *TERT* status
- Not all 60 MBCs could undergo WES or MSK-IMPACT sequencing due to limited DNA yield; 16 cases had only Sanger sequencing of targeted hotspots
- Sanger sequencing in 16 cases cannot detect *TERT* gene amplification, making the estimate of amplification frequency conservative
- Multi-institutional cohort prevents definitive survival analysis due to incomplete clinical follow-up data
- Mutual exclusivity of *TERT* and *TP53* may be confounded by histologic subtype distributions
- No RNA-seq or transcriptomic data; TERT expression level and telomerase activity not directly measured
- Study includes samples from both FFPE and fresh frozen sources; technical batch effects may influence some molecular analyses
