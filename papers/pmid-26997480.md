## Summary

WES and RNA-seq analysis of 38 pretreatment metastatic melanoma biopsies from patients treated with anti-PD-1 (pembrolizumab or nivolumab), including 21 responders and 17 non-responders. High mutational load does not separate responders from non-responders but significantly associates with improved overall survival. *BRCA2* loss-of-function mutations are significantly enriched in responding tumors (28% vs. 6%; OR=6.2, p=0.002), likely through increased mutational load and possibly via immunogenic cell death. Non-responding tumors are characterized by a 26-signature transcriptional program (IPRES — Innate anti-PD-1 Resistance) co-enriched in ~1/3 of metastatic melanomas and in a subset of tumors across all major cancer types, featuring upregulation of mesenchymal transition, angiogenesis, hypoxia, and wound-healing genes. MAPK inhibitor (MAPKi) treatment induces IPRES-like signatures, suggesting a mechanism for cross-resistance between MAPKi and anti-PD-1 therapies. Data in `mel_ucla_2016` and `mel_iatlas_hugo_ucla_2016` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: High mutational load predicts improved survival but not objective tumor response to anti-PD-1

**Finding:** Patients with tumors in the top third of mutational load have significantly improved overall survival after anti-PD-1 therapy compared to those in the bottom third. However, responding and non-responding tumors do not differ significantly in median nsSNV counts (495 vs. 281, p=0.30). Critically, responding tumors with low mutational loads have better survival than non-responding tumors with high mutational loads — demonstrating that factors beyond mutation burden determine response patterns.

**Detail:** Median nsSNVs across 38 tumors: 489 (range 73–3,985). HLA class I neoepitope loads also do not significantly differ between responders and non-responders (231 vs. 156, p=0.41). The Snyder et al. tetrapeptide neoantigen signature (from CTLA-4 studies) is not enriched among anti-PD-1 responders in this cohort. High mutational load may augment the prognostic benefit of anti-PD-1 therapy beyond its baseline prognostic value: high TMB trends toward improved survival even without anti-PD-1 in TCGA melanoma, but the effect is amplified with anti-PD-1 therapy.

**Genes / cancer types:** (Genome-wide TMB) · Metastatic melanoma

### F2: BRCA2 loss-of-function mutations enriched in anti-PD-1 responding melanomas

**Finding:** *BRCA2* non-synonymous mutations are present in 28% of responding tumors vs. 6% of non-responding tumors (Fisher exact p=0.002, OR=6.2, FDR-corrected). The background *BRCA2* mutation rate in melanoma is 6% (469 TCGA melanomas). All *BRCA2* mutations affect critical functional domains (NPM1-interacting region, POLH-interacting domain, FANCD2-interacting helical domain), consistent with loss-of-function. *BRCA2*-mutated melanomas have significantly higher total mutational loads than wild-type (validated in two independent cohorts).

**Detail:** The enrichment of *BRCA2* mutations among responders likely operates through at least two mechanisms: (1) BRCA2 LOF causes defective homologous recombination and double-strand DNA break repair, increasing somatic mutation accumulation and neoantigen load; (2) cellular stress from defective DNA repair may independently trigger immunogenic cell death or altered antigen presentation. No recurrent copy number alterations distinguish responder from non-responder groups. *BRCA2* is the only gene reaching FDR-significant enrichment in either response group.

### F3: IPRES transcriptional signature defines innate anti-PD-1 resistance across melanoma and pan-cancer

**Finding:** A coordinated set of 26 transcriptomic signatures (IPRES — Innate anti-PD-1 RESistance) is co-enriched en bloc in 9/13 non-responding vs. 1/15 responding pre-treatment tumors (Fisher p=0.013, OR=4.6 for over-representation among non-responders). IPRES is present in ~1/3 of metastatic melanomas across four independent cohorts (126/384 total) and in a subset of all major cancer types tested (lung, colon, kidney, pancreatic adenocarcinoma), where it constitutes the majority of pancreatic tumors.

**Detail:** IPRES content includes signatures of: mesenchymal transition (AXL, ROR2, WNT5A, LOXL2, TWIST2, TAGLN, FAP upregulation; CDH1 downregulation), angiogenesis (VEGFA, VEGFC), wound healing, hypoxia, and monocyte/macrophage chemotaxis (CCL2, CCL7, CCL8, CCL13). Notably, IPRES includes MAPKi-induced signatures in melanoma, establishing a mechanistic link: MAPKi therapy (BRAF/MEK inhibitors) induces the same mesenchymal-invasive-angiogenic phenotype seen in innately anti-PD-1-resistant tumors, explaining clinical observations of cross-resistance. IPRES is distinct from anti-CTLA-4 resistance (not enriched in CTLA-4 non-responders). Only 6/69 primary cutaneous melanomas show IPRES vs. 90/282 metastatic melanomas, consistent with EMT and metastasis signatures in IPRES.

**Genes / cancer types:** AXL, ROR2, WNT5A, LOXL2, TWIST2, VEGFA, VEGFC, CDH1, CCL2, IL10, FAP · Metastatic melanoma, pan-cancer

### F4: MAPKi treatment induces IPRES-like signatures — predicting cross-resistance to anti-PD-1

**Finding:** MAPK-targeted therapy (BRAFi/MEKi) of melanoma tumors and cell lines induces transcriptome-wide reprogramming that includes the same IPRES signatures (mesenchymal transition, angiogenesis, wound healing). This MAPKi-induced phenotype switch is observed in residual patient tumors during MAPKi treatment, providing a mechanistic basis for clinical observations that prior MAPKi therapy negatively affects subsequent anti-PD-1 response.

**Detail:** Retrospective clinical analyses had previously suggested that prior MAPKi failure was a negative predictor for subsequent anti-PD-1 response. Acquired MAPKi resistance (after BRAF/MEK inhibition) correlates with depletion of intra-tumoral T cells, CD8 T cell exhaustion, and loss of antigen presentation — all immunosuppressive phenotypes. The IPRES overlap between MAPKi-induced signatures and innate anti-PD-1 resistance suggests that targeting the IPRES-related biological processes (e.g., AXL/WNT5A-driven mesenchymal transition, VEGFA-driven angiogenesis) may improve anti-PD-1 combination therapy response rates.

### F5: T cell marker and checkpoint gene expression do not distinguish responders in pre-treatment biopsies

**Finding:** Key T cell-related genes (CD8A/B, PD-L1, LAG3, CTLA4, IFNG, GZMA, PRF1) and interferon gamma signatures are not significantly differentially expressed between pre-treatment responding and non-responding tumors in this cohort. This contrasts with anti-CTLA-4 studies where GZMA, PRF1, PD-L2, and CTLA4 were enriched in responders.

**Detail:** The lack of T cell gene signature differentiation may reflect: (1) the small cohort size (n=28 RNA-seq samples) and relaxed statistical thresholds; (2) mechanistic differences between anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 therapy; (3) the fact that innate anti-PD-1 resistance (IPRES) operates through a distinct, non-T-cell-based mechanism (mesenchymal/angiogenic immunosuppression). HLA class I gene expression (HLA-A, -B, -C) trends higher in responding tumors but does not reach significance. Homozygous PTEN deletion is present in only one non-responder, precluding analysis of PTEN-mediated resistance in this dataset.

**Genes / cancer types:** CD8A, CD8B, PD-L1 (CD274), LAG3, IFNG, GZMA, PRF1, PTEN, HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C · Metastatic melanoma

## Open Questions

- Can IPRES status be measured non-invasively (e.g., from circulating tumor DNA or plasma RNA) to prospectively stratify patients before anti-PD-1 therapy?
- Does targeted inhibition of IPRES pathway components (AXL, WNT5A, VEGFA) in combination with anti-PD-1 overcome innate resistance in clinical trials?
- Is the *BRCA2*-mutation-associated anti-PD-1 sensitivity a neoantigen-load effect, or does defective DNA repair independently activate innate immune signaling (cGAS-STING)?
- Why does IPRES predict anti-PD-1 but not anti-CTLA-4 resistance — do the two checkpoint blockade mechanisms engage distinct tumor cell-intrinsic resistance programs?
- Does de novo MAPKi resistance (acquired during therapy) convert IPRES-negative tumors to IPRES-positive, and can monitoring IPRES signature on re-biopsy guide switching to or adding anti-PD-1?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (38 WES, 28 RNA-seq); statistical analyses required relaxed thresholds for gene-level differential expression (no FDR correction yielded only 3 DEGs)
- HLA typing and neoepitope prediction based on computational methods (ATHLATES, NetMHCpan); no experimental validation of immunogenic neoantigens
- IPRES validation in pan-cancer used TCGA data (primary tumors) rather than matched therapy cohorts, so predictive value in non-melanoma cancers is inferential
- Prior MAPKi exposure was an uncontrolled variable (14/38 patients had prior MAPKi), potentially confounding response associations
- Cohort is limited to cutaneous metastatic melanoma; generalizability to uveal melanoma, mucosal melanoma, or other tumor types requires separate validation
