## Summary

This 2024 study reports the real-world implementation of CSF circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) sequencing using the FDA-authorized MSK-IMPACT™ platform across 1,007 CSF samples from 711 patients with CNS tumors at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center (November 2018 – November 2022). Genetic alterations were detected in 53% (489/922) of CSF samples from patients with known CNS disease, and no alterations were found in any of the 85 samples from patients without CNS involvement (100% specificity). CSF ctDNA positivity was associated with a three-fold increased risk of death (HR 3.23, P < 0.001). Detected alterations were consistent with tumor-type-specific landscapes; 50.7% of ctDNA-positive samples harbored OncoKB Level 1 actionable alterations. In NSCLC patients, serial CSF sampling captured the emergence of acquired EGFR resistance mutations (T790M, C797S, L718Q, L718V, G724S), amplifications in MET and EGFR, and off-target resistance alterations. CSF VAFs were significantly higher than matched plasma or tumor tissue. This study is the primary data source for the `csf_msk_2024` cBioPortal study.

## Key Findings

### F1: CSF ctDNA sequencing detects genetic alterations in 53% of CNS disease samples with 100% specificity in patients without CNS involvement

**Finding:** Among 922 CSF samples from patients with clinical CNS disease, 489 (53%) were ctDNA-positive (harbored ≥1 somatic alteration). All 85 samples from patients without any documented CNS involvement were negative — establishing 100% specificity.

**Detail:** A total of 7,110 somatic alterations were identified across 489 ctDNA-positive samples: 3,944 non-synonymous mutations, 2,980 copy number alterations, and 186 structural rearrangements. Median of 4 mutations per sample (IQR 2–8). Median VAF was 38.7% (IQR 23.2–51.1%), reflecting the low background of non-neoplastic cfDNA in CSF. GI cancers had the highest ctDNA positivity rate and highest VAFs; embryonal tumors had the lowest. *TP53* was the most frequently altered gene (49% of ctDNA-positive samples), consistent with its pan-cancer prevalence.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `EGFR`, `IDH1`, `PIK3CA`, `KRAS`, `BRAF`, `ERBB2`, `MET` · lung, breast, glioma, melanoma, GI and all CNS-involving tumor types

### F2: 50.7% of ctDNA-positive CSF samples carry OncoKB Level 1 actionable alterations; lung cancer has highest actionability

**Finding:** Of 489 ctDNA-positive samples, 248 (50.7%) harbored a Level 1 OncoKB actionable alteration. Lung carcinomas had the highest Level 1 actionability due to the large number of FDA-approved precision oncology drugs in NSCLC. Resistance-predictive alterations (OncoKB R1/R2) were also most common in lung cancer.

**Detail:** The actionability distribution in CSF was broadly comparable to the AACR GENIE MSK cohort (n = 47,271 solid tumors sequenced from tissue), validating the clinical representativeness of CSF ctDNA profiling. In addition to therapeutic guidance, genomic profiles from CSF resolved tumor subclassification, established clonal relatedness to known malignancies, and in some cases identified previously unsuspected tumors. Mutational signatures (UV in cutaneous melanoma, smoking in lung adenocarcinoma) assisted in tumor-of-origin determination.

**Genes / cancer types:** `EGFR`, `ALK`, `ROS1`, `RET`, `MET`, `ERBB2`, `BRAF`, `PIK3CA`, `IDH1`, `KRAS` · lung, breast, glioma, melanoma

### F3: ctDNA positivity in CSF is associated with three-fold increased risk of death across all tumor types

**Finding:** Detection of a genetic alteration in CSF was associated with a 3.23-fold increased risk of death (HR 3.23; 95% CI 2.58–4.05; P < 0.001). Median survival was 854 days shorter in ctDNA-positive patients (235 days vs. 1,089 days).

**Detail:** The survival association was consistent across tumor subtypes (except embryonal and GI cancers), with no statistically significant heterogeneity (P = 0.13 interaction term). In NSCLC specifically, ctDNA-positive patients had significantly shorter survival than ctDNA-negative patients. ctDNA positivity likely reflects active, shedding CNS disease — potentially leptomeningeal involvement — rather than incidental tumor DNA. This prognostic signal supports use of CSF ctDNA positivity as a surrogate marker of disease burden and as an endpoint in clinical trials.

**Genes / cancer types:** All CNS-involving tumor types (lung, breast, glioma, melanoma, GI, others)

### F4: Serial CSF sampling captures clonal evolution and emergence of resistance mutations in real time

**Finding:** In NSCLC patients with EGFR sensitizing mutations, repeated CSF sequencing captured the sequential emergence of multiple distinct EGFR resistance mutations (*T790M*, *C797S*, *L718Q*, *L718V*, *G724S*) and amplifications in *MET* and *EGFR*, demonstrating real-time monitoring of resistance evolution.

**Detail:** 150/711 patients (21.1%) had multiple CSF samples (median 2, range 2–12). Among lung cancer patients, driver alterations initially detected in tumor tissue were universally detected in CSF. Resistance mechanisms captured included EGFR gatekeeper mutations (T790M to first-generation inhibitors; C797S, L718Q, L718V, G724S to third-generation inhibitors), MET amplification, and off-target alterations in BRAF fusions, KRAS, and PIK3CA. ALK-positive patients showed ALK resistance mutations (G1202R, G1269A) and MET exon 14 skipping patients showed MET Y1230N. These findings directly guided therapeutic decision-making in individual patients.

**Genes / cancer types:** `EGFR`, `MET`, `ERBB2`, `ALK`, `BRAF`, `KRAS`, `PIK3CA` · NSCLC, CNS metastases/leptomeningeal disease

### F5: CSF ctDNA VAF is significantly higher than plasma and tumor tissue; sample volume ≥5 mL optimizes detection

**Finding:** Among matched pairs, CSF ctDNA VAFs were significantly higher than in paired plasma (CSF median 36.4% vs. plasma 2.3%, P < 0.01) and tumor tissue (CSF median 32% vs. tissue 24%, P < 0.01). ctDNA positivity rates were critically impacted by sample volume below 2 mL and increased substantially at ≥5 mL.

**Detail:** 56 CSF–tissue pairs showed 43% of alterations shared, 27% private to CSF, and 29% private to tissue. For Level 1–3A OncoKB alterations, 77% were shared. TMB in CSF and tissue correlated strongly (r = 0.81, P < 0.001). For 31 CSF–plasma pairs (MSK-IMPACT vs. MSK-ACCESS), 54% of alterations were shared overall, 75% for Level 1–3A. The high CSF VAF reflects absence of hematopoietic cfDNA dilution (unlike plasma). Volume-positivity relationship: rates of 8.3%/20.0% (primary/metastatic) for <2 mL vs. 37.8%/68.6% at ≥5 mL. Collection in Streck tubes and processing within 14 days were confirmed as key preanalytic requirements.

**Genes / cancer types:** All CNS-involving tumor types

## Open Questions

- Can CSF ctDNA TMB serve as a reliable companion diagnostic for immune checkpoint inhibitor eligibility in patients with CNS metastases where tumor tissue is unavailable?
- What is the optimal serial sampling frequency in patients on targeted CNS therapy, and can ctDNA kinetics (rising VAF) predict clinical progression before radiographic evidence?
- Why do GI cancers show the highest CSF ctDNA positivity and VAF — does this reflect more frequent leptomeningeal spread, greater tumor shedding per cell, or a distinct biology?
- Does the 27% of alterations private to CSF (not seen in tumor tissue) represent true spatial heterogeneity, CNS-specific subclones, or technical sampling differences?
- Would CSF ctDNA sequencing in patients with parenchymal-only brain metastases (no leptomeningeal involvement) change clinical management compared to prior tissue biopsy?

## Limitations

- Retrospective real-world study; no standardized treatment protocol or systematic clinical outcome tracking beyond OS.
- 53% detection rate means nearly half of patients with CNS disease had uninformative CSF results; factors driving false-negativity (deep parenchymal disease, poor shedding) are not fully characterized.
- Comparison of CSF to tumor tissue and plasma based on small convenience subsets (56 and 31 pairs, respectively), not a prospectively designed comparative study.
- Lumbar puncture — while minimally invasive — is not without risk and may not always reflect intracranial tumor biology, particularly for supratentorial parenchymal lesions with intact CSF barriers.
- MSK-IMPACT panel captures selected genes; whole-genome or whole-exome CSF profiling may reveal additional alterations not captured here.
- No validation cohort from an independent institution; single-center real-world experience may not generalize to settings without CLIA-certified cfDNA processing infrastructure.
