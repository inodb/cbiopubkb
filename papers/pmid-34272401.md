## Summary

Meta-analysis of exome/genome sequencing data from 105 cSCC tumors across 10 studies (88 after QC) defines a consensus catalog of 30 driver cancer genes in cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma — the second most common cancer in the US (~8,000 annual deaths), but one that was excluded from TCGA. By pooling data, reprocessing from raw files with uniform pipelines, and applying stringent QC including tumor cellularity-adjusted power calculations, the study overcomes the high UV mutational burden (~hundreds of mutations/Mb) that obscures driver/passenger distinction in individual studies. NOTCH pathway mutations are found in 80% and p53 pathway in 71% of tumors. Four novel driver genes — *EP300*, *PBRM1*, *USP28*, and *CHUK* — each mutated in >10% of cases are identified for the first time, with strong functional support. Five biologically distinct cSCC subtypes are resolved by mutational signature analysis.

## Key Findings

### F1: NOTCH pathway (80%) and p53 pathway (71%) are near-ubiquitous and defining features of cSCC

**Finding:** Loss-of-function mutations in NOTCH pathway genes occur in 80% of cSCC tumors, and p53 pathway mutations in 71% — the two highest-frequency pathway alterations. These are present across all cSCC subtypes regardless of UV burden, immunosuppression status, or underlying genetic disorder, establishing them as the molecular hallmarks of the disease.

**Detail:** NOTCH1 and NOTCH2 are the primary NOTCH pathway drivers and were nominated in a majority of prior studies as well as in this meta-analysis. TP53 dominates the p53 pathway; CDKN2A (deletion) affects 39% via cell-cycle checkpoint control and also impacts p53 indirectly. TERT promoter mutations — not captured in exome studies but documented separately — are also highly prevalent. Ubiquity of NOTCH and p53 pathway disruption suggests they occur early, possibly in pre-malignant UV-exposed keratinocytes, preceding neoplastic transformation.

**Genes / cancer types:** `NOTCH1`, `NOTCH2`, `TP53`, `CDKN2A` · Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma

### F2: Four novel driver genes (EP300, PBRM1, USP28, CHUK) each mutated in >10% of cSCC

**Finding:** Using meta-analysis statistical power unavailable to individual studies, four previously unrecognized cSCC driver genes are confidently identified with mutation frequencies ≥10%: *EP300* (histone acetyltransferase, NOTCH co-activator), *PBRM1* (SWI/SNF complex subunit), *USP28* (deubiquitinase, DNA damage response), and *CHUK* (IKKα, NFκB/keratinocyte differentiation).

**Detail:** *EP300* harbors frequent loss-of-function mutations including missense mutations clustered in the histone acetyltransferase (HAT) domain, functionally confirmed to eliminate HAT activity; EP300 is a critical NOTCH transcriptional co-activator, placing its loss functionally within the NOTCH pathway. *PBRM1*, a canonical tumor suppressor in clear cell renal carcinoma and other cancers, shows deleterious mutations throughout its length; *ARID2* (another SWI/SNF member) is also a driver, making SWI/SNF disruption present in 38% of cSCC. *USP28* mutations are predominantly truncating; USP28 stabilizes DNA repair proteins and mediates Chk2–p53–PUMA apoptotic signaling. *CHUK* (IKKα) knockout mice and humans display skin differentiation failure; a transposon screen links Chuk loss to keratinocyte tumorigenesis in Pten-sensitized mice; biallelic somatic inactivation is confirmed in multiple tumors.

**Genes / cancer types:** `EP300`, `PBRM1`, `USP28`, `CHUK`, `ARID2`, `CREBBP` · Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma

### F3: Five biologically distinct cSCC subtypes defined by mutational signature profiles

**Finding:** Mutational signature decomposition resolves five cSCC subtypes: (1) sporadic UV-induced tumors (canonical UV signature, high TMB), (2) xeroderma pigmentosum (XP) tumors (high TMB, distinct trinucleotide context from canonical signature 7 due to XPC deficiency), (3) azathioprine-treated immunosuppressed patients (Signature 32, highest TMB), (4) other immunosuppressed patients (UV-dominant, lower TMB than azathioprine group), (5) recessive dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa (RDEB) tumors (low TMB, APOBEC-mediated mutagenesis).

**Detail:** XP tumors lack the canonical Signature 7 (SBS7) because their XPC-deficient NER produces UV mutations in different trinucleotide contexts — revealing that the COSMIC signature catalog remains incomplete. Azathioprine is both immunosuppressive and a potent mutagen (Signature 32); patients on other immunosuppressive regimens have ~UV-only profiles and lower TMB. RDEB tumors are chronically inflamed and blistering, selecting for *CASP8* mutations (apoptosis escape from inflammatory cytokines); RDEB tumors are enriched for CASP8 alterations relative to other subtypes (statistically significant). Sporadic tumors share common mutations with UV-exposed normal skin, placing tumor-initiating mutations in the field of normal tissue.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CASP8`, `XPC` · Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (all subtypes)

### F4: Recurrent pathways disrupted: Hippo (37%), SWI/SNF (38%), MAPK/PI3K (31%), cell cycle (39%)

**Finding:** Beyond NOTCH and p53, four additional pathway-level alterations are recurrently disrupted in 30–40% of cSCC: cell-cycle checkpoint control (39%, primarily *CDKN2A*), SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling (38%, *PBRM1* + *ARID2* + others), Hippo pathway (37%, *AJUBA*, *YAP1*), and MAPK/PI3K (31%, *HRAS*, *KRAS*, *PIK3CA*, *PTEN*, *MTOR*). A stress-response cluster (*CASP8*, *CHUK*, *NFE2L2*) is altered in 33%.

**Detail:** *AJUBA* shows biallelic inactivation (focal heterozygous deletion + point mutation on the remaining allele in one tumor), confirming it as a recessive driver. Focal amplifications of *CCND1*, *MDM2*, *YAP1*, and *RAP1B* are identified. The *RAP1B* amplification is accompanied by a RAS-activating point mutation on the amplified allele, suggesting a novel mechanism of Ras pathway activation. *HRAS* is mutated in ~30% of cases (consistent with prior studies), making it the dominant Ras family driver in cSCC (vs KRAS in most other cancers). Immunosuppressed (non-azathioprine) and sporadic subgroups do not significantly differ in pathway-level alteration frequencies after multiple-hypothesis correction.

**Genes / cancer types:** `HRAS`, `KRAS`, `PIK3CA`, `PTEN`, `MTOR`, `AJUBA`, `YAP1`, `CCND1`, `MDM2`, `RAP1B`, `NFE2L2`, `EZH2` · Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma

### F5: Statistical power analysis reveals cSCC driver gene discovery is far from saturated

**Finding:** Power calculations demonstrate that even this largest-to-date cSCC meta-analysis (88 tumors) can only detect genes mutated in ≥15% of tumors through statistical significance testing alone. Many real driver genes mutated at lower frequencies remain undiscovered. This study partially compensates by leveraging cancerhotspots.org pan-cancer hotspot overlap, but many low-frequency drivers still escape detection.

**Detail:** The four cancer gene discovery algorithms (MutSig, dN/dS, LOFsigrank, OncodriveFML) collectively nominated only 12 genes with any significance, with 7 supported by ≥2 tools. The hotspot overlay added additional candidates (e.g., *KRAS*, *EZH2*, *MTOR*, *PIK3CA* at established pan-cancer hotspots). Low-frequency focal CNAs identified further candidates (*AJUBA*, *RAP1B*). By contrast, 34 TCGA cancer types, with hundreds of samples each, have substantially saturated their driver catalogs at ≥1–2% frequency. The cSCC gap is especially consequential because immune checkpoint blockade fails ~50% of patients, and immunosuppressed patients (organ transplant recipients, ~100-fold elevated risk) cannot receive immunotherapy — making alternative targeted therapy imperative.

## Open Questions

- What are the driver genes mutated in <15% of cSCC? A prospective collection of 500+ tumors would be needed for adequate power.
- Do NOTCH pathway mutations occur as the initiating event in UV-exposed normal skin keratinocyte clones, preceding malignant transformation?
- Why does EP300 loss — a NOTCH co-activator — co-occur with NOTCH1/2 loss-of-function mutations rather than being mutually exclusive, as pathway convergence would predict?
- Can CHUK (IKKα) inhibition be exploited therapeutically in CHUK-mutant cSCC, and does CHUK loss sensitize tumors to specific immunologic or targeted agents?
- What is the clinical impact of azathioprine-associated Signature 32 mutations — do these tumors respond differently to immune checkpoint blockade?
- Are TERT promoter mutations early (UV-driven clonal expansion in normal skin) or late (tumorigenic) events relative to NOTCH and TP53 mutations?
- Do RDEB-associated cSCC (APOBEC-driven, low UV) require a distinct therapeutic approach from UV-driven sporadic cSCC?

## Limitations

- Exome-only in most contributing studies: non-coding drivers (TERT promoter, regulatory elements), structural variants, and viral integrations not systematically assessed
- Meta-analysis of heterogeneous cohorts: different capture kits, sequencing platforms, and center-specific biases despite uniform re-processing
- Only 88 tumors after QC — insufficient power to detect drivers mutated in <15% of cases by statistical methods alone
- Subclonal mutations filtered (≥40% of tumor cells required) to avoid contaminating normal keratinocyte clones; low-frequency clonal events may be systematically excluded
- No functional validation of most nominated drivers; CHUK and EP300 rely on prior literature and mouse models rather than direct cSCC functional experiments
- KNSTRN annotation error identified (promoter vs. coding mutation) — highlights ongoing risk of incorrect driver calls from annotation artifacts in high-TMB cancers
- Inter-study heterogeneity in patient populations (immunosuppressed, XP, RDEB, sporadic) means frequency estimates may not reflect the true population-level distribution
