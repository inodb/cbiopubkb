## Summary

Largest comprehensive genomic profiling (CGP) study of sarcomas to date, using the FoundationOne platform (465 genes + RNA-seq, 333 fusion genes) to sequence 7,494 sarcomas representing 44 histological subtypes from 2012–2018. This study catalogues the full landscape of pathogenic short variants, CNAs, rearrangements, MSI, TMB, and genomic loss of heterozygosity (gLOH) across sarcomas. Key findings: 10.5% of patients had diagnoses that could be refined or corrected by sequencing; 31.7% harbored potentially actionable alterations (OncoKB-defined); kinase fusions (ALK, NTRK, FGFR, RET, ROS1, BRAF) were present in 2.6% of all sarcomas; TMB was low overall (median 2.4 mut/Mb) with TMB≥10 in only 3.9%; MSI-H was rare (<0.3%); and high gLOH (≥19.3%) was found in 15% of sarcomas, largely unexplained by HRD gene mutations. A clinically annotated MSK subset of 118 patients validated genomic-matched treatment decisions in 29%.

## Key Findings

### F1: CGP identifies potential diagnostic errors or refinements in 10.5% of sarcoma patients

**Finding:** Sequencing results were discordant with submitted pathological diagnoses in 789/7,494 (10.5%) patients, with most reclassifications driven by presence or absence of pathognomonic translocations (e.g., SS18-SSX1/2/4 in synovial sarcoma, NAB2-STAT6 in solitary fibrous tumor) or specific copy number signatures (MDM2 amplification in liposarcoma).

**Detail:** Among 1,411 cases initially diagnosed as "sarcoma NOS," 8.9% (126 cases) could be reclassified to a specific subtype. Cases lacking expected pathognomonic fusions were termed "sarcoma, unclassified" (n=172). In the MSK subset of 118 patients, initial diagnosis was changed in 4% based on sequencing: two leiomyosarcomas reclassified as dedifferentiated liposarcoma (altered therapy to MDM2/CDK4 inhibitors), one sarcoma NOS reclassified as PEComa (TSC2 loss → mTOR inhibitor), one MPNST reclassified as synovial sarcoma (SS18-SSX2 → NY-ESO-1 T cell therapy). This finding highlights the diagnostic value of CGP, particularly outside sarcoma-expert centers.

**Genes / cancer types:** SS18 (SSX1/2/4 fusions), NAB2-STAT6, MDM2, TSC2, SMARCB1 · Synovial sarcoma, solitary fibrous tumor, liposarcoma, PEComa, MPNST, sarcoma NOS

### F2: p53 and Rb pathways co-altered in nearly half of all sarcomas; distinct from translocation-driven subtypes

**Finding:** The p53 pathway (TP53, MDM2, MDM4) was altered in 47.8% and Rb pathway (RB1, CDKN2A/B, CDK4/6, CCND1/2/3, CCNE1) in 46.8% of all sarcomas, with strong co-occurrence (OR=4.2, p<1.3×10⁻¹⁹⁶). Genomically complex sarcomas had far higher rates (p53: 58.2%, Rb: 56%) than translocation-associated sarcomas (p53: 12.9%, Rb: 15.8%).

**Detail:** TP53 alterations were mostly short variants (79.3%); CDKN2A/B were predominantly homozygous deletions (84.5% and 99.0%); MDM2 and CDK4 were typically amplified (>94% of alterations). Within each pathway, individual genes were mutually exclusive (TP53 vs. MDM2/4; RB1 vs. CDKN2A/B vs. CDK4), consistent with functional redundancy. Subtype-enriched alterations included SMARCB1 in epithelioid sarcoma (56%) and malignant rhabdoid tumor (60%); IDH1 in chondrosarcoma (22.9%); PIK3CA/PTEN/AKT in myxoid liposarcoma (36.3%/17.8%/3.7%); CDKN2A/B in MPNST (47.3%).

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, MDM2, RB1, CDKN2A, CDKN2B, CDK4, MDM4, CCND1/2/3, CCNE1 · All sarcoma subtypes

### F3: Kinase fusions in 2.6% of sarcomas represent actionable therapeutic targets

**Finding:** Potentially actionable kinase fusions—involving ALK, BRAF, FGFR1–4, NTRK1–3, RET, and ROS1—were identified in 2.6% (196/7,494) of all sarcomas, spanning diverse histologies including IMT (62.1%), MPNST (4.9%), UPS (3.6–4.3%), and LMS (1.9%).

**Detail:** A novel recurrent TNS1-ALK fusion was identified in 16 cases (predominantly uterine and non-uterine LMS), previously reported only in 1 uterine LMS. Multiple ALK breakpoints at exon 12 (near the glycine-rich domain) were identified. In the MSK clinical subset, a patient with an ETV6-NTRK3 fusion had a durable complete response to larotrectinib. NTRK amplification (vs. fusion) was non-actionable as demonstrated by a clinical trial. Overall, kinase fusions serve as the most directly targeted actionable alterations across sarcoma histologies.

**Genes / cancer types:** ALK (TNS1-ALK, others), NTRK1/2/3 (ETV6-NTRK3, others), FGFR1–4, RET, ROS1, BRAF · IMT, LMS, MPNST, UPS, sarcoma NOS

### F4: TMB is low across sarcomas (median 2.4 mut/Mb); MSI-H <0.3%; high gLOH in 15% not explained by HRD

**Finding:** Median TMB was 2.4 mut/Mb across all sarcomas; TMB≥10 mut/Mb was seen in only 3.9%; MSI-H (evaluable in 6,206 cases) in only 0.29% (18 cases). High gLOH (≥19.3%, ≥1 SD above mean) was observed in 15% of sarcomas, but only 5% of those with high gLOH had HRD gene alterations—indicating that high genomic scarring arises largely through non-HRD mechanisms (e.g., aneuploidy).

**Detail:** TMB≥10 was most frequent in angiosarcoma (15.0%), UPS (10.9%), and MPNST (10.8%). Pediatric/AYA patients (≤30 years) had significantly lower TMB and gLOH than adults across all subtypes. UV mutational signature was dominant in cutaneous angiosarcoma (35/41 evaluable) and associated with very high TMB (≥100 mut/Mb). Biallelic HRD gene alterations (BRCA1/2, PALB2, RAD51 paralogs) were most common in uterine leiomyosarcoma (4.8%) and angiosarcoma (2.0%). CD274 (PD-L1) amplification occurred in 1.0% of sarcomas.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, RAD51, MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, CD274 · Angiosarcoma, UPS, MPNST, LMS, osteosarcoma

### F5: Genomic profiling informed matched therapy in 29% of a clinically annotated MSK subset

**Finding:** In 118 MSK patients with documented clinical decisions following FoundationOne profiling, at least one alteration was deemed actionable by the treating physician in 47% (n=50), and 29% of the cohort (n=31/106 evaluable) was enrolled in a matched trial or received off-label FDA-approved therapy.

**Detail:** Case examples of clinical benefit included: SMARCB1-deleted sarcoma NOS → durable partial response to tazemetostat (EZH2 inhibitor); BRAF V600E sarcoma → rapid near-complete response to vemurafenib+trametinib; ATM-truncating mutation in osteosarcoma → durable stable disease >1 year with PARP+ATR inhibitor combination; high-TMB UPS (20 mut/Mb) → near-complete response to pembrolizumab; NTRK3 fusion IMT → durable complete response to larotrectinib. The gap between having an actionable alteration and receiving matched therapy reflects barriers: ineligibility, lack of trial access, late sequencing (median 1.1 years after needing systemic therapy).

**Genes / cancer types:** SMARCB1, BRAF V600E, ATM, NF1, NTRK3, TSC2 · Sarcoma NOS, osteosarcoma, IMT, PEComa, UPS

**Data availability:** `partial`

## Open Questions

1. What is the mechanism underlying high gLOH in 15% of sarcomas in the absence of HRD gene mutations—does chromothripsis or aneuploidy-driven LOH drive this pattern, and does it predict PARP inhibitor sensitivity?
2. Should kinase fusion testing (RNA-seq) become standard of care for all sarcomas, not just IMT, given the 2.6% actionable rate across diverse histologies?
3. Can TMB ≥10 mut/Mb predict checkpoint inhibitor benefit in sarcoma, and what is the appropriate cutoff within mesenchymal tumors where the immune microenvironment differs from carcinomas?
4. How should the 10.5% diagnostic reclassification rate influence practice—should all sarcoma biopsies undergo reflex NGS before initiating systemic therapy?
5. What are the resistance mechanisms to targeted therapies identified by CGP in sarcoma (e.g., RB1 loss with CDK4 inhibitors, TP53 loss with MDM2 inhibitors), and can serial liquid biopsy monitor their emergence?

## Limitations

- Tumor sequenced without matched normal DNA, potentially including germline variants as putative somatic alterations (though FoundationOne pipeline applies germline filtering).
- Pathological diagnosis not re-reviewed by central expert sarcoma pathologists; diagnostic reclassifications are computational inferences, not confirmed histopathologically.
- Clinical outcomes (stage, grade, treatment history) were unavailable for the full 7,494-patient cohort; the annotated MSK subset (n=118) is small for robust subgroup analyses.
- Selection bias: patients with refractory/advanced disease more likely to be profiled, potentially inflating alteration frequencies vs. earlier-stage disease.
- cBioPortal study `sarcoma_msk_2022` reflects a companion MSK-IMPACT dataset, not the FoundationOne data; exact findings may differ between the two cohorts.
