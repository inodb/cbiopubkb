## Summary

The GLASS (Glioma Longitudinal AnalySiS) consortium epigenomic study analyzes 132 matched initial and first-recurrent glioma samples from the same patients using Illumina 450K/EPIC methylation arrays, integrated with RNA-seq (54 patients), WGS/WXS (64 patients), and ChIP-seq (17 samples). IDH-wildtype (IDHwt) gliomas exhibit a constitutively low, stable methylome consistent with their aggressive phenotype at diagnosis. IDH-mutant (IDHmut) gliomas present with high global DNA methylation (glioma CpG island methylator phenotype, GCIMP-high) that progressively declines at recurrence after treatment (TMZ, RT, or both) — in the most extreme cases approaching IDHwt-like hypomethylation. Treatment-induced epigenetic evolution is associated with GCIMP-high → GCIMP-low transition, progression to higher WHO grade, HOXD13 as a master regulatory driver, and changes in the tumor microenvironment (increased CD8+ T cells and endothelial cells). Untreated IDHmut gliomas showed stable epigenomes at recurrence, demonstrating that methylation loss is driven by treatment rather than time alone.

## Key Findings

### F1: IDHwt gliomas have a constitutively low, stable methylome; IDHmut gliomas show progressive treatment-induced methylation loss

**Finding:** IDHwt gliomas exhibited consistently low global DNA methylation at both initial diagnosis and recurrence, with minimal longitudinal change. By contrast, IDHmut gliomas had high initial genome-wide DNA methylation (GCIMP) that progressively declined at recurrence in treated patients. In the most extreme treated cases, recurrent IDHmut tumors reached DNA hypomethylation levels comparable to IDHwt gliomas. Untreated IDHmut gliomas showed a stable methylome at recurrence, demonstrating treatment — rather than elapsed time — as the driver of epigenetic change.

**Detail:** 620 CpG probes were differentially methylated between the four treatment groups (Kruskal-Wallis, FDR<0.01, |Δβ|>20%). All treatment regimens (TMZ only, RT only, RT+TMZ) were associated with consistent CpG hypomethylation at recurrence relative to the initial tumor, while watch-and-wait patients showed no significant CpG changes. IDHwt initial and recurrent tumors showed the same hypomethylated CpG pattern as IDHmut treated recurrent tumors. 34% (10/29) of GCIMP-high tumors in the treatment arm progressed to GCIMP-low at recurrence vs only 4% (1/27) in untreated patients (P=0.005). The findings were confirmed in the independent GLASS-NL validation cohort.

### F2: Standard treatment (TMZ/RT) is associated with progression to higher WHO grade and worse post-recurrence survival despite longer PFS

**Finding:** In the discovery cohort, treatment was significantly enriched for progression to IDHmut astrocytoma CNS WHO grade 4 at recurrence vs untreated patients (Fisher P=0.03). Additionally, 68% (15/22) of IDHmut astrocytomas in the treatment arm progressed to high-grade astrocytoma in the Pan-CNS molecular classification vs 23% (6/26) in the untreated arm (P=0.003). Despite this, treated patients had a significantly longer progression-free interval (PFI: 40.5 months treated vs 27 months untreated, P=0.009). Post-recurrence survival from second surgery was markedly worse in previously treated patients (P=0.0001 in GLASS-International; confirmed in GLASS-NL).

**Detail:** These findings provide epigenomic mechanistic context for the known paradox of IDHmut glioma treatment: standard-of-care chemotherapy and/or radiotherapy extends time to first recurrence but appears to trigger epigenetic evolution toward a more aggressive phenotype. The 24 epigenetically regulated genes associated with treatment-induced methylation loss included known oncogenes: MYB (cell survival/proliferation) and RSPO4 (Wnt/β-catenin agonist). Hypermutator samples (n=5) occurred exclusively in the treatment arm, consistent with TMZ-induced MMR-defective hypermutation. These findings are retrospective and non-randomized; causal inference requires prospective studies.

### F3: HOXD13 identified as master epigenetic regulator of IDHmut astrocytoma progression

**Finding:** Integrating epigenomics, gene expression, and functional genomics (ChIP-seq H3K27ac and H3K4me3) identified *HOXD13* as a master transcriptional regulator of IDHmut astrocytoma epigenetic evolution. HOXD13 was identified via a computational pipeline linking differentially methylated CpGs to regulatory elements and transcription factor binding motifs. CRISPR knockout of HOXD13 in IDHmut cell lines altered cell proliferation, validating its functional role.

**Detail:** Master regulator analysis combined differential H3K27ac binding (active enhancers), NEUROD1 and HOX motif enrichment at differentially methylated loci, and correlation with gene expression changes. HOXD13 was selected as the top candidate regulator of the epigenetic evolution signature. While HOX genes are well known for their role in developmental patterning and have been implicated in various cancers (AML, solid tumors), this represents the first identification of HOXD13 as a driver of glioma epigenetic evolution specifically in IDHmut astrocytoma. The exact HOXD13-regulated target network in glioma requires further elucidation.

### F4: Treatment-induced epigenetic evolution is accompanied by tumor microenvironment changes — increased CD8+ T cells and endothelial cells

**Finding:** MethylCIBERSORT deconvolution of DNA methylation profiles revealed that, compared with untreated recurrences, recurrent IDHmut tumors that received treatment after initial surgery showed increased infiltration of endothelial cells (CD31+) and CD8+ T lymphocytes. These in silico findings were validated by IHC staining in representative IDHmut samples. Untreated recurrent IDHmut gliomas did not show these TME changes. Recurrent IDHwt gliomas showed a significant increase in macrophages.

**Detail:** The increased CD31+ endothelial cell fraction suggests enhanced neoangiogenesis in treated IDHmut recurrences — consistent with emerging evidence that temozolomide and radiotherapy can alter the vascular architecture of glioma. The CD8+ T-cell increase may reflect immune priming by treatment-released neoantigens or TMZ-induced immunogenic cell death. Together, the epigenomic drift toward IDHwt-like hypomethylation and the concurrent TME changes indicate a convergence of treated IDHmut gliomas toward features of the more aggressive IDHwt phenotype. This has implications for the design of combination immunotherapy strategies at recurrence.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-glioma (IDH-mutant treated) · Glioma

### F5: Epigenomic profiling differentiates IDHmut glioma subtypes (GCIMP-high vs GCIMP-low) and tracks progression with clinical significance

**Finding:** Longitudinal methylation classification showed that GCIMP-high → GCIMP-low transition at recurrence was strongly enriched in treated vs untreated patients (34% vs 4%, P=0.005). GCIMP-low tumors have previously been associated with poor prognosis. Similarly, Pan-CNS methylation-based classification showed that 68% of treated IDHmut astrocytomas progressed to high-grade molecular classification at recurrence vs 23% in untreated (P=0.003). Recurrent histologic grade 4 (IDHmut CNS WHO grade 4) was significantly enriched in the treatment arm (P=0.03).

**Detail:** The GCIMP methylation subtype system provides a molecularly grounded framework for tracking glioma progression that supplements histologic grading. The convergence of treated IDHmut tumors toward GCIMP-low at recurrence predicts the observed worse post-recurrence survival. HOXD13 upregulation (see F3) and oncogene activation (MYB, RSPO4) likely mediate the transition. These findings suggest that serial methylation profiling — potentially via liquid biopsy — could be a biomarker for treatment response and early detection of progression in IDHmut glioma.

## Open Questions

- Does the treatment-induced epigenomic evolution of IDHmut glioma create vulnerabilities that can be targeted at recurrence (e.g., with immune checkpoint blockade given increased CD8+ T-cell infiltration)?
- Can serial cfDNA methylation profiling detect GCIMP-high → GCIMP-low transitions non-invasively, enabling early detection of epigenetic progression?
- Is the observed HOXD13 upregulation a driver or a passenger of IDHmut glioma progression, and what are its direct transcriptional targets?
- Do different treatment regimens (TMZ vs RT vs combined) produce distinct downstream epigenomic and TME changes at recurrence, or are all treatments equivalent in their epigenetic effects?
- Does the increased neoangiogenesis (CD31+ cells) in treated IDHmut glioma predict sensitivity to VEGF/VEGFR-targeted therapies at recurrence?
- Can the 620-CpG treatment-associated methylation signature be used prospectively to identify patients at highest risk of aggressive histologic progression after initial therapy?

## Limitations

- Retrospective, non-randomized study; treatment assignment was not controlled — more aggressive initial tumors may have been more likely to receive treatment, confounding survival analyses
- Relatively small cohort for subgroup analyses (e.g., n=12 TMZ-only, n=6 RT+TMZ)
- DNA methylation arrays (450K/EPIC) provide CpG-level resolution but not full genome coverage; non-CpG methylation changes are not captured
- ChIP-seq performed in only 17 samples (fresh-frozen requirement); does not represent the full cohort diversity
- MethylCIBERSORT cell type deconvolution is an in silico estimate; IHC validation was performed in a limited representative subset
- The GLASS-NL validation cohort consists exclusively of IDHmut astrocytomas; generalizability to oligodendroglioma and IDHwt subtypes is limited
- HOXD13 functional validation was performed in cell lines only; in vivo relevance not yet established
- Temporal heterogeneity: time between surgeries varied widely; time-dependent confounders not fully accounted for
