## Summary

This bi-institutional study (MSK + Erasmus, n=412) is the largest to date integrating comprehensive genomic profiling with detailed clinical outcomes in intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (iCCA). Using MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing across three gene panel versions (341, 410, 468 genes), the study established *TP53*, *KRAS*, and *CDKN2A* as independent genomic predictors of worse overall survival across all disease stages and treatment strategies. While *IDH1/2* and *FGFR2* alterations are clinically actionable targets, neither was associated with survival outcome. Combining high-risk genomic alterations with clinical risk factors (multifocal disease, lymph node metastasis) identified patient subgroups with outcome extremes, from median OS of 18.3 to 74.2 months.

## Key Findings

### F1: TP53, KRAS, and CDKN2A are independent genomic predictors of worse OS across all disease stages

**Finding:** *TP53* mutation, *KRAS* mutation, and *CDKN2A* deletion each independently predict inferior overall survival in iCCA when controlling for stage and clinical variables—establishing these as the primary prognostic genomic markers in this disease.

**Detail:** In the full cohort (n=412), *TP53* (P<0.0001), *KRAS* (P=0.0001), and *CDKN2A* (P<0.0001) alterations were significantly associated with worse OS by univariable Cox regression. These associations persisted in multivariable models controlling for multifocal disease, lymph node involvement, bile duct type, and periductal infiltration. In resected patients (n=209), *TP53* mutation (HR 1.82; 95% CI 1.08–3.06; P=0.03) and *CDKN2A* deletion (HR 3.40; 95% CI 1.95–5.94; P<0.001) independently predicted shorter OS; *KRAS* mutation trended toward significance (HR 1.69; 95% CI 0.97–2.93; P=0.06). All three markers similarly predicted worse outcomes in unresected patients.

### F2: High-risk genomic and clinical factors combine to define outcome extremes (OS 18.3 vs. 74.2 months)

**Finding:** Combining high-risk genomic alterations (*TP53*, *KRAS*, or *CDKN2A*) with high-risk clinical variables (multifocal liver disease or lymph node metastasis) identifies patient subgroups with dramatically different median OS, enabling refined prognostication.

**Detail:** In resected patients, the presence of both high-risk genomic and clinical factors resulted in median OS of 18.3 months; neither factor present: 74.2 months (P<0.001). Patients with only genomic risk (median OS 38.6 months; 95% CI 28.8–73.5) or only clinical risk (median OS 37.0 months; 95% CI 27.6-NA) had intermediate outcomes. Notably, *CDKN2A*-deleted tumors with high-risk clinical features showed limited survival and no benefit of resection over chemotherapy, directly informing surgical decision-making.

### F3: IDH1/2 and FGFR2 alterations are mutually exclusive but neither is associated with outcome

**Finding:** *IDH1* (20%) and *IDH2* mutations are mutually exclusive with *FGFR2* fusions/rearrangements (15%), consistent with their distinct molecular subgroups—yet neither alteration independently impacts overall survival in this cohort.

**Detail:** *IDH1/2* mutations and *FGFR2* fusions were mutually exclusive (Fisher's exact P<0.001), confirming prior reports of distinct molecular subtypes. However, neither *IDH1* mutation (P=NS) nor *FGFR2* fusion (P=NS) was associated with OS in univariable or multivariable analysis. The most common alterations were: *IDH1/2* 20%, *ARID1A* 20%, *TP53* 17%, *CDKN2A* 15%, *BAP1* 15%, *FGFR2* 15%, *PBRM1* 12%, *KRAS* 10% (OncoKB-filtered oncogenic variants). IDH1/2 and FGFR2 remain therapeutically important (ivosidenib/pemigatinib approved) despite lack of prognostic impact.

### F4: High-risk genomic alterations are enriched in advanced disease but predict survival across all stages

**Finding:** *TP53*, *KRAS*, and *CDKN2A* alterations are more common in metastatic iCCA but retain independent prognostic significance even in localized, resected tumors with no lymph node involvement.

**Detail:** Oncogenic alteration frequencies increased with disease extent (solitary liver tumor → multifocal → metastatic) for *TP53*, *KRAS*, and *CDKN2A*. However, in multivariate analysis restricted to resected patients—which eliminates most advanced disease—all three markers retained independent prognostic significance after controlling for known pathologic correlates (multifocal disease, lymph node status, bile duct type, periductal infiltration). This stage-independent prognostic effect supports integrating molecular profiling into pretreatment decision-making even for surgical candidates.

### F5: CDKN2A deletion identifies patients who may not benefit from surgery over chemotherapy

**Finding:** Patients with *CDKN2A* deletions and high-risk clinical features had such limited survival that surgical resection appeared to offer no advantage over systemic chemotherapy, providing a potential genomic criterion for de-escalating surgical intervention.

**Detail:** *CDKN2A*-deleted iCCA patients with high-risk clinical features (multifocal liver disease or lymph node metastasis) had notably short survival with no apparent benefit of resection over chemotherapy. *CDKN2A* deletion conferred the highest HR among all genomic factors in the resected cohort (HR 3.40; 95% CI 1.95–5.94; P<0.001). This finding supports preoperative genomic profiling to identify patients unlikely to benefit from curative-intent surgery.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can preoperative genomic profiling (*TP53*, *KRAS*, *CDKN2A*) be integrated into MSK and other institutional criteria to guide resection vs. chemotherapy allocation in iCCA?
- Does *IDH1/2* inhibition (ivosidenib) or *FGFR2* inhibition (pemigatinib/infigratinib) alter the OS impact of these alterations compared to the chemotherapy-era outcomes described here?
- Can the genomic-clinical risk stratification model (18.3 vs. 74.2 months median OS) be prospectively validated for use as an eligibility or stratification factor in iCCA trials?
- What is the prognostic significance of tumor mutational burden (TMB) and DNA damage repair pathway alterations in iCCA relative to the three main genomic predictors?

## Limitations

- Bi-institutional cohort predominantly from MSKCC (390/412 patients); generalizability to other populations may be limited.
- MSK-IMPACT panel size varied across three versions during the study period; systematic differences in alteration detection between panels were mitigated by analyzing genes captured in ≥70% of samples.
- Therapeutic landscape has evolved during the study period (1993–2019); *IDH* and *FGFR* inhibitors entered clinical use late in the study and were not available to most patients.
- Promoter methylation, microsatellite instability (MSI), and tumor immune microenvironment features were not systematically assessed.
- Resection vs. chemotherapy comparison is not randomized; confounding by patient selection for surgery is inherent.
