## Summary

This landmark cancer genome study performed whole-genome/exome sequencing of 14 non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL) tumor-normal pairs (13 DLBCL, 1 FL) with RNA-seq on 127 NHL samples total, identifying 109 genes with recurrent somatic mutations. The dominant finding is that histone-modifying genes are the most frequently and recurrently mutated gene class in B-cell NHL: MLL2 (KMT2D) is mutated in 32% of DLBCL and 89% of FL cases; MEF2B in 11.4% of DLBCL and 13.4% of FL; and previously known histone modifiers EZH2, CREBBP, and EP300 are recurrently mutated. These findings establish epigenetic/chromatin dysregulation ÔÇö particularly histone H3K4 methylation and H3K27 acetylation ÔÇö as a central oncogenic mechanism in B-cell lymphomagenesis. Data are available in cBioPortal as `nhl_bcgsc_2011`.

## Key Findings

### F1: MLL2 (KMT2D) is mutated in 32% of DLBCL and 89% of follicular lymphoma ÔÇö the most frequently mutated gene in NHL

**Finding:** MLL2 (now designated KMT2D), encoding a histone H3K4 methyltransferase, is the most frequently mutated gene in this NHL cohort: 32% of DLBCL and 89% of FL cases harbor somatic mutations. MLL2 had no prior known role in lymphoma.

**Detail:** MLL2 mutations were predominantly inactivating (truncating/nonsense), consistent with a tumor suppressor role. MLL2 encodes the catalytic subunit of a COMPASS-like H3K4 methyltransferase complex that regulates gene activation via histone modification. The very high mutation frequency in FL (89%) suggests MLL2 loss is a near-universal early event in FL pathogenesis, potentially preceding the BCL2 translocation in clonal evolution. Identification of MLL2 established the gene (subsequently renamed KMT2D) as a major lymphoma driver and has since been confirmed in large TCGA and other datasets.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KMT2D` (`MLL2`), `EZH2`, `CREBBP` ┬À DLBCL, Follicular lymphoma (FL)

### F2: MEF2B is recurrently mutated in 11% of DLBCL and 13% of FL ÔÇö a novel calcium-regulated histone acetylation cofactor

**Finding:** MEF2B, a calcium-regulated transcription factor that cooperates with CREBBP and EP300 in acetylating histones, is somatically mutated in 11.4% of DLBCL and 13.4% of FL ÔÇö representing a novel and previously unappreciated driver in lymphoma.

**Detail:** MEF2B mutations cluster at two hotspot sites (Y69 and D83), and RNA-seq data revealed preferential expression of the mutant allele in at least two cases, suggesting gain-of-function or dominant-negative effects. MEF2B normally coordinates with CREBBP/EP300 to regulate H3K27 acetylation at target gene enhancers. Its mutation disrupts a regulatory node distinct from but convergent with the EZH2-CREBBP-EP300 axis. The identification of MEF2B as a recurrently mutated gene provided new insight into how calcium signaling and histone acetylation intersect in B-cell lymphomagenesis.

### F3: Histone-modifying gene mutations are significantly enriched in NHL ÔÇö establishing chromatin dysregulation as a central lymphoma oncogenic mechanism

**Finding:** Among 109 recurrently mutated genes confirmed by re-sequencing, genes involved in histone methylation (p=2.2├ù10Ôü╗Ôü┤) and acetylation (p=1.2├ù10Ôü╗┬▓) were the most significantly enriched functional categories ÔÇö implicating chromatin biology as a previously unappreciated core mechanism in B-cell lymphomagenesis.

**Detail:** Genes with positive selection evidence included known lymphoma oncogenes (BCL2, CD79B, CARD11, MYD88, EZH2) and newly identified chromatin regulators. EZH2 ÔÇö a H3K27 methyltransferase ÔÇö was confirmed at known hotspots and additional novel sites (A682G, A692V). CREBBP and EP300 (H3K27 acetyltransferases) were recurrently mutated and represent a functionally opposing pathway to EZH2 gain-of-function (both converge on H3K27 regulation). The enrichment of histone modifier mutations across both DLBCL and FL ÔÇö despite their distinct biology and clinical behavior ÔÇö suggests chromatin dysregulation as a unifying early oncogenic event in germinal centre B-cell-derived malignancies.

### F4: Novel mutation hotspots identified in FOXO1, BCL7A, B2M, CCND3, and IKZF3 ÔÇö expanding the NHL driver landscape

**Finding:** RNA-seq-driven hotspot discovery identified 10 additional mutated genes not found in whole-genome sequencing, including five novel targets: FOXO1 (start codon + T24 hotspot), B2M (start codon affecting MHC-I), BCL7A (start codon), CCND3, and IKZF3.

**Detail:** FOXO1 start codon mutations (observed in three cases) produce an N-terminal truncation (confirmed by Western blot in a cell line), while T24 hotspot mutations prevent AKT-mediated nuclear export, thereby disrupting B-cell receptorÔÇôAKTÔÇôFOXO1 signaling. B2M start codon mutations reduce MHC class I surface expression, suggesting immune evasion. BCL7A mutations affect a chromatin remodeling complex subunit. CCND3 mutations affect the cyclin D3 cell cycle regulator. The use of RNA-seq mutation hotspot discovery as a complementary strategy to whole-genome sequencing substantially expanded the catalogue of NHL drivers and established a multi-platform discovery paradigm.

### F5: Allelic imbalance in RNA-seq reveals preferential mutant allele expression in BCL2, CARD11, EZH2, MEF2B, and TP53

**Finding:** Of 380 expressed heterozygous mutant alleles, 16.8% showed preferential expression of the mutant allele. Seven genes showed significant preferential mutant allele expression in ÔëÑ2 cases: BCL2, CARD11, CD79B, EZH2, IRF4, MEF2B, and TP53 ÔÇö functionally consistent with gain-of-function or selection for the translocated (BCL2) or oncogenic allele.

**Detail:** For BCL2 (27/43 cases), preferential expression of the mutant allele is consistent with somatic hypermutation targeting the translocated and transcriptionally active BCL2 allele ÔÇö a known mechanism. For EZH2, novel hotspot sites A682G and A692V (in addition to the canonical Y641 hotspot) showed mutant-alleleÔÇôfavored expression, suggesting oncogenic gain-of-function at these previously unreported sites. For MEF2B, hotspots Y69 and D83 demonstrated mutant allele expression bias. The approach of integrating RNA-seq expression with somatic variant data enables prioritization of likely driver mutations from background passenger variants without requiring additional functional assays.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BCL2`, `EZH2`, `CARD11`, `MEF2B`, `TP53`, `IRF4`, `CD79B` ┬À DLBCL, Follicular lymphoma

## Open Questions

- Does the near-universal MLL2/KMT2D mutation in follicular lymphoma (89%) represent the founding mutation or a secondary event following BCL2 translocation?
- Can the convergent chromatin dysregulation in FL (MLL2 + EZH2 + CREBBP) be therapeutically targeted combinatorially ÔÇö e.g., EZH2 inhibitor (tazemetostat) + CREBBP/EP300 activator?
- Do MEF2B hotspot mutations (Y69, D83) confer gain-of-function, dominant-negative, or loss-of-function effects on H3K27 acetylation at specific B-cell enhancers?
- Can the frequency of KMT2D/MLL2 mutations in FL be used as a progression or transformation biomarker from indolent to aggressive lymphoma?
- Do FOXO1 mutations (start codon vs. T24 phosphosite) have distinct effects on BCR signaling dependency that could predict sensitivity to PI3K-delta or BTK inhibitors?

## Limitations

- Discovery genome/exome cohort is very small (14 tumors); statistical power for novel driver identification depends on RNA-seq hotspot strategy across 127 samples
- COO subtype (GCB vs. ABC DLBCL) was not systematically annotated for all cases, limiting subtype-specific mutation frequency analyses
- No paired diagnostic and relapse samples; clonal evolution of histone modifier mutations is not addressed
- Functional consequences of MLL2/KMT2D mutations (H3K4 methylation levels, target gene expression) were not directly measured in patient tumors
- Allelic imbalance analysis in RNA-seq may be confounded by RNA editing, alternative splicing, or nonsense-mediated decay ÔÇö limiting direct inference of selection
- cBioPortal study `nhl_bcgsc_2011` reflects the original 2011 dataset; subsequent large-scale DLBCL/FL genomic studies (TCGA, HMRN, LLMPP) provide substantially expanded mutation frequency estimates
