## Summary

MSK-IMPACT-based comprehensive genomic profiling of 195 soft tissue LMS (STLMS) and 238 uterine LMS (ULMS) with clinical follow-up generates genomic risk stratification models validated in AACR GENIE. In STLMS, a 3-tier model based on *RB1* mutation, *ATRX* mutation, and chr12q deletion outperforms traditional FNCLCC grade + size for disease-specific survival (DSS). In ULMS, a model incorporating *TP53* mutation, *ATRX* mutation, and chr20q amplification predicts both DSS and PFS comparably to clinicopathologic parameters. IHC for TP53, RB1, ATRX can serve as surrogate biomarkers. Longitudinal sequencing confirms most driver alterations are early truncal events. Data are publicly available at `lms_msk_2024`.

## Key Findings

### F1: 3-tier genomic risk model for STLMS (RB1 mutation + chr12q deletion/ATRX) outperforms histologic grade

**Finding:** Machine-learning (OncoCast-MPM) identified a 3-tier genomic risk stratification for STLMS DSS: high risk = co-occurring *RB1* mutation + del12q or *ATRX* mutation (median DSS 75 months); intermediate = any one of *RB1* mutation, *ATRX* mutation, or del12q (median DSS 101 months); low = none (median DSS 158 months; global log-rank P=0.04 in primaries; P=0.00057 in full cohort).

**Detail:** 5-year DSS by genomic risk: high 43%, intermediate 64%, low 79% (full cohort). FNCLCC grade predicted DSS (P=0.047) but tumor size did not (P=0.47). On multivariate Cox regression, only the high genomic risk group remained significant over mitotic rate and necrosis. Traditional risk stratification was found to under-grade some cases classified as genomically high-risk. For PFS in primary STLMS, the combination of *RB1* mutation, *ATRX* mutation, or *PTEN* deletion predicted worse PFS (P=0.049). In metastatic STLMS, *RB1* mutation alone predicted worse DSS (P=0.044).

**Genes / cancer types:** `RB1`, `ATRX`, `PTEN` (del12q locus) · Soft tissue leiomyosarcoma

### F2: 3-tier genomic risk model for ULMS (TP53 mutation + ATRX/amp20q) predicts DSS and PFS

**Finding:** In 177 primary ULMS, the genomic risk model identified: high risk = co-occurring *TP53* mutation + *ATRX* mutation or + chr20q amplification (median DSS 52 months, 5-year DSS 41%); intermediate = any one (median DSS 92 months, 5-year DSS 67%); low = none (median DSS 157 months, 5-year DSS 79%; global log-rank P=0.00012).

**Detail:** The same model predicted PFS: high (median 15 months), intermediate (25 months), low (29 months; P=0.014). Traditional ULMS risk factors (tumor size >20cm, mitotic rate >10/10 HPF, necrosis) were also significant, but on multivariate analysis, only tumor size and high genomic risk remained significant for DSS. Note that *TP53 mutations* (not deletions) drive the model, consistent with gain-of-function/dominant-negative effects being more oncogenic than simple LOH. Similarly, *ATRX mutations* (not deletions) were prognostically significant.

### F3: RB1 and ATRX genomic risk model validated in AACR GENIE (n=317 STLMS)

**Finding:** A simplified 3-tier model using *RB1* alterations (mutations + deletions) and *ATRX* mutations predicted overall survival in the AACR GENIE STLMS cohort (global log-rank P=0.00013): high = co-occurring RB1 + ATRX (median OS 23 months, 5-year OS 10%); intermediate = either (median OS 29 months, 5-year OS 21%); low = neither (median OS 44 months, 5-year OS 43%).

**Detail:** Del12q was excluded from validation because GENIE cohort lacked arm-level CNV data for most cases (tumor-only panels). Frequencies in GENIE: RB1 mutation 20%, RB1 deletion 16% (vs. 26%/24% at MSK). Differences in detection reflect variable panel coverage. The simplified model (excluding del12q) also remained significant for DSS in the MSK cohort (confirmatory). Despite platform differences, the core RB1-ATRX relationship was reproducible, supporting clinical applicability across institutions.

### F4: IHC for TP53, RB1, and ATRX can serve as surrogate prognostic biomarkers

**Finding:** In 20 randomly selected STLMS cases, IHC for TP53, RB1, and ATRX correlated with genomic alterations: TP53 (sensitivity 83%, specificity 86%), RB1 (sensitivity 100%, specificity 25%), ATRX (sensitivity 83%, specificity 69%). PTEN IHC was less reliable.

**Detail:** Null/complete loss of expression by IHC aligned with loss-of-function mutations or deletions. "Mutant pattern" diffuse TP53 overexpression corresponded to TP53 missense mutations (gain-of-function). Some IHC losses occurred without detectable genetic alterations, suggesting sensitivity > specificity for molecular event detection (or undetected alterations). RB1 specificity was low (25%), meaning many IHC-negative cases lacked genomically confirmed RB1 alteration — possibly due to epigenetic silencing or other mechanisms.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `RB1`, `ATRX`, `PTEN` · Soft tissue leiomyosarcoma

### F5: Most genomic driver alterations are early clonal events that persist at progression

**Finding:** Longitudinal sequencing of 18 STLMS and 15 ULMS patients (sequential paired samples, median interval 3–4 years) showed that most molecular alterations (TP53, RB1, ATRX, PTEN) were present as early truncal events at diagnosis and persisted at progression, consistent with clonal selection models.

**Detail:** TP53 and RB1 alterations showed higher variant allele frequencies than ATRX and PTEN, consistent with earlier clonal establishment and/or LOH. A subset of cases acquired additional alterations (e.g., RB1 loss, ATRX mutations) not detected in the initial tumor, indicating some acquired genomic evolution at progression. Frequencies of recurrent alterations did not differ significantly between primary vs. metastatic paired cohorts. This suggests that the genomic risk markers are stable and detectable at initial diagnosis.

## Open Questions

- Can the genomic risk model be combined with traditional clinicopathologic factors (size, grade, necrosis) into a composite nomogram for improved prediction?
- Why do *RB1 mutations* (not deletions) confer worse prognosis in STLMS, while RB1 deletions are hallmarks of benign mesenchymal lesions? Is there a gain-of-function effect analogous to TP53?
- Can chr12q deletion (del12q) and chr20q amplification be reliably detected by tumor-only NGS panels without arm-level CNV data?
- Do HRD and MMR mutational signatures in a subset of LMS predict response to PARP inhibitors or checkpoint immunotherapy?
- Is there a clinically actionable therapeutic consequence of ATRX loss — e.g., sensitivity to ALT-targeting strategies?

## Limitations

- AACR GENIE validation cohort uses tumor-only panels (vs. matched tumor-normal MSK-IMPACT), limiting sensitivity for detecting true somatic alterations and arm-level CNVs
- OS calculated from time of sequencing in GENIE (not diagnosis), introducing lead-time bias
- GENIE cohort enriched for metastatic presentation (40% vs. 23% at MSK), complicating direct comparison
- Some muscle-related driver genes (MYOCD, DMD) not covered by MSK-IMPACT, limiting assessment of these alterations
- MSK cohort biased toward higher-grade retroperitoneal STLMS due to preferential genomic sequencing of aggressive cases
- IHC surrogate markers have suboptimal specificity for some targets (RB1 IHC specificity 25%)
