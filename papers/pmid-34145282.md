## Summary

This paper describes the design, analytical validation, and prospective clinical implementation of MSK-ACCESS (Memorial Sloan Kettering – Analysis of Circulating cfDNA to Examine Somatic Status), a hybridization capture-based NGS assay targeting 826 exons and selected introns/SNPs across 129 cancer genes in cell-free DNA (cfDNA) from plasma. The key methodological innovation is paired matched normal sequencing (white blood cells from the same blood draw), which eliminates germline variants and clonal hematopoiesis (CH) mutations — the dominant source of false positives in plasma-only liquid biopsy assays. Analytical validation demonstrated 92% sensitivity for de novo mutation calling to 0.5% VAF and 99.9% specificity. In 681 prospective clinical samples from 617 patients (predominantly NSCLC, prostate, bladder, pancreatic, biliary), somatic alterations were detected in 73% of samples; 56% of positive samples harbored actionable alterations. Matched normal sequencing eliminated >10,000 germline and CH variants per cohort and enabled fragment-size-based confirmation of tumor DNA origin. All results are publicly available via `msk_access_2021` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: UMI-based duplex consensus sequencing reduces background error to 1.7 × 10⁻⁶, enabling detection to 0.5% VAF

**Finding:** MSK-ACCESS employs unique molecular indexes (UMIs) and dual-barcode indexing to generate duplex consensus sequences from both strands of the original cfDNA molecule, reducing median background error rate by ~200-fold compared to standard sequencing and enabling detection of mutations at 0.5% allele frequency with 92% sensitivity.

**Detail:** In 47 healthy donor plasmas: median error rate in standard BAMs = 3.3 × 10⁻⁴; simplex = 1.2 × 10⁻⁵; duplex = 1.7 × 10⁻⁶. In standard BAMs <1% of targeted positions had zero error; in duplex BAMs 99% of positions had zero error. Analytical validation (70 samples, 100 known SNVs/indels in AKT1, ALK, BRAF, EGFR, ERBB2, ESR1, KRAS, MET, PIK3CA, TP53): 92% de novo sensitivity at VAF ≥0.5% (76/83; 95% CI 84–96.5%), 99% genotyping sensitivity (82/83; 95% CI 92.5–99.9%), 99.9% de novo specificity. Reproducibility: 99% of 152 expected mutations detected across 7 samples, 3 replicates within-run and across 4 runs; coefficient of variation median 0.16.

**Genes / cancer types:** `AKT1`, `ALK`, `BRAF`, `EGFR`, `ERBB2`, `ESR1`, `KRAS`, `MET`, `PIK3CA`, `TP53` (validation panel) · Pan-cancer

### F2: Matched normal (WBC) sequencing eliminates >10,000 germline and clonal hematopoiesis variants per cohort

**Finding:** Paired WBC sequencing from the same blood draw eliminates the majority of non-somatic variant calls (>10,000 in the prospective cohort), including germline variants misclassified as somatic by commercial assays and clonal hematopoiesis (CH) mutations at low allele frequencies.

**Detail:** Plasma-only calling across 681 samples yielded 24,561 variant calls. Database-driven filtering (curated panel of normals + gnomAD ≥0.5% population frequency) removed 14,508 calls (median 14 ± 8 per sample). VAF-based germline filtering (35–65% in both compartments) would remove 721 additional calls, but would incorrectly eliminate 70 verified somatic mutations (15 actionable). After database filtering, 10,053 variants remained requiring matched WBC to resolve. Using patient-matched WBC: 7,760 variants (77%) below 10% VAF were properly classified as CH, and confirmed germline deletions in *ATM*, *BRCA2*, and *RB1* (retinoblastoma patients) were correctly attributed. Cases reclassified from commercial somatic to germline: *ATM* p.E522fs*43, *TP53*, *BRCA2*, *ROS1*, *KRAS* G12S (CH vs. tumor-derived).

**Genes / cancer types:** `DNMT3A`, `ATM`, `BRCA2`, `RB1`, `KRAS`, `TP53`, `ROS1` (examples of reclassified variants) · Pan-cancer

**Data availability:** `available`

### F3: ctDNA fragment size distinguishes tumor-derived from germline and CH variants

**Finding:** Tumor-derived cfDNA fragments are significantly shorter than fragments supporting germline or CH variants, providing an orthogonal line of evidence to confirm somatic vs. non-somatic variant origin — independent of matched normal sequencing.

**Detail:** General cfDNA fragment size: bimodal peaks at 161 bp and 317 bp (nucleosomal spacing). Fragments supporting confirmed somatic mutations (absent in WBC; n=1,558) were significantly shorter than wild-type fragments (bootstrapped p < 0.0001). Germline variants present at ~50% VAF in both cfDNA and WBC showed equivalent fragment length to wild-type (bootstrapped p = 0.94). Variants classified as CH (low VAF in both plasma and WBC) showed fragment lengths indistinguishable from germline/wild-type (bootstrapped p = 0.99). In ambiguous cases where a variant was present at lower frequency in WBC than plasma, shorter fragments confirmed tumor (not CH) origin (bootstrapped p < 0.0001). This validates WBC-based calls and adds a mechanistic explanation: tumor cell apoptosis generates shorter nucleosome-depleted fragments.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer (all 129 ACCESS genes)

**Data availability:** `not-available`

**Tool support:** `custom`

### F4: 73% of clinical samples have detectable alterations; 56% are actionable — tissue and cfDNA are complementary

**Finding:** Somatic alterations were detected in 73% of 681 prospective clinical samples (median 3 per positive sample), 56% of which harbored clinically actionable alterations. Comparison with MSK-IMPACT tissue reveals complementarity: 12% of MSK-ACCESS-only mutations are actionable and missed by tissue, while 18% of tissue-only mutations are actionable and missed by cfDNA.

**Detail:** Among 617 patients, 617 samples (99%) passed QC. Cancer types: NSCLC 51%, followed by prostate, bladder, pancreatic, biliary. Level 1 OncoKB actionable alterations highest in bladder (48%), breast (37%), NSCLC (33%). Among 383 patients with matched MSK-IMPACT tissue: 1,206 total mutations in overlapping regions; 59% shared, 21% MSK-IMPACT-only, 20% MSK-ACCESS-only. Of MSK-ACCESS-only calls: 18 mutations (12%) were actionable. Of MSK-IMPACT-only calls: 46 (18%) were actionable. For 234 patients without any MSK-IMPACT: MSK-ACCESS detected 79 actionable mutations in 26% (61 patients). Key cancer-specific actionable finds: EGFR (lung), FGFR2 mutations/fusions (cholangiocarcinoma), FGFR3/ERBB2 (bladder), ESR1/AR (breast/prostate), KRAS (pancreatic), AR (prostate).

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `EGFR`, `KRAS`, `ERBB2`, `BRAF`, `MET`, `ESR1`, `AR`, `PIK3CA`, `FGFR2`, `FGFR3`, `IDH1`, `EML4-ALK`, `KIF5B-RET`, `ROS1` · NSCLC, breast, prostate, bladder, pancreatic, biliary/cholangiocarcinoma

### F5: Clonal mutations shed ctDNA more reliably than subclonal mutations; timing of collection matters for concordance

**Finding:** Concordance between MSK-ACCESS cfDNA and MSK-IMPACT tissue is highest for clonal mutations and when the two samples are collected close in time; subclonal and temporally distant mutations are frequently discordant, reflecting tumor heterogeneity and disease evolution.

**Detail:** Among mutations shared by both assays vs. MSK-IMPACT-only calls: shared mutations were significantly more likely to be clonal in tissue (p = 8.10 × 10⁻¹³ for all mutations; p = 6.06 × 10⁻³ for actionable only). Time between tissue and blood collection (ΔDOP): MSK-IMPACT-only mutations had mean ΔDOP 598 days (median 491), MSK-ACCESS-only 616 days (median 288), shared 351 days (median 83 days). Tumor purity in tissue was not significantly different between concordant vs. discordant cases (p = 0.812), ruling out low tumor cellularity as the primary driver. Specific example of resistance mutation: FGFR3 point mutations conferring resistance to FGFR inhibitor therapy found in MSK-ACCESS cfDNA of FGFR3-TACC3 bladder cancers — not in prior tissue.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR3` (resistance mutations in FGFR3-TACC3 bladder cancer); general pan-cancer concordance analysis

## Open Questions

1. Can MSK-ACCESS-detected cfDNA alterations serve as a surrogate for minimal residual disease after surgery in localized cancers, predicting relapse before clinical or imaging evidence?
2. How does cfDNA profiling with MSK-ACCESS perform in early-stage cancers (stage I–II), where ctDNA shedding is known to be lower?
3. What is the false-positive rate from low-level clonal hematopoiesis mutations that are present in cfDNA but at frequencies too low to detect in the matched WBC?
4. Can MSK-ACCESS detect resistance mechanisms in real time during targeted therapy, and can this be used to guide sequential therapy decisions (e.g., FGFR inhibitor resistance in cholangiocarcinoma)?
5. Will patient-specific bespoke cfDNA panels (customized to individual tumor exome profiles) outperform MSK-ACCESS for MRD detection in low-shedding tumors?

## Limitations

- Raw sequencing data not publicly available (privacy laws); only processed results in cBioPortal.
- Tissue–plasma concordance analysis uses the first cfDNA sample per patient, which may not represent the most informative timepoint.
- MSK-ACCESS cannot replace tissue biopsy: 38% of samples had no detectable alteration, reflecting low disease burden, effective disease control, or low tumor shedding.
- The panel covers 129 genes (~400 kb) — comprehensive but fixed; novel actionable genes identified after panel design are not covered.
- Pan-cancer sensitivity varies by disease (91% of breast and 94% of NSCLC tumors expected to have ≥1 targeted mutation, but lower for some cancer types with low mutational burden).
- Fragment-size-based somatic confirmation is not part of routine clinical reporting; requires custom bioinformatics pipeline.
- Study population is predominantly NSCLC patients (51%), which may not reflect performance characteristics in other tumor types.
