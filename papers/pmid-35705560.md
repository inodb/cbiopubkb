## Summary

Comprehensive prospective cohort study of 2,138 sarcomas across 45 pathological entities, analyzed using MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing with matched normal DNA (tumor-normal pairs). This MSK-IMPACT companion to the FoundationOne sarcoma atlas (PMID35705558) uses paired normal sequencing, enabling more precise somatic variant calling. Key contributions: characterization of subtype-specific mutations across 45 entities; identification of TERT amplification in 44% of intimal sarcoma (novel); SWI/SNF alterations in 43% of uterine adenosarcoma (novel); ATRX loss-of-function as a recurrent cross-subtype event; and unsupervised genomic clustering yielding 17 distinct clusters that do not align with histological identity. Whole-genome doubling (WGD) was associated with worse OS in metastatic sarcomas. Data are fully available in cBioPortal (`sarcoma_mskcc_2022`).

## Key Findings

### F1: Subtype-specific oncogenic drivers across 45 sarcoma entities reveal novel and confirmed targets

**Finding:** MutSig and MuSiC analyses identified significantly recurrently mutated genes in all subtypes with ≥20 samples. Novel findings include FLT4 (VEGFR3) and EPHA5 kinase-domain mutations in angiosarcoma (9% each), MED12 G44 missense mutations in ULMS (16%), and BCOR alterations in ERMS (19%) mutually exclusive with DICER1 (12%).

**Detail:** Within GIST, novel recurrently mutated genes beyond canonical drivers (KIT, PDGFRA, SDHA) included SETD2 (4%), MAX (4%), and MGA (3%)—all related to MYC-MAX transcription factor complex biology. Angiosarcoma harbored recurrent KDR (VEGFR2) mutations in 19% of cases, providing rationale for anti-angiogenic targeting. The mutual exclusivity of BCOR and DICER1 in ERMS (p<0.05) delineates genetically distinct subgroups within this pediatric histology. Driver mutation cancer cell fractions (CCF) were close to 1.0 in PECOMA, SFT, LMS, ULMS, and ES—indicating early, clonal acquisition—whereas MFS, OS, and UPS had VUS with similarly high CCF suggesting uncharacterized functional drivers.

**Genes / cancer types:** KDR, FLT4, EPHA5, MED12, BCOR, DICER1, SETD2, MAX, MGA, KIT, PDGFRA · Angiosarcoma, ULMS, ERMS, GIST

### F2: Whole-genome doubling (WGD) is common in OS, UPS, ERMS, and MPNST and associates with worse OS in metastatic disease

**Finding:** WGD frequency was ~50% in OS, UPS, ERMS, and MPNST—among the highest across any cancer type in a pan-cancer comparison. WGD was associated with worse overall survival in metastatic sarcoma (p=0.042) and specifically in metastatic UPS (p=0.022).

**Detail:** WDLS, DDLS, and translocation-driven subtypes (SYNS, ES, DSRCT, MRLS) had lower WGD rates despite being CNA-driven, suggesting WGD is a feature of genomic complexity rather than CNA-driven biology per se. In primary sarcoma, WGD was not associated with OS (p=0.391), suggesting WGD becomes prognostically relevant only in the metastatic context. MFS and UPS had similar WGD rates, consistent with the genetic continuum hypothesis for these subtypes. WGD warrants further study as a prognostic biomarker in sarcoma clinical trials.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, MDM2, RB1, CDK4 (as context for WGD-associated instability) · OS, UPS, ERMS, MPNST, MFS

### F3: TERT amplification in 44% of intimal sarcoma; TERT promoter mutations in 79% of myxoid liposarcoma and 46% of solitary fibrous tumor

**Finding:** TERT oncogenic alterations were identified in a subtype-specific and mechanistically distinct manner: amplifications in 44% of intimal sarcoma (INTS, novel finding) and in 16% of DDLS (mostly amplification); promoter mutations in 79% of MRLS, 46% of SFT, and 35% of dedifferentiated chondrosarcoma.

**Detail:** TERT amplification in INTS occurs at a locus distinct from the canonical MDM2/CDK4 amplification at 12q (a hallmark of INTS), implicating TERT as an independent oncogenic driver. In DDLS, TERT amplification (not promoter mutation) was the dominant mechanism. In MRLS and SFT, TERT promoter mutations were identified at rates exceeding prior reports (SFT rate substantially higher than in smaller previous cohorts), potentially reflecting enrichment for aggressive disease in this referral cohort. TERT and ATRX alterations were largely non-overlapping within UPS and liposarcoma, suggesting two independent telomere maintenance mechanisms.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, MDM2, CDK4, ATRX · Intimal sarcoma, DDLS, MRLS (myxoid/round cell liposarcoma), SFT, DDCHS

### F4: ATRX loss-of-function recurs across ≥7 sarcoma subtypes; SWI/SNF alterations define uterine adenosarcoma

**Finding:** Loss-of-function ATRX alterations were found in ≥10% of cases in 7 subtypes: ULMS (~33%), PLLS, UPS, MFS, PECOMA, LMS, and ANGS. In uterine adenosarcoma (UAS), SWI/SNF complex alterations (ARID1A, PBRM1) were found in 43% of cases—a novel and unexpected finding in this rare subtype.

**Detail:** ATRX loss was more frequently caused by truncating mutations than deletions, independent of subtype—unusual given the overall low mutation rate in sarcoma. ATRX alterations were present in both CNA-driven and translocation-driven subtypes, though at lower frequency in the latter, suggesting a universal tumor-suppressive function. ATRX regulates H3.3 deposition and ALT (alternative lengthening of telomeres); its loss may explain telomere maintenance in tumors lacking TERT alterations. The UAS SWI/SNF finding provides rationale for EZH2 inhibitor trials (by analogy with SMARCB1-deleted epithelioid sarcoma and the FDA-approved tazemetostat).

**Genes / cancer types:** ATRX, ARID1A, PBRM1, SMARCB1 · ULMS, PLLS, UPS, MFS, LMS, ANGS, PECOMA, uterine adenosarcoma, epithelioid sarcoma

### F5: Unsupervised genomic clustering yields 17 clusters distinct from histological groupings; common subtypes have high intra-subtype heterogeneity

**Finding:** Unsupervised UMAP+HDBSCAN clustering of oncogenic alteration patterns across all 2,138 samples yielded 17 distinct genomic clusters. Some histologic subtypes (EPIS, DSRCT, WDLS/DDLS) had low entropy (uniform genomic profiles), while UPS, ULMS, and OS had high entropy—reflecting multiple genetically distinct subgroups within a single histological label.

**Detail:** Cluster-defining alterations aligned with known drivers in low-entropy subtypes: SMARCB1 cluster → EPIS; WT1 cluster → DSRCT; MDM2-CDK4 cluster → WDLS/DDLS/INTS. Extraskeletal OS had substantially greater ATRX-RB1-TP53 cluster enrichment than conventional OS, supporting distinct biology. Dedifferentiated chondrosarcoma (DDCHS) clustered with OS subtypes rather than conventional CHS, suggesting a genetic shift underlies dedifferentiation. These findings provide a rationale for genomics-based stratification in clinical trials rather than pure histologic grouping, and support basket trial designs based on genetic cluster rather than histology.

**Genes / cancer types:** SMARCB1, WT1, MDM2, CDK4, ATRX, TP53, RB1, TERT · All sarcoma subtypes

## Open Questions

1. Can the 17 genomic clusters identified by unsupervised analysis predict response to treatment better than histological subtype—and should future sarcoma trials enroll by genomic cluster rather than histology?
2. ATRX loss is predicted to sensitize tumors to ATR inhibition—are ongoing trials (NCT03718091, NCT05071209) enrolling ATRX-altered sarcomas and can results validate this therapeutic hypothesis?
3. What drives the high SWI/SNF alteration rate (43%) in uterine adenosarcoma—could EZH2 inhibition (tazemetostat) by analogy with SMARCB1-deleted epithelioid sarcoma be effective?
4. Does TERT amplification in intimal sarcoma correlate with TERT mRNA overexpression, and does it confer sensitivity to direct telomerase inhibitors or other TERT-dependent vulnerabilities?
5. Why is WGD prognostically relevant only in metastatic (not primary) sarcoma—does WGD reflect an acquired event during metastatic evolution, or is it a pre-existing feature that only becomes clinically manifest in the metastatic context?

## Limitations

- Targeted sequencing (MSK-IMPACT) not optimized to detect novel rearrangements outside the panel, focal CNA in untargeted regions, or full mutational signatures (limited by panel size and number of SNVs in low-TMB tumors).
- WGD estimates from targeted sequencing may differ from WGS-derived estimates; WGD frequency per subtype should be interpreted with this caveat.
- Combined analysis of primary and metastatic samples (verified not to differ substantially in most subtypes, except GIST and UPS JUN amplification), but treatment-induced genomic changes cannot be excluded.
- Limited racial diversity in the study population, reducing generalizability across populations with different sarcoma epidemiology.
- Some rare subtypes have <20 samples in the core analysis, limiting statistical power for subtype-specific driver gene identification.
