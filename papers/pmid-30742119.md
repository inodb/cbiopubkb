## Summary

Longitudinal multi-omics study of 66 adult GBM patients (17 long-term responders) treated with anti-PD-1 therapy (nivolumab or pembrolizumab) upon recurrence at Northwestern University (n=20) and Columbia University (n=46). Whole-exome sequencing (58 samples), transcriptomics (38 samples), a cancer gene panel (39 patients), single-cell RNA-seq (4,000 cells, 3 tumors), quantitative multiplex immunofluorescence (17 matched pre/post pairs), and TCR/immunoglobulin RNA sequencing were integrated to characterize genomic and immune correlates of response. Key findings include enrichment of PTEN mutations in non-responders (associated with immunosuppressive microenvironment), enrichment of MAPK pathway mutations (BRAF, PTPN11) in responders, distinct branched clonal evolution with neoantigen elimination in responders, and greater T cell clonal diversity expansion in non-responders. Response — not TMB or neoepitope load — was the strongest predictor of survival.

## Key Findings

### F1: Response to anti-PD-1 therapy strongly predicts overall survival in GBM

**Finding:** Responders to anti-PD-1 therapy had markedly better overall survival: 15.5 months vs. 5.7 months for non-responders (p=2.2e-5, log-rank test), and this was the only clinical or demographic factor significantly associated with survival in univariate analysis.

**Detail:** Responders were defined as patients with ≥6 months of stable or shrinking tumor on MRI, or whose surgical specimen showed only inflammatory response after treatment. In a survival analysis excluding patients with <6-month survival (to avoid confounding the temporal response definition), responders had 14.3 months median survival vs. 10.1 months for non-responders (p=0.0081). Age, sex, and choice of PD-1 inhibitor (nivolumab vs. pembrolizumab) were not significantly associated with survival. Overall, <10% of GBM patients show long-term responses, underscoring the need for molecular predictors to guide patient selection for immunotherapy.

### F2: PTEN mutations are enriched in anti-PD-1 non-responders and drive an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment

**Finding:** Among IDH1 wild-type tumors, PTEN was mutated in 23 of 32 non-responders but only 3 of 13 responders (OR=8.5; Fisher p=0.0063; FDR corrected p<0.05), and PTEN mutations were associated with higher PI3K-AKT pathway activity, immunosuppressive transcriptional signatures, and increased CD68+HLA-DR− macrophage infiltration.

**Detail:** PTEN mutations in non-responders were also significantly enriched relative to TCGA background GBM mutation rate (~33%; Fisher p=0.0018, OR=3.3). PTEN-mutant tumors showed significantly higher PI3K-AKT pathway enrichment (ssGSEA; p=0.049). Single-cell RNA-seq of 9,000 cells from 3 GBMs identified a CD44+ tumor cell population enriched in PTEN-mutated tumors (p<1e-16) as the source of FOXP3-related immunosuppressive transcriptional signatures — not conventional FOXP3+ Treg cells, as IHC of 5 post-treatment specimens found no CD4+FOXP3+ T cells. TCGA RNA-seq analysis of 172 GBM samples confirmed that PTEN mutations correlated with higher macrophage, microglia, and neutrophil infiltration (p<0.05, FDR corrected).

### F3: MAPK pathway mutations (BRAF, PTPN11) are enriched in anti-PD-1 responders

**Finding:** MAPK pathway mutations (BRAF or PTPN11) were found in 4 of 13 responders vs. 1 of 32 non-responders (OR=12.8; Fisher p=0.019; FDR corrected p<0.05), and were also significantly enriched relative to TCGA GBM background (mutation rate 7.8%; Fisher p=0.018, OR=5.1).

**Detail:** The rarity of BRAF mutations in IDH1 wild-type GBM (~7.8%) makes this enrichment in responders particularly striking and potentially actionable. MAPK pathway activation in melanoma is associated with immunotherapy success, and a genome-wide CRISPR screen in melanoma implicated the MAPK pathway in modulating T cell recognition of tumor cells. The observation in GBM supports the rationale for combined MAPK inhibitor and checkpoint inhibitor therapy. The authors note a potential PTEN/PTPN11 axis for immunosuppression described in prostate cancer, suggesting cross-cancer mechanistic links.

### F4: Branched tumor evolution with neoantigen elimination characterizes responders; linear evolution characterizes non-responders

**Finding:** Evolutionary tree analysis of 5 patients with matched pre/post-immunotherapy tumor WES (3 responders, 2 non-responders) revealed that responders followed a branched model with elimination of clones harboring predicted immunogenic neoantigens, while non-responders followed a linear model of tumor progression.

**Detail:** In 3 responders, specific mutations absent after anti-PD-1 therapy were highly expressed (RPKM >5) and predicted to generate neoantigens, including CYP27B1 G194E (Patient 55), FNIP1 T409M (Patient 71), and TCF12 A605S (Patient 101). These findings are consistent with immune editing — negative selection against immunogenic clones — as a mechanism of initial response followed by later relapse. The non-responders showed a higher fraction of mutations exclusive to post-treatment tumors, consistent with classical linear evolution without immune-mediated selection. TMB and HLA-I neoepitope load did not differ significantly between responders and non-responders (median 26 vs. 40 nsSNVs, p=0.11), indicating that raw mutation count is insufficient to predict response in GBM.

### F5: Non-responders show greater T cell clonal diversity expansion after immunotherapy; responders show selective T cell recruitment

**Finding:** Across 7 patients with pre/post-treatment TCR RNA-seq, non-responders had a significantly greater increase in T cell clonal diversity (Shannon entropy) after immunotherapy compared to responders (p=0.024, Mann-Whitney U test), with the same trend in B cell immunoglobulin clonality (p=0.048).

**Detail:** Greater T cell diversity in non-responders suggests a non-selective, polyclonal immune infiltration that fails to generate focused antitumor responses — contrasting with the more selective T cell clonal expansion in responders consistent with antigen-driven responses. This pattern aligns with the observed neoantigen elimination in responders. Prior to immunotherapy, gene sets related to regulatory T cells were enriched in non-responders (p=0.037 ssGSEA), while after immunotherapy, immunosuppressive gene sets (FOXP3, STAT3, immune evasion) were paradoxically enriched in responders — potentially reflecting tumor immune editing leading toward eventual immune evasion even in initially responsive tumors. CD3+ T cell density significantly increased in PTEN-wild-type tumors after immunotherapy (p=0.0095), but not in PTEN-mutant tumors.

**Genes / cancer types:** PTEN, FOXP3 · Glioblastoma (recurrent)

## Open Questions

- Can the PTEN mutation + immunosuppressive microenvironment signature be used prospectively to exclude GBM patients unlikely to benefit from anti-PD-1 monotherapy in randomized trials?
- Does combined MAPK inhibition (via BRAF or MEK inhibitors) with PD-1 blockade improve outcomes in BRAF/PTPN11-mutant GBM, as suggested by preclinical evidence?
- What is the mechanistic basis for the FOXP3/STAT3 immunosuppressive signature upregulation in post-treatment responders, and does this represent the molecular basis for eventual resistance?
- Can deeper TCR repertoire sequencing (>7 patients) identify specific T cell clonotypes associated with durable response, enabling monitoring or adoptive transfer strategies?
- Does IDH mutation status modulate the relationship between PTEN and immunotherapy response in lower-grade gliomas treated with checkpoint inhibitors?

## Limitations

- Retrospective case series without a randomized control arm; response definitions mix radiographic and pathologic criteria, introducing potential inconsistency
- Small cohort (66 patients; only 13 responders in IDH1 wild-type analysis) with limited statistical power for subgroup analyses
- WES performed on only 33 tumors (17 patients); most patients had gene panel sequencing only, limiting neoantigen and evolutionary analyses
- TCR and Ig diversity analysis performed in only 7 patients; larger validation is needed to establish T cell clonality as a reliable predictor
- Single-cell RNA-seq performed in only 3 tumors, limiting generalizability of the CD44+ tumor cell immunosuppressive signature finding
