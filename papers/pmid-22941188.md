## Summary

Comprehensive integrative genomic analysis of small-cell lung cancer (SCLC) from the Clinical Lung Cancer Genome Project (CLCGP), including 29 exomes, 2 whole genomes, 15 transcriptomes, and 63 CNA arrays. SCLC exhibits an extremely high mutation rate (7.4±1 protein-changing mutations/Mb), the highest reported at the time among solid tumors, driven by tobacco carcinogen-induced C:G>A:T transversions. TP53 and RB1 are inactivated in 100% of cases (early, necessary events via CNLOH). Histone-modifying genes CREBBP and EP300 are the second most frequently mutated gene class (18%). Novel putative tumor suppressors SLIT2 (10%), EPHA7, and COBL are identified. FGFR1 focal amplification occurs in 6% and represents a potentially targetable alteration. MYC family members (MYCL1, MYCN, MYC) are mutually exclusively amplified in 16% of cases. Mouse SCLC (p53/Rb1 knockout) recapitulates the human CNA landscape. Data available in `sclc_clcgp` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: TP53 and RB1 biallelic inactivation is universal and an early event in SCLC pathogenesis

**Finding:** At least one allele of TP53 and RB1 is affected by a genomic event (mutation, hemizygous deletion, or LOH) in 100% of sequenced SCLC cases. Copy-neutral loss of heterozygosity (CNLOH) events are enriched at both loci, and mutations within CNLOH regions are confirmed as early clonal events based on allelic fraction analysis.

**Detail:** Broad arm-level deletions affect 3p and 13q (containing RB1) in nearly all cases; 17p losses (containing TP53) are also near-universal. A case with prior lung adenocarcinoma (sharing TP53 Val73fs) developed SCLC with an additional RB1 Arg251X mutation restricted to the SCLC component, supporting RB1 loss as a mechanistic driver of adenocarcinoma-to-SCLC trans-differentiation — a phenomenon now linked to acquired resistance to EGFR inhibitors. No difference in TP53/RB1 mutation frequency between surgically resected (early-stage) and autopsy (late-stage) samples confirms these as early, ubiquitous driver events. Mouse SCLC from p53/Rb1 knockout mice harbors amplifications of Mycl1, Mycn, and Nfib also seen in human SCLC.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, RB1 · Small-cell lung cancer

### F2: CREBBP and EP300 histone acetyltransferase mutations define the second most frequently altered gene class (18%)

**Finding:** Mutations in CREBBP and EP300 — both encoding histone acetyltransferases (HAT) — cluster within the HAT domain in 18% of SCLC cases (including point mutations, indels, and gene rearrangements). These mutations significantly reduce histone H3K18 acetylation in functional reconstitution experiments, establishing CREBBP/EP300 as bona fide SCLC tumor suppressors.

**Detail:** HAT domain mutations at CREBBP Asp1435 and EP300 Asp1399 homologous residues affect acetylase activity; Gly1411 in CREBBP is a hotspot also seen in follicular lymphoma and relapsed ALL. CREBBP/EP300 mutations are mutually exclusive (consistent with epistasis), occur predominantly as heterozygous events (haploinsufficiency), and hemizygous deletions occur in ≥10% of non-mutant cases. A CREBBP-RHBDF1 gene fusion causing N-terminal truncation is also identified. Knockdown of CREBBP in DMS114 cells increases proliferation. All HAT domain missense mutations are computationally damaging and located at the substrate-binding interface, providing structural mechanistic support. The TP53 tumor suppressive function requires CREBBP/EP300 acetylation of TP53, but since TP53 is universally lost in SCLC, CREBBP/EP300 loss must exert TP53-independent oncogenic effects.

### F3: FGFR1 focal amplification in 6% is a potentially targetable genomic alteration

**Finding:** Focal amplification of the 8p12 region encompassing FGFR1 occurs in 6% of SCLC cases (copy number ≥3.5) in the primary discovery set, validated by FISH in 51 independent specimens (also 6%, n=3 amplified). FGFR inhibitors were already being tested in FGFR1-amplified squamous-cell lung cancer, suggesting a directly applicable clinical hypothesis.

**Detail:** FGFR1 focal amplifications in SCLC are less focal than in squamous-cell lung cancer (where FGFR1 amplification was first described) but involve the same region. FGFR1 amplification in SCLC and squamous-cell lung cancer are independently confirmed, supporting a shared targetable oncogenic mechanism across histologies. CCNE1 focal amplification at 19q12 also identified. PTEN mutations (3/29, ~10%) are predicted to be phosphatase-inactivating (Gly165Glu, His61Arg, Arg130Gly), activating PI3K signaling without concurrent PIK3CA mutations — suggesting PI3K pathway as a second therapeutic axis.

### F4: MYC family amplifications (MYCL1, MYCN, MYC) are mutually exclusive and occur in 16% of SCLC

**Finding:** Focal amplifications of MYCL1 (5/63 cases, ~8%), MYCN (4/63 cases, ~6%), and MYC (1 case) occur in a mutually exclusive pattern (epistasis), affecting a total of 16% of SCLC cases. This pattern mirrors mouse SCLC data (p53/Rb1 knockout mice showing Mycl1, Mycn, Nfib amplifications) and supports MYC family activation as a critical convergent driver event.

**Detail:** All three MYC family amplifications are mutually exclusive, suggesting they operate through the same oncogenic pathway and that selection of one precludes benefit from the other. NFIB amplification is also seen in mouse SCLC, with later occurrence in primary vs. metastatic mouse tumors suggesting a progression event. SOX2 gains of 3q also observed in SCLC but less focal than in squamous-cell lung cancer. FHIT (3p14.2) is the only significant focal deletion. The mouse model validation of human CNAs provides orthogonal support for the relevance of MYC family amplification.

### F5: SLIT2 (10%), EPHA7 mutations — novel putative tumor suppressors; extreme tobacco-driven mutation rate (7.4/Mb)

**Finding:** SCLC has a mutation rate of 7.4±1 protein-changing mutations per million base pairs — among the highest of any solid tumor analyzed at the time — driven by tobacco-associated C:G>A:T transversions. Despite this high background, an integrative filtering approach (significance + expression + clustering) identifies SLIT2, EPHA7, and COBL as novel candidate tumor suppressors in addition to canonical drivers.

**Detail:** SLIT2 is mutated in 5/29 primary discovery cases (clustered mutations: 2 nonsense, 1 frameshift, 2 missense) with additional genomic losses; extended sequencing in 26 tumors + 34 cell lines confirms 10% overall frequency. SLIT2 encodes a secreted ligand for ROBO receptors involved in axon guidance; ROBO1 knockout mice develop bronchial hyperplasia; ROBO1 is a serum biomarker for SCLC. EPHA7 mutations contribute to the invasive phenotype given its role in embryonic development; EPHA7 loss was previously described in lymphoma. MLL recurrent Ile960Met mutation implicates methyltransferase activity. The integrated analytical framework (filtering by expression level, mutational clustering, COSMIC overlap) provides a generalizable template for extracting driver genes from high-background cancer genomes.

**Genes / cancer types:** SLIT2, EPHA7, MLL, COBL, TP53, RB1 · Small-cell lung cancer

## Open Questions

- Why does FGFR1 amplification occur at similar frequency (~6%) in both SCLC and squamous-cell lung cancer despite very different molecular backgrounds (universal TP53+RB1 loss in SCLC) — does the oncogenic mechanism differ?
- Can CREBBP/EP300 HAT domain mutations be therapeutically exploited through synthetic lethality with EZH2 inhibitors or other epigenetic dependencies?
- Does RB1 loss mechanistically drive EGFR-mutant adenocarcinoma-to-SCLC trans-differentiation, and can restoration of RB1 function prevent or reverse this resistance mechanism?
- Are SLIT2 mutations in SCLC functionally loss-of-function for the SLIT-ROBO1 interaction, and does this contribute to the characteristically aggressive metastatic behavior of SCLC?
- Given the universal TP53/RB1 loss, what genomic events distinguish the ~84% of SCLC without MYC family amplification in terms of alternative proliferative driver mechanisms?

## Limitations

- Small primary cohort (29 exomes) due to rarity of surgically resected SCLC specimens; most SCLC biopsied by bronchoscopy without adequate tissue for sequencing
- WES only (no WGS except 2 samples); structural variants and rearrangements comprehensively characterized only in 2 genomes and 15 transcriptomes
- No matched normal tissue for 5 cell lines; germline vs. somatic distinction potentially imperfect in cell line data
- Validation set for SLIT2 and CREBBP/EP300 used Sanger/454 sequencing of targeted regions rather than WES, potentially missing other alterations
- No clinical outcome or treatment response data; actionability predictions (FGFR1, PTEN) are purely genomic hypotheses at this stage
