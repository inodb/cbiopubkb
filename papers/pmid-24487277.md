## Summary

Multiregion whole-exome sequencing (M-seq) of 10 clear cell renal cell carcinomas (ccRCC) — sampling 2–9 spatially distinct regions per tumor plus matched normal, with ultra-deep amplicon validation (>400×) — resolves the branched evolutionary architecture and clonal hierarchy of ccRCC. Key findings: intratumor heterogeneity (ITH) affects 67% of mutations on average and is present in all 10 tumors. 73–75% of all driver events (mutations and copy-number alterations) are subclonal. Only chromosome 3p loss and VHL inactivation are ubiquitous (truncal) events. PBRM1 is a facultative early event (truncal in ~60% of cases harboring it). All other driver gene mutations — including SETD2, BAP1, KDM5C, PTEN, PIK3CA, MTOR — are subclonal and spatially separated. Parallel evolution targeting SETD2, BAP1, PIK3CA, and PBRM1 is detected in four additional tumors, indicating strong selective pressure on these pathways. Single biopsies underestimate driver prevalence dramatically (e.g., TP53 is detected in 6% of biopsies but 40% of cases), with implications for biomarker development. Data available in `ccrcc_irc_2014` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Intratumor heterogeneity is universal and the majority of driver mutations are subclonal

**Finding:** On average 67% (range 28–92%) of nonsynonymous somatic mutations are heterogeneous (not present in all sampled regions) across all 10 tumors. 73% of driver mutations (36/49) and 75% of driver copy-number alterations (57/76) are subclonal. ITH increased with each additional biopsy without reaching saturation in 7 of 10 tumors, indicating that M-seq still underestimates true heterogeneity.

**Detail:** All tumors — whether treatment-naïve, everolimus-pretreated, or sunitinib-pretreated — showed substantial mutational ITH, indicating that prior therapy does not account for the observed heterogeneity. Six of 62 tumor regions showed evidence of multiple intraregional subclones (detectable by bimodal VAF distributions), further confirming the underestimation of heterogeneity by regional sampling alone. Driver mutations that appear clonally dominant within a single region (71% of branch drivers) may still be absent from other regions, challenging single-biopsy VAF-based assessments of clonal dominance.

**Genes / cancer types:** VHL, PBRM1, SETD2, BAP1 · Clear cell renal cell carcinoma (ccRCC)

### F2: VHL inactivation and chromosome 3p loss are obligatory truncal founder events in ccRCC

**Finding:** VHL-inactivating alterations (somatic mutation or DNA methylation in EV003) were present on the phylogenetic trunk of all 10 tumors. Chromosome 3p loss was the only driver somatic copy-number alteration (SCNA) found in all regions of all 10 tumors, sometimes evolving into copy-neutral LOH in subclones. These are the only two events consistent across the entire cohort, establishing them as the obligatory early drivers of ccRCC tumorigenesis.

**Detail:** VHL inactivation through DNA methylation was confirmed in EV003 by bisulfite treatment and is functionally equivalent to somatic mutation. Chromosome 3p loss encompasses both VHL (3p25) and other candidate tumor suppressors, and its universality exceeds even VHL mutation frequency, suggesting additional 3p targets. In contrast, chromosomal events on 4q, 8p, 14q, and 5q — while found ubiquitously in some tumors — occurred as heterogeneous (branch) events in other tumors, meaning they are not obligatory early events. Targeting VHL or its downstream synthetic lethal vulnerabilities may thus be the most broadly applicable therapeutic strategy.

### F3: PBRM1 is a facultative early event; all other ccRCC drivers are subclonal

**Finding:** PBRM1, the second most commonly mutated gene in ccRCC, was located on the trunk in 3 of 6 tumors harboring a PBRM1 mutation (50% truncal rate), making it a "facultative early event." All other driver gene mutations — SETD2 (30%), BAP1 (40%), KDM5C (10%), PTEN (20%), PIK3CA (20%), MTOR (10%), ATM (10%), TP53 (40% per-case) — were exclusively located on branches (subclonal in all cases).

**Detail:** BAP1 mutations and chromosome 9p loss are both markers of poor prognosis in ccRCC and were heterogeneous in every case in which they were identified, calling into question their use as reliable single-biopsy biomarkers. TP53 was detected in 6% of individual biopsies but 40% of cases when all regions were examined — a 6.7-fold underestimation from single biopsies. PI3K-mTOR pathway alterations (PTEN, PIK3CA, TSC2, MTOR combined) were detected in 28% of biopsies but 60% of cases. These data challenge TCGA-derived prevalence estimates for all ccRCC driver genes except VHL/PBRM1.

### F4: Parallel evolution targeting the same driver genes provides evidence for strong positive selection

**Finding:** Parallel evolution — independent mutations converging on the same gene or complex in distinct spatially separated subclones — was identified in 6 of 10 tumors. SETD2 was targeted by parallel mutations in 3 tumors (and in 2 previously reported cases). PIK3CA had two activating mutations (E545K and H1047R) in spatially separated subclones of EV005. BAP1 had three independent truncating mutations in RMH008. PBRM1 had two independent frameshifts in RMH004, plus concurrent ARID1A and SMARCA4 mutations.

**Detail:** This pattern of convergent evolution targeting SETD2, BAP1, PIK3CA, and SWI/SNF complex members demonstrates that these pathways confer strong selective advantage in ccRCC. The phenomenon of parallel evolution affecting multiple subunits of the same multiprotein complex (SWI/SNF in RMH004) has not been previously observed. No tumor showed parallel evolution for a gene in which it lacked any mutation, suggesting that the particular evolutionary trajectory each tumor takes is determined by early (likely stochastic) events that set the stage for subsequent selection.

### F5: Mutational spectrum shifts during tumor evolution — C>T transitions at CpG sites increase in branches

**Finding:** The proportion of C>T transitions at CpG sites is significantly higher in branch mutations than in trunk mutations across the cohort (q=0.007), and this increase is observed in 8 of 10 tumors. Conversely, A>G transitions are significantly decreased in branch mutations (q=0.007). These findings indicate that the mutational processes generating diversity during ccRCC progression differ from those operative in the founding tumor cell.

**Detail:** C>T transitions at CpG sites reflect spontaneous deamination of methylated cytosines — a process that dominates the background mammalian mutation rate. The progressive enrichment of this signature in branches may reflect altered DNA methylation landscapes or reduced repair efficiency during tumor evolution, consistent with the high frequency of SETD2 (H3K36 methyltransferase) and other epigenetic regulator mutations in subclonal populations. This contrasts with breast cancer, where this signature decreases during progression, highlighting cancer-type specificity of mutational process dynamics.

## Open Questions

- Do subclonal BAP1 and chromosome 9p loss events confer the same poor prognosis as truncal events, and can liquid biopsy or multi-region biopsy strategies reliably detect them?
- What environmental or microenvironmental factors determine whether individual ccRCCs acquire PBRM1 vs BAP1 as early driver events — and do these alternative evolutionary trajectories predict differential therapeutic sensitivity?
- Can targeting truncal VHL-dependent synthetic lethalities (e.g., HIF2α inhibitors) prevent the emergence of subclonal driver diversity?
- Is parallel SETD2 evolution a universal feature of ccRCC or specific to this patient cohort, and does it predict outcome or response to epigenetic therapies?
- Do metastases recapitulate the heterogeneity seen within primary tumors, or do they arise from single founding clones? (Only 3 metastatic biopsies were available in this study.)

## Limitations

- Small cohort of 10 cases limits statistical power; prevalence estimates per case are highly imprecise
- WES depth of >70× per region is insufficient to detect all subclonal events; ultra-deep amplicon validation covers only validated SNVs
- Only SNVs and indels were subjected to amplicon validation; SCNA phylogenies are reconstructed from exome coverage data and may contain errors
- Metastatic tissue was available for only 3 cases, limiting conclusions about primary-to-metastasis evolutionary dynamics
- Most tumors were pre-treated (6 with everolimus, 1 with sunitinib), which may have influenced subclonal compositions, though tumor size changes were minimal
- Intraregional subclone reconstruction was limited by the number of mutations detectable by WES
