## Summary

This study performed drug sensitivity profiling on patient-derived organoids (PDOs) from pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) to identify genomic and transcriptomic correlates of chemotherapy response, with the goal of enabling personalised treatment selection. By correlating ex-vivo organoid drug response with molecular features and clinical outcomes, the study identified subsets of patients who are common responders across multiple chemotherapy regimens. SMAD4 loss and basal-like transcriptomic subtype were associated with chemotherapy sensitivity patterns, while genomic alterations in canonical PDAC driver genes (KRAS, TP53, CDKN2A, SMAD4) were characterised across the organoid biobank. Clinical correlation with patient outcomes supported the predictive value of the organoid drug response platform. Primary organoid drug response data are not directly available in cBioPortal; genomic data from PDAC studies such as `paad_tcga_pan_can_atlas_2018` provide context for pathway interpretation.

## Key Findings

### F1: Patient-derived organoids recapitulate canonical PDAC driver gene frequencies and can predict clinical chemotherapy response

**Finding:** PDOs established from PDAC patients faithfully recapitulate the canonical genomic landscape of PDAC — with near-universal KRAS mutations, frequent TP53 mutations, and variable SMAD4/CDKN2A alterations — and ex-vivo drug response in organoids correlates with clinical chemotherapy response in matched patients.

**Detail:** KRAS mutations (predominantly G12D, G12V) were present in >90% of organoid lines; TP53 mutations in ~75%; SMAD4 loss and CDKN2A alterations at lower frequencies consistent with primary PDAC cohorts. The organoid platform provides a functional complement to DNA sequencing — capturing drug pharmacodynamics in patient-specific tumour biology. Correlation of organoid drug sensitivity area-under-the-curve (AUC) with clinical outcome (radiographic response, progression-free survival) provides prospective evidence that the model is clinically predictive rather than merely descriptive.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `TP53`, `SMAD4`, `CDKN2A` · Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC)

### F2: SMAD4 loss associates with distinct chemotherapy sensitivity patterns in PDAC organoids

**Finding:** SMAD4 loss — occurring in ~55% of PDAC — correlates with altered sensitivity to platinum-based chemotherapy and oxaliplatin (a component of FOLFIRINOX) in patient-derived organoid profiling, identifying a potential genomic biomarker for treatment selection in PDAC.

**Detail:** SMAD4 (also known as DPC4) is a TGF-β pathway tumour suppressor lost through homozygous deletion or mutation in approximately half of PDACs. In organoid profiling, SMAD4 status modulates sensitivity to specific chemotherapy agents — particularly platinum compounds — possibly through effects on DNA damage response and replication stress. These findings suggest that SMAD4 status could help guide the choice between FOLFIRINOX (oxaliplatin-containing) and gemcitabine+nab-paclitaxel regimens in newly diagnosed PDAC patients, a decision currently made without molecular guidance.

**Genes / cancer types:** `SMAD4` · Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC)

### F3: Transcriptomic subtyping of organoids identifies basal-like and classical PDAC subtypes with distinct drug sensitivity profiles

**Finding:** RNA-Seq based transcriptomic subtyping of PDAC organoids into basal-like and classical subtypes — analogous to subtypes defined in primary tumour cohorts — reveals differential chemotherapy sensitivity, with basal-like organoids showing distinct drug response patterns compared to classical organoids.

**Detail:** PDAC transcriptomic subtypes (basal-like / squamous vs. classical / pancreatic progenitor) were originally described in primary tumour cohorts (Moffitt et al. 2015, Bailey et al. 2016). The demonstration that PDOs faithfully recapitulate these transcriptomic subtypes validates the organoid model as a faithful representation of in-vivo biology. The finding that basal-like organoids have distinct chemotherapy sensitivity — potentially including differential sensitivity to FOLFIRINOX vs. gemcitabine-based regimens — provides a mechanistic rationale for subtype-guided treatment selection. Transcriptomic subtype is not yet in routine clinical use for treatment selection in PDAC.

### F4: Common responders to multiple chemotherapy regimens are identifiable, enabling potential stratification for clinical trials

**Finding:** A subset of PDAC organoids exhibited broad chemotherapy sensitivity across multiple regimens (FOLFIRINOX components and gemcitabine-based regimens), defining "common responders" — a potentially favourable patient subgroup that might be identified prospectively through organoid profiling before treatment initiation.

**Detail:** The identification of common responders as a biologically distinct subset rather than a continuous spectrum of drug sensitivity supports the concept of personalised chemotherapy selection in PDAC. Conversely, organoids resistant to all tested regimens may represent candidates for investigational or novel therapy. The organoid profiling platform thus provides functional complementarity to genomic biomarkers: some responders may not carry a specific genomic alteration, while some carriers of a putative biomarker may not respond in the organoid assay, reflecting inter-patient biology beyond DNA sequence.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-PDAC (all driver genes) · Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC)

### F5: Genomic correlates of organoid drug response overlap with clinically actionable alterations in PDAC

**Finding:** Genomic alterations associated with organoid drug response patterns in PDAC — including DNA damage response gene mutations (BRCA1/2, ATM) and SMAD4 loss — overlap with clinically actionable or potentially actionable alterations, supporting integration of organoid profiling with genomic sequencing for treatment selection.

**Detail:** BRCA1/2 and ATM germline and somatic mutations confer sensitivity to platinum-based chemotherapy and PARP inhibitors in PDAC. The organoid model confirms this sensitivity at the functional level, providing pharmacodynamic evidence complementary to genomic biomarkers. SMAD4 loss modulates platinum sensitivity, as noted above. These convergent signals from functional and genomic characterisation support the development of multi-modal biomarker frameworks for PDAC treatment selection, integrating DNA sequencing, organoid drug testing, and potentially transcriptomic subtyping.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRCA1`, `BRCA2`, `ATM`, `SMAD4` · Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC)

## Open Questions

- Can organoid drug response be validated as a prospective clinical biomarker in a randomised trial comparing organoid-guided vs. standard chemotherapy selection in newly diagnosed PDAC?
- What is the minimum organoid establishment success rate needed to make this approach clinically feasible, and what are the barriers (tissue adequacy, time to result, cost)?
- Does the organoid drug sensitivity profile remain stable across serial biopsies as tumours evolve under treatment pressure?
- Can transcriptomic subtype (basal-like vs. classical) serve as a standalone surrogate for organoid drug response without requiring functional testing?
- Do organoid drug response results generalise from tertiary academic centre populations to community-based PDAC patients with less extensive prior workup?
- Can organoid profiling predict response to immunotherapy or targeted agents (KRAS G12C inhibitors, PARP inhibitors) beyond classical chemotherapy regimens?

## Limitations

- Organoid establishment success rate is limited (<50% of biopsies yield viable PDO lines), introducing selection bias toward certain tumour types and patient subgroups
- Organoid models lack tumour microenvironment components (stroma, immune cells, vasculature) — potentially limiting prediction of response to immunotherapy or stroma-dependent drug effects
- Clinical correlation cohorts are small and retrospective; prospective validation in randomised trials is pending
- Time from biopsy to organoid drug response results may exceed the clinical window for treatment initiation in rapidly progressive PDAC
- Drug concentrations and exposure conditions in ex-vivo organoid assays may not perfectly model in-vivo pharmacokinetics/pharmacodynamics
