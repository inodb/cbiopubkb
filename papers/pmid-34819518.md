## Summary

This prospective study characterizes the molecular and clinical profile of colorectal cancer (CRC) in Nigeria (West Africa), comparing 380 Nigerian patients to 458 MSKCC patients using MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing, IHC, and methylation analysis on 64 and 1,145 tumor specimens, respectively. The central finding is that Nigerian CRC has a markedly different molecular landscape from Western cohorts: MSI-high frequency is 28.1% — more than triple the MSKCC rate (8.5%) and double the TCGA rate (14.2%). Among MSS tumors, APC mutations and WNT pathway alterations are significantly less common (37% vs. 76%), while RAS pathway alterations are more prevalent (76% vs. 60%). BRAF V600E is absent from Nigerian MSI-H tumors. Nigerian patients present younger, with more rectal and stage IV disease, and have dramatically worse outcomes (median OS 11.9 vs. not-reached months at MSKCC). These findings indicate that Nigerian CRC is driven by distinct molecular pathways, potentially necessitating region-specific diagnostic algorithms and treatment guidelines.

## Key Findings

### F1: MSI-high frequency is 28.1% in Nigerian CRC — more than 3× the MSKCC rate

**Finding:** 28.1% of Nigerian CRC tumors are MSI-high by MSK-IMPACT, significantly exceeding rates at MSKCC (8.5%) and TCGA (14.2%, p<0.001). This excess persists in patients ≤45 years old (18% Nigeria vs. 9.5% MSKCC). MSI-H is more common in right-sided Nigerian tumors (66.7%). The rate of MSI-H is also higher than among African-American MSKCC patients (28.1% vs. 7.2%).

**Detail:** MMR IHC shows 21.3% deficiency (20/94), consistent with MSI-H by sequencing. Germline analysis of 18 MSI-H Nigerian patients identified only 4 (22.2%) with high-penetrance pathogenic germline variants: 3 Lynch syndrome MMR mutations (MLH1 x2, MSH2 x1) and 1 incidental BRCA1. This is similar to expected Lynch rates in HIC MSI-H populations (11–18.5%), suggesting germline Lynch is not the primary explanation for the elevated MSI burden. Among MSS tumors, 2 additional germline mutations were identified (1 APC/FAP, 1 MLH1).

### F2: MLH1 methylation and CIMP are not the dominant MSI pathway in Nigerian CRC

**Finding:** In contrast to high-income country CRC where ~80% of sporadic MSI-H tumors arise from MLH1 promoter hypermethylation, only 28.6% (2/7) of MSI-H Nigerian specimens showed MLH1 methylation, and only 8% were CIMP-high. This suggests that an alternative molecular pathway — such as biallelic somatic inactivation of MMR genes — drives MSI tumorigenesis in Nigerian patients.

**Detail:** Methylation analysis was performed on 25 Nigerian specimens (convenience sampling). 32% (8/25) were highly methylated overall. 7/25 were MSI-H; only 2 of these had MLH1 methylation. Only 2 of 25 total specimens were CIMP-high. BRAF V600E, which is associated with sporadic MLH1-methylated MSI-H CRC in Western populations, was absent from all 18 Nigerian MSI-H tumors (vs. 25.8% MSKCC, p=0.01). TCF7L2 alteration was more frequent in Nigerian MSI-H patients (64.3% vs. 34.2% MSKCC, p=0.04). These observations collectively suggest a distinct etiologic pathway for MSI in this population.

### F3: APC and WNT pathway alterations are markedly reduced in Nigerian MSS CRC

**Finding:** Among MSS tumors, APC mutations are present in only 36.9% of Nigerian patients vs. 76.0% at MSKCC (p<0.001). The WNT pathway as a whole is altered in 47.8% of Nigerian MSS CRC vs. 81.9% MSKCC (p<0.001). This suppression of WNT pathway alterations holds by sidedness and is not compensated by CTNNB1 activating mutations (4.4% Nigeria vs. 3.7% MSKCC, p=0.68).

**Detail:** Among right-sided Nigerian MSS tumors, only 25% (2/8) had APC mutations vs. 68.4% of MSKCC non-African-American right-sided tumors. This difference persists in left-sided disease (42.1% Nigeria vs. 78.8% MSKCC). TP53 mutation rates were not significantly different overall (71.7% Nigeria vs. 77.5% MSKCC, p=0.37), but left/right sidedness-stratified differences were noted (25% TP53 in right-sided Nigerian tumors vs. 66.7% left-sided). PI3K pathway alterations were more common in Nigeria: PIK3R1 (8.7% vs. 2.2%, p=0.02) and PIK3CB (4.3% vs. 0.5%, p=0.04).

### F4: RAS pathway alterations are elevated in Nigerian MSS CRC, including right-sided KRAS predominance

**Finding:** RAS pathway alterations are more prevalent in Nigerian MSS CRC (76.1%) versus MSKCC non-African-American patients (59.6%, p=0.03). KRAS mutations are present in 56.5% of Nigerian MSS tumors (vs. 44.1% MSKCC, p=0.13 — trend only), and are notably common in right-sided (62.5%) and left-sided (55.3%) disease alike. This elevated RAS signaling in an APC-low context implies a distinct oncogenic initiation pathway in Nigerian CRC.

**Detail:** In MSS Nigerian tumors, the combination of low APC/WNT and high RAS pathway activation is biologically coherent — RAS activation may substitute for WNT in driving proliferation in this population. KRAS mutation frequency in Nigerian MSI-H tumors (61.1%) was higher than MSKCC MSI-H (41.1%), though not statistically significant (p=0.20). The absence of BRAF V600E in all Nigerian MSI-H tumors and the higher KRAS rate collectively distinguish Nigerian MSI-H CRC from the classical sporadic serrated/CIMP pathway of high-income countries.

### F5: Nigerian CRC patients present at younger age, more advanced stage, and with dramatically worse survival

**Finding:** Median age at diagnosis is 55.8 years in Nigeria vs. 60.0 years at MSKCC (p<0.001). 53.8% of Nigerian patients present with stage IV disease vs. 36.2% at MSKCC (p<0.001). 50.8% have rectal primaries vs. 33.7% at MSKCC (p<0.001). Median OS is 11.9 months (Nigeria) vs. not-reached (MSKCC, p<0.001). Recurrence-free survival is also dramatically shorter (12.5 vs. 60.4 months, p<0.001).

**Detail:** The survival disparity persists even after stratification by stage: stage IV OS is 7.3 months in Nigeria vs. 34.3 months at MSKCC (p<0.001). Access to standard care differs significantly: fewer Nigerian patients received neoadjuvant chemotherapy (57.5% vs. 83.8%) or surgery (71.0% vs. 87.3%). Peritoneal metastasis is more common in Nigeria (30.3% vs. 18.1%), while liver (26.8% vs. 41.0%) and lung (10.5% vs. 29.3%) metastasis are less frequent. These phenotypic differences may partially reflect the different molecular biology (e.g., higher MSI-H rate could influence prognosis but MSI status did not significantly affect OS by cohort, p=0.15).

## Open Questions

- What alternative molecular mechanisms (biallelic somatic MMR inactivation, novel epigenetic pathways) account for the high MSI-H burden in Nigerian CRC beyond Lynch syndrome and MLH1 methylation?
- Is the low APC frequency in Nigerian MSS CRC driven by different environmental exposures, germline modifiers, or ancestry-linked mutational processes — and what is the cell of origin?
- How do the findings from this Nigerian cohort apply to the broader West African and sub-Saharan African CRC landscape? Are similar patterns seen in Ghana, Kenya, or South Africa?
- Do Nigerian patients with MSI-H CRC respond to checkpoint immunotherapy similarly to patients from high-income countries? Prospective data are needed.
- Can the molecular differences be translated into region-specific screening tools (e.g., optimized MSI testing algorithms) that are feasible in low-resource settings?

## Limitations

- Only 64/380 Nigerian patients had MSK-IMPACT molecular profiling (convenience sample), limiting statistical power for subgroup analyses; the sequenced subset may not fully represent all Nigerian CRC patients.
- No matched germline sequencing for most Nigerian patients; systematic Lynch syndrome assessment was limited.
- Methylation analysis was performed on only 25 specimens (convenience sample), making CIMP/MLH1 methylation findings provisional.
- Short follow-up for the Nigerian cohort (median 16.9 months) compared to MSKCC (64.8 months), limiting mature survival analysis.
- Treatment disparities (access to chemotherapy, surgery) between Nigeria and MSKCC confound survival comparisons and prevent attribution of outcome differences to molecular biology alone.
