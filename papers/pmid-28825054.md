## Summary

This prospective clinical genomic profiling study uses MSK-IMPACT to sequence 504 tumors from 451 patients with prostate cancer spanning all three clinical disease states: locoregional, metastatic noncastrate, and metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC). This is distinct from prior studies (TCGA, SU2C-PCF) that each sampled only a single disease state. Key findings include: 27% of patients harbored germline or somatic DDR gene alterations (BRCA2, BRCA1, ATM, CHEK2) — with germline analysis alone capturing only ~half; *AR* amplification/mutation dominates mCRPC enrichment; *APC* alterations are specifically enriched in metastatic states (a candidate metastasis driver); *ATM* is enriched in mCRPC (castration resistance); *TP53* and *BRCA2* are early clonal events identified in primary tumors that later developed metastases; and 3% of patients harbor MMR gene alterations. The study establishes the clinical feasibility of tumor-normal sequencing in prostate cancer — including challenging bone biopsies — and argues for routine germline plus somatic profiling for all patients with advanced prostate cancer.

## Key Findings

### F1: 27% of patients harbored germline or somatic alterations in BRCA2, BRCA1, ATM, or CHEK2 — germline analysis alone captures only ~half

**Finding:** 22% of patients carried somatic alterations in DDR genes involved in homologous recombination (BRCA2, BRCA1, ATM, FANCA, RAD50, PALB2, CDK12). Of 221 patients who underwent germline analysis, 42 (19%) had a pathogenic germline mutation: BRCA2 (9%), CHEK2 (4%), ATM (2%), BRCA1 (1%), FH (1%), and PMS2/NBN/PALB2/BRIP1 (<1% each). Combining germline and somatic analysis, 27% of patients had an alteration in BRCA2, BRCA1, ATM, or CHEK2. Critically, germline-only analysis accounted for approximately half of DDR-altered patients — indicating that somatic sequencing is necessary to identify the full DDR-deficient population.

**Detail:** The high prevalence of DDR gene alterations has immediate therapeutic relevance given evidence of PARP inhibitor sensitivity in BRCA-deficient prostate cancer (later confirmed by TRITON2, PROfound trials). Somatic alterations in BRCA2 were predominantly truncating mutations (loss-of-function), while somatic ATM alterations were primarily missense mutations across coding regions. CDK12 alterations were primarily truncating. 3% of patients had somatic MMR gene alterations (MSH2, MLH1, PMS2, MSH6), accounting for the majority of hypermutated cases (>20 somatic mutations on MSK-IMPACT), making them candidates for immune checkpoint blockade.

### F2: AR amplification/mutation dominates mCRPC; AR alterations arise as treatment-related acquired events

**Finding:** *AR* amplification/mutation was the most enriched alteration in mCRPC compared to locoregional disease and compared to metastatic noncastrate disease, serving as an internal positive control consistent with the established role of AR as the primary driver of castration resistance. AR antiandrogen resistance mutations were identified in mCRPC, including an F877L enzalutamide/ARN509 resistance mutation in a patient who progressed after 4 years on ARN509. Notably, 4% alteration frequency in *AR* was observed in metastatic noncastrate disease — tumors likely transitioning to castration resistance that had not yet manifested clinically. Four locoregional tumors harbored AR mutations including H875Y (flutamide resistance) in a treatment-naïve prostatectomy patient who had received dutasteride for BPH.

**Detail:** Matched tumor analysis (44 patients with >1 tumor profiled, including 16 with primary + metastasis) showed that *AR* alterations arose late — not present in primary tumors, appearing only in castration-resistant metastases. This contrasts sharply with *TP53* and *BRCA2* alterations that were identified in primary tumors and all subsequent samples (early clonal events). mCRPC tumors displayed the highest burden of copy number alterations overall, with recurrent losses at 6q, 8p, 13q, 16q and gains at 1q, 3q, 7, 8q, and X chromosome.

### F3: APC alterations enriched specifically in metastatic disease states — implicating Wnt/β-catenin pathway in prostate cancer metastasis

**Finding:** Cross-disease-state enrichment analysis identified *APC* as the gene most specifically enriched in metastatic prostate cancer (both metastatic noncastrate and mCRPC) versus locoregional disease. *APC* enrichment was observed in metastatic states relative to locoregional disease, implicating Wnt/β-catenin pathway dysregulation as a driver of metastasis independent of castration resistance. 15% of all patients harbored somatic Wnt/β-catenin pathway alterations, including *APC*, *CTNNB1*, and *RNF43*.

**Detail:** *APC* alterations are primarily deletions and truncating mutations predicted to inactivate the protein — releasing β-catenin from APC-mediated degradation and activating Wnt target genes. *CTNNB1* alterations were primarily hotspot N-terminal missense mutations preventing phosphorylation and degradation of β-catenin, converging on the same pathway. Conversely, *SPOP* mutations were enriched in locoregional disease compared to mCRPC, suggesting that SPOP-mutant tumors may have increased sensitivity to ADT — an unexpected finding warranting laboratory validation. Only *APC* and *ARID5A* were enriched in metastatic noncastrate vs. locoregional disease, making *APC* the strongest candidate gene for prostate cancer metastasis.

### F4: ATM alterations enriched in mCRPC versus earlier disease states — implicating DDR defects in the development of castration resistance

**Finding:** *ATM* emerged as enriched in mCRPC compared to both locoregional disease and metastatic noncastrate disease, making it a candidate driver of castration resistance distinct from its role as a metastasis driver. *FANCA* and *CDK12* showed similar (though not always statistically significant) trends toward enrichment in mCRPC. The combined enrichment of multiple DDR genes (*ATM*, *FANCA*, *CDK12*) in mCRPC suggests that DNA repair defects may contribute to castration resistance — either directly or indirectly through genomic instability driving AR-related alterations.

**Detail:** The enrichment of *ATM* specifically in the mCRPC state (not just in metastatic noncastrate) distinguishes it from *APC* (which is enriched in all metastatic states). *ATM* alterations were primarily missense mutations across the ATM coding region (not predominantly truncating), which may have distinct functional consequences compared to the frameshift/truncating *ATM* alterations more commonly observed in ATM-deficient leukemia or breast cancer. The functional validation of ATM's role in castration resistance remains an active research priority.

### F5: TP53 and BRCA2 are early clonal truncal events; matched multi-tumor phylogenetics reveals disease evolution

**Finding:** In 16 patients with matched primary localized tumor + subsequent metastasis, *TP53* alterations were present in all tumors from each affected patient — including at high cancer cell fraction (≥0.9) in metastases — confirming early clonal truncal origin. Similarly, somatic *BRCA2* alterations were present in all matched tumors from affected patients, indicating early acquisition. In contrast, *AR* alterations arose late (not present in primary tumors of castration-sensitive cases). In patient P-0002149, an activating *PIK3CA* E545K mutation arose de novo in a recurrent tumor nearly 3 years after radical prostatectomy — illustrating late acquisition of actionable alterations.

**Detail:** Phylogenetic analysis of 2–3 matched tumors per patient confirmed the truncal nature of *TP53* alterations. Epigenetic modulating genes (*EP300*, *KDM5A*) were also identified as truncal in one patient's metastatic tumors. *KMT2D* appeared subclonally in mechronous bone metastases from distinct sites but was absent from a liver metastasis in the same patient — suggesting site-specific clonal divergence. This longitudinal, phylogenetic view of prostate cancer evolution demonstrates that early molecular features (*TP53*, *BRCA2*) may identify patients with high risk of metastatic progression — warranting prospective validation.

## Open Questions

- Does *ATM* alteration status (specifically somatic missense vs. truncating) differentially predict PARP inhibitor response, as subsequently observed in breast cancer?
- Can *APC* alterations in localized prostate cancer identify patients at elevated risk of metastatic progression who warrant more aggressive local or systemic therapy?
- Does *SPOP* mutation status predict differential response to androgen deprivation therapy, and can this guide initial treatment selection?
- Are germline DDR gene alterations (*BRCA2*, *ATM*, *CHEK2*) equivalent to somatic-only alterations in predicting PARP inhibitor response — or does germline status confer distinct biology?
- Do hypermutated MMR-deficient prostate cancers (3% of this cohort) respond to pembrolizumab as robustly as colorectal MSI-high tumors (later validated by KEYNOTE-158)?
- What fraction of prostate cancer patients with bone-restricted disease could benefit from liquid biopsy (ctDNA) to overcome the 42–52% sequencing failure rate for bone biopsies?

## Limitations

- Bone biopsy success rate only 42–52%, causing selection bias toward lymph node and soft tissue metastases in the mCRPC cohort; bone-restricted disease underrepresented
- Germline analysis only in 221/451 patients (those who specifically consented); germline alteration rate may be underestimated in the full cohort
- ATM missense variants of uncertain significance are likely included in the 2% germline ATM rate; functional impact not systematically validated
- Cross-disease-state comparisons affected by tumor tissue age differences (archival vs. fresh) and potential assay performance differences between prostate and metastatic samples
- SPOP enrichment in locoregional disease (candidate ADT sensitivity predictor) requires functional validation and cannot be tested retrospectively from this dataset alone
- Sample size per disease state limited (especially metastatic noncastrate, n~50), reducing power for enrichment analyses
- Locoregional cohort in this dataset is enriched for high-risk disease (>50% Gleason 8–10) relative to TCGA; results may not generalize to average-risk localized prostate cancer
