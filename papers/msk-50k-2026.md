## Summary

This paper characterizes cancer type-specific patterns of driver alterations across 54,331 matched tumor-normal MSK-IMPACT samples spanning 448 histological subtypes (the MSK-50K cohort). The central finding is that one-third of all driver alterations arise in "non-canonical" cancer type contexts and exhibit distinct properties: higher subclonality, later emergence, and less biallelic TSG inactivation compared to canonical drivers. The full dataset is publicly available on cBioPortal (`msk_impact_50k_2026`).

## Key Findings

### F1: 164 newly identified cancer hotspots, mostly in TSGs

**Finding:** Leveraging cohort size, 164 new statistically significant hotspot codons were identified; 99% were mutated in ≤0.1% of patients but collectively affect 5% of patients.

**Detail:** 52% were already annotated as oncogenic by OncoKB. 70% (114/164) fall in tumor suppressor genes. Notable new hotspots include `KEAP1`/`STK11` (lung), `SMAD2/3/4`/`TCF7L2` (GI), and three `RRAS2` hotspots (endometrial and germ cell tumors). New hotspots show clonality patterns indistinguishable from known hotspots, supporting pathogenicity.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KEAP1`, `STK11`, `SMAD2`, `SMAD3`, `SMAD4`, `TCF7L2`, `RRAS2`, `PIK3CA`, `KRAS`, `EGFR` · Pan-cancer (lung, GI, germ cell enriched)

**Data availability:** `available`
> All mutations are loaded in `msk_impact_50k_2026`. Hotspot annotations are available via OncoKB overlay in cBioPortal. The 164 novel hotspots are not yet in OncoKB but could be explored as specific variant queries.

**Tool support:** `api`
> To reproduce: query specific variants (e.g., RRAS2 G23/G24) via Mutations tab or API. Statistical hotspot discovery (Poisson model adjusting for gene/position/sample mutation rates) requires custom code, not available natively.

**Concepts:** #hotspots #TSG #rare-drivers #cohort-size

### F2: ~1/3 of driver alterations arise in non-canonical cancer type contexts

**Finding:** 32% of all driver alterations occur in cancer types not significantly associated with the altered gene, and these non-canonical drivers exhibit distinct biological properties.

**Detail:** Compared to canonical drivers, non-canonical drivers are: significantly more subclonal, arise later in longitudinal sequencing, show lower rates of biallelic TSG inactivation (68% of TSGs show this difference), and occur in patients with higher burdens of high-penetrance germline variants (8% vs 5%, p=2e-31).

**Genes / cancer types:** `PIK3CA`, `BRAF`, `TP53`, `ERBB2`, `NRAS` examples cited · Pan-cancer

**Data availability:** `available`
> Mutation and CNA data in `msk_impact_50k_2026`. Cancer type labels and clinical metadata available. Clonality estimates are in the dataset (CCF values loaded as clinical/genomic attributes).

**Tool support:** `api`
> Cancer type filtering + mutation queries are native. Clonality-stratified analysis (CCF per variant per cancer type) requires API + scripting. The canonical/non-canonical classification requires defining the 1,215 gene–cancer type pairs from the paper (available as supplementary data).

**Concepts:** #driver-context #tissue-specificity #clonality #canonical-vs-noncanonical

### F3: 1,215 canonical gene–cancer type associations defined

**Finding:** Systematic enrichment analysis across 96 cancer types (≥50 patients) identified 1,141 significant + 74 borderline gene–cancer type associations, creating a map of canonical oncogenic drivers per histology.

**Detail:** Median of 4 driver alterations per tumor; 2 in canonical genes. 153 associations involve rare alterations (<2% frequency) including well-known genes in unexpected contexts (e.g., `ERBB2` activating in cutaneous melanoma and pancreatic ADCA; `NRAS` in LUAD).

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer; `ERBB2`, `NRAS`, `RRAS`, `CUL3`, `ARAF` highlighted as rare canonical drivers

**Data availability:** `available`
> Full cohort in cBioPortal. The canonical association table is available as supplementary data from the paper.

**Tool support:** `api`
> OncoPrint and cancer type–level mutation frequencies are native. Statistical enrichment analysis (Fisher's exact + mutual exclusivity testing with multiple correction) requires custom scripting. The canonical association list from the paper could be loaded as a custom gene set.

**Concepts:** #canonical-drivers #tissue-specificity #pan-cancer-map #mutual-exclusivity

### F4: BRAF functional class usage is strongly cancer type-specific

**Finding:** In canonical BRAF-associated cancers, >2/3 of alterations are Class I (V600E/K/R); in non-canonical cancers, ~49% are Class II (RAS-independent dimers). Class I alterations are significantly more clonal.

**Detail:** Class III BRAF alterations co-occur with `HRAS` and `NF1`. Class I tumors co-occur with WNT (`RNF43`), PI3K (`AKT1`, `MTOR`), and TGF-β (`SMAD4`) alterations. `SETD2` co-occurs with `BRAF`V600 in LUAD (47% of BRAF-V600 LUADs have SETD2 alterations). `RNF43` + `BRAF`V600E co-occurrence in CRC notable but no survival difference observed.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `RNF43`, `HRAS`, `NF1`, `SETD2`, `AKT1`, `MTOR`, `SMAD4` · Melanoma, CRC, LUAD, thyroid

**Data availability:** `available`
> BRAF mutation class annotations (Class I/II/III) may need OncoKB or manual annotation; underlying variants available. Co-mutation analysis native in cBioPortal.

**Tool support:** `native`
> OncoPrint + co-occurrence analysis (Mutual Exclusivity tab) available natively in `msk_impact_50k_2026`. BRAF class grouping requires variant-level annotation as custom clinical attribute or OncoKB lookup.

**Concepts:** #BRAF #functional-class #co-mutation #clonality #MAPK-pathway

### F5: RTK gene fusions broadly associated with early age of cancer onset

**Finding:** Oncogenic fusions in RTK pathway genes (`ALK`, `RET`, `ROS1`, `NTRK1/2/3`, `FGFR2`) are consistently associated with significantly earlier age of diagnosis across multiple cancer types; point mutations in the same genes often are not.

**Detail:** Analysis controlled for germline carrier status and mutual exclusivity confounders. `H3F3A` + `ATRX` co-alteration in gliomas has synergistic early onset (median 28 years vs 32 for H3F3A alone). In papillary thyroid: `RET` fusion → early onset, but `TERT` co-mutation shifts to later onset. `FGFR3` fusions (not mutations) → early onset in bladder cancer. `BRAF` fusions → younger onset in melanoma than other BRAF alterations.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ALK`, `RET`, `ROS1`, `NTRK1`, `NTRK2`, `NTRK3`, `FGFR2`, `FGFR3`, `BRAF`, `H3F3A`, `ATRX`, `TERT` · LUAD, thyroid, bladder, glioma, melanoma

**Data availability:** `available`
> Age at diagnosis, fusion events, and mutation data all available in `msk_impact_50k_2026`.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Clinical data (age) + mutation/fusion query: accessible via API. Multivariable age-association analysis with mutual exclusivity correction requires custom scripting. Survival/clinical plots in cBioPortal do not yet support age-at-diagnosis association testing natively.

**Concepts:** #fusion-oncogenes #age-of-onset #RTK-pathway #alteration-type-matters

### F6: Ancestry-dependent HLA allele frequencies create large disparities in TCR therapy eligibility

**Finding:** Neoantigen-directed TCR therapies (targeting KRAS G12D/V and TP53 R175H/R248W/Y220C) require specific HLA alleles; HLA allele frequencies vary dramatically by ancestry, leaving 0–6% of patients with African ancestry eligible vs. median 26% in other ancestries for KRAS G12V/G12D mutations.

**Detail:** 14% of all patients carry one of the evaluated driver mutations; 22% of those also carry a restricting HLA allele. All 5 restricting HLA alleles show ancestry-specific differences. East Asian ancestry has highest A*11:01 (44%) and C*01:02 (32%) frequencies. African ancestry: A*11:01 and C*01:02 each ~1%.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS` (G12D, G12V), `TP53` (R175H, R248W, Y220C) · Pan-cancer

**Data availability:** `not-available`
> HLA class I allele calls (POLYSOLVER or equivalent) are not standard cBioPortal data. Ancestry calls are in the dataset but HLA genotypes are typically not loaded into cBioPortal studies.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> Requires HLA genotyping pipeline + ancestry inference; not doable in cBioPortal natively. Mutation frequency by ancestry could be approximated via clinical filters on available ancestry data in `msk_impact_50k_2026`.

**Concepts:** #HLA #TCR-therapy #ancestry #health-equity #neoantigen #immunotherapy

### F7: Somatic HLA LOH occurs in 25% of patients; restricting allele preferentially lost in specific contexts

**Finding:** 25% of patients show somatic LOH at HLA class I loci. In tumors harboring specific driver mutations + restricting HLA alleles, there is preferential loss of the restricting allele (vs. alternate), in a driver-mutation-, HLA-allele-, and cancer type-specific manner.

**Detail:** TP53 R175H + A*02:01 → preferential loss of A*02:01. KRAS G12V (but not G12D) + A*11:01 → preferential loss (explained by differential binding affinity). TP53 R175H restricting allele loss enriched in CRC but not LUAD or PANCREATIC. ~15% of therapy-eligible patients have somatic loss of the restricting HLA allele → intrinsic resistance.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `KRAS` · CRC, LUAD, pancreatic (cancer type-specific effects)

**Data availability:** `not-available`
> Allele-specific copy number and HLA LOH require matched tumor-normal WES or deep panel + specialized tools (FACETS, LOHHLA). Not standard cBioPortal fields.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> LOHHLA or equivalent for HLA LOH. Fraction genome altered as a proxy for LOH burden is available natively in cBioPortal but cannot resolve HLA locus-specific LOH.

**Concepts:** #HLA-LOH #immune-escape #TCR-therapy-resistance #allele-specific-CN

### F8: MSI absent in cutaneous melanoma and invasive lobular breast carcinoma despite large sample sizes

**Finding:** MSI was observed in 2.7% of all patients but was completely absent in cutaneous melanomas (n=881) and breast invasive lobular carcinomas (n=606), despite cohort sizes large enough to detect rare events.

**Detail:** MSI most frequent in endometrial (15%), CRC (10%), esophagogastric (6%). At least one MSI+ tumor found in 97/448 histological subtypes. Findings support universal MSI screening given its role as tumor-agnostic biomarker for ICI response.

**Genes / cancer types:** MSI · Endometrial, CRC, esophagogastric, cutaneous melanoma (absent), breast ILC (absent)

**Data availability:** `available`
> MSI status available as clinical attribute in cBioPortal for most IMPACT studies including `msk_impact_50k_2026`.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Filter by MSI status + cancer type in cBioPortal Study View. Cross-study reproducibility testable in TCGA and GENIE studies also available in cBioPortal.

**Concepts:** #MSI #ICI-biomarker #universal-screening #tumor-agnostic

### F9: TMB-H frequency and dominant mutational signatures are cancer type-specific

**Finding:** 14% of patients are TMB-H (≥10 mut/Mb). TMB-H most common in bladder, melanoma, lung, and vaginal cancers (≥20%); rarest in sarcomas, gliomas, prostate and pancreatic ADCA (≤3%). In 80% of TMB-H tumors a dominant etiology signature is identifiable (MMRd 25%, APOBEC 22%, smoking 17%, UV 12%, POLE 2.7%).

**Detail:** Signatures align with expected biology: smoking in lung, UV in skin, temozolomide in brain, APOBEC in breast/bladder/cervical. Findings relevant for TMB as ICI biomarker and for understanding mutagenic etiologies.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer; `POLE` · Bladder, melanoma, lung, sarcoma, glioma, prostate, pancreatic ADCA

**Data availability:** `available`
> TMB values available as clinical attribute in cBioPortal. Mutational signature decomposition not directly available but can be derived.

**Tool support:** `api`
> TMB filtering and cancer type cross-tabulation: native via Study View. Signature analysis requires SigProfiler or similar external tool applied to SNV data pulled via API.

**Concepts:** #TMB #mutational-signatures #ICI-biomarker #etiology

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

_What could be tested in cBioPortal based on these findings that the paper did not test?_

- Do non-canonical driver alterations (e.g., PIK3CA in LUAD) correlate with worse outcomes compared to canonical drivers in the same tumor type? (Clinical data + survival analysis available natively)
- Is the SETD2 + BRAF-V600 co-occurrence in LUAD associated with distinct clinical outcomes or response to BRAF inhibitors? (Survival + treatment data potentially available)
- Do the 1,215 canonical gene–cancer type associations from this paper replicate in independent cohorts on cBioPortal (e.g., TCGA Pan-Cancer, AACR GENIE)?
- For genes with both fusion and mutation drivers (ALK, RET, FGFR3), do fusion patients show distinct co-mutation profiles beyond age of onset?
- Does the MSI-absent observation in cutaneous melanoma and breast ILC hold across other large cohorts on cBioPortal (TCGA, GENIE)?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Referral center cohort — heterogeneous timing of specimen collection and prior treatment exposures
- Sample size imbalance across cancer types limits inferences in rare cancers
- Hotspot discovery prioritized specificity; multi-cohort aggregation may reveal additional hotspots
- Restricted to MSK-IMPACT gene panel — no WGS, no RNA expression, no non-targeted genomic alterations
- HLA analysis requires specialized tools and is not generalizable without matched tumor-normal data
