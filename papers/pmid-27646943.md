## Summary

This study performed WES (discovery, n=19) and MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing (validation, n=161) on 180 germ cell tumors (GCTs) enriched for cisplatin-resistant disease to identify the molecular basis of chemotherapy failure. *TP53* mutations and deletions were found exclusively in cisplatin-resistant tumors, and *MDM2* amplifications cooperated with *TP53* pathway inactivation. Combined *TP53/MDM2* pathway alterations independently predicted shorter progression-free survival beyond the established IGCCCG clinical risk model. Primary mediastinal nonseminomas had an exceptionally high rate of *TP53* alteration (72%), mechanistically explaining their well-known chemoresistance. Overall, 55% of cisplatin-resistant GCTs harbored at least one actionable alteration, including novel *RAC1* mutations.

## Key Findings

### F1: TP53 alterations occur exclusively in cisplatin-resistant GCTs and predict inferior PFS

**Finding:** *TP53* mutations and deletions are found exclusively among cisplatin-resistant GCT patients and independently predict inferior progression-free survival beyond the IGCCCG clinical risk model.

**Detail:** In the combined cohort (n=180), *TP53* mutations/deletions were present in 17/104 cisplatin-resistant tumors (16.3%) vs. 0/76 cisplatin-sensitive tumors (P<0.001). *TP53* and/or *MDM2* alterations were present in 24% of resistant vs. 2.6% of sensitive tumors (P<0.001). With median follow-up of 38.8 months, patients with *TP53/MDM2* pathway alterations had significantly shorter PFS. In multivariable analysis including IGCCCG risk group, *TP53/MDM2* alteration was an independent predictor of disease progression (HR 1.83; 95% CI 1.12–2.98; P=0.016). *MDM2* amplification alone was mutually exclusive with *TP53* mutation (as in other tumor types).

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `MDM2` · Germ cell tumors (GCT)

### F2: Primary mediastinal nonseminomas have a markedly elevated rate of TP53 alteration (72%)

**Finding:** *TP53* alteration rate is dramatically higher in mediastinal primary GCTs (59%) than testicular primary GCTs (2.5%), with mediastinal nonseminomas reaching 72%—mechanistically explaining their well-documented cisplatin resistance.

**Detail:** Among 22 patients with mediastinal primary tumors, 13 (59.1%) had *TP53* alterations vs. 4/158 (2.5%) testicular primary tumors (P<0.001). Restricting to mediastinal nonseminoma (n=18), the rate rose to 72.2%; all 4 mediastinal seminomas were *TP53* wild-type. This extraordinary enrichment of *TP53* mutations in mediastinal nonseminoma—a subtype known for near-universal cisplatin resistance—provides the first molecular explanation for its poor chemosensitivity.

### F3: MYCN amplification cooperates with TP53 pathway inactivation in cisplatin-resistant GCT

**Finding:** *MYCN* amplification was found in 5 cisplatin-resistant patients (all *TP53/MDM2* wild-type), suggesting it may represent an alternative mechanism of TP53 pathway disruption in GCT.

**Detail:** All 5 *MYCN*-amplified cases were cisplatin-resistant, and 4/5 were *TP53/MDM2* wild-type, paralleling the role of MYCN in neuroblastoma where it transcriptionally targets both *TP53* and *MDM2* and serves as a poor-prognosis marker despite a low *TP53* mutation rate (<2%). This suggests that TP53 pathway disruption via *MYCN* amplification may contribute to cisplatin resistance through a mechanism distinct from direct *TP53* mutation.

### F4: Novel RAC1 mutations identified in cisplatin-resistant GCT

**Finding:** Novel activating *RAC1* mutations at positions analogous to KRAS hotspots (residues 12, 34, 61) were identified in cisplatin-resistant GCTs and functionally shown to increase PAK1 and ERK phosphorylation.

**Detail:** *RAC1* mutations were identified in cisplatin-resistant GCTs, with mutations clustering at positions analogous to canonical RAS oncogene hotspots. Functional validation demonstrated that expression of GCT-derived *RAC1* mutations resulted in increased phosphorylation of PAK1 and ERK, confirming oncogenic activity. Co-occurring *KRAS* mutations at residues 12, 34, and 61 were also identified. These RAS/RAC pathway activating mutations represent potential therapeutic targets in cisplatin-resistant GCT.

### F5: 55% of cisplatin-resistant GCTs harbor actionable alterations

**Finding:** More than half of cisplatin-resistant GCT patients carry at least one potentially actionable genomic alteration, supporting the rationale for comprehensive molecular profiling at time of disease progression.

**Detail:** Actionable alterations were detected in 55% of cisplatin-resistant tumors (vs. a much lower fraction of sensitive tumors), including *TP53/MDM2* pathway alterations, *RAC1* activating mutations, *KIT* mutations, *KRAS/NRAS* mutations, amplifications of RTKs, and other targeted alteration categories. *KIT* mutations in GCT differ in distribution from GI stromal tumors. The high actionable alteration rate supports genomic profiling of all cisplatin-resistant GCT patients to identify candidates for targeted therapy clinical trials.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can *TP53/MDM2* alteration status be used prospectively to intensify upfront therapy (e.g., BEP vs. TIP) or direct patients toward clinical trials for high-risk GCT?
- Are RAC1-activating mutations amenable to therapeutic targeting (e.g., via MEK, PAK1, or SOS1 inhibitors) in cisplatin-resistant GCT?
- Does TP53 pathway status predict response to salvage high-dose chemotherapy (HDCT) with autologous stem cell transplant?
- Can liquid biopsy (cfDNA) detect TP53 or MDM2 alterations in GCT patients before clinical resistance is manifest?

## Limitations

- Enrichment for cisplatin-resistant tumors in the validation cohort may overestimate population-level prevalence of TP53/MDM2 alterations.
- Differences in sample timing (pre- vs. post-chemotherapy) and type (primary vs. metastatic) between sensitive and resistant groups may introduce bias, though sensitivity analyses were performed.
- IGCCCG model comparison is retrospective; prospective validation of TP53/MDM2 as a clinical risk stratifier is needed.
- RAC1 functional assays performed in overexpression systems; relevance in primary GCT cells requires further validation.
- Low overall tumor mutational burden in GCT (mean 0.9 mut/Mb) limits the breadth of actionable targets compared to other solid tumors.
