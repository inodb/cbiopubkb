## Summary

A single-center retrospective study characterizing the pan-cancer landscape of *CDK12* alterations in 26,743 patients across 25 solid tumor types profiled with MSK-IMPACT clinical sequencing. *CDK12* alterations are rare (1.5% overall) but are most frequent and biologically significant in prostate (5.3%) and ovarian (4.2%) cancers, where biallelic inactivation is the dominant event. In prostate cancer, *CDK12* biallelic loss shapes a distinct genomic architecture — high breakpoint burden, a tandem duplicator–like copy-number pattern, and enrichment of cell-cycle pathway alterations — and is associated with somewhat more aggressive clinical behavior independent of standard prognostic factors. This study provides the largest single-institutional description of *CDK12*-altered prostate cancer and frames the rationale for ongoing immunotherapy trials (NCT03570619) in this subset.

## Key Findings

### F1: CDK12 alterations are rare pan-cancer (1.5%) but significantly enriched in prostate (5.3%) and ovarian (4.2%) cancers, where they are clonal and predominantly truncating

**Finding:** Across 26,743 patients, only 404 (1.5%) harbored *CDK12* alterations; however, MutSigCV identified *CDK12* as significantly recurrently mutated specifically in prostate (FDR < 0.001) and ovarian cancer (FDR = 0.056). In these two types, truncating mutations predominate, missense mutations cluster in the kinase domain (83%), and *CDK12* alterations are clonal in 91% and 87% of cases respectively — implying early driver status.

**Detail:** 404/26,743 patients (1.5%) had ≥1 *CDK12* alteration. Prostate: 100/1,875 (5.3%), ovarian: 43/1,034 (4.2%). In prostate, 96% of truncating mutations lie within or upstream of the kinase domain; 83% of missense mutations cluster in the kinase domain (higher than chance, *p* < 0.001). In 14 patients with longitudinally matched tumors, 13/14 shared *CDK12* alterations across all time points (up to 7.8 years apart), indicating early clonal emergence. Biallelic inactivation frequency did not differ between primary (70%) and metastatic (75%) samples.

### F2: CDK12 biallelic inactivation in prostate cancer produces a tandem duplicator–like genomic architecture with elevated breakpoints and small CNA segments

**Finding:** *CDK12* biallelic (CDK12-Bi) prostate cancers display significantly higher numbers of genomic breakpoints and smaller median copy-number segment sizes compared to CDK12-WT and CDK12-monoallelic tumors, consistent with a tandem duplicator phenotype. Overall TMB and FGA are comparable across groups, but CDK12-Bi shows higher fraction of genome gain and distinct focal CNA enrichment including 11q13.3 (CCND1/FGF locus).

**Detail:** CDK12-Bi vs CDK12-WT: higher breakpoint count (Kruskal-Wallis + pairwise Mann-Whitney FDR < 0.05) and smaller median CNA segment size. 13% of the evaluated genome is differentially copy-number altered between CDK12-Bi and CDK12-WT tumors. Chr 11q13.3 (containing *CCND1*, *FGF19*, *FGF4*, *FGF3*) is more frequently gained in CDK12-Bi. FGA increased over time in longitudinal paired samples, while TMB remained stable — consistent with progressive structural genomic instability without hypermutation.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDK12`, `CCND1`, `FGF19`, `FGF3`, `FGF4` · Prostate cancer

### F3: CDK12-Bi prostate cancer is enriched for cell-cycle pathway alterations and depleted of TP53 mutations and TMPRSS2-ERG fusions — defining a genomically distinct subtype

**Finding:** Interrogation of 53 recurrent oncogenic alterations and 10 canonical pathway categories reveals that *CDK12* biallelic inactivation defines a prostate cancer subtype with a unique co-alteration landscape. *CCND1*, *MCL1*, and *MYC* amplifications are enriched; *FANCA* loss and *FOXA1* mutations are more frequent; *TP53* mutations and *TMPRSS2-ERG* fusions are significantly depleted. The cell-cycle pathway is the most significantly enriched pathway; p53, PI3K/Akt, and Wnt/β-catenin pathway alterations are less frequent.

**Detail:** Eight recurrent oncogenic alterations differ by CDK12 allelic status (FDR < 0.05): CCND1 amp ↑, MCL1 amp ↑, MYC amp ↑, FANCA loss ↑, FOXA1 mut ↑; TP53 mut ↓, TMPRSS2-ERG fusion ↓. Cell-cycle pathway significantly more altered in CDK12-Bi (*p* FDR < 0.05). No significant co-occurrence of *CDK12* alteration with DNA-repair gene alterations (BRCA1/2, etc.). The mutual exclusivity with TMPRSS2-ERG and TP53 indicates CDK12-Bi prostate cancer is a molecularly distinct entity from AR-pathway and TP53-driven subtypes.

### F4: CDK12 alteration independently predicts shorter OS (median 64 vs 75 months) and faster time to castration resistance (10.8 vs 13.1 months)

**Finding:** Among 1,465 evaluable prostate cancer patients, CDK12-altered cases had significantly worse OS from first metastasis (median 64.4 vs 74.9 months, log-rank *p* = 0.032) and shorter time to development of castration-resistant disease (median 10.8 vs 13.1 months). Both associations were maintained in multivariable models adjusting for age, Gleason score, PSA, visceral metastasis, de novo metastatic status, and FGA (OS aHR 1.80, 95% CI 1.12–2.89, *p* = 0.024; CRPC aHR 1.49, 95% CI 1.09–2.03, *p* = 0.017). Time on first-line abiraterone or enzalutamide was similar between groups.

**Detail:** 315 total deaths; CDK12-altered: 25/71 evaluable; CDK12-WT: 290/874. Median follow-up for censored patients: 30.2 months. CDK12-altered patients had worse baseline features: higher de novo metastatic rate (40% vs 26%), higher PSA at diagnosis (14.8 vs 9.0 ng/mL), Gleason ≥8 in 80% vs 57%. CDK12-Bi subgroup had worst baseline characteristics. Clinical outcome differences are independent of FGA, suggesting CDK12 loss acts through a mechanism beyond broad copy-number instability (e.g., intronic polyadenylation of HR genes, pathway-specific CNA enrichment, or neoantigen generation).

### F5: CDK12-altered prostate cancer is a candidate for immune checkpoint blockade due to a tandem duplicator–derived neoantigen burden; PARP inhibitors show limited efficacy

**Finding:** Despite *CDK12* loss impairing HR gene expression (via intronic polyadenylation), clinical data show PARP inhibitors have limited efficacy in CDK12-altered mCRPC. The tandem duplicator phenotype generates fusion neoantigens, suggesting sensitivity to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade instead. A multi-institutional Phase 2 study (NCT03570619) combining nivolumab + ipilimumab in CDK12-altered mCRPC is ongoing.

**Detail:** Wu et al. demonstrated that CDK12 loss produces a novel prostate cancer molecular subtype with higher fusion neoantigen burden and potential immune infiltration (cited reference). PARP inhibitor studies (referenced) showed limited responses in CDK12-altered mCRPC, distinguishing CDK12 loss from BRCA1/2 loss as a functional HR deficiency. MSK data found no significant CDK12 co-occurrence with canonical HRD gene alterations (BRCA1, BRCA2, ATM, etc.), consistent with CDK12 loss being a distinct genomic context. The current study does not show significant differences in time on abiraterone/enzalutamide (median 9.7 vs 8.7 months), supporting focus on immunotherapy rather than AR-targeted escalation.

## Open Questions

1. Does CDK12-Bi prostate cancer respond to PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint blockade? NCT03570619 results are pending.
2. Can tandem duplication burden be accurately quantified from panel sequencing (vs WGS), and does it better predict immunotherapy response than CDK12 biallelic status alone?
3. What is the mechanism by which CDK12 loss drives cell-cycle pathway co-alteration (CCND1, MYC) independently of broad copy-number instability?
4. Are CDK12 alterations in ovarian cancer (LOH-dominant) biologically equivalent to the prostate cancer multi-mutation mechanism?
5. Does intronic polyadenylation-mediated suppression of HR genes create partial HRD that could be exploited with platinum or DNA-damaging agents in specific contexts?

## Limitations

- Retrospective single-institution study with potential referral bias (tertiary cancer center).
- MSK-IMPACT targeted panel sequencing may miss rearrangements and underestimate tandem duplication events (direct WGS would be ideal).
- CDK12 rearrangements excluded from biallelic analysis due to hybrid-capture false-negative limitations.
- Tumor purity and clonality data derived from FACETS; low purity samples may misclassify allelic status.
- Clinical outcome comparisons not adjusted for treatment sequence or clinical trial participation.
