## Summary

Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) of 57 prostate adenocarcinomas and two NEPC metastases (paired normal) at mean 61× tumor/34× normal coverage identifies "chromoplexy" as a previously undescribed mechanism of complex genome restructuring, distinct from chromothripsis, operating in 88% of prostate cancers. The ChainFinder algorithm detects interdependent chains of rearrangements and deletions that coordinately dysregulate multiple cancer genes in a single event. Clonal hierarchy analysis using WGS germline SNP allelic fractions reconstructs a consensus oncogenic progression path: NKX3-1/FOXP1 loss or TMPRSS2-ERG fusion → CDKN1B/TP53 loss → PTEN loss. Chromoplexy is observed across multiple cancer types (melanoma, lung, head-and-neck, breast), supporting punctuated evolution as a broadly relevant cancer genome model.

## Key Findings

### F1: Chromoplexy — a novel mechanism of complex genome restructuring — occurs in 88% of prostate cancers and coordinately disrupts multiple cancer genes

**Finding:** ChainFinder analysis reveals that 50/57 (88%) of prostate tumors harbor chromoplectic chains of ≥5 rearrangements (≥10 breakpoints), and 63% contain two or more such chains. Overall, 39% of all rearrangements participate in chains. Chromoplexy generates interdependent chains of translocations and deletions — statistically unlikely to arise independently — that simultaneously disrupt multiple geographically distant cancer genes in a single event, providing a mechanistic basis for punctuated cancer evolution.

**Detail:** The probabilistic ChainFinder model identifies chains where neighboring breakpoints could not have arisen independently (false positive rate: 2.8% in simulated, 0.2% in scrambled genomes vs. 39% observed). A chain of 27 rearrangements across 6 chromosomes simultaneously created the TMPRSS2-ERG fusion (21q) and disrupted SMAD4 (18q). A 25-rearrangement chain across 3 chromosomes simultaneously deleted CDKN1B, ETV6, and ETV3. Co-deletion of PIK3R1 (5q) with PTEN (10q) and TP53 (17p) with CHEK2 (22q) occurred in single chains in two tumors. ChainFinder detected chromoplexy in all tumor types analyzed (melanoma, NSCLC, HNSCC, breast), suggesting pan-cancer relevance. Among 57 prostate tumors, 26 (46%) have chromoplectic deletion or rearrangement of ≥1 prostate TSG; adding TMPRSS2-ERG and 10 putative prostate cancer genes raises this to 61%.

### F2: ETS-positive tumors show inter-chromosomal chromoplexy linked to transcriptionally active loci; CHD1-deleted tumors show intra-chromosomal chromoplexy resembling chromothripsis

**Finding:** Chromoplexy manifests in two biologically distinct forms depending on ETS fusion status. ETS+ (ERG/ETV1 fusion) tumors: significantly more inter-chromosomal rearrangements (P<10⁻⁴), greater maximum chromosome involvement per chain (P=0.009), with breakpoints enriched near highly expressed prostate loci that co-localize in interphase nuclei — suggesting transcription-coupled DNA damage. ETS− tumors with CHD1 deletion: predominantly intra-chromosomal, clustered, GC-poor, late-replicating heterochromatic breakpoints — resembling chromothripsis.

**Detail:** ERG fusions arose from chromoplexy in 15/26 (58%) of ETS+ cases, indicating chromoplexy precedes ETS fusion rather than being a consequence. ETS+ chromoplexy breakpoints are enriched near highly expressed prostate tumor genes (validated in 16 transcriptomes) and co-localize in interphase nuclei, consistent with AR-driven transcriptional DNA damage (androgen receptor-mediated promoter co-occupation creating spatial proximity). Seven ETS−/CHD1del tumors contain up to 7-fold more rearrangements than cohort average; predominantly intra-chromosomal chains (P=2×10⁻⁴); breakpoints in late-replicating, GC-poor heterochromatin. In extended 199-tumor cohort, CHD1 loss significantly associates with increased recurrent SCNAs (P=1.5×10⁻⁸). CHD1's role in genome stability and chromatin architecture maintenance may explain the chromothripsis-like pattern upon its loss.

### F3: Clonal hierarchy reconstruction reveals consensus oncogenic progression path in prostate cancer

**Finding:** Exploitation of WGS-derived germline heterozygous SNP allelic fractions enables genome-wide clonal status assessment for each somatic deletion and mutation. Systematic pairwise clonal-subclonal directionality analysis across co-deleted gene pairs in multiple tumors defines a "consensus path" of oncogenic events: early — NKX3-1/FOXP1 deletion or TMPRSS2-ERG fusion; intermediate — CDKN1B and TP53 loss; late — PTEN loss.

**Detail:** NKX3-1 deletions (chr8p) and TMPRSS2-ERG fusions (21q) are strictly clonal (present in founder clone), consistent with their detection in PIN (prostatic intraepithelial neoplasia, a cancer precursor). PTEN deletions are often subclonal (P=10⁻⁵ vs. NKX3-1), as are CDKN1B deletions — indicating later emergence during progression. Point mutations: SPOP and FOXA1 are predominantly clonal (early events); most other mutations are subclonal. Tumors with predominantly Gleason 4 histology are significantly enriched for recurrent SCNAs vs. Gleason 3 (P=0.0059), suggesting that accumulation of chromoplexy-induced structural alterations is reflected in histological grade. Several chains appear to involve subclonal deletion bridges, implying multiple rounds of chromoplexy can occur as tumors progress — chromoplexy is not a single initiating event but is ongoing.

### F4: Recurrent disruptive rearrangements in PTEN, RB1, GSK3B, FOXO1 — beyond TMPRSS2-ERG as driver structural variants

**Finding:** Beyond the canonical TMPRSS2-ERG fusion, WGS identifies multiple genes recurrently targeted by disruptive rearrangements: PTEN (9 cases via chromoplexy), NKX3-1 (8 cases), RB1 (2 cases), CDKN1B (3 cases), GSK3B, FOXO1, and MAGI2. Additionally, novel singleton oncogenic fusions are detected: NRF1-BRAF (sense-preserving, kinase domain intact) and CRKL-MAPK1 (sense-preserving, ERK-2 kinase domain intact).

**Detail:** PTEN rearrangements frequently occur in the context of chromoplexy chains — 9 cases have chromoplectically disrupted PTEN. This is clinically significant as PTEN structural disruption may not be captured by exome sequencing alone, and WGS-detected PTEN rearrangements may explain a subset of PTEN-pathway-active tumors without apparent PTEN point mutations or focal deletions. NRF1-BRAF fusion (tumor PR-4240) and CRKL-MAPK1 fusion (tumor P04-1084) potentially activate BRAF and ERK-2 kinase signaling — rationale for MEK inhibitor sensitivity in these specific cases. MAGI2 rearrangements (previously described in Berger 2011) are recurrent mechanisms of PTEN pathway activation through disruption of the PTEN scaffold protein.

### F5: Chromoplexy is pan-cancer — detected across melanoma, NSCLC, head-and-neck, and breast cancer genomes

**Finding:** Analysis of 59 additional tumor genomes from four additional cancer types (melanoma, non-small cell lung cancer, head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, breast adenocarcinoma) demonstrates that chromoplexy is not prostate-cancer-specific. Every cancer type analyzed shows multiple instances of chains involving ≥5 rearrangements (≥10 breakpoints), supporting chromoplexy as a universal mechanism of punctuated genome evolution.

**Detail:** The pan-cancer prevalence of chromoplexy implies that punctuated evolution — rather than gradual stepwise accumulation of genomic alterations — is a common mode of cancer development across cancer types. This challenges the traditional "gradual Darwinian" model and has implications for the timing of early detection: if multiple driver events can arise simultaneously in one chromoplectic event, the window for early intervention may be narrower than previously assumed. The ChainFinder algorithm (available for application to any WGS dataset) enables systematic chromoplexy detection and may serve as a tool for identifying cancers with complex rearrangement landscapes that are more genomically unstable.

## Open Questions

- Can chromoplexy signatures in liquid biopsies (cfDNA) serve as early detection markers for prostate cancer progression?
- Does AR-driven transcription physically co-localize TMPRSS2 and ERG loci (3C/Hi-C evidence), and can anti-androgen therapy reduce ERG fusion formation rates?
- Are NRF1-BRAF and CRKL-MAPK1 fusion-positive prostate cancers sensitive to BRAF/MEK inhibitors in small-scale clinical evaluation?
- Does CHD1 deletion status predict sensitivity to PARP inhibitors (via chromatin instability) or HDAC inhibitors (via aberrant chromatin remodeling)?
- Can ChainFinder applied to liquid biopsy ctDNA identify chromoplexy events as early progression markers before clinical relapse?
- Does the consensus oncogenic path (NKX3-1→ERG→CDKN1B→TP53→PTEN) predict therapeutic sensitivity at each stage, enabling stage-stratified precision treatment?

## Limitations

- WGS cohort is 57 primary tumors — limited to localized disease (Gleason 6–9), with only 2 NEPC metastases; metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) not represented
- No matched germline RNA-seq; transcriptome analysis limited to 20 of 57 tumors
- ChainFinder is a computational model inferring chromoplexy from breakpoint distributions — mechanistic validation of simultaneous multi-break events in a single cell division requires experimental proof
- WGS era (2013) predates routine ctDNA profiling, immunotherapy, and PARP inhibitor data in prostate cancer context
- No protein expression or phosphoproteomic data — functional consequences of chromoplexy (activated vs. inactive signaling pathways) inferred from genomic alone
- ABSOLUTE-based clonal inference has uncertainty at low purity; 2 samples showed discrepant purity estimates between WGS and SNP array
- Chromoplexy detection sensitivity depends on sequencing coverage; at 61× mean, low-frequency subclonal rearrangements may be missed
- SNP array (Affymetrix 6.0) used for SCNA profiling in extended 199-tumor cohort — lower resolution than WGS for small focal CNVs
