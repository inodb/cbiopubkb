## Summary

The largest WGS study of metastatic cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (CSCC) to date, profiling 25 lymph node metastases from head and neck CSCC using matched tumor–germline WGS (mean ~95× tumor coverage). The study extends previous coding-genome analyses by performing a multifaceted computational survey of the non-coding genome — 3′ UTRs, lncRNAs, indel signatures, structural variants and copy-number alterations — in addition to somatic coding drivers. Key findings include extremely high tumor mutational burden (median 167 mutations/Mb at WGS scale), dominance of UV-associated SBS signatures 7a/7b, a novel dominance of indel signature ID9 (distinct from melanoma), and functionally altered non-coding regions in *EVC*, *PPP1R1A*, *ABCA4* (3′UTR) and *LINC01003* (lncRNA). Coding driver analysis across three tools converges on *TP53*, *CDKN2A*, and *C9* as the most recurrently mutated drivers. Recurrent *PTPRD* deletion (24% of samples) and absence of significant *NOTCH1/2* mutations distinguish metastatic from primary CSCC.

## Key Findings

### F1: Extremely high TMB at WGS scale (median 167 mutations/Mb) dominated by UV signatures SBS7a/7b; majority of variation in non-coding genome

**Finding:** Metastatic CSCC exhibits one of the highest TMBs recorded for any cancer type: median 166.99 mutations/Mb for SNVs (range 32.52–995.66 mutations/Mb) and 2.25 indels/Mb. Over 80% of SNVs are C>T transitions consistent with UV radiation damage. SBS7a and SBS7b dominate, with substantial SBS7c; one azathioprine-exposed sample uniquely harbors SBS32. The majority of somatic variants occur in non-coding regions.

**Detail:** The whole-genome scale TMB is substantially higher than previously reported WES-based estimates for CSCC (~61 mutations/Mb), since non-coding DNA has proportionally higher mutation density from UV exposure. No correlation was found between TMB and age, histologic grade, nodal stage, or extracapsular spread. The single female patient had the second highest TMB (499 mutations/Mb). The high TMB underpins the favorable response rates to immune checkpoint inhibitors observed clinically in advanced CSCC.

### F2: Novel dominance of indel signature ID9 in metastatic CSCC — distinct from melanoma; ID8 and ID13 also prevalent

**Finding:** Indel signature analysis using COSMIC v3.2 reveals ID8, ID9, and ID13 as the dominant indel signatures (present in 100% of samples). Crucially, ID9 is dominant in CSCC but is rare in melanoma (2/104 PCAWG cases) and more commonly associated with soft tissue sarcoma — highlighting a mechanistic divergence between two UV-induced skin cancers. ID13 co-occurs with SBS7a/7b, consistent with a UV-mediated mechanism. ID8 is associated with double-strand break repair dysfunction and aging.

**Detail:** This is the first characterization of ID signatures in metastatic CSCC at the WGS level. The PCAWG CSCC dataset does not exist, making this cohort the reference for CSCC indel signature patterns. The dominance of ID9 with the highest mutations/Mb among all cancer types for that signature (from COSMIC v3.2) identifies a novel biological feature distinguishing metastatic CSCC from primary CSCC and from melanoma — potentially relevant to understanding DNA repair vulnerabilities in this disease.

### F3: TP53, CDKN2A, and novel driver C9 identified by all three driver detection tools; NOTCH1/2 absent as metastatic drivers

**Finding:** Three tools (OncoDriveFML, MutSigCV, dNdScv) converge on *TP53*, *CDKN2A*, and *C9* (complement component 9) as the most significant coding driver genes. *TP53* was mutated in 24/25 samples; *CDKN2A* mutations co-occurred with high TMB (samples lacking *CDKN2A* mutations averaged 201% of cohort mean TMB). *C9* was altered in 52% of this cohort (vs. 31% primary and 10% metastatic CSCC in cBioPortal). Notably, *NOTCH1/2* — key drivers in primary CSCC — were not significantly recurrently mutated in metastatic tumors.

**Detail:** *C9* encodes complement component 9 of the membrane attack complex (MAC) and may modulate the tumor microenvironment; its high recurrence in primary CSCC (31%) but lower frequency in metastatic CSCC (10% in cBioPortal) raises questions about tumor stage and immune selection. Additional consensus drivers include *C9orf131*, *SLC22A6*, *KHDRBS2*, *COLEC12*, *LINGO2*, *CDHR5*, *ZNF442*, *PRLR*, and *DHRS4*. The absence of recurrent *NOTCH1/2* mutations in metastatic CSCC contrasts sharply with primary CSCC, suggesting different evolutionary pressures during metastatic progression.

### F4: Non-coding drivers — 3′UTR mutations in EVC, PPP1R1A, ABCA4; lncRNA LINC01003 altered in 64% of samples

**Finding:** OncodriveFML analysis of non-coding regions identifies significant (Q < 0.05) 3′UTR driver mutations in *EVC* (48% of samples, Q=0.011), *PPP1R1A* (48%, Q=0.022), *ABCA4* (20%), and *LUM*, plus four significantly altered lncRNAs: *LINC01003* (64% of samples, Q=0.016), *LINC01474* (Q=0.016), *RP4-597N16.4*, and *RP11-61J19.4* (48%). A unique recurrent missense mutation in *PPP1R1A* 3′UTR (c.*491C>T, Chr12:54579896) was found in 5 samples.

**Detail:** *EVC* affects ciliary Hedgehog signaling; its 3′UTR mutations may deregulate Hh pathway activation. *PPP1R1A* encodes a protein phosphatase inhibitor with context-dependent roles in metastasis (pro-metastatic in Ewing sarcoma, reduced in breast cancer). *LINC01003* acts as a tumor suppressor in multiple myeloma by sponging miR-33a-5p/PIM1. These non-coding alterations are not captured in WES or panel-based studies, underscoring a gap in the existing cBioPortal CSCC datasets which rely on MSK-IMPACT or WES.

**Genes / cancer types:** `EVC`, `PPP1R1A`, `ABCA4`, `LUM`, `LINC01003` · Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (metastatic)

### F5: Recurrent PTPRD deletion (24%) and CNA landscape — Chr5p/8q gains, 18q losses; rare gene amplifications in metastatic CSCC

**Finding:** Structural and copy-number analysis reveals recurrent deletions of *PTPRD* (Chr9p, 24% of samples) as the most frequently deleted gene, never co-occurring with deep *CDKN2A* deletion but frequently co-occurring with *CDKN2A* point mutations. Chromosomal arm-level gains in Chr5p, 7, and 8q and losses in Chr8p, 18q, and 21q are predominant. Gene amplifications are rare; *MYC* duplication in one sample. Structural variants are characterized by complex events, deletions and unbalanced translocations; *SMAD4* disrupted in one sample by single-end break.

**Detail:** *PTPRD* is a receptor-type protein tyrosine phosphatase and established tumor suppressor in multiple cancers; its deletion in 24% of metastatic CSCC is consistent with prior reports in primary/metastatic disease. The scarcity of gene amplifications in metastatic CSCC contrasts with other highly amplified cancers (e.g., HNSCC), suggesting that this tumor type's high TMB/UV mutagenesis rather than CNA-driven amplification is the primary oncogenic mechanism. Pathway enrichment analysis highlights "TGF-beta regulation of extracellular matrix" and "G1 to S cell cycle checkpoints" as the top enriched functional terms.

## Open Questions

- Does the novel dominance of indel signature ID9 in metastatic CSCC reflect a distinct DNA repair vulnerability that could be therapeutically targeted (e.g., PARP inhibitors)?
- Why do *NOTCH1/2* — key primary CSCC drivers — disappear as significant drivers in metastatic disease? Is this selection against NOTCH-mutated clones, or a sampling artifact from small cohort size?
- Does *C9* mutation in CSCC promote immune evasion through disruption of complement-mediated tumor cell killing, and can it predict immunotherapy response?
- Are the non-coding 3′UTR alterations in *EVC*, *PPP1R1A*, and *LINC01003* functional drivers, or passenger events in the context of high UV-driven TMB?
- The predominantly male cohort (96%) limits sex-specific genomic analyses — do female metastatic CSCC patients show distinct genomic patterns given the outlier high-TMB female case?
- Does *PTPRD* deletion in metastatic CSCC correlate with clinical outcomes or response to specific therapies?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=25); 96% male, limiting sex-stratified analysis
- All samples are lymph node metastases — no matched primary tumor sequencing; cannot definitively identify metastasis-specific alterations vs. those present in primary tumor
- No survival or treatment outcome data reported
- 5′UTR and promoter non-coding analyses were not feasible due to methodological limitations in high-TMB settings
- WGS-specific findings (non-coding drivers, ID signatures) are not captured in the existing cscc_ranson_2022 cBioPortal entry, which likely reflects coding regions from this or related datasets
- lncRNA driver analysis is exploratory and lacks functional validation for most candidates
- No tumor microenvironment (TME) or immune cell composition analysis performed despite the high TMB and immunotherapy relevance
