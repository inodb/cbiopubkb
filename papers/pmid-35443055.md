## Summary

Single-institution MSK-IMPACT cohort study of 119 pathologically re-reviewed low-grade serous ovarian carcinomas (LGSC) — a rare, platinum-resistant subtype accounting for 5–10% of serous ovarian cancers. Tumors were sequenced using the FDA-cleared MSK-IMPACT panel (341–505 genes). MAPK pathway alterations were found in 60% of cases (KRAS 33%, NRAS 11%, BRAF 11%, EIF1AX 10%). Key clinical finding: MAPK pathway alterations were independently associated with prolonged overall survival (multivariate HR=2.5 for absence of MAPK alteration; P=0.019), and KRAS mutations were specifically associated with platinum-sensitive disease (P=0.03). All 102 evaluable LGSCs were microsatellite stable with low TMB (median 1.8 Muts/Mb). No BRCA1/2 germline mutations were identified. Data available in `lgsoc_mapk_msk_2022` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: MAPK pathway altered in 60% of LGSC; KRAS, BRAF, NRAS are mutually exclusive major drivers

**Finding:** 71 of 119 LGSCs (60%) harbored somatic MAPK pathway alterations. The most common were KRAS hotspot mutations (33%; G12V n=19, G12D n=14, G12C n=3, G12R n=2, G12A n=1), BRAF alterations (11%; V600E n=8, D594N n=2, N581I n=1, plus BRAF-MKRN1 and BRAF-AGAP3 fusions), and NRAS hotspot mutations (11%; Q61R n=9, Q61K n=4). EIF1AX alterations were found in 10% (n=12). KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF mutations were mutually exclusive in all but one case (KRAS vs. NRAS: P=0.004).

**Detail:** ERBB2 amplification (n=2) and one ERBB2 hotspot mutation (Y772_A775dup) were observed. NF1 (n=2), NF2 (n=1), and MAP3K1 (n=1) were the only other MAPK-related genes with alterations. No alterations were found in MAP2K1, MAP2K2, MAP2K4, RAF1, DUSP4, RRAS, RRAS2, and others. CDKN2A homozygous deletions were present in 8% (n=9). 21 LGSCs (18%) had no identifiable somatic mutations or CNA — with similar tumor purity to mutated cases (median 30% vs. 40%; P=0.12), suggesting these are not sequencing failures.

### F2: MAPK pathway alterations independently associated with prolonged OS (multivariate HR=2.5 for absence)

**Finding:** On multivariate analysis, MAPK pathway alterations were independently associated with improved overall survival (HR=2.5 for absence of MAPK alteration; 95% CI 1.2–5.2; P=0.019). Median OS was 339 months for MAPK-altered LGSC vs. 125 months for MAPK-unaltered LGSC. Platinum sensitivity was the other independent predictor of OS (HR=0.4 for sensitivity; P=0.005). Univariate analysis confirmed both associations (MAPK: P=0.005; platinum sensitivity: P=0.001). Age, stage, and residual disease were not significantly associated with OS.

**Detail:** Among 90 patients included in landmark multivariate analysis, 26% of patients with MAPK-altered tumors died compared to 49% without MAPK alterations. The survival advantage of MAPK-altered tumors may reflect their biologic dependence on a single oncogenic pathway, making them more predictable in behavior and potentially more amenable to targeted MEK inhibitor therapy. This finding is consistent with a prior report of improved OS in KRAS/BRAF-mutated LGSC (n=21) from Gershenson et al., but extends it to a broader MAPK pathway definition and a larger, molecularly re-reviewed cohort.

### F3: KRAS mutations specifically associated with older age at diagnosis and platinum sensitivity

**Finding:** Among the three major mutually exclusive driver alterations, only KRAS mutations showed statistically significant clinical associations: age at diagnosis >50 years (P=0.02) and platinum-sensitive disease (P=0.03). NRAS and BRAF alterations showed no significant associations with age or platinum sensitivity. 90% of NRAS-altered patients were platinum sensitive (P=0.28 vs. NRAS-WT), and 50% of BRAF-altered patients were sensitive (P=0.20 vs. BRAF-WT), but these were not significant.

**Detail:** LGSC has a bimodal age distribution (20–30 and 50–60 years), and KRAS mutations being enriched in patients >50 years may reflect an older cell-of-origin or distinct oncogenic trajectory. The association of KRAS mutations with platinum sensitivity aligns with the binimetinib phase III trial findings, which showed PFS benefit for KRAS-mutated LGSC patients treated with MEK inhibition. This study provides the OS context that was missing from the PFS-focused trial analysis. Of 4 patients who received MEK inhibitors for recurrence, 3 had MAPK-altered tumors; all discontinued due to toxicity.

### F4: All LGSC tumors are microsatellite stable with very low TMB; no BRCA1/2 germline mutations

**Finding:** All 102 evaluable LGSC cases were microsatellite stable (MSIsensor score range 0–3.83; median 0.12). TMB was very low (median 1.8 Muts/Mb; range 0–17.8), with a median of 1 non-synonymous somatic mutation. Chromosomal instability was low (median fraction of genome altered: 0.16). No BRCA1/2 germline pathogenic mutations were identified in 79 tested patients.

**Detail:** Among 79 patients tested for germline mutations, only 7 pathogenic germline mutations were found: one biallelic MUTYH (c.1187G>A, p.Gly396Asp) with somatic LOH confirmed in the tumor (the only case of biallelic inactivation), plus 6 mono-allelic mutations without tumor LOH (including ATM, CHEK2, and others). The absence of BRCA1/2 germline mutations contrasts sharply with high-grade serous carcinoma (HGSC), further distinguishing LGSC's molecular profile. The low TMB and MSS status suggest LGSC patients would not benefit from standard pembrolizumab monotherapy or PD-1 checkpoint inhibition.

### F5: 18% of LGSCs have no identified somatic alterations; paired tumors show clonal continuity at recurrence

**Finding:** 21 of 119 LGSCs (18%) had no somatic mutations or copy number alterations detectable by MSK-IMPACT — with tumor purity comparable to mutation-bearing cases — suggesting a true absence of alterations in the panel's gene coverage rather than technical failure. In 8 patients with sequencing of both primary and recurrent tumors, 3 cases had shared genetic alterations confirming clonality; 2 recurrences in mutation-negative primaries acquired driver alterations (CDKN2A/B deletion; BRAF G469V), consistent with subclonal drivers undetectable at the primary time point.

**Detail:** These findings suggest that LGSC may harbor oncogenic drivers outside the 505-gene MSK-IMPACT panel — including activating mutations in promoters, structural rearrangements not captured by the current panel design, or epigenetic mechanisms. The BRAF G469V mutation seen in one recurrence was not present in the primary tumor, suggesting either clonal evolution from a subclonal precursor or emergence of a de novo alteration. BRAF non-V600 mutations (including D594N and G469V) are known to have distinct signaling characteristics from BRAF V600E, potentially predicting differential response to MEK inhibitors.

## Open Questions

- What oncogenic mechanisms drive the 18% of LGSCs with no detectable somatic alteration in a 505-gene panel — are there epigenetic drivers, non-coding mutations, or structural variants outside panel coverage?
- Does BRAF non-V600 mutation status (D594N, N581I) confer differential sensitivity to MEK inhibition versus BRAF V600E or KRAS/NRAS mutations in LGSC?
- Can the MAPK alteration–OS survival benefit be extended prospectively in clinical trials of MEK inhibitors (binimetinib, trametinib, cobimetinib) combined with CDK4/6 or PI3K inhibitors in recurrent LGSC?
- Is the absence of BRCA1/2 germline mutations in LGSC universal, and should PARP inhibitor trials be pursued based on somatic BRCA-like signatures rather than germline status?
- Does the biallelic MUTYH mutation (the only case of biallelic germline inactivation with LOH) represent a distinct MUTYH-associated LGSC subtype with a unique somatic mutation signature?

## Limitations

- Single-institution retrospective study (MSK) with potential referral bias — sensitivity analysis restricted to patients presenting at diagnosis (n=60) was underpowered to detect significant associations
- 38% of patients had sequencing performed on recurrent rather than primary tumor specimens, potentially missing alterations present only in primary tissue
- Germline data available for only 66% (79/119) of patients, and 28/79 came from outside institutions with variable panel coverage
- Median follow-up was 62.7 months for OS but only 16.3 months for PFS — the latter is insufficient to draw conclusions about PFS-associated biomarkers
- Small cohort size (n=119) limits statistical power for subgroup analyses (e.g., NRAS and BRAF mutation associations with outcome); multi-institutional validation needed
- MSK-IMPACT panel does not cover the full genome; structural variants, non-coding mutations, and genes outside the 505-gene panel are undetected
