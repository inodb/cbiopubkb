## Summary

Comprehensive genomic characterization of 44 MD Anderson prostate cancer patient-derived xenograft (MDA PCa PDX) models from 38 clinically annotated patients, using WGS (30×), targeted sequencing (T200.1, 263 genes, 400×), and RNA-seq. PDXs span the full prostate cancer spectrum: adenocarcinoma (Ad), neuroendocrine prostate cancer (NEPC), poorly differentiated, and sarcomatoid variants from primary and metastatic sites. 91% of models harbor oncogenic alterations in AR, RB1, TP53, or PTEN, faithfully recapitulating the mCRPC landscape. Paired heterogeneity models reveal conserved driver alterations across tumor regions. NEPC PDXs upregulate DNA damage response (DDR) genes, while Ad models show higher rates of DDR gene driver mutations. FGFR1 expression is regulated epigenetically via promoter CpG methylation rather than genomic alteration. Data publicly available in `prad_msk_mdanderson_2023` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: MDA PCa PDXs faithfully recapitulate the mCRPC genomic landscape; 91% harbor driver alterations

**Finding:** 91% of PDX models have oncogenic alterations in at least one of the four canonical prostate cancer drivers (AR, RB1, TP53, PTEN), consistent with the clinical mCRPC landscape from large-scale genomic studies. Chromosome 8q gains and 8p losses, characteristic of prostate cancer, are also observed in PDXs.

**Detail:** AR alterations include amplification (with high expression), oncogenic point mutations (H875Y, T878A), and ARv7 splice variant expression detected by RNA-seq and confirmed by IHC. PTEN and RB1 deep deletions are prevalent. Cases without genomic alterations in the four drivers (~9%) are explained by other events: CDK12 biallelic inactivation, ETS family fusions, or AR enhancer amplification not captured by standard gene-level analysis. Multi-omic integration (WGS + targeted + RNA-seq) was essential to detect these cryptic mechanisms.

**Genes / cancer types:** AR, PTEN, RB1, TP53, CDK12, ERG (TMPRSS2-ERG) · Prostate adenocarcinoma, NEPC

### F2: Spatial intra-tumor heterogeneity: driver alterations conserved; minor clones and transcriptomes differ

**Finding:** PDX pairs derived from different areas of the same primary tumor or from CTCs collected simultaneously share canonical driver alterations (e.g., PTEN deletion, TP53 mutation, RB1 loss), but harbor model-specific mutations reflecting intra-tumor heterogeneity. Transcriptomic profiles can diverge despite genomic similarity.

**Detail:** 144-13 and 144-4 (derived from two regions of a single NEPC primary) both carry PTEN/KDM6A deep deletion and TP53 mutation, but 144-4 uniquely harbors an NRAS Q61K oncogenic mutation. 146-10 (NEPC) and 146-12 (Ad), derived from a mixed Ad-NEPC primary, share TP53, RB1 mutations, and PTEN deletion but exhibit different morphologies. 316-1 (bone met) and 316-2 (CTCs) share PTEN, SPOP, and SPEN mutations but each have private mutations, suggesting microenvironment influence. These findings indicate driver alterations are primary disease determinants while private mutations may reflect niche-specific selection.

**Genes / cancer types:** PTEN, RB1, TP53, NRAS, SPOP, SPEN, KDM6A · Prostate cancer

### F3: NEPC PDXs upregulate DDR programs; Ad PDXs enriched for DDR driver mutations

**Finding:** Transcriptome-based PCA and IPA pathway analysis reveal that NEPC PDXs significantly upregulate DDR-associated gene expression (including BRCA, ATM, CHEK signaling) compared to Ad PDXs. Paradoxically, driver genomic mutations in DDR genes are more prevalent in Ad models. RB1 genomic alterations are significantly more frequent in NEPC (P=0.0002).

**Detail:** NEPC PDXs also show elevated MYCN and AURKA expression (not due to amplification), consistent with a GR-MYCN-CDK5-RB1-E2F1 axis linked to enzalutamide-induced NEPC differentiation. The inositol phosphate synthesis pathway (involved in DNA repair regulation) is also augmented in NEPC. The same NEPC-enriched DDR expression profile is validated in the SU2C human mCRPC dataset when samples are ranked by NEPC score. This cross-validation uses the cBioPortal Group Comparison function directly.

**Genes / cancer types:** RB1, BRCA1, BRCA2, ATM, CHEK1, CHEK2, MYCN, AURKA · NEPC, prostate adenocarcinoma

**Data availability:** `available`

### F4: CDK12 biallelic inactivation detectable only through multi-omic integration

**Finding:** Four PDX models (9% of cohort) harbor CDK12 biallelic inactivation through diverse mechanisms including biallelic point mutations, structural variants causing exon loss, aberrant splicing of the non-mutated allele, and focal copy number gains typical of CDK12 loss. Many of these would be missed by standard gene-level sequencing alone.

**Detail:** 174-6 and 322-2 carry two CDK12 mutations each (likely biallelic). 117-9 shows WGS-detected loss of the last two CDK12 exons (structural variant) along with focal gains typical of CDK12 deficiency. 328-5 carries a monoallelic missense mutation in the kinase domain, but RNA-seq shows ~80% of transcripts carry the mutation and ~20% show aberrant splicing of the wild-type allele, indicating biallelic inactivation through a transcriptomic mechanism. CDK12 loss has clinical relevance as it generates focal tandem duplications enriched for immune neoantigen formation.

**Genes / cancer types:** CDK12 · Prostate cancer (castration-resistant)

**Data availability:** `available`

### F5: FGFR1 expression regulated by promoter CpG methylation, not genomic alteration; bone-met enriched

**Finding:** FGFR1 expression levels in PDX models do not correlate with genomic alterations at the FGFR1 locus. Instead, elevated CpG methylation at the FGFR1 promoter inversely correlates with FGFR1 expression. FGFR1 expression is highest in bone metastasis models, validated in the SU2C clinical dataset.

**Detail:** FGFR1 alpha and beta isoforms were overexpressed in PC3 cells; RNA-seq of PC3-FGFR1 cells identified a 75-gene signature, of which three (NRP2, LRP4, TGFBI) accurately stratify PDX models by FGFR1 level. These three genes correlate with FGFR1 expression in both SU2C (prad_su2c_2015) and TCGA-PRAD, and are elevated in bone metastases. FGFR1 methylation-expression correlation also confirmed in TCGA-PRAD. This finding supports an epigenetic rather than genomic FGFR1 activation mechanism and the need to assess expression (not only CNA) when selecting patients for FGFR inhibition.

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR1, NRP2, LRP4, TGFBI · Prostate cancer, metastatic

**Data availability:** `available`

## Open Questions

- Does CDK12 biallelic inactivation in these PDXs confer sensitivity to PARP inhibition or immune checkpoint blockade as predicted by the focal tandem duplication/neoantigen model?
- Can the NRP2/LRP4/TGFBI FGFR1 co-expression signature identify patients most likely to benefit from FGFR inhibitors like dovitinib?
- Why do NEPC models upregulate DDR gene expression but harbor fewer DDR driver mutations than Ad models — is this a transcriptional/epigenetic rewiring in response to loss of RB1?
- Do longitudinal PDX pairs fully reflect clonal evolution under treatment, or does subcutaneous growth in untreated mice confound treatment-resistance modeling?
- What is the functional consequence of ARv7 expression as a standalone driver vs. requiring full-length AR co-expression?

## Limitations

- 44 PDXs from 38 patients is a relatively small cohort; statistical power limited for subgroup comparisons (e.g., Ad vs. NEPC genomic differences)
- All PDXs grown subcutaneously in SCID mice without treatment, removing treatment pressure and microenvironment effects present clinically
- No methylation arrays or ATAC-seq performed; epigenomic mechanisms suggested by FGFR1 data cannot be fully characterized
- Organoid drug testing data presented qualitatively in supplementary; not integrated with genomic features in a systematic pharmacogenomic framework
- No matched germline sequencing; germline BRCA/ATM variants not assessed
