## Summary
This MSK study profiled 212 patients with BRAF fusion-positive cancers from 97,024 NGS-tested samples, identifying 82 unique 5′ fusion partners across 52 histologies. BRAF fusions are most enriched in pilocytic astrocytomas (56%) and are frequently acquired as a resistance mechanism to EGFR TKIs in lung adenocarcinoma. Durable responses to MEK inhibitors were seen primarily in pilocytic astrocytomas, with implications for tumor-agnostic BRAF fusion testing and post-progression rebiopsy.

## Dataset
- **Cohort**: Adult and pediatric patients with BRAF fusion-positive cancers identified through prospective NGS genotyping at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, Jan 2014–Nov 2022
- **Assay**: MSK-IMPACT (DNA hybrid capture NGS), MSK-Fusion (RNA anchored multiplex PCR), MSK-ACCESS (ctDNA NGS)
- **N**: 97,024 samples screened; 212 patients with BRAF fusions (195 de novo, 17 post-treatment); 24 patients treated with MAPK-directed therapies
- **cBioPortal study ID(s)**: braf_msk_impact_2024, braf_msk_archer_2024

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: BRAF fusions are rare but span 52 histologies
- **Detail**: BRAF fusions occurred in 0.3% of patients (212/69,337); 195 de novo and 15 confirmed acquired. Found across 52 distinct adult and pediatric tumor types.
- **Genes**: BRAF
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: fusion, clinical

### Finding 2: Remarkable diversity of fusion partners — 82 unique 5′ partners, 39 novel
- **Detail**: 82 distinct 5′ fusion partners identified; 39 (48%) were not previously reported. Most common: KIAA1549 (23%), SND1 (9%), AGK (8%), TRIM24 (5%). 29% of fusions had non-recurrent (singleton) partners. Contrasts with other fusion oncogenes (e.g., ALK, ROS1) where one partner dominates.
- **Genes**: BRAF, KIAA1549, SND1, AGK, TRIM24, MKRN1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: fusion

### Finding 3: Histology-specific enrichment and partner preferences
- **Detail**: Pilocytic astrocytoma most enriched (56% prevalence); 90% of pilocytic astrocytoma cases carried KIAA1549-BRAF. SND1 dominated in prostate adenocarcinoma (21%) and pancreatic adenocarcinoma (56%). TRIM24-BRAF enriched in colorectal cancer (43%). BRAF fusions were mutually exclusive with other MAPK pathway alterations across all histologies.
- **Genes**: BRAF, KIAA1549, SND1, TRIM24, EGFR, KRAS, NRAS, RAF1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: fusion, mutation, clinical

### Finding 4: Modest and histology-dependent responses to MAPK-directed therapies
- **Detail**: Of 24 patients treated with MAPK therapies (MEK inhibitor alone 58%, MEK+BRAF inhibitor 46%, ERK inhibitor 4%), 20 were RECIST-evaluable: PR=2, SD=11, PD=7. Median time on MEK+BRAF inhibitors was 1 month (range 0–18); on MEK inhibitors alone was 8 months (range 1–26). Response rate is far below the 60–80% seen with ALK/ROS1/RET inhibitors.
- **Genes**: BRAF
- **Data availability**: partial
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: clinical, survival

### Finding 5: Durable benefit with MEK inhibitors in pilocytic astrocytoma
- **Detail**: All 6 pilocytic astrocytoma patients treated with MEK inhibitors (selumetinib N=4, trametinib N=2) remained on therapy >6 months; median time on treatment 11 months (range 6–26 months); 4/6 had disease shrinkage. One Erdheim-Chester patient on cobimetinib achieved 67% tumor reduction and remained on therapy 22+ months.
- **Genes**: BRAF, KIAA1549
- **Data availability**: partial
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: clinical, survival, fusion

### Finding 6: BRAF fusions as acquired resistance mechanism to targeted therapies
- **Detail**: 15 confirmed acquired BRAF fusions after targeted therapy: EGFR-mutant lung adenocarcinoma (N=10), FGFR3 fusion-positive bladder cancer (N=1), NTRK1 fusion-positive lung adenocarcinoma (N=1), BRAF V600E lung (N=1), CRC (N=1), papillary thyroid cancer (N=1). Median time from EGFR TKI start to BRAF fusion detection was 23 months (range 9–37). AGK-BRAF and TRIM24-BRAF were recurrent acquired partners in EGFR-mutant lung.
- **Genes**: BRAF, EGFR, FGFR3, NTRK1, AGK, TRIM24
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: fusion, mutation, clinical

### Finding 7: Co-alterations and mutual exclusivity with MAPK pathway
- **Detail**: Across all histologies, most common co-alterations were TP53 mutations (22%), TERT mutations (18%), CDKN2A deletions (14%), CDKN2B deletions (12%). Pilocytic astrocytomas had virtually no concurrent alterations. Colorectal cancer co-alterations included RNF43 (64%), TP53 (57%), KMT2D (43%). BRAF fusions were mutually exclusive with EGFR, KRAS, HRAS, NRAS, RAF1, and MAPK1 alterations.
- **Genes**: BRAF, TP53, TERT, CDKN2A, CDKN2B, RNF43, KMT2D, EGFR, KRAS, NRAS
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: mutation, copy_number, fusion, pathway

## Open Questions
- Do specific BRAF fusion partners (e.g., KIAA1549 vs. SND1 vs. AGK) predict differential response to MEK vs. pan-RAF inhibitors across histologies?
- Can cBioPortal data identify additional tumor types where BRAF fusions are enriched beyond those with ≥5 cases at MSK?
- Are acquired BRAF fusions more common in specific EGFR TKI sequences (e.g., osimertinib vs. earlier-generation TKIs)?
- Does TMB or co-occurring TP53 mutation status modulate response to MAPK-targeted therapy in BRAF fusion-positive tumors?

## Limitations
- Retrospective, single-institution design (MSK only); potential referral and ascertainment biases
- Heterogeneous patient population treated at varying disease stages and with multiple different MAPK therapy regimens, limiting cross-comparison
- Small number of patients per histology-treatment combination; underpowered for robust efficacy conclusions
- DNA-based NGS may underdetect BRAF fusions due to intronic rearrangements; RNA-based assay (MSK-Fusion) used in only 12 de novo cases
- Two patients lacked pre-treatment NGS, so acquired vs. de novo status could not be definitively confirmed
