## Summary

This MSK study prospectively sequenced 1,004 primary and recurrent tumors from 923 glioma patients using MSK-IMPACT and FoundationOne, integrating genomic data with clinical phenotypes and treatment histories. Key findings include cell-cycle alterations (especially CDKN2A/B deletions) as drivers of malignant progression in IDH-mutant astrocytomas, alkylating therapy-induced hypermutation in 22% of recurrent post-TMZ tumors, and BRAF mutation clonality/context as a determinant of MAPK-targeted therapy response. Data are deposited in cBioPortal as `glioma_mskcc_2019`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Cell-cycle alterations drive malignant progression and MRI enhancement in IDH-mutant astrocytomas

**Finding:** Genomic alterations in cell-cycle effectors (predominantly CDKN2A/B deletions) are strongly associated with contrast enhancement on MRI and poor prognosis in IDH-mutant, 1p19q-intact astrocytomas, independent of IDH status.

**Detail:** Cell-cycle alterations arose in 44% of enhancing vs. 8% of non-enhancing IDH-mutant 1p19q-intact tumors (P<0.001), but were essentially absent in 1p19q co-deleted oligodendrogliomas (3–4% regardless of enhancement). In recurrent grade II/III IDH-mutant 1p19q-intact tumors, cell-cycle alterations independently predicted shorter PFS (HR=2.6, 95% CI 1.6–5.7, P=0.02). Longitudinal sequencing of 22 patients showed cell-cycle aberrations temporally coincided with acquisition of contrast enhancement specifically in astrocytomas (6/11), not oligodendrogliomas (0/11, P=0.01). CDKN2A deletion was the most common alteration; CDK4 amplification and RB1 mutations also observed.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDKN2A`, `CDKN2B`, `CDK4`, `RB1` · IDH-mutant astrocytoma, lower-grade glioma

**Data availability:** `available`
> Mutation and copy-number alteration data for CDKN2A/B, CDK4, RB1 are in `glioma_mskcc_2019`. Clinical annotations including MRI enhancement status are available.

**Tool support:** `native`
> OncoPrint and Mutations tabs show CDKN2A/B alterations by subtype. Survival analysis by cell-cycle alteration status can be performed natively. CN alteration data viewable in Genomic Data tab.

### F2: Alkylating therapy-induced hypermutation is subtype-dependent and linked to MGMT methylation

**Finding:** TMZ-associated hypermutation occurs in 22% of recurrent post-alkylator tumors and is significantly more common in IDH-mutant gliomas (29%) than IDH-WT (12%), and in MGMT-methylated tumors (30% vs. 10%).

**Detail:** 38 tumors were hypermutated (14 IDH-WT, 8 IDH-mutant 1p19q-intact, 16 IDH-mutant 1p19q co-deleted). IDH-mutant tumors were more susceptible to therapy-induced hypermutation (P=0.004); MGMT methylation further enriched (P=0.006). Among 1p19q-intact astrocytic tumors with TMZ hypermutation, 82% harbored MMR gene mutations and all had pre-existing cell-cycle alterations that preceded hypermutation onset (confirmed in 3 matched pre/post-treatment cases). In 1p19q co-deleted oligodendrogliomas, hypermutation arose without cell-cycle alterations, suggesting a distinct mechanism of TMZ susceptibility.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MGMT`, `MLH1`, `MSH2`, `MSH6`, `CDKN2A` · Recurrent glioma, all subtypes

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Somatic mutation burden and mutational signature data are in `glioma_mskcc_2019`. MGMT methylation status is available for a subset of patients. Signature 11 (TMZ-associated) calls require external analysis.

**Tool support:** `api`

### F3: BRAF V600 clonality and co-mutational context determine response to MAPK-targeted therapy

**Finding:** BRAF-mutant gliomas comprise two subtypes: clonal V600E mutations in atypical/epithelioid GBMs and grade-progressed tumors (associated with RAF inhibitor responses), and subclonal V600/non-V600 mutations in conventional GBMs (associated with no response).

**Detail:** BRAF mutations occurred in 29/1,004 samples (2%). Clonal BRAF V600E mutations arose exclusively in atypical IDH-WT GBMs with epithelioid histology or tumors progressing from lower-grade lesions (17/17). In conventional GBMs, BRAF mutations (V600 and non-V600: G466, G469, L597) were largely subclonal. Among 7 BRAF V600E patients treated with MAPK-directed therapy, 4 had partial or near-complete responses (ongoing at 9–41 months) and 2 had stable disease; the sole non-responder had a subclonal BRAF V600E. One non-responding lesion in a mixed-response patient harbored a homozygous CDKN2A deletion absent from responding lesions—mirroring findings in BRAF V600-mutant melanoma.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `CDKN2A` · IDH-WT GBM, epithelioid GBM, lower-grade glioma

**Data availability:** `available`
> BRAF mutation data including allele fraction (clonality estimates) are in `glioma_mskcc_2019`. Treatment response data and clinical annotations partially available.

**Tool support:** `native`
> BRAF mutations viewable in Mutations tab with VAF. OncoPrint shows BRAF co-occurrence with CDKN2A. OncoKB annotation flags V600E as actionable. Clonality data queryable via API.

### F4: 13% of glioma patients harbor pathogenic germline variants, frequently targeting DNA repair

**Finding:** Pathogenic or likely pathogenic germline variants were detected in 13% (103/764) of evaluable glioma patients; 28% of these variants targeted DNA repair effectors including MMR, BRCA2, and ATM genes.

**Detail:** Germline findings included variants in NF1, TP53, PTEN, RB1 (high-penetrance syndromes), and DNA repair genes (BRCA2 n=3; MSH2 n=2, PMS1 n=2, MSH6 n=1, PMS2 n=1). Two MMR germline carriers had somatic biallelic inactivation with microsatellite instability and hypermutation consistent with Lynch syndrome. Pathogenic germline prevalence did not associate with age, grade, or IDH status, suggesting broad relevance across glioma subtypes. 32 patients had germline findings signed out in their medical record with genetic counseling referral.

**Genes / cancer types:** `NF1`, `TP53`, `PTEN`, `RB1`, `BRCA2`, `MSH2`, `MSH6`, `PMS1`, `PMS2` · All glioma subtypes

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Somatic mutation data in `glioma_mskcc_2019`; germline variant calls are not routinely deposited in cBioPortal for privacy reasons. MSI/hypermutation phenotype data available.

**Tool support:** `custom`

### F5: Core glioma subtype-defining alterations remain stable through recurrence and treatment

**Finding:** Cardinal genomic lesions defining glioma subtypes (IDH mutations, 1p19q codeletion, TERT promoter mutations, EGFR amplification, chromosome 7 gain/10 loss) are largely stable in frequency between primary and recurrent tumors.

**Detail:** Comparison of 514 primary vs. 135 recurrent IDH-WT tumors, and 219 primary vs. 136 recurrent IDH-mutant tumors, showed no significant shift in the frequencies of IDH, TERT, 1p19q, EGFR, PTEN, NF1, TP53, CIC, or FUBP1 alterations. However, among IDH-WT recurrences, NF1 and PTPN11 were more frequent in tumors originating from lower-grade disease. Among IDH-mutant tumors, significant differences occurred only in less frequently altered genes (<15%). FGFR3-TACC3 fusions were found in 16 patients; rare EGFR and BRAF kinase domain-retaining fusions in 5 patients.

**Genes / cancer types:** `IDH1`, `IDH2`, `TERT`, `EGFR`, `PTEN`, `NF1`, `TP53`, `CIC`, `FUBP1`, `ATRX`, `FGFR3` · All glioma subtypes

**Data availability:** `available`
> Full somatic alteration data for primary and recurrent specimens in `glioma_mskcc_2019`. Sample type (primary/recurrent) is annotated in clinical data.

**Tool support:** `native`
> OncoPrint and Comparison tab allow stratification by sample type. Gene frequency comparison between primary and recurrent subgroups can be performed natively.

### F6: 32% of glioma patients harbor actionable alterations; BRAF/RAF pathway most frequently targeted

**Finding:** 32% of patients had a potentially therapeutically actionable genomic lesion (OncoKB levels 1–3), but only 11% received a matched targeted therapy, highlighting an implementation gap in precision oncology for glioma.

**Detail:** Actionable alterations were more common in IDH-WT tumors (39% of 596) than IDH-mutant tumors (20% of 327, P<0.001), though IDH mutations themselves were not counted as actionable at the time of analysis (pre-ivosidenib approval). 32 patients received a targeted therapy on the basis of OncoKB level 1–3 evidence; an additional 32 received targeted agents based on preclinical evidence. Patients receiving targeted therapy were predominantly IDH-WT GBMs (60%). Identified novel hotspot mutations in EGFR and PDGFRA (34 previously uncharacterized) and novel candidate driver mutations in MAP3K1 and SF3B1 via analysis of 3,130 combined glioma samples.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `EGFR`, `PDGFRA`, `FGFR3`, `IDH1`, `IDH2`, `MAP3K1`, `SF3B1` · IDH-WT GBM, all glioma subtypes

**Data availability:** `available`
> Somatic mutations with OncoKB annotation available in `glioma_mskcc_2019`. Clinical treatment data partially available.

**Tool support:** `native`
> OncoKB integration in cBioPortal highlights actionable alterations. Mutations tab shows actionability tier. OncoPrint can filter for actionable alterations across the cohort.

## Open Questions

- Can cell-cycle alteration status (especially CDKN2A/B deletion) at initial sequencing serve as a prospective biomarker to select IDH-mutant astrocytoma patients for earlier aggressive intervention?
- Does MGMT methylation combined with IDH status and cell-cycle alteration status create a multivariate model that better predicts TMZ hypermutation risk and treatment failure?
- What mechanisms underlie differential susceptibility to TMZ-induced hypermutation between 1p19q-intact astrocytomas (cell-cycle-dependent) and oligodendrogliomas (cell-cycle-independent)?
- Can clonal BRAF V600E status be reliably determined from liquid biopsy (cfDNA) to guide RAF/MEK inhibitor selection in glioma patients who cannot undergo repeat biopsy?
- Do the newly identified hotspot mutations in MAP3K1 and SF3B1 represent functional drivers in glioma, and are they therapeutically actionable?

## Limitations

- MSK single-institution cohort; referral bias toward complex/recurrent cases may skew prevalence estimates
- FoundationOne samples (n=167) lack matched normal controls, limiting germline analysis and allele-specific copy number calls
- MGMT methylation available for only 636/923 patients (69%), limiting power for methylation-stratified analyses
- Small numbers of BRAF-mutant patients receiving targeted therapy (n=7) preclude formal efficacy conclusions; response data are exploratory
- MRI contrast enhancement as a surrogate for malignant progression is a radiographic endpoint with known limitations (pseudo-progression, steroid effects)
- Germline findings are not publicly deposited in cBioPortal, limiting reproducibility of the germline analysis
