## Summary

Extra-cranial malignant rhabdoid tumors (MRT) are aggressive pediatric solid tumors (median diagnosis age 11 months, 4-year OS 23.2%) driven almost universally by biallelic *SMARCB1* inactivation. This first whole-genome sequencing study of extra-cranial MRT (n=40) — augmented by RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, WGBS, and H3K27me3/H3K27ac ChIP-seq — reveals that despite a remarkably quiet mutational landscape (median 612.5 SNVs/tumor), these tumors exhibit reproducible molecular heterogeneity partitioned into two subgroups at the miRNA, transcriptomic, and DNA-methylation levels. Convergent multi-omic evidence implicates dysregulated neural crest developmental programs, aberrant HOX gene activation via super-enhancers, and loss of H3K27me3 at homeobox gene promoters as downstream consequences of SMARCB1 loss.

## Key Findings

### F1: SMARCB1 biallelic inactivation is nearly universal; somatic mutation rate is exceptionally low

**Finding:** *SMARCB1* inactivation (via mutation, copy-number loss, or somatic LOH) was confirmed in 39 of 40 WGS cases; the single exception harbored a germline *SMARCA4* deletion with somatic LOH, establishing SWI/SNF subunit loss as the universal driver of extra-cranial MRT.

**Detail:** Somatic SNV rates were among the lowest reported in cancer: median 612.5 mutations per case (0.231 mutations/Mb), with 97.1% of mutations in non-coding regions and a median of only 5 non-synonymous amino acid substitutions per tumor (range 1–17). A positive correlation between age at diagnosis and mutation rate was observed. *DSCAM* was the only recurrently mutated gene beyond *SMARCB1* (2 cases with heterozygous somatic NS substitutions). Eleven recurrent focal copy-number deletion loci were identified, 9 of 15 on chromosome 22 where *SMARCB1* resides.

**Genes / cancer types:** `SMARCB1` · `SMARCA4` · `DSCAM` · Malignant Rhabdoid Tumor

### F2: miRNA profiling identifies two MRT subgroups with distinct tissue-of-origin affinities

**Finding:** Unsupervised clustering of miRNA-seq profiles from 66 primary MRTs against 11,819 TCGA/normal samples (37 cancer types, 27 normal tissue types) partitions MRT into two groups: a large group (n=57) clustering with normal cerebellum and neural-crest-origin cancers (pheochromocytomas/paragangliomas), and a small group (n=9) clustering with synovial sarcomas (SWI/SNF-dysregulated, neural-origin tumors).

**Detail:** 535 mature miRNAs were detected in MRT. NMF consensus clustering (n=66) independently confirmed two miRNA subgroups: 17 miRNAs over-expressed in subgroup 1 and 12 in subgroup 2 (log₂ FC >1, FDR <0.05). All 5 members of the EMT-repressing miR-200 family (miR-200a, miR-200b, miR-200c, miR-141, miR-429) were expressed at lower levels in subgroup 1, suggesting more active epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT). miRNA target gene GO analysis identified neuron development and differentiation genes (e.g., *VANGL2*, *ULK1*, *NGFR*) as top candidate targets (Spearman rho < −0.65). *SMARCE1* was identified as a putative target of miR-200a, suggesting miRNA-mediated feedback on SWI/SNF.

**Genes / cancer types:** `VANGL2` · `ULK1` · `NGFR` · `SMARCE1` · Malignant Rhabdoid Tumor · Pheochromocytoma · Paraganglioma

### F3: RNA-seq reveals two transcriptomic subgroups with differential neural crest and developmental pathway activation

**Finding:** NMF clustering of RNA-seq data from 40 MRT cases identifies two transcriptomic subgroups with 880 differentially expressed genes (FDR <0.05), with subgroup 1 enriched for extra-renal tumors (all 6 liver/soft-tissue cases) and subgroup 2 more similar to renal MRT, mirroring the AT/RT vs RTK distinction.

**Detail:** Compared to both hESC and fetal cerebellum comparators, MRT over-expressed immune-function genes (*IGKC*, *IGKJ5*) and embryonic development genes (*MGP*, *LUM*), while under-expressing neuron-function genes (*GABRA3*, *CADPS*) and embryonic development genes (*ZIC3*, *SOX3*). Between subgroups: subgroup 1 showed high expression of BMP-signaling (*BMP4*, *SOSTDC1*) and differentiation (*DLK1*, *MEOX2*) genes; subgroup 2 showed high expression of WNT signaling (*WNT5A*, *HIC1*) and cell adhesion (*PCDH18*, *SMOC2*) genes. GO analysis revealed significant enrichment of neural crest development and differentiation terms (BH-adjusted p=0.049). Annotated tumor suppressors and oncogenes were enriched among differentially expressed genes (hypergeometric p=1.03e-39).

**Genes / cancer types:** `BMP4` · `SOSTDC1` · `DLK1` · `WNT5A` · `HIC1` · `ZIC3` · `SOX3` · `GABRA3` · Malignant Rhabdoid Tumor

### F4: DNA methylation subgroups correlate with age at diagnosis; homeobox TSG promoters silenced in older patients

**Finding:** WGBS of 40 MRT cases identifies two DNA methylation subgroups — subgroup A (n=10, higher global methylation, enriched for patients >1 year old) and subgroup B (n=30, lower methylation, clustering with hESC) — where promoter CpG island hypermethylation in subgroup A preferentially targets homeobox genes and known tumor suppressor genes.

**Detail:** Subgroup A had significantly higher promoter CGI methylation than subgroup B (Welch's t-test p=4.629e-16) and higher non-CGI methylation (p=2.2e-16). Enrichment for patients older than one year in subgroup A was significant (one-sided Fisher's exact p=0.0013). Homeobox terms were enriched among hypermethylated promoters in subgroup A (BH-adjusted Welch's t-test p<0.05). TSG promoters with methylation gain in MRT vs hESC (Fisher's exact p=0.02041) included *RASSF1*, *IRX1*, *TWIST2*, *DLEC1*, and *TBX5* — TSGs reported as methylated in other cancer types.

**Genes / cancer types:** `RASSF1` · `IRX1` · `TWIST2` · `DLEC1` · `TBX5` · Malignant Rhabdoid Tumor

### F5: H3K27me3 is depleted at homeobox gene promoters in MRT; 158 promoters show decreased density

**Finding:** H3K27me3 ChIP-seq of 35 primary MRT samples reveals 158 promoters with decreased H3K27me3 density compared to normal cell types, with highly significant enrichment of homeobox, HOX, and homeodomain terms (FDR range: 4.17e-44 to 1.67e-37), while only 42 promoters show increased H3K27me3 (no significant pathway enrichment).

**Detail:** ChIP-seq was performed on 35 primary MRT samples, 3 MRT cell lines, 2 hESC lines, and comparators including normal kidney, fetal brain, and neural progenitor cells (NPC). Hierarchical clustering of the 200 most differentially methylated promoters between MRT and normals identified three gene clusters — one with H3K27me3 gain and two with loss. Other enriched terms in the H3K27me3-depleted set included pattern formation, morphogenesis, organogenesis, DNA binding, and transcription regulation — all driven primarily by homeobox genes. This reprogramming was heterogeneous within the MRT cohort despite near-universal SMARCB1 loss.

**Genes / cancer types:** `HOXA` · `HOXB` · `HOXC` · `HOXD` · Malignant Rhabdoid Tumor

### F6: MRT-specific super-enhancers at HOXA/B/C clusters and HOTAIR locus drive HOX gene overexpression

**Finding:** H3K27ac ChIP-seq of 10 primary MRT samples identifies 136 MRT-specific super-enhancers (present in ≥50% of MRT, absent in fetal brain and hESC) associated with 197 genes, most strikingly at *HOXA*, *HOXB*, and *HOXC* gene clusters and the lncRNA *HOTAIR*, with significant enrichment for homeobox/homeodomain terms (FDR <0.0001).

**Detail:** Super-enhancers were defined by high regional H3K27ac density relative to typical enhancers. MRT-specific super-enhancer-associated genes were enriched for members of *HOXA*, *HOXB*, *HOXC*, *HIST1H*, and *HIST2H* gene clusters, as well as *FLT3LG* and *STAT3* (both regulators of cellular differentiation and ESC/neural crest self-renewal). The super-enhancer at the *HOXC* cluster included *HOTAIR*, providing a mechanistic explanation for *HOTAIR* overexpression in MRT (previously reported in AT/RT). High *HOXC* member expression in MRT vs normal cell types was confirmed by RNA-seq. Super-enhancer findings are consistent with, and complementary to, the H3K27me3 loss at HOX loci (F5).

**Genes / cancer types:** `HOTAIR` · `STAT3` · `FLT3LG` · `HOXA` · `HOXB` · `HOXC` · Malignant Rhabdoid Tumor

**Data availability:** `partial`

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Do the two transcriptomic and miRNA subgroups (subgroup 1 ≈ AT/RT-like; subgroup 2 ≈ RTK-like) represent genuinely distinct cells of origin within the neural crest lineage, or do they reflect different stages of a single differentiation trajectory?
- What determines whether SMARCB1 loss produces the high-methylation (subgroup A, older patients) vs low-methylation (subgroup B, younger patients) phenotype — is this a developmental timing effect of when SMARCB1 loss occurs during embryogenesis?
- Could the EMT activity implied by low miR-200 family expression in subgroup 1 be therapeutically targeted (e.g., with EMT inhibitors), and does it explain the worse outcomes of extra-renal/soft-tissue MRT?
- Are the MRT-specific super-enhancers at HOX loci directly causally linked to neural crest gene expression programs, and can BET bromodomain inhibitors (targeting H3K27ac-marked super-enhancers) suppress HOX-driven oncogenesis in MRT?
- *SPECC1L* intron mutations in 6 cases and *KCNJ3* intron mutations in 8 cases are associated with significant expression changes — are these recurrent non-coding driver events or passengers co-selected with SMARCB1 deletions on chromosome 22?
- Given the age-methylation correlation (subgroup A enriched for patients >1 year), do older MRT patients have distinct prognoses or therapeutic vulnerabilities compared to younger patients, and can epigenetic subgroup status serve as a clinical biomarker?

## Limitations (from paper)

- The WGS cohort (n=40) is small for a molecularly heterogeneous disease, and overlapping but non-identical sample sets were used across different assay types (miRNA n=66, RNA-seq n=40, WGBS n=40, H3K27me3 ChIP-seq n=35, H3K27ac ChIP-seq n=10), limiting fully integrated multi-omic analyses across all cases.
- The cohort is enriched for renal MRT (58/67 primary tumors from kidney), so findings in extra-renal sites (soft tissue n=7, liver n=2) may not be fully representative; the extra-renal cases were too few for robust subgroup characterization independent of renal cases.
- Cell of origin remains unknown; the neural crest hypothesis is supported by multiple indirect lines of evidence (miRNA clustering, gene expression, pathway enrichment) but is not definitively established by lineage-tracing or functional experiments.
- Comparison to cranial MRT (AT/RT) was limited to publicly available gene expression data (Grupenmacher et al. 2013) rather than a directly profiled cohort, precluding multi-omic comparison at equivalent depth across brain vs extra-cranial sites.
- H3K27ac ChIP-seq was performed on only 10 primary tumors, limiting the statistical power to define super-enhancer prevalence and their relationship to expression subgroups; the reported 136 MRT-specific super-enhancers should be considered exploratory.
- No survival analysis correlating molecular subgroups with patient outcomes was presented, leaving the clinical prognostic significance of the identified subgroups uncharacterized.
