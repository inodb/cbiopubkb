## Summary

Papaemmanuil et al. sequenced 111 cancer genes plus cytogenetics in 1,540 AML patients enrolled in three prospective German-Austrian trials, identifying 5,234 driver mutations across 76 genes and defining 11 mutually exclusive genomic subgroups. The two largest novel subgroups—chromatin–spliceosome AML (18%) and TP53–aneuploidy AML (13%)—carry poor prognoses but are largely miscategorized as intermediate risk under existing European LeukemiaNet (ELN) guidelines. Beyond subgroup membership, co-occurring driver mutations interact non-additively to further shape individual prognosis, most strikingly in NPM1-mutated AML where co-mutation context determines whether outcomes are favorable or adverse.

## Key Findings

### F1: Eleven genomic subgroups comprehensively reclassify AML

**Finding:** A Bayesian co-mutation model partitions 1,540 AML patients into 11 mutually exclusive genomic subgroups that supersede the 2008 WHO morphologic classification.

**Detail:** The 11 subgroups and their frequencies are: NPM1-mutated (27%), chromatin–spliceosome (18%), TP53–aneuploidy (13%), inv(16)/CBFB–MYH11 (5%), biallelic CEBPA (4%), t(15;17)/PML–RARA (4%), t(8;21)/RUNX1–RUNX1T1 (4%), MLL fusions (3%), inv(3)/GATA2-MECOM (1%), IDH2-R172 (1%), and t(6;9)/DEK–NUP214 (1%). A total of 80% of patients with driver mutations were unambiguously assigned to a single subgroup; 11% remained unclassified. The classification was independently validated in the TCGA AML cohort with equivalent subgroup frequencies.

**Genes / cancer types:** `NPM1` · `CEBPA` · `TP53` · `IDH2` · `RUNX1` · `ASXL1` · `SRSF2` · AML

**Data availability:** partial — mutation and cytogenetic data for the original AMLSG cohort are deposited at EGA (EGAS00001000275), not in cBioPortal; the `laml_tcga` study used for validation is available in cBioPortal.

**Tool support:** native — subgroup classifications (fusion genes, NPM1, CEBPA, TP53) are viewable via oncoprint and patient views in cBioPortal for the TCGA AML study.

### F2: Chromatin–spliceosome AML is misclassified as intermediate risk by ELN but has adverse outcomes

**Finding:** The chromatin–spliceosome subgroup (18% of cohort, n=275) is characterized by mutations in RNA-splicing and chromatin-regulator genes and carries a prognosis as poor as MLL-fusion or t(6;9) AML.

**Detail:** Key defining genes include RUNX1 (39%), MLL-PTD (25%), SRSF2 (22%), ASXL1 (17%), STAG2 (16%), and SF3B1. Under ELN 2010 criteria, 84% (232/275) of these patients would be classified as intermediate risk. This subgroup showed lower white-cell and blast counts, higher relapse rates, and only 9% had an antecedent chronic myeloid disorder, while 91% presented as de novo AML. The same genes are recurrently mutated in MDS and high-risk myeloproliferative neoplasms, suggesting a shared biology across diagnostic boundaries.

**Genes / cancer types:** `RUNX1` · `ASXL1` · `SRSF2` · `STAG2` · `MLL` · `SF3B1` · `U2AF1` · `ZRSR2` · `BCOR` · `EZH2` · AML

**Data availability:** partial — RUNX1, ASXL1, SRSF2, and related genes are queryable in `laml_tcga` via cBioPortal; the full AMLSG chromatin–spliceosome cohort is in EGA.

**Tool support:** native — co-mutation patterns for these genes are accessible via OncoPrint and mutual exclusivity tools in cBioPortal.

### F3: TP53 mutations and chromosomal aneuploidy have independent and additive adverse effects on survival

**Finding:** TP53 mutations (HR 1.7, 95% CI 1.4–2.2, P=7×10⁻⁶) and complex karyotype (HR 1.4, 95% CI 1.2–1.7, P=2×10⁻⁶) each independently worsen overall survival, and their co-occurrence is cumulatively devastating.

**Detail:** The TP53–aneuploidy subgroup (n=199; 13% of cohort) encompasses complex karyotype (68%), del(5q) (47%), del(7q) (44%), TP53 mutation (44%), and del(17p) (31%). This group is broader than previously defined "monosomal karyotype AML" or "complex karyotype AML" categories. Patients in this subgroup were older and had fewer RAS-pathway mutations than other subgroups, possibly because chromosomal aneuploidies create RAS-pathway activation without point mutation. Outcomes are dismal regardless of whether TP53 mutation, complex karyotype, or both define the subgroup assignment.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` · AML

**Data availability:** partial — TP53 mutation and cytogenetic data are available in `laml_tcga` in cBioPortal; del(5q), del(7q), and del(17p) are queryable as copy-number alterations.

**Tool support:** native — TP53 mutation status and copy-number alterations are viewable natively in cBioPortal; survival analysis by TP53 status accessible via the Survival tab.

**Concepts:** #TP53 #copy-number #prognosis #survival #AML

### F4: Genomic features explain two-thirds of variation in overall survival, outperforming ELN criteria

**Finding:** A multivariate Cox model incorporating all genomic, clinical, and demographic variables achieves ~71% concordance for overall survival ranking, versus ~64% using ELN variables alone.

**Detail:** Among all predictor categories, genomic features (fusion genes, copy-number alterations, and point mutations) account for approximately two-thirds of explained variation in overall survival, with the remaining third contributed by demographic (age, sex), clinical (performance status, blast count, WBC), and treatment variables. The model included 228 variables with 18 reaching q<0.1 significance. Key independent adverse factors include inv(3)/MECOM (HR 2.9), TP53 (HR 1.7), complex karyotype (HR 1.4), FLT3-ITD (HR 1.4), SRSF2 (HR 1.4), and del(5q) (HR 1.3). Key independent favorable factors include t(15;17)/PML-RARA (HR 0.3), inv(16)/CBFB-MYH11 (HR 0.3), biallelic CEBPA (HR 0.6), and NPM1 (HR 0.7).

**Genes / cancer types:** `FLT3` · `NPM1` · `CEBPA` · `SRSF2` · `ASXL1` · `TP53` · `DNMT3A` · `IDH2` · AML

**Data availability:** partial — individual gene mutation data and survival data are available in `laml_tcga`; the full multivariate model requires the complete AMLSG dataset from EGA.

**Tool support:** native — survival analysis by mutation status is accessible in cBioPortal's Survival tab; multivariable modeling requires custom analysis (api/custom).

### F5: NPM1-mutated AML prognosis is profoundly modified by co-mutation context

**Finding:** Within the NPM1-mutated subgroup (27% of cohort, n=418), co-mutation patterns—particularly with FLT3-ITD, DNMT3A, and NRAS—create subsets with markedly different prognoses.

**Detail:** A three-way NPM1–DNMT3A–FLT3-ITD interaction was the most frequent three-gene co-occurrence (n=93, 6% of all patients; q=0.004), where FLT3-ITD carries its worst prognostic effect specifically in the context of co-mutated NPM1 and DNMT3A (HR ~1.5 for triple vs. much smaller effects in pairwise combinations). Conversely, NPM1–DNMT3A–NRAS-G12/13 carried an unexpectedly favorable prognosis (P=0.04). NPM1 co-occurs with IDH2-R140 (OR 3.6, P=5×10⁻¹⁰) but is mutually exclusive with IDH2-R172 (OR 0.06, P=4×10⁻⁵). Overall, 73% of NPM1-mutated patients (319/436) also harbored mutations in DNA methylation or hydroxymethylation genes (DNMT3A, IDH1, IDH2-R140, TET2).

**Genes / cancer types:** `NPM1` · `FLT3` · `DNMT3A` · `IDH1` · `IDH2` · `NRAS` · `TET2` · AML

**Data availability:** partial — NPM1, DNMT3A, FLT3-ITD, IDH1, IDH2, NRAS, and TET2 mutation data are available in `laml_tcga` in cBioPortal; the full interaction analysis requires the AMLSG cohort.

**Tool support:** native — co-mutation exploration via OncoPrint and comparison groups in cBioPortal; three-way interaction survival analysis requires custom statistical modeling.

### F6: Clonal evolution in AML follows ordered evolutionary trajectories

**Finding:** Epigenetic modifier mutations (DNMT3A, ASXL1, IDH1/2, TET2) are acquired earliest in clonal evolution and function as founding-clone events, while RAS-pathway mutations typically occur late and recurrently.

**Detail:** Allele-fraction inference across 1,540 patients showed that DNMT3A, ASXL1, IDH1/2, and TET2—genes also mutated in clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP)—are almost always present in the founding clone and rarely found in isolation, suggesting they are necessary but not sufficient for overt AML. NPM1 mutations were usually secondary events, often following DNMT3A, IDH1, or NRAS mutations. RAS-pathway mutations (KRAS, NRAS, PTPN11) occurred late and were found multiple times in the same patient, indicating convergent evolution. This ordered trajectory explains why IDH2-R140 co-occurs with NPM1 (via shared early epigenetic priming) whereas IDH2-R172 does not.

**Genes / cancer types:** `DNMT3A` · `ASXL1` · `IDH1` · `IDH2` · `TET2` · `NPM1` · `NRAS` · `KRAS` · AML

**Data availability:** partial — mutation data for these genes are available in `laml_tcga`; clonal hierarchy and allele-fraction data are not available in standard cBioPortal views.

**Tool support:** api — mutation allele frequencies are accessible via the cBioPortal REST API; clonal ordering analysis requires custom computational tools (e.g., PyClone, ABSOLUTE).

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Does the chromatin–spliceosome AML subgroup respond differently to hypomethylating agents or venetoclax combinations compared with conventional intensive chemotherapy, given its transcriptional overlap with MDS?
- Can FLT3 inhibitor benefit in NPM1-mutated AML be predicted more precisely by requiring co-mutation with DNMT3A (the adverse three-way interaction context) rather than FLT3-ITD allele burden alone?
- Is IDH2-R172 AML a distinct entity warranting its own WHO classification entry, given its mutual exclusivity with NPM1, distinct methylation and metabolic profiles, and relatively favorable outcomes similar to NPM1-mutated AML?
- What are the clinical consequences of treating TP53-aneuploidy AML (a broad group) as homogeneous, versus stratifying by TP53 mutation status versus isolated aneuploidy versus both?
- Do the ordered evolutionary trajectories (epigenetic founders → NPM1/RAS subclones) suggest windows for clonal interception in CHIP carriers before AML transformation?
- How will the introduction of targeted therapies (FLT3 inhibitors, IDH1/2 inhibitors, venetoclax) alter survival differences among the 11 subgroups without changing the fundamental biology of subgroup membership?

## Limitations (from paper)

- The cohort is drawn from intensive-chemotherapy clinical trials, enriching for younger (18–84 years) and relatively fit patients; older, frail, or unfit patients are underrepresented, limiting generalizability.
- Sequencing used a 111-gene targeted panel rather than whole-genome or whole-exome sequencing, so drivers outside the panel (estimated to account for some of the 11% unclassified patients) were missed.
- The chromatin–spliceosome subgroup cannot be further subdivided due to overlapping co-mutation patterns among its defining genes, obscuring potential biologically distinct sub-entities.
- Gene–gene interaction terms (n=126 in the model) require large sample sizes to estimate accurately; many interactions remain underpowered and the findings require prospective validation.
- The IDH2-R172 subgroup (n=18, 1% of cohort) is too small for definitive prognostic conclusions and is labeled "provisional."
- The classification and prognostic models were validated only in the TCGA AML cohort, which overlaps in patient characteristics; external validation in independently treated cohorts with modern targeted therapies is needed.
