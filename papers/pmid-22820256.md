## Summary

WES of 92 primary medulloblastoma/normal pairs — the largest genomic analysis of this childhood brain tumor at the time. Median mutation rate of 0.35 non-silent mutations/Mb, among the lowest for solid tumors, consistent with other pediatric cancers. Twelve genes achieved statistical significance by MutSig, including established drivers CTNNB1 (WNT subgroup) and PTCH1 (SHH subgroup), plus novel findings: DDX3X (RNA helicase, co-mutated with CTNNB1 in WNT tumors), and N-CoR complex members GPS2, BCOR, and LDB1. Six of 12 significant genes are chromatin/histone modifiers. Subtype-specific analysis revealed that Group 3 and Group 4 tumors are driven primarily by structural copy number changes rather than point mutations. DDX3X mutants potentiate WNT/β-catenin transactivation functionally. Dataset available in `mbl_broad_2012` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Subtype-specific mutation landscape — WNT and SHH tumors are mutation-driven; Group 3/4 are copy-number-driven

**Finding:** The 12 significantly mutated genes are concentrated in WNT and SHH subgroup tumors; Group 3 (c1/c5) and Group 4 (c2/c4) tumors with extensive somatic copy number alterations show virtually no recurrent point mutations in these genes, suggesting fundamentally different oncogenic mechanisms by subtype.

**Detail:** WNT tumors (n=6): all had CTNNB1 mutations with concurrent loss of chromosome 6, and several had concurrent DDX3X, TP53, SMARCA4, or CSNK2B mutations. SHH tumors: all PTCH1 mutations co-occurred with 9q loss (hemizygosity); SUFU, SMO, GLI2, and other Hh pathway genes also mutated exclusively in SHH. Group 3 tumors had SMARCA4 and GPS2 mutations enriched within the c1 subtype. Group 4 showed enrichment of MLL3, HDAC2, and KDM6A mutations in 'i17q-only' tumors. KDM6A mutations occurred exclusively in tumors with i17q as the sole autosomal alteration.

**Genes / cancer types:** CTNNB1, PTCH1, DDX3X, SMARCA4, GPS2, KDM6A, SUFU, SMO, GLI2 · Medulloblastoma

### F2: DDX3X — a novel RNA helicase recurrently mutated in WNT medulloblastoma that potentiates β-catenin signaling

**Finding:** DDX3X was mutated in 7 tumors (7.6%), making it the fourth most significantly mutated gene (q=2.3×10⁻⁸). Half of WNT subgroup tumors harbored DDX3X mutations concurrent with CTNNB1 mutations (P=0.005, Fisher's exact test). All 7 mutations were missense variants clustering in the helicase ATP-binding (residues 211–403) and helicase C-terminal (414–575) domains.

**Detail:** Functional studies in 293T cells and D425 medulloblastoma cells showed that DDX3X mutants potentiated TCF4-luciferase (TOPflash) reporter activity and cell viability specifically in combination with mutant (S33Y) but not wild-type β-catenin. Wild-type DDX3X showed no such effect. DDX3X mutations have also been reported in CLL and head/neck cancers with WNT pathway dysregulation, suggesting a conserved role. An additional 15% of medulloblastomas had mutations in other RNA helicases (DHX9, DHX32, DHX57, FANCM, SKIV2L, SETX), suggesting broad disruption of RNA helicase activity across the disease.

**Genes / cancer types:** DDX3X, CTNNB1, DHX9, DHX32, FANCM, SETX · Medulloblastoma

### F3: Histone modification genes are the most broadly disrupted pathway across medulloblastoma subtypes

**Finding:** Six of 12 significantly mutated genes encode histone/chromatin modifiers: MLL2, GPS2, KDM6A, BCOR, SMARCA4, and LDB1. As a gene set, histone methyltransferases (HMTs) were enriched for somatic mutation across 21 tumors (q=5.8×10⁻⁹). MLL3 and HDAC2 were additionally significant in subtype-specific analysis of Group 4.

**Detail:** MLL2 mutations (10 mutations in 8 patients) were predominantly loss-of-function, replicating findings from Parsons et al. KDM6A (UTX) mutations occurred exclusively in i17q tumors and were enriched in females. BCOR (X-linked) frameshift mutations were found in 3 tumors, mostly SHH subgroup — allele fractions near 0.9 in males indicating hemizygosity. LDB1 mutations (2 SHH tumors) also appeared hemizygous due to 10q loss. GPS2 mutations cluster in residues 53–90, disrupting its N-CoR complex assembly domain. Collectively, the N-CoR complex (GPS2, BCOR, LDB1, NCOR2) is disrupted in multiple subtypes.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLL2, KDM6A, GPS2, BCOR, LDB1, SMARCA4, HDAC2, MLL3, NCOR2 · Medulloblastoma

### F4: Germline predisposition in SHH medulloblastoma via PTCH1 and SUFU

**Finding:** Two SHH patients had germline loss-of-function variants in PTCH1 (c.3030delC frameshift; T1052M reported in holoprosencephaly), each with somatic 9q loss creating hemizygosity. Two additional patients had germline SUFU loss-of-function variants (1 frameshift, 1 nonsense) that became hemizygous via somatic chromosome 10 loss or copy-neutral LOH.

**Detail:** These findings confirm PTCH1 and SUFU as medulloblastoma predisposition genes in the germline context. The two-hit mechanism (germline variant + somatic LOH) mirrors classical tumor suppressor biology. Germline testing implications are significant for pediatric SHH medulloblastoma patients and their families. These cases illustrate that somatic copy number alterations (9q, chr10 loss) cooperate with germline variants to complete tumor suppressor inactivation in this subtype.

**Genes / cancer types:** PTCH1, SUFU · Medulloblastoma (SHH subtype)

### F5: Low overall mutation rate reflects pediatric cancer biology; adult medulloblastomas are hypermutated relative to pediatric

**Finding:** Median 16 somatic mutations per tumor (12 non-silent, 4 silent), corresponding to 0.35 non-silent mutations/Mb — among the lowest for solid tumors and consistent with other pediatric cancers. The 6 highest-mutation tumors were from the oldest patients (16–31 years at diagnosis; P=7.7×10⁻⁵, Wilcoxon rank-sum), indicating age-dependent mutation accumulation.

**Detail:** The low mutation rate is in stark contrast to adult epithelial cancers (10–100× higher). This has implications for immunotherapy: low neoantigen burden in pediatric medulloblastoma may limit PD-1/CTLA-4 response. The age correlation with mutation burden is consistent with increased time for somatic mutation accumulation and potential carcinogen exposure in older patients. The statistical discovery framework (MutSig) had to account for this low background to identify the 12 significant genes with adequate power in n=92 samples.

## Open Questions

1. Can DDX3X inhibition suppress WNT-driven medulloblastoma growth, given its mechanistic role in potentiating mutant β-catenin signaling?
2. What is the full penetrance and clinical management implication of germline PTCH1/SUFU variants in pediatric SHH medulloblastoma patients?
3. Do N-CoR complex disruptions (GPS2, BCOR, LDB1) define a targetable vulnerability — for example, through HDAC or BET inhibitors?
4. Why do Group 3 and Group 4 tumors accumulate copy number changes rather than point mutations, and what drives their structural instability?
5. Could histone modifier gene mutations (MLL2, KDM6A) predict sensitivity to epigenetic therapies such as EZH2 or DOT1L inhibitors?

## Limitations

- Sample size of 92 limits statistical power to detect genes mutated in <5% of tumors, particularly within subtypes.
- WGS was not performed; structural rearrangements within Group 3/4 are inferred from SNP array CNA data only.
- Functional studies for DDX3X focused on WNT signaling; its roles in splicing, RNA transport, and translation in medulloblastoma context remain unexplored.
- Germline vs. somatic distinction for some low-allele-fraction mutations may be imprecise without deep germline validation.
- No RNA-seq data: splicing consequences of spliceosome-related mutations cannot be assessed.
