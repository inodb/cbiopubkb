## Summary

Comprehensive genomic and transcriptomic analysis of 38 acral lentiginous melanomas (ALMs) from 34 patients using paired exome sequencing, long-insert whole-genome sequencing (LIWG), and RNA-seq. Key findings: ALM is dominated by structural variation rather than point mutations; UV mutational signatures are largely absent; only 38% of tumors carry the canonical BRAF/NRAS/NF1 drivers found in cutaneous melanoma; and TERT aberrations (promoter mutations, copy gains, translocations, germline events) occur in 41% of patients. PAK1 copy gains are found in 15% of patients. In vitro TERT inhibition shows selective cytotoxicity in ALM cell lines, suggesting TERT as a therapeutic target. Data available in `mel_tsam_liang_2017` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: TERT aberrations are frequent (41%) and mechanistically diverse in ALM

**Finding:** 14 of 34 (41%) ALM patients carry somatic or germline TERT aberrations, including promoter mutations (9%), copy number gains (Chr 5p), structural translocations, coding missense mutations, and germline damaging variants. This contrasts with cutaneous melanoma, where TERT promoter mutations dominate (>70%) via UV-induced C>T transitions, a mechanism largely absent in sun-shielded acral skin.

**Detail:** TERT gains were the most common event and co-occurred with CLPTM1L gains on Chr 5p. Sanger sequencing identified promoter mutations (Chr 5:1,295,113 G>A) in 9% of patients. LIWG detected four patients with TERT structural breakpoints; complex rearrangements (inversions, translocations to ADCY2, PDCD1LG2/PDL2, FER) were observed. Germline damaging TERT variants (A1062T, T1110M; predicted pathogenic by FATHMM) were found in two additional patients mutually exclusive of somatic events. Nearly all TERT-aberrant tumors showed detectable TERT mRNA expression.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, CLPTM1L, ADCY2, PDCD1LG2 · Acral lentiginous melanoma

### F2: ALM is driven by structural variation, not UV point mutations

**Finding:** ALM has a low somatic point mutation burden (median 42 coding mutations; range 0–869) and lacks a dominant UV signature. C>T transitions at dipyrimidines fall below the 60% threshold defining UV signature in only 2 of 34 patients. Structural variants (SVs) dominate the landscape: 74% of patients have at least one somatic SV (median 31 breakpoints), with some tumors showing >200 events including chromothripsis.

**Detail:** 2,490 somatic breakpoints were identified across 31 patients with LIWG data. Patient 14 showed chromothripsis with 503 breakpoints on Chr 12. ADCY2 (21% of patients) and CLPTM1L (15%) are the most frequently rearranged genes by breakpoint count. Mutational signature analysis confirmed the absence of a UV-dominant signature across the cohort (ANOVA p=0.699 across signature distributions). Comparison of mutation burden between primary and metastatic tumors showed a non-significant trend toward higher burden in metastases (mean=113 vs. 50, p=0.06), partially attributable to prior immunotherapy.

### F3: Only 38% of ALMs carry canonical BRAF/NRAS/NF1 drivers; KIT is rare

**Finding:** In contrast to cutaneous melanoma where BRAF (50%), NRAS (30%), and NF1 (15%) mutations account for most drivers, only 10 of 34 ALM patients (29%) carry mutually exclusive BRAF or NRAS mutations. NF1 homozygous loss is observed in 9% of patients. KIT mutations are rare (1 patient with KIT L576P), challenging prior estimates of 11% in ALM.

**Detail:** BRAF V600E was present in 4 patients (12%), with additional patients carrying BRAF G466E, V600K, and R462K. NRAS Q61K was found in 3 patients (9%), plus one A59G event. BRAF and NRAS were mutually exclusive. No correlation was found between mutation burden and BRAF/NRAS status. Additional low-frequency alterations were found in NF1, EGFR, KRAS, TP53, ERBB3, and PREX2. The majority of patients (71%) carry driver alterations outside the canonical BRAF/NRAS/NF1 triad, including the TERT and PAK1 alterations described in F1 and F4.

### F4: PAK1 copy gains (15%) occur exclusively in BRAF/NRAS wild-type ALMs

**Finding:** PAK1 (p21-activated kinase 1) focal copy gains are present in 5 of 34 patients (15%), with one patient additionally showing multiple SVs impacting PAK1. Elevated PAK1 expression is found in 2 of 5 gain-positive patients. All PAK1-gain patients are BRAF/NRAS wild-type (NF1 or triple-wild-type subtype), suggesting PAK1 as an alternative MAPK pathway activator.

**Detail:** PAK1 gains were validated by real-time PCR in 4 of 5 available samples (80%). PAK1 has been proposed as a therapeutic target in BRAF wild-type melanoma based on its role in promoting cell proliferation, survival, invasion, and metastasis. These data support PAK1 as a potential actionable target in the ALM subset without conventional MAPK drivers.

### F5: TERT inhibition is selectively cytotoxic in primary ALM cell lines

**Finding:** In vitro treatment of two primary ALM cell lines (one with TERT CNV gain, one with homozygous TERT promoter mutation) with Telomerase Inhibitor IX at 2.5 µM for 72 hours results in ≥75% reduction in cell viability, with <12% effect in normal melanocyte controls. TERT mRNA expression is also reduced ≥25% in ALM cell lines.

**Detail:** SMC-09 (TERT CNV gain) and SU2C-001-002 (TERT promoter mutation Chr 5:1,295,113 G>A) were tested against normal human melanocyte control NHM-002. Selective cytotoxicity was observed only in the TERT-aberrant ALM lines. This provides proof-of-concept that TERT dependency is functionally relevant in ALM bearing TERT aberrations, and raises the possibility of TERT-directed therapeutic strategies in a subset of ALM patients.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT · Acral lentiginous melanoma

## Open Questions

- What are the precise molecular mechanisms by which TERT translocations and breakpoints activate TERT in the absence of detectable RNA fusions?
- Does PAK1 amplification confer sensitivity to PAK inhibitors in BRAF/NRAS wild-type ALM, supporting a clinical trial hypothesis?
- Can TERT promoter or copy number status serve as a clinical biomarker to stratify ALM patients for checkpoint inhibitor therapy or TERT-targeted treatment?
- Why do two ALM cases with UV signatures (both BRAF-mutant) share features with cutaneous melanoma, and do they represent distinct etiologic subgroups?
- What drives tumorigenesis in the ~29% of patients with no identified driver in BRAF/NRAS/NF1/TERT/PAK1 pathways?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=34 patients, n=38 tumors); limited statistical power to detect rare events or correlate alterations with outcomes
- Mixed primary (n=21) and metastatic (n=17) samples; some patients received prior therapy (ipilimumab, pembrolizumab), inflating mutation burden in treated tumors
- TERT promoter mutations were identified by Sanger, not exome sequencing; coverage of non-coding regulatory elements may be incomplete
- Germline TERT variants were identified in only 2 patients; broader germline sequencing in larger ALM cohorts is needed
- LIWG at ~6× coverage detects SVs but not all breakpoints in repetitive or GC-rich regions (e.g., patient 17 TERT intronic SV was low confidence)
- No matched germline available for one patient; in vitro data based on two cell lines only
