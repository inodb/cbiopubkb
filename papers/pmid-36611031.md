## Summary

Largest annotated clinical and genomic series of colitis-associated cancers (CAC), from Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center. 174 patients with CAC (inflammatory bowel disease–associated bowel cancer) were profiled, including 131 with full clinical data and next-generation sequencing (MSK-IMPACT targeted exon sequencing or WES), alongside 29 synchronous or isolated dysplasia specimens. Key findings: TP53 alterations are the dominant early and highly recurrent driver (90% of CACs, 50% of dysplasias), arising as convergent independent events; Wnt pathway alterations are strikingly infrequent (APC mutated in only 20%), and CAC organoids/PDXs grow without Wnt supplementation, demonstrating transcriptional rewiring away from Wnt; IDH1 R132 mutations and MYC amplification are enriched vs. sporadic CRC; multiple synchronous and metachronous lesions are genetically independent (no shared field effect); and germline predisposition variants appear in 14% of patients. Full data available in `bowel_colitis_msk_2022` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: TP53 is the dominant early driver in CAC, present clonally in the vast majority of tumors

**Finding:** TP53 alterations were present in 90% of CAC and ~50% of dysplasia samples. The majority of CAC harbored clonal TP53 alterations (by FACETS algorithm), with 14% subclonal and 12% absent. Convergent independent TP53 mutations were observed across synchronous lesions, confirming independent clonal origin.

**Detail:** Clonal TP53 alterations were enriched in tumors with widespread copy number changes and whole genome duplication (WGD) — suggesting a close functional association between TP53 loss and genomic instability. Subclonal TP53 cases were significantly enriched for Wnt pathway alterations (P=0.017), suggesting a distinct tumorigenic route. IDH1 mutations did not require early TP53 events — one dysplastic lesion harbored IDH1 mutation without TP53 alteration. Fraction genome altered and WGD were significantly greater in CAC vs. dysplasia, consistent with progressive genomic instability through the dysplasia-carcinoma sequence. Mucosa adjacent to tumors showed no detectable TP53 alterations, arguing against a genomic field effect driving cancer risk.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, APC, KRAS, IDH1, WGD · Colitis-associated colorectal cancer, IBD-associated dysplasia

### F2: CAC lacks canonical Wnt pathway activation — transcriptional rewiring away from Wnt is a cardinal feature

**Finding:** APC was mutated in only 20% of CAC (vs. >80% of sporadic CRC). No APC-activating alternative mechanism compensated in APC-WT cases; beta-catenin IHC showed membranous (non-nuclear, inactive) staining in Wnt-WT tumors. RNA-seq confirmed lower Wnt signaling ssGSEA scores in CAC vs. CRC, and no CMS2 (Wnt-dominant) tumors were identified. CAC organoids and PDXs grew independently of exogenous Wnt, unlike normal colonoids.

**Detail:** Wnt wild-type CAC organoids (two independent models) and APC-mutant CAC organoids showed no growth difference with or without Wnt supplementation (all P>0.05), while normal colonic organoids required Wnt for growth. Pharmacologic Wnt inhibition (porcupine inhibitor LGK-974; tankyrase inhibitor G007-LK) in three CAC PDXs (two Wnt-WT, one APC-mutant) did not suppress tumor growth, with RNA-seq showing tumors clustered by PDX model not treatment arm. This functional Wnt independence, combined with the genomic absence of Wnt pathway alterations and low transcriptional Wnt output, suggests that CAC arises from a Wnt-low cell population that does not depend on Wnt for tumor initiation — a fundamental mechanistic difference from sporadic CRC.

### F3: IDH1 R132 mutations and MYC amplification are enriched in CAC vs. sporadic CRC and are targetable

**Finding:** IDH1 R132 mutations occurred at higher frequency in CAC than sporadic CRC, and were enriched in Crohn's disease vs. ulcerative colitis (along with PIK3CA). IDH1 R132 mutant CAC showed high global methylation (2-HG oncometabolite effect), confirmed by high 2-HG production in a patient-derived xenograft. PDX treatment with selective IDH1 and FGFR inhibitors confirmed tumor growth dependence on these alterations. MYC amplification (20% of CAC) was also significantly enriched vs. sporadic CRC.

**Detail:** Global methylation analysis showed that 5/6 IDH1-mutant CACs clustered with MSI tumors in terms of hypermethylation phenotype, suggesting IDH1 R132–driven epigenetic remodeling (analogous to IDH1 mutant gliomas). FGFR2 amplification or fusion — rare in sporadic CRC but common in intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma — was also identified in CAC, both of which now have FDA-approved matched targeted therapies (ivosidenib for IDH1; infigratinib/pemigatinib for FGFR2). Focal amplifications of MYC were more common than broad copy number changes in CAC (vs. sporadic CRC, which had more broad CNA; P<0.001).

**Genes / cancer types:** IDH1, FGFR2, MYC, PIK3CA, KRAS, SMAD4, APC · Colitis-associated colorectal cancer (Crohn's and UC subsets)

### F4: Multiple synchronous and metachronous lesions in IBD arise as genetically independent events — no field effect

**Finding:** Sequencing of multiple synchronous/metachronous lesions from the same patient — in both the AOM/DSS mouse model and in human patients (one with 6 synchronous lesions across the bowel) — revealed rare or absent shared genetic alterations between lesions in close anatomical proximity. Convergent TP53 mutations were the only recurrent shared feature, but as independent events.

**Detail:** In the AOM/DSS mouse model, two mice with multiple bowel lesions showed minimal shared alterations (one mouse shared only a CTNNB1 mutation; the other shared no mutations between intramucosal carcinomas). In human patients, 6 synchronous lesions from one total colectomy specimen had no shared genetic alterations by sequencing. Analysis of 15 paired dysplasia-cancer samples showed that the majority of mutations were private to each lesion, not shared. Six additional patients with multifocal primaries (synchronous or metachronous, n=2 and 4 respectively) likewise showed no shared driver alterations between tumors. This definitively refutes the hypothesis of a genomic field defect in IBD and suggests that the increased cancer risk in IBD is driven by non-genomic (epigenetic, microenvironmental) mechanisms acting on multiple independent clones.

### F5: Germline cancer predisposition variants in 14% of CAC patients; APC I1307K most common

**Finding:** Among 73 CAC patients who consented to germline testing via MSK-IMPACT germline panel, pathogenic alterations were identified in 10 (14%). APC I1307K (a low-penetrance Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry variant conferring ~2× CRC risk) was most common (3 cases), followed by alterations in PMS2, ATM, RAD51B, DICER1, FANCA, and FANCC.

**Detail:** APC I1307K is not associated with FAP but increases susceptibility of the APC gene to further somatic changes leading to CRC. Two PMS2 carriers had microsatellite-stable CAC — consistent with PMS2 as an incidental finding in some cancer patients rather than a causative Lynch syndrome driver. ATM and RAD51B variants are associated with evolving cancer predisposition evidence. FANCA and FANCC monoallelic variants reflect carrier status only. Overall germline predisposition rate (14%) was similar to that in sporadic CRC at comparable age, suggesting that germline predisposition does not uniquely elevate CAC risk beyond the IBD-driven baseline.

## Open Questions

- If CAC development does not require a genomic field effect, what non-genomic (epigenetic, microenvironmental) mechanisms underlie the IBD-associated cancer risk elevation?
- Can IDH1 inhibitors (ivosidenib) or FGFR inhibitors produce durable responses in IDH1/FGFR2-altered CAC, analogous to their established roles in cholangiocarcinoma?
- What is the clinical significance of the Wnt-low, L1CAM-high cell population proposed as the CAC-initiating cell type — can targeting this population prevent CAC development?
- Why do MYC focal amplifications occur more frequently in CAC than broad copy number changes (common in sporadic CRC)?
- Does the absence of CMS2 (canonical WNT) tumors in CAC translate into distinct chemotherapy sensitivity or resistance patterns?

## Limitations

- Retrospective design at a single academic center (MSK); CAC is rare, limiting total cohort size and statistical power for subgroup analyses
- Targeted panel sequencing (MSK-IMPACT) rather than WGS for the majority of cases; structural variants and non-coding alterations may be missed
- RNA-seq available for only a small subset of cases (n=11 patients), limiting transcriptomic characterization of the full cohort
- Organoid/PDX functional experiments performed in limited models (2 Wnt-WT organoid lines, 3 PDXs for Wnt inhibitor experiments)
- Germline testing available for only 73/174 patients (45%), potentially underestimating germline predisposition frequency
- Wnt signaling assessment by IHC (n=14) and RNA-seq (n=11) in small subsets limits generalizability of transcriptional rewiring conclusion
