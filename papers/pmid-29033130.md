## Summary

This landmark study from Memorial Sloan Kettering performs comprehensive multi-omic genomic profiling — whole-exome sequencing (WES), transcriptome sequencing, and T-cell receptor (TCR) sequencing — on paired pre- and on-therapy tumor biopsies from 68 advanced melanoma patients treated with nivolumab (anti-PD-1) in the CA209-038 trial. The study reveals that successful nivolumab responses are associated with reduction in mutation and neoantigen load (immunoediting), differential clonal evolution with preferential contraction of neoantigenic clones, expansion of specific immune cell subsets and transcriptional networks in the tumor microenvironment (TME), and TCR clonal expansion coinciding with neoantigen loss. These findings provide mechanistic insight into how PD-1 blockade reshapes tumor genomics and the immune microenvironment. The full genomic and clinical dataset is publicly available in cBioPortal as mel_iatlas_riaz_nivolumab_2017.

## Key Findings

### F1: Tumor mutation and neoantigen load decrease in responding patients after nivolumab initiation — evidence of immunoediting

**Finding:** Among nivolumab-treated patients with complete or partial response (CR/PR), there was a significant reduction in both total mutation load and putative neoantigen load comparing pre-therapy to on-therapy biopsies (cycle 1, day 29). This reduction was not observed in patients with stable disease (SD) or progressive disease (PD), providing direct in vivo evidence of immunoediting — immune-mediated selection against neoantigenic mutations — during PD-1 blockade.

**Detail:** Deep sequencing (300×) confirmed that many mutations present pre-therapy were still detectable on-therapy in small fractions, ruling out that the apparent reduction was solely due to decreased tumor purity. The mean fraction of variants remaining detectable on-therapy was 19% for CR/PR patients (range: 1%–99%), 82% for SD patients, and 101% for PD patients (values >100% indicate new mutation acquisition). Power calculations confirmed that changes in tumor purity alone could not explain the observed magnitude of mutation load reduction in responders. Neoantigen load reduction tracked closely with mutation load reduction, consistent with immune selection targeting neoantigenic peptides.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide (WES) · Melanoma (advanced, ipilimumab-pretreated and naive)

### F2: Clonal evolution during nivolumab therapy — genomic contraction in responders, genomic expansion in progressors

**Finding:** Analysis of clonal architecture via cancer cell fraction (CCF) estimation in paired pre/on-therapy biopsies revealed that responders (CR/PR) showed marked "genomic contraction" — loss of clonal and subclonal variants — while progressors (PD) showed "genomic expansion" — gain of new variants. Net genomic change (contraction minus persistence) strongly predicted both response and OS, outperforming simple mutation load change.

**Detail:** CCF was estimated from WES allele frequencies for each mutation in paired samples. Genomic contraction (variants lost or with decreasing CCF on-therapy) was ubiquitous in CR/PR samples. In PD patients, 26% had >50% of SNVs with variant gain on-therapy; in SD patients, 0% exceeded this threshold. Conversely, 38% of SD patients had >50% of SNVs under variant loss (vs. 0% of PD patients). This hierarchical pattern (CR/PR > SD > PD) suggests that net genomic contraction is a continuous biological process reflecting immune clearance of sensitive clones. Four cases of focal CDKN2A loss emerged in PD on-therapy samples (with concurrent 9p IFN cluster deletion), suggesting active genomic adaptation for immune evasion.

### F3: Pre-therapy tumor mutation load predicts OS in ipilimumab-naive but not ipilimumab-pretreated patients

**Finding:** Higher tumor mutation load (>100 mutations) was associated with significantly improved overall survival after nivolumab in ipilimumab-naive (Ipi-N) patients, but this association was not observed in ipilimumab-pretreated (Ipi-P) patients. Stratification by clonal mutation count further improved survival prediction in Ipi-N patients.

**Detail:** This differential predictive value of TMB in Ipi-N vs. Ipi-P patients suggests that prior ipilimumab exposure alters the immunoediting landscape in ways that decouple mutation load from nivolumab benefit — perhaps because ipilimumab itself induces immune-mediated clonal selection, changing the neoantigen repertoire available to anti-PD-1. Ipi-P patients had a trend toward fewer clonal mutations (p=0.08), consistent with prior immune editing. No single-gene mutation was significantly associated with nivolumab response or resistance, though B2M frameshift + LOH was observed in one patient with PR who subsequently progressed (acquired resistance mechanism). SERPINB3/B4 mutations trended toward disease control (5/6 patients; p=0.21, not significant).

### F4: Transcriptome analysis reveals immune cell infiltration, checkpoint upregulation, and distinct transcriptional networks associated with nivolumab response

**Finding:** RNA-seq of pre- and on-therapy tumor biopsies revealed that responding patients showed greater increases in distinct immune cell subsets (CD8+ T cells, NK cells), activation of specific pro-inflammatory transcriptional networks, and upregulation of multiple immune checkpoint genes (including LAG3, TIM3, TIGIT) during therapy — consistent with an active, expanding immune response that simultaneously triggers adaptive immune resistance via checkpoint upregulation.

**Detail:** Transcriptome analysis was performed on a subset of biopsies (n varies by analysis). Immune cell composition was deconvoluted from RNA-seq data. CD8+ T cell infiltration increased significantly on-therapy in responders vs. non-responders. Upregulation of multiple immune checkpoints (beyond PD-1/PD-L1) in responding tumors suggests that successful anti-PD-1 therapy generates an immunologically active TME that secondarily upregulates other resistance checkpoints — providing a mechanistic rationale for combinatorial checkpoint blockade. Specific transcriptional networks (IFN-γ signaling, T cell effector programs) were identified as potential response biomarkers.

### F5: TCR clonal expansion in the TME during nivolumab correlates with neoantigen loss — linking adaptive immunity to immunoediting

**Finding:** TCR sequencing of tumor biopsies revealed that T-cell clonal expansion during nivolumab therapy was temporally correlated with neoantigen loss in responding patients, suggesting that TCR clones specific for immunogenic neoantigens expand and mediate elimination of neoantigen-expressing tumor cells — directly linking the immune effector response to the genomic immunoediting observed in F1.

**Detail:** TCR repertoire analysis demonstrated increased TCR clonality and expansion of specific TCR clones in on-therapy biopsies from responding patients. Several expanded T-cell clones could be predicted (by TCR-neoantigen pairing algorithms) to target neoantigens derived from mutations that were subsequently lost in on-therapy biopsies. This provides functional evidence that the anti-tumor immune response generated by nivolumab specifically targets clonal neoantigenic mutations, and that successful elimination of neoantigen-expressing clones is a mechanism of durable response. Conversely, in non-responders, TCR clonal expansion was limited and neoantigen loss was not observed.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide (TCR-seq + WES neoantigen prediction) · Melanoma

## Open Questions

- Does the immunoediting-mediated reduction in mutation/neoantigen load during successful nivolumab response predict durable vs. transient responses?
- Can the specific TCR clones expanding during nivolumab response be used to develop neoantigen-directed adoptive cell therapies or personalized vaccines for patients who subsequently relapse?
- Why does tumor mutation load predict nivolumab benefit in ipilimumab-naive but not ipilimumab-pretreated patients — and can pre-treatment clonal neoantigen architecture (rather than total TMB) explain this discordance?
- Do secondary checkpoint upregulation (LAG3, TIM3, TIGIT) during nivolumab response identify targets for rational subsequent combination therapies?
- Can CDKN2A/IFN gene cluster co-deletion on 9p serve as an early molecular marker of acquired resistance during PD-1 blockade?
- Can blood-based TCR profiling or liquid biopsy ctDNA neoantigen tracking non-invasively monitor immunoediting dynamics during therapy?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=68; 41 with paired WES) limits statistical power for single-gene association analyses
- Response rates were low (~21%) in both Ipi-N and Ipi-P groups; confounded by mixed patient population and variable prior therapies
- On-therapy biopsy at fixed early timepoint (cycle 1, day 29) may not capture later immune dynamics or acquired resistance mechanisms
- Neoantigen predictions are computational (MHC binding affinity) and lack T-cell functional validation for most predicted epitopes
- TCR-neoantigen pairing is inferred rather than experimentally confirmed (e.g., by pMHC tetramer staining)
- Transcriptome and TCR sequencing performed on tissue biopsies with variable tumor cellularity, potentially confounding immune cell quantification
- No matched peripheral blood immune profiling to correlate tumor-infiltrating immune changes with systemic response
- HLA typing for neoantigen prediction assumes classical MHC-I presentation; non-classical and MHC-II neoantigens not fully considered
