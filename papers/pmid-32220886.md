## Summary

A paired WES study from MSK characterizing the clonal architecture and mechanisms of progression from non-low-grade DCIS to invasive ductal carcinoma of no special type (IDC-NST). The study profiled 27 synchronous DCIS and 26 IDC-NSTs from 25 patients, plus 7 pure DCIS (no invasive component) using high-depth WES or MSK-IMPACT panel sequencing. Key findings: (1) synchronous DCIS and IDC-NSTs have nearly identical mutational repertoires (*TP53*, *PIK3CA*, *GATA3* at similar frequencies), supporting DCIS as a genetically advanced precursor; (2) 92% (23/25) of synchronous DCIS–IDC-NST pairs are clonally related by pairwise clonality index; (3) progression to invasive disease follows diverse evolutionary pathways — clonal selection (minor DCIS subclone becoming dominant in IDC-NST) in 28% of cases, and a non-selection (multiclonal invasion) pattern in 72%; (4) pure DCIS lacks *PIK3CA* mutations, unlike synchronous DCIS. The study provides direct evidence that DCIS is a non-obligate precursor of IDC-NST with heterogeneous progression mechanisms.

## Key Findings

### F1: Synchronous DCIS and IDC-NSTs share nearly identical mutational repertoires; TP53, PIK3CA, GATA3 similarly frequent in both

**Finding:** WES analysis reveals that the most frequently mutated cancer genes are nearly identical between synchronous DCIS and IDC-NST within the same patients: *TP53* (54% DCIS vs. 52% IDC-NST), *PIK3CA* (42% vs. 41%), and *GATA3* (23% vs. 26%), with no statistically significant differences (P >0.05, Fisher's exact test). Most *PIK3CA* mutations (9/12) affect hotspot residues H1047R/L. Most *TP53* mutations are inactivating and associated with LOH of the wild-type allele. DCIS and IDC-NSTs also have comparable CNA profiles without significant frequency differences.

**Detail:** The genetic similarity between DCIS and synchronous IDC-NST implies that the major oncogenic alterations are established early in the in situ phase, not acquired during the transition to invasion. Most *GATA3* mutations are loss-of-function (frameshift, truncating, splice-site). *PIK3CA* and *TP53* mutations are predicted clonal in the majority of cases (11/12 and 12–13/14 respectively), meaning they are likely founder events. Notably, amplification of *CCND1* and *MYC* was restricted to the invasive component in individual cases, suggesting a role for these specific amplifications in invasion.

### F2: 92% of synchronous DCIS–IDC-NST pairs are clonally related; geographic separation predicts clonal independence

**Finding:** Pairwise clonality index (CI) analysis based on shared somatic mutations demonstrates that 92% (23/25) of synchronous DCIS and IDC-NSTs are clonally related — sharing mutations not expected to occur by chance. Two exceptions: Case 24 (DCIS and IDC-NST in different breast quadrants — not clonally related) and one focus of multifocal DCIS in Case 5 (different quadrant from IDC-NST, no clonal relatedness). The two cases with DCIS and IDC-NST in the same quadrant of multifocal Case 2 and Case 5 were clonally related.

**Detail:** This finding provides strong evidence against the independent evolution model (which predicts no clonal relationship) in the vast majority of non-low-grade DCIS cases. The geographic correlation — clonal relatedness is associated with same-quadrant location — is particularly instructive for understanding local spread and treatment planning. The two genetically independent lesions (different quadrants) could represent field cancerization or de novo independent origin. This finding contrasts with the authors' prior observations in LCIS, where clonal relatedness was found regardless of anatomic location.

### F3: Progression follows diverse evolutionary pathways — clonal selection in 28%, multiclonal invasion in 72%

**Finding:** PyClone clonal decomposition and MEDICC CNA-based phylogenetics reveal two distinct progression patterns: (1) clonal selection — a minor DCIS subclone expands and becomes dominant in IDC-NST — observed in 7/25 cases (28%); (2) no evidence of clonal selection — likely multiclonal invasion — in 18/25 cases (72%). DCIS cases with clonal selection had higher intra-lesion genetic heterogeneity (Shannon index) than those without. Genes mutated exclusively in the invasive component (potential invasion drivers) include *PTEN*, *GATA3*, *TP53*, *KMT2D*, *CHD4*, *RAD51B*, *SOX2*, *SPEN*, *SMO*, *RB1*, *NCOA1*, *TET2*, and *NBN*.

**Detail:** In clonal selection cases, a minor subclone of DCIS (harboring branch mutations beyond the DCIS trunk) becomes the dominant clone in the synchronous IDC-NST. The pattern where DCIS branch mutations become IDC-NST trunk mutations is the hallmark of selection-driven progression. In the 72% of cases without clonal selection, genetic alterations enabling invasion appear to have been acquired early — potentially before intra-lesion heterogeneity developed — followed by multiclonal invasion. Amplifications of *CCND1*, *PIK3CA*, *IDH2*, *MYC*, *MYCL1*, *ERBB2*, and *HRAS* were also found restricted to IDC-NST in individual cases.

### F4: Pure DCIS lacks PIK3CA mutations; synchronous DCIS harbors PIK3CA at invasive-cancer frequency — defining a molecular distinction

**Finding:** All 7 pure DCIS cases (not associated with invasion) lacked *PIK3CA* mutations — a striking contrast to synchronous DCIS cases where *PIK3CA* mutations occur at 41–42% frequency, matching IDC-NST. This observation, consistent with a prior report by Sakr et al., suggests that *PIK3CA* mutations may be associated with the transition to or co-selection with invasive growth, or that pure (non-progressing) DCIS represents a biologically distinct entity.

**Detail:** The absence of *PIK3CA* mutations in pure DCIS, despite similar *TP53* mutation frequencies, raises the possibility that PI3K pathway activation is a later event or is specifically enriched in DCIS destined to progress. An alternative interpretation is that small cohort size (n=7 pure DCIS) limits the power to detect mutations. This finding, if confirmed in larger studies, could have significant clinical implications for stratifying DCIS into progression-prone vs. stable subtypes and guiding treatment decisions (e.g., active surveillance vs. intervention for pure DCIS lacking *PIK3CA* mutations).

### F5: Mutational signatures in DCIS and IDC-NST consistent with APOBEC and aging; signature analysis enables trunk/branch dissection

**Finding:** Mutational signature analysis identifies APOBEC-associated and aging-related signatures in DCIS and IDC-NSTs. Comparison of trunk (clonal) and branch (subclonal) mutations enables inference of temporal signature activity. The study demonstrates that phylogenetic dissection of DCIS and IDC-NST mutations is feasible from WES data, providing a framework for studying mutational process dynamics during progression.

**Detail:** While specific per-sample signature exposures are not the primary focus of this paper, the use of mutational signatures as supporting evidence for clonal relationships and evolutionary inference is methodologically important. APOBEC-associated signatures (SBS2/13) enrichment in high-grade DCIS is consistent with the broader breast cancer literature. The feasibility of applying clonal decomposition and phylogenetics to microdissected FFPE-derived WES data (with stringent purity requirements) is an important methodological demonstration for similar studies.

## Open Questions

- Does *PIK3CA* mutation status in pure DCIS predict likelihood of future progression to invasive carcinoma in a prospective cohort?
- What genomic events specifically enable invasion in the 72% of DCIS cases that progress without evidence of clonal selection — are there epigenetic, microenvironmental, or structural alterations driving invasion?
- Do the cancer genes found exclusively in IDC-NST (*PTEN*, *RB1*, *TET2*, *ERBB2* amplification) represent generalizable invasion gene candidates across DCIS subtypes, or are they case-specific?
- How do these findings from non-low-grade DCIS translate to low-grade DCIS, which was explicitly excluded from this study?
- Can the clonal architecture of DCIS at diagnosis predict which patients can safely forgo treatment (active surveillance) vs. require intervention?
- What is the relationship between DCIS intra-lesion heterogeneity (Shannon index) and long-term recurrence rates?

## Limitations

- Non-low-grade DCIS only — results may not generalize to low-grade or intermediate-grade DCIS, which follows a different evolutionary path
- FFPE tissue used for all sequencing — may introduce artifactual mutations and reduce variant calling sensitivity
- Small cohort (n=25 with matched pairs) limits statistical power for rare event discovery
- Clonal decomposition (PyClone) requires assumptions about tumor purity and ploidy; errors in these estimates can affect phylogenetic reconstruction
- No longitudinal follow-up data — unable to determine which cases would have recurred or progressed if left untreated
- Study is enriched for high-grade, ER-negative, HER2-variable DCIS; limited representation of ER+/HER2- low-grade biology
- No spatial transcriptomics or single-cell data — microenvironmental contributions to invasion mechanism are not captured
