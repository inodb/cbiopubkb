## Summary

Case-control study comparing 64 patients with secondary rectal cancer (SRC) developing ≥5 years after prostate radiotherapy to 604 male patients with primary rectal cancer (PRC) treated at MSK (1994–2022). SRC patients are older (median 78 vs. 55 years), have more distal/anterior tumors, receive far less neoadjuvant therapy, and have significantly worse DFS and OS. Genomic profiling (MSK-IMPACT, n=31 SRC; WES, n=17 SRC) reveals that SRC has lower TMB, fewer *APC* alterations, and higher *SMAD4* inactivation rates than PRC. SRC tumors also harbor excess frameshift deletions consistent with radiation-induced DNA damage repair patterns. This is the first comprehensive genomic characterization of radiation-associated SRC.

## Key Findings

### F1: SRC patients have markedly worse survival than clinically matched PRC patients

**Finding:** Secondary rectal cancer patients have significantly shorter 5-year OS (45.7% vs. 64.9%; p=0.01), DFS (40.3% vs. 71.2%; p=0.006), and distant recurrence–free survival (60.8% vs. 89.8%; p=0.01) compared to propensity score–matched PRC patients.

**Detail:** Clinical matching for sex, age, stage, tumor distance from anal verge, tumor size, and circumferential location (1:1). In multivariate Cox models, SRC was independently associated with worse DFS (HR 6.16; 95% CI 2.10–18.07; p<0.001) and DRFS (HR 12.68; 95% CI 3.06–52.47; p<0.001) but not OS (HR 1.95; 95% CI 0.58–6.60; p=0.28). Having surgery was the only factor independently associated with improved OS (HR 0.38; 95% CI 0.16–0.88; p=0.02). No difference in local recurrence-free survival (92.6% vs. 95.3%; p=0.50). SRC median latency from prostate RT to rectal cancer diagnosis: 11.50 years (IQR 7.00–17.25).

**Genes / cancer types:** Clinical outcomes (not gene-specific) · Secondary rectal cancer after prostate radiotherapy

### F2: SRC has distinct clinical presentation: distal, anterior rectal wall location, treated less aggressively

**Finding:** SRC tumors are more often located in the distal rectum (58.7% vs. 22.5%; p<0.001) and along the anterior rectal wall (35.1% vs. 13.6%; p<0.001), and SRC patients receive substantially less neoadjuvant therapy (51.6% vs. 94.4%; p<0.001).

**Detail:** Consistent with irradiated field anatomy (anterior prostate bed abutting anterior rectum). SRC patients significantly older (median 78 vs. 55 years; p<0.001). More SRC patients present with stage I (27.8% vs. 5.0%) and stage II (31.5% vs. 12.4%) despite worse outcomes — suggesting occult aggressive biology beyond clinical stage. More SRC patients underwent abdominoperineal resection (50% vs. 25%), pelvic exenteration (16.7% vs. 1.5%), reflecting complex surgical anatomy. 20.3% of SRC patients received total neoadjuvant therapy (re-irradiation uptake increased after 2014: 14/22 patients, 63.6%).

### F3: SRC tumors have lower TMB and fewer APC alterations vs. PRC

**Finding:** SRC tumors have significantly lower tumor mutational burden (median 4.4 vs. 5.8 mut/Mb; p=0.047 vs. PRC; p=0.006 vs. matched PRC) and far fewer *APC* inactivating alterations (48.4% vs. 79.9%; p<0.001).

**Detail:** MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing of 31 SRC vs. 541 PRC tumors. TMB: SRC median 4.4 (IQR 3.2–6.7) vs. PRC median 5.8 (IQR 4.4–7.0) per Mb. APC inactivating alteration frequency: 15/31 SRC (48.4%) vs. 432/541 PRC (79.9%), p<0.001; vs. matched PRC 45/62 (72.6%), p=0.04. WNT pathway alteration frequency (driven by APC): SRC 54.8% vs. PRC 83.2%, p<0.001. No significant differences in FGA or whole-genome duplication rates. None of the SRC tumors were MSI-H or POLE-mutant. Lower APC frequency and APC wild-type status linked to more aggressive tumor biology in prior studies.

### F4: SMAD4 inactivation is enriched in SRC vs. PRC

**Finding:** *SMAD4* inactivating mutations are significantly more frequent in SRC (25.8%) vs. PRC (10.0%; p=0.01) and vs. matched PRC (8.1%; p=0.03), consistent with more aggressive tumor biology.

**Detail:** SMAD4 inactivation in SRC: 8/31 (25.8%) vs. 54/541 (10.0%) PRC (p=0.01) and 5/62 (8.1%) matched PRC (p=0.03). *SMAD4* wild-type status and its inactivation have been linked to more aggressive tumors in CRC; this enrichment may partially explain the worse DFS and DRFS observed in SRC. No other individual driver genes reached significance after multiple testing correction.

### F5: SRC tumors harbor excess frameshift deletions consistent with radiation-induced DNA damage

**Finding:** Whole-exome sequencing reveals significantly more frameshift deletions in SRC vs. PRC (median 5.0 vs. 2.5 variants; p<0.001), as well as more inframe deletions and splice site variants, despite similar total variant counts — consistent with radiation-induced double-strand break repair signatures.

**Detail:** WES of 17 SRC vs. 28 PRC: frameshift deletions — SRC median 5.0 (IQR 4.0–9.0) vs. PRC 2.5 (IQR 1.0–4.2), p<0.001. Inframe deletions: SRC median 1.0 vs. PRC 1.0 (IQR 0.0–1.0), p=0.03. Splice site variants: SRC median 2.0 vs. PRC 1.0, p=0.02. Total variant count similar (SRC 122 vs. PRC 138, p=0.72). No significant differences in missense variants (SRC 70 vs. PRC 85.5, p=0.54). Most common signature: SBS1 (aging/clocklike) in both groups. No significant differences in COSMIC signatures (SBS6, SBS15 for MMR deficiency) between groups. Excess frameshift deletions could generate more potent neoantigens, potentially making SRC better candidates for immune checkpoint inhibition.

**Genes / cancer types:** Mutational signatures (SBS1), frameshift deletions (pan-genomic) · Secondary rectal cancer

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Given excess frameshift deletions and potentially enhanced neoantigen burden, would SRC patients with low TMB (and thus PD-1 resistance under current TMB-H thresholds) benefit from immune checkpoint inhibition based on frameshift neoantigen load?
- Does the SMAD4 inactivation rate in SRC translate to resistance to specific therapies (e.g., TGFβ pathway inhibitors under investigation)?
- Does the lower APC/WNT alteration rate in SRC reflect a fundamentally different cell of origin (radiation-damaged epithelium vs. adenomatous polyp), and does this affect treatment response to neoadjuvant chemoradiation?
- Can re-irradiation strategies be expanded safely for more SRC patients given the promising uptake after 2014?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Retrospective study spanning ~3 decades; heterogeneity in diagnosis, treatment, and surveillance protocols for both prostate and rectal cancer
- Small SRC genomic cohort (n=31 MSK-IMPACT; n=17 WES); limited statistical power for gene-level and signature analyses
- Some SRC cases may represent sporadic rectal cancer not causally related to prior radiation; however, latency ≥5 years and field-specific tumor location support radiation causality
- Changes in radiation dose, delivery, and modality (EBRT vs. brachytherapy) across decades introduce cross-patient variability
- WES PRC comparison group (n=28) is small relative to the MSK-IMPACT comparison (n=541)
- Mutational signatures from WES showed no significant COSMIC differences (including radiation-associated signatures); radiation signature enrichment was inferred from structural variant patterns rather than SBS signatures
