## Summary

The most comprehensive multi-region, longitudinal genomic study of high-risk neuroblastoma (HR-NB) to date, profiling 470 sequential and spatially separated samples from 283 patients using a combination of WGS and targeted sequencing. The study characterizes subtype-specific evolutionary trajectories (MYCN-A, TERT-SV, ATRX, MDM2-CDK4, CNA subtypes), demonstrates that disease initiation is timed to embryogenesis, and establishes that metastatic clones are seeded before therapy—often lying dormant for years. Therapy shapes the mutation landscape via platinum and temozolomide signatures, and relapse is driven by a combination of continuous structural variant accumulation at driver loci and convergent subclonal mutations in shared pathways (RAS-MAPK, PI3K-mTOR, p53). An interactive clonal viewer accompanies the paper.

## Key Findings

### F1: Disease initiation is timed to embryogenesis; subtype-specific molecular origins

**Finding:** Clonal reconstruction in 39 evaluable patients shows that the most recent common ancestor (MRCA) emerged in utero in 6 patients and post-natally in 9, with large confidence intervals for the remaining 25. Trunk length (clock-like mutations on the shared tumor trunk) is comparable to non-malignant clones in placenta enriched for the same SBS18 signature, indicating an embryonic-to-post-natal window for disease initiation. MYCN-A disease is predominantly embryonic in origin (5/6 cases); ATRX-mutant disease is enriched for post-natal onset (7/9), especially with ATRX truncating mutations.

**Detail:** Trunk length (median = 753 SNVs) is low compared to adult tumors and correlates with age at diagnosis only when controlling for disease subtype, stage, and number of tumors. Low-stage disease tends to have shorter trunks. The SBS18 signature (C>A, associated with reactive oxygen species and MYCN-driven glutaminolysis) dominates MYCN-A tumors at diagnosis and remains stable during disease progression, providing a mechanistic link: MYCN amplification → enhanced glutaminolysis → oxidative stress → SBS18 mutagenesis.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MYCN`, `ATRX` · High-risk neuroblastoma

### F2: Therapy dramatically reshapes the mutational landscape; chemotherapy signatures provide molecular timestamps

**Finding:** TMB increases 6-fold at first relapse and 14-fold at further relapses compared to diagnosis (median diagnosis: 0.52 mut/Mb, further relapse: median ~7+ mut/Mb). This is driven by therapy-associated mutational signatures: platinum (SBS31, SBS35) and temozolomide (TMZ signature), with strong dose-response relationships. However, only 12% of oncogenic driver substitutions at relapse are attributed to therapy-related signatures vs. 34% of all relapse-specific SNVs, indicating that most new driver mutations arise from endogenous processes.

**Detail:** At diagnosis, neuroblastoma is immune-cold with poor predicted neoantigen burden and immunotherapy responses; despite increased TMB at relapse generating putative neoantigens, no transcriptional evidence of an immunomodulatory switch was observed. Tumors from prior irradiated sites accumulate excess small deletions, SV deletions, reciprocal translocations, and complex events—radiation leaves a distinct molecular scar. The timing of therapy-related signature accumulation in clonal phylogenies allows retrospective reconstruction of when individual clones arose relative to treatment cycles.

### F3: Subtype-specific evolutionary trajectories driven by continuous structural variant accumulation at driver loci

**Finding:** In all four major HR-NB subtypes (MYCN-A, TERT-SV, ATRX, MDM2-CDK4), disease progression is predominantly shaped by continuous accumulation of structural variants (SVs) at the same driver loci—not by point mutations—with parallel/convergent evolution across subtypes in RAS-MAPK, PI3K-mTOR, and p53 pathway mutations at the subclonal level.

**Detail:** MYCN-A: Amplification initiates on the trunk (often as extrachromosomal DNA); continuous rearrangements at MYCN locus in 9/10 patients at relapse, including chromothripsis events. TERT-SV: Unlike MYCN, TERT-SVs are predominantly subclonal (10/13 SVs) and arise after segmental CNAs; continuous TERT rearrangements in 5/6 patients during progression increase copy number and expression (increasing TERT addiction). ATRX: Subclonal acquisition in 9/29 events; parallel ATRX mutations at distinct metastatic sites in 2 patients; enrichment for PTPRD SVs as a co-event. MDM2-CDK4: Co-amplification can co-exist with ATRX/TERT events; continuous MDM2-CDK4 evolution via incorporation of TERT, WNT3A, IGF2BP3.

### F4: Metastatic spread precedes therapy; dormant clones seed late relapses years after diagnosis

**Finding:** In 13 evaluable patients, 18 of 21 metastasizing subclones showed no evidence of therapy exposure (no therapy-related mutation signatures), indicating that metastatic dissemination—including to CNS sites—is established before therapy begins. Among 72 patients with multiple relapses, 24% of clonal transitions involved the exact same clone with no new genetic changes (dormancy); 4% involved an ancestral clone from earlier in the phylogeny (reversion).

**Detail:** A striking example: patient H134821 (9-year-old, stage-I, MYCN non-amplified) had a PIK3CA-mutant subclone drive the first two locoregional relapses (4–6 months post-diagnosis) and an MTOR-mutant clone drive relapses 8 years later—both clones were detectable at diagnosis, confirming multi-year dormancy. The majority of relapses (72%) involved accumulation of new CNAs or oncogenic mutations/SVs at recurrent loci. In 31/38 patients with clonal transitions between branches, the same pathway was exploited—preserving pathway-level dependencies even when individual genes change.

### F5: Polyclonal and metastasis-to-metastasis seeding documented post-therapy

**Finding:** In 10 patients with 2+ distant metastases, the same subclone seeded all metastatic tumors in 5/7 evaluable patients. Polyclonal seeding (multiple distinct subclones seeding the same metastatic site simultaneously) is documented during/after therapy, as evidenced by therapy-related signatures in multiple co-occurring metastatic subclones.

**Detail:** Met-to-met seeding clearly documented in patient H118706: at diagnosis, metastatic clones in lung and liver were distinct. At autopsy 16 months later, all 14 autopsy metastatic sites (lung and liver) were seeded by the same therapy-resistant TP53-mutant subclone originating from the liver metastasis—indicating chemotherapy cleared the original lung metastasis but a therapy-resistant liver-originating clone escaped and re-seeded both anatomical compartments. Both locoregional and distant metastatic spread utilize the same number of seeding subclones (average 1.5 vs. 1.3), indicating no differential propensity for local vs. distant spread.

## Open Questions

- Can pre-treatment genomic profiling of diagnostic samples identify dormant chemo-resistant subclones (e.g., PIK3CA- or MTOR-mutant) that predict late relapse risk?
- Does the TERT promoter mutation (in MYCN-A tumors) represent a tractable therapeutic target, and does it uniformly predict rapid progression?
- Given that neoantigens increase substantially at relapse but immunomodulatory transcriptional programs do not activate, what is the mechanism of immune evasion in progressive HR-NB?
- Can the ISABL/interactive clonal viewer data be integrated into cBioPortal as a multi-timeline sample view for routine clinical research queries?
- Is metastasis-to-metastasis seeding a unique feature of neuroblastoma's widespread metastatic biology, or comparable to other pediatric solid tumors?

## Limitations

- WGS performed in a multi-sample subset (45 patients); targeted sequencing provides less resolution for clonal reconstruction in the remainder
- Tumor purity and stromal contamination affect clonal inference, especially in bone marrow samples
- Inference of dormancy based on absence of therapy-related signatures—cannot rule out very low-frequency therapy-exposed clones below sequencing sensitivity
- CNS metastases are clinically rare and under-sampled; findings about CNS seeding before therapy are based on a small number of patients
- No single-cell or spatial sequencing; intratumor heterogeneity within a biopsy is estimated from bulk sequencing
- Study focused on HR-NB; findings may not apply to low-risk or intermediate-risk neuroblastoma subtypes
