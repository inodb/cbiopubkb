## Summary

Largest integrated genomic analysis of adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC) to date: 1,045 cases (177 primary localized, 868 recurrent/metastatic [R/M]), using WES/WGS (n=193) and targeted NGS including MSK-IMPACT (n=852). Key findings: NOTCH1 mutations are markedly enriched in R/M versus primary ACC (26.3% vs. 8.5%) and confer poor prognosis; chromatin-remodeling genes (KDM6A, MLL3, ARID1B, ARID1A, BCOR, MLL2, CREBBP) are also enriched in R/M disease; TERT promoter mutations are mutually exclusive with both NOTCH1 mutations and MYB/MYBL1 fusions, defining distinct oncogenic trajectories. These observations define four ACC molecular subgroups and identify previously unrecognized intratumoral genetic heterogeneity, including branching evolution. Full genomic data are accessible in `acc_2019` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: NOTCH1 mutations are 3× enriched in R/M ACC and predict poor overall survival

**Finding:** NOTCH1 mutations occurred in 26.3% of R/M ACC vs. 8.5% of primary tumors (OR 3.86, p<0.0001). Among the 225 R/M samples with NOTCH1 alterations, 337 distinct mutations were identified; 221 (65.6%) were activating mutations in the heterodimerization negative regulatory region or PEST domain. NOTCH1-mutant R/M patients had median OS of 55.1 months vs. 204.5 months for NOTCH1-WT (p=1.10×10⁻⁴); activating NOTCH1 mutations conferred even shorter OS (31.1 vs. 73.8 months among NOTCH1-mutant patients, p=0.042).

**Detail:** In the MSKCC R/M cohort (n=84, median follow-up 8.1 years), NOTCH1 mutation was the strongest single-gene prognostic marker. Very few NOTCH1 mutations were subclonal by VAF, indicating early clonal selection. Mutations across the broader Notch pathway (including SPEN 6.7%, FBXW7 3.8%) affected 39.6% of R/M cases in aggregate. Gamma secretase inhibitors (GSIs), which prevent Notch cleavage and activation, are actively being investigated in ACC clinical trials (NCT03691207, NCT03422679, NCT01695005). Approximately 18.3% of R/M cases with activating NOTCH1 mutations represent the approximate proportion anticipated to benefit from Notch-directed therapy.

**Genes / cancer types:** NOTCH1, NOTCH2, NOTCH3, NOTCH4, SPEN, FBXW7 · Adenoid cystic carcinoma (R/M)

### F2: Chromatin-remodeling genes (KDM6A, MLL3, ARID1 family, BCOR, CREBBP) are enriched in R/M disease and co-occur with NOTCH1

**Finding:** Seven chromatin-remodeling genes were significantly enriched in R/M vs. primary ACC: KDM6A (15.2% vs. 3.4%), MLL3/KMT2C (14.3% vs. 4.0%), ARID1B (14.1% vs. 4.0%), ARID1A (13.7% vs. 2.3%), BCOR (13.3% vs. 1.7%), MLL2/KMT2D (12.8% vs. 4.5%), CREBBP (11.1% vs. 4.5%); all with BH-FDR <0.10. KDM6A mutation predicted poor OS comparable to NOTCH1 (median OS 48.5 vs. 169.3 months, p=1.32×10⁻³). NOTCH1 mutations co-occurred significantly with KDM6A, ARID1A, and CREBBP mutations (all q<3.2×10⁻⁵).

**Detail:** The co-occurrence of NOTCH1 with chromatin-remodeling alterations suggests biological synergy: CREBBP and EP300 are known coactivators of MYB, and both are involved in recruiting the Notch transcriptional complex (NTC) via histone acetyltransferase and demethylase activity. This cooperativity may specifically facilitate the R/M phenotype via epigenetic Notch pathway amplification. The enrichment of these genes in R/M versus primary tumors supports their role in disease progression rather than tumor initiation. Sensitivity analyses (VAF downsampling) confirmed that differential mutation rates were not artifacts of sequencing depth differences between WES and targeted NGS.

**Genes / cancer types:** KDM6A, KMT2C (MLL3), ARID1B, ARID1A, BCOR, KMT2D (MLL2), CREBBP, EP300 · Adenoid cystic carcinoma (R/M)

### F3: TERT promoter mutations define a distinct oncogenic trajectory mutually exclusive with NOTCH1 and MYB/MYBL1 fusions

**Finding:** TERT promoter mutations were present in 13.1% of R/M cases and were mutually exclusive with both NOTCH1 mutations (q=3.3×10⁻⁴) and MYB/MYBL1 fusions (q=5.6×10⁻³), indicating three discrete, alternative oncogenic pathways in ACC. MYB/MYBL1 rearrangements (22.2% of R/M cases with available data) were not associated with R/M status or survival independently. TERT mutations appeared disproportionately in cases lacking MYB rearrangement (OR 3.71).

**Detail:** The mutual exclusivity of NOTCH1 and TERT implies these alterations each serve a distinct role in enabling replicative immortality or proliferative dysregulation via non-overlapping mechanisms. TERT promoter mutations reconstitute telomerase activity (silenced in normal somatic cells) and are enriched in diverse carcinomas. The finding that TERT defines an ACC subgroup independent of MYB suggests that TERT-driven ACCs represent a clinically and biologically distinct entity that may have different treatment vulnerabilities. MYB/MYBL1 fusions, despite being the canonical ACC driver (~59% of all ACC), did not confer differential prognosis in this cohort.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT (promoter), MYB, MYBL1 (fusions) · Adenoid cystic carcinoma (R/M)

### F4: Four molecular subgroups of R/M ACC with distinct survival outcomes

**Finding:** Integration of MYB, NOTCH1, and TERT status defined four R/M ACC subgroups accounting for 53.2% of cases with available data: MYB⁺NOTCH1⁺ (worst prognosis), MYB⁺/other, MYBᵂᵀNOTCH1⁺ (also poor prognosis), and MYBᵂᵀTERT⁺. Survival differed significantly across subgroups (p<0.0001). Triple-negative (MYBᵂᵀNOTCH1ᵂᵀTERTᵂᵀ) cases frequently harbored chromatin-remodeling gene mutations (60.1%, 160/266).

**Detail:** The MYBᵂᵀNOTCH1⁺ subgroup had the worst OS, even worse than MYB⁺NOTCH1⁺, indicating that NOTCH1 activation independent of MYB is particularly aggressive. MYB-WT status should be interpreted cautiously, as many such cases were only assessed for MYB intron 14 fusions and may harbor other MYB breakpoints. Among triple-negative cases, the dominant molecular feature was chromatin-remodeling gene mutations (60%), pointing to epigenetic dysregulation as an alternative oncogenic driver. These subgroups provide a framework for stratified clinical trials targeting NOTCH1 (GSIs), TERT, and epigenetic regulators in R/M ACC.

**Genes / cancer types:** MYB, MYBL1, NOTCH1, TERT · Adenoid cystic carcinoma (R/M)

### F5: Intratumoral genetic heterogeneity and branching evolution despite a quiet genome

**Finding:** In 58 ACC samples with WES data sufficient for clonality analysis (PyClone + FACETS), 34.5% showed intratumoral heterogeneity (ITH) with ≥2 subclonal populations, and 6.9% had ≥3 subclonal populations. Multi-region sequencing of one clinically aggressive salivary ACC (6 primary subregions + 8 lung metastases) revealed only 7 of 36 total mutations shared across all sites; 70% of metastasis-specific mutations were present in only 1–3 of 6 primary subregions.

**Detail:** NMF clustering of cancer cell fractions from the multi-region case identified three clusters: one comprising exclusively metastatic lesions harboring distinct mutations in SF3B1, XDH, LTF, and TMEM2 — all implicated in metastasis and/or poor prognosis in other cancers. This suggests that ACC metastatic spread is fueled by subclonal populations already present in spatially restricted primary tumor regions (branching evolution), rather than by uniform dissemination. The MYB-NFIB fusion was clonal across all regions, confirming its role as an early trunk event. Two breast ACC cases showing transformation to high-grade triple-negative breast cancer also showed clonal MYB-NFIB fusions with subclonal NOTCH1 mutations and additional subclonal mutations at the high-grade transformation site.

**Genes / cancer types:** SF3B1, XDH, LTF, TMEM2, MYB (trunk), NOTCH1 · Adenoid cystic carcinoma (primary + metastatic)

### F6: Pathogenic germline mutations identified in one-third of R/M ACC patients

**Finding:** Of 90 R/M ACC cases with matched germline sequencing (76-gene panel, MSKCC cohort), 36.7% (33/90) harbored at least one pathogenic germline mutation. 38 distinct pathogenic variants were identified (mean 0.42 per sample), predominantly splicing mutations (94.7%). 31 (34.4%) were potentially clinically actionable; 30 (33.3%) were high penetrance. 22.2% had DNA repair pathway germline alterations; four patients had BRCA1/2 variants (monoallelic, no evidence of second hit).

**Detail:** 15 patients (16.7%) had MLH1 or MSH6 germline mutations, but all were microsatellite stable by MSISensor analysis, and none had elevated TMB. This unexpected germline finding is notable because ACC is not traditionally considered a hereditary cancer. Most variants would not have been identified through standard clinical genetics guidelines. The monoallelic BRCA1/2 variants have unclear penetrance in salivary tissue, and the prevalence may partly reflect selection bias (R/M patients at a tertiary cancer center). These findings argue for broader germline sequencing consideration in R/M ACC patients.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRCA1, BRCA2, MLH1, MSH6 (germline) · Adenoid cystic carcinoma (R/M)

**Data availability:** `not-available`

## Open Questions

- Do NOTCH1-mutant R/M ACCs respond to gamma secretase inhibitors (GSIs) in prospective clinical trials?
- What is the clinical penetrance of BRCA1/2 and MMR germline mutations specifically in salivary gland tissue?
- Can the four molecular subgroups (MYB+NOTCH1+, MYB+/other, MYBWTNOTCH1+, MYBWTTERT+) prospectively stratify patients for subgroup-directed therapy?
- Does the degree of intratumoral heterogeneity correlate with clinical aggressiveness beyond what is captured by single-gene biomarkers?

## Limitations

- Most R/M cases were profiled with targeted NGS panels, while primary cases were predominantly sequenced with WES/WGS; differences in sequencing depth (100× vs. 600×) could theoretically inflate R/M mutation rates, though sensitivity analyses at downsampled 100× depth showed minimal impact (<1% affected mutations).
- MYB status was assessed using a single breakpoint (intron 14) in many cases; tumors with other MYB fusion variants or MYBL1 fusions may be misclassified as MYB-WT.
- Germline analysis was limited to 90 R/M patients at MSKCC, analyzed anonymously, precluding correlative outcome analyses.
- Low overall TMB (0.34 mut/Mb) and rarity of MSI in ACC limits potential for immunotherapy biomarker discovery.
