## Summary

Preprint (bioRxiv 2024) combining spatial tissue profiling with somatic genomic analysis of colorectal cancer (CRC) to characterize the tumor microenvironment (TME) beyond MSI/MMR status. Key finding: approximately 20% of mismatch repair-proficient (pMMR) CRCs exhibit T cell infiltration comparable to dMMR tumors — a newly defined subgroup termed tipMMR (T cell-infiltrated pMMR). tipMMR tumors have better survival following resection, express high levels of PD1, PDL1, and T regulatory cell markers (consistent with active immunosuppression), but do not harbor a specific somatic mutation signature. These findings suggest tipMMR CRC may benefit from immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy, potentially doubling the number of treatment-naïve CRC patients eligible for ICIs. A spatially enhanced version of cBioPortal was developed to enable multi-modal data exploration. Data available in `crc_orion_2024` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: ~20% of pMMR CRCs are T cell-infiltrated at dMMR levels (tipMMR subgroup)

**Finding:** Approximately 20% of mismatch repair-proficient (pMMR) CRC tumors show T cell infiltration levels comparable to dMMR tumors when quantified by spatial profiling. This tipMMR subgroup is associated with better post-resection survival but is not identified by standard genomic features (MSI status, TMB, somatic mutations). tipMMR tumors are indistinguishable from low-T cell pMMR tumors by standard clinical or genomic assays.

**Detail:** The TME, not genetics alone, determines immune infiltration status in a substantial subset of CRC. dMMR tumors are well-established as immune-infiltrated due to high neo-antigen burden from microsatellite instability; however, the existence of a similarly immune-infiltrated pMMR subgroup reveals that MMR status is not the only determinant of CRC immune phenotype. The low TMB of tipMMR tumors (consistent with pMMR biology) indicates that mechanisms other than neo-antigen load drive T cell recruitment in this subgroup.

**Genes / cancer types:** (No specific somatic mutation enrichment identified) · Colorectal carcinoma

### F2: tipMMR tumors contain PD1/PDL1-expressing cells and T regulatory cells — an immunosuppressed phenotype

**Finding:** tipMMR CRCs are enriched for cells expressing PD1 and PDL1 as well as T regulatory cells (Tregs), consistent with an actively immunosuppressed microenvironment. This phenotype mirrors the immune checkpoint-suppressed state observed in dMMR CRC and supports the hypothesis that tipMMR CRC, like dMMR CRC, may respond to immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy.

**Detail:** Spatial profiling of immune markers reveals that high T cell infiltration in tipMMR tumors coexists with markers of immune exhaustion and regulation. The presence of Tregs alongside effector T cells suggests an active suppressive program limiting anti-tumor immunity. This is distinct from immune-excluded or immune-desert pMMR tumors. The findings establish a biological rationale for clinical trials of PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibition in tipMMR CRC, independent of MSI status — a patient population currently ineligible for ICI therapy under standard criteria.

**Genes / cancer types:** PDCD1 (PD1), CD274 (PDL1), FOXP3 (Treg marker) · Colorectal carcinoma

### F3: tipMMR is not driven by a specific somatic mutation — immune infiltration is TME-intrinsic

**Finding:** No specific somatic mutation, gene, or mutational signature is significantly associated with tipMMR status. T cell infiltration in pMMR tumors appears to be driven by TME-intrinsic factors (cellular interactions, spatial organization, stromal composition) rather than tumor genomics. This decouples immune phenotype from genotype in a clinically meaningful fraction of CRC.

**Detail:** The absence of a genomic correlate for tipMMR makes it undetectable by DNA sequencing alone (WES, panel sequencing, TMB measurement). This highlights the limitation of purely genomic approaches to predict immune phenotype in CRC and supports the clinical deployment of spatially resolved protein profiling assays. A mid-plex imaging assay capable of quantifying T cell, Treg, and checkpoint marker densities in tumor sections is proposed as the diagnostic approach for tipMMR identification.

**Genes / cancer types:** (No enriched somatic mutations identified) · Colorectal carcinoma

### F4: Spatially enhanced cBioPortal enables multi-modal CRC data exploration

**Finding:** This study introduces a spatially enhanced version of cBioPortal for the `crc_orion_2024` dataset that integrates spatial protein profiling data with standard somatic genomics and clinical data. The portal supports visualization of spatial immune cell density maps alongside mutation profiles and survival data, enabling multi-modal hypothesis generation.

**Detail:** The spatially enhanced cBioPortal extends the standard data model to accommodate tissue-level spatial features (cell density, marker co-expression, spatial topology) alongside per-sample genomic and clinical attributes. This represents a new data modality for cBioPortal beyond the traditional genomics + clinical data model, and establishes a framework for future integration of spatial transcriptomics and spatial proteomics across other cancer types.

### F5: tipMMR may double the number of treatment-naïve CRCs eligible for ICI therapy

**Finding:** If tipMMR CRCs (~20% of pMMR) respond to ICIs at rates comparable to dMMR CRC, the clinically actionable population for first-line ICI therapy in CRC could approximately double. Current eligibility is restricted to dMMR/MSI-H tumors (~15% of CRC); adding tipMMR (~20% of the remaining ~85% pMMR patients, i.e., ~17% of all CRC) could substantially expand ICI eligibility at treatment-naïve stages.

**Detail:** The clinical significance relies on the assumption that PD1/PDL1 expression and Treg infiltration in tipMMR translate to ICI sensitivity — an assumption requiring prospective clinical validation. The mid-plex imaging assay used to detect tipMMR has potential for clinical deployment given its relative simplicity compared to spatial transcriptomics. Validation in prospective clinical cohorts or retrospective ICI-treated CRC datasets is needed to confirm that tipMMR status predicts ICI response independent of MMR/MSI status.

**Genes / cancer types:** PDCD1, CD274, FOXP3 · Colorectal carcinoma

**Data availability:** `partial`
> tipMMR classification and survival data available in `crc_orion_2024`. ICI treatment response data are not available in this cohort (treatment-naïve resected patients).

**Tool support:** `custom`
> tipMMR classification requires spatial profiling data and the enhanced cBioPortal. Survival analysis by tipMMR status accessible via the custom portal.

## Open Questions

- Does tipMMR status predict objective response to PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibitors in prospective clinical trials of treatment-naïve CRC?
- What molecular or cellular mechanisms drive T cell recruitment in tipMMR tumors in the absence of high neo-antigen burden — candidate mechanisms include STING pathway activation, viral mimicry, or specific stromal cell programs?
- Can tipMMR be detected in pre-surgical biopsies or liquid biopsy, or does it require full resection specimens for spatial quantification?
- Does the tipMMR phenotype overlap with previously described immune CRC subtypes (e.g., CMS1 by the CMS classifier), and can existing transcriptomic classifiers approximate tipMMR identification?
- What is the stability of the tipMMR phenotype over time and across tumor regions — is it clonal or spatially heterogeneous within a tumor?

## Limitations

- Preprint (bioRxiv, 2024): findings have not yet completed peer review at time of database entry; conclusions should be treated as preliminary
- Full text is not available in PMC due to preprint license terms; detailed cohort size, patient characteristics, and analytical methods are not fully extractable from the abstract and significance statement alone
- tipMMR classification relies on a spatial profiling assay; clinical deployment requires assay standardization, validation across sites, and regulatory approval
- Prospective ICI treatment data are not available in this resected-tumor cohort; the ICI benefit hypothesis is inferential based on immune marker expression, not demonstrated by clinical outcomes
- No specific somatic mutation driver for tipMMR means genomic screening alone cannot identify this subgroup — limiting biomarker integration with existing clinical genomic tests
- Cohort composition, sequencing platform details, and spatial assay specifications require the full manuscript for complete critical appraisal
