## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing of 42 CTCL cases — 25 Sézary syndrome (SS) and 17 mycosis fungoides (MF) and other CTCL — with matched normal DNA reveals a genomic landscape dominated by large chromosomal copy-number losses targeting tumor suppressors in Sézary syndrome, and a broad spectrum of somatic mutations in epigenetic regulators and T-cell receptor (TCR) signaling genes in both subtypes. Sézary syndrome displays a median of 21 CNAs per sample with recurrent deletions at *TP53* (52%), *PTEN* (20%), *DNMT3A* (20%), *RB1* (16%), and *CDKN1B* (20%). Mutation analysis identifies loss-of-function events in epigenetic regulators (*TET2*, *CREBBP*, *MLL3*, *SMARCA4*, *BRD9*, *CHD3*) and gain-of-function mutations in signaling genes (*CARD11* linker-domain mutations activating NFκB, *PRKG1/cGKIβ* loss-of-function mutations increasing NFAT activity, *BRAF* K601E, *MAPK1* E322K). Functional characterization confirms that CARD11 and cGKIβ mutations potentiate TCR-controlled NFκB and NFAT signaling, respectively, identifying new therapeutic vulnerabilities in these aggressive skin lymphomas.

## Key Findings

### F1: Sézary syndrome is characterized by massive chromosomal deletion of key tumor suppressors — TP53, PTEN, DNMT3A, RB1, CDKN1B

**Finding:** Copy number analysis from exome data reveals a median of 21 CNAs per Sézary sample (range 0–56), with a highly characteristic pattern of recurrent deletions. *TP53* (17p13.1) is deleted in 52% (13/25), *PTEN* (10q23.3) in 20% (5/25), *DNMT3A* (2p23.3) in 20% (5/25; including 2 focal homozygous deletions), *RB1* (13q14.2) in 16% (4/25), and *CDKN1B* (12p13.1) in 20% (5/25). Expression analysis confirms reduced or absent expression of DNMT3A and TP53 in samples with the respective deletions. By contrast, non-leukemic MF cases show dramatically fewer CNAs (median 1, range 0–2), highlighting a qualitative genomic difference between SS and MF.

**Detail:** The prevalence of DNMT3A deletion in SS is particularly notable given DNMT3A's established role as a tumor suppressor in myeloid malignancies (AML, MDS). Homozygous focal deletion in 2/5 DNMT3A-deleted cases indicates strong selection for complete loss. The loss of multiple cooperating tumor suppressors (TP53, PTEN, RB1, DNMT3A) in a single disease entity suggests extensive genomic instability during SS pathogenesis. Chromosome-level gains include 7 (20%), 8q (52%), and 17q (8%) in Sézary syndrome.

### F2: Epigenetic regulator mutations — TET2, CREBBP, MLL3, SMARCA4, BRD9, CHD3 — are recurrent in SS and MF

**Finding:** Both SS and MF harbor mutations in genes encoding key epigenetic regulators. In Sézary syndrome: *TET2* (3 mutations: Q1654*, C1932F, Q649*, a known DHSBH domain hotspot), *CREBBP* (Q839*, S1207fs), *MLL3* (T3941fs), *BRD9* (Q479H; H467_L468del), *SMARCA4* (S1238Y), and *CHD3* (Q660H; S230L). In MF: *SMARCA4* (R251K), *MLL2* (Q2418*), and *MLL3* (G1246*). The strongly deleterious nature of most alleles (protein truncations, frameshifts, high PolyPhen-2 scores) supports functional significance.

**Detail:** TET2 mutations in SS are striking given TET2's established role in myeloid malignancies and angioimmunoblastic T-cell lymphoma. The C1932F allele disrupts the DHSBH catalytic domain, the same region recurrently mutated in myeloid tumors. CHD3 Q660H is in the second chromodomain (PolyPhen-2 = 0.99) and S230L is in the PHD domain, which reads repressive histone marks. BRD9 is a core SWI/SNF (PBAF) complex component — its mutations represent a novel finding in CTCL. The parallel enrichment of epigenetic regulator mutations in CTCL and peripheral T-cell lymphomas suggests a shared epigenetic vulnerability in mature T-cell malignancies.

### F3: CARD11 linker-domain mutations constitutively activate NFκB signaling in Sézary syndrome

**Finding:** Two novel CARD11 mutations — S615F and E626K — are identified in two separate Sézary patients. Both map to the linker domain of CARD11, distinct from the coiled-coil domain where DLBCL-associated CARD11 mutations cluster. Both mutations are functionally validated: they increase NFκB luciferase reporter activity in HEK293T cells, drive NFκB-dependent GFP expression in Jurkat T-cells, and synergize with PMA+ionomycin stimulation (mimicking TCR activation) to produce particularly strong and sustained NFκB activation with increased JNK phosphorylation. Activity is intermediate between weak DLBCL mutants and strong DLBCL mutants at baseline, but comparable to strong mutants after TCR stimulation.

**Detail:** CARD11 acts as a scaffold that assembles a BCL10/MALT1/TRAF6/TAK1/IKKγ complex upon T-cell receptor engagement and PKCθ-mediated phosphorylation of its linker domain. CARD11 linker phosphorylation normally converts CARD11 from a closed (inactive) to an open (active) conformation. SS CARD11 mutations at S615 and E626 likely pre-activate or stabilize the open conformation, mimicking constitutive PKCθ phosphorylation. The context-dependent potentiation (stronger after TCR stimulation) suggests CARD11 linker mutations amplify rather than simply mimic TCR signaling, which has important therapeutic implications for targeting downstream NFκB effectors (bortezomib active across all CTCL lines).

### F4: Loss-of-function PRKG1/cGKIβ mutations enhance NFAT signaling via disrupted homodimerization

**Finding:** Two Sézary patients harbor cGKIβ mutations (E17K and R21Q) in the N-terminal leucine/isoleucine zipper dimerization domain of the cGMP-dependent protein kinase encoded by *PRKG1*. These mutations reduce the stability of cGKIβ homodimers (urea dissociation assays), impair cGMP-induced ERK1/2 activation and RhoA S188 phosphorylation, and increase NFAT luciferase reporter activity in PMA+ionomycin-stimulated Jurkat cells — demonstrating that cGKIβ normally dampens TCR-triggered NFAT activation, and that these mutations are loss-of-function alleles that enhance T-cell activation responses.

**Detail:** cGKIβ normally inhibits IL-2 release and proliferation in TCR-stimulated T-cells; its loss thus shifts T-cells toward a constitutively active phenotype. E17K and R21Q are located at residues critical for leucine zipper-mediated dimerization, with the zipper domain being required for proper kinase activation and substrate recognition. The combination of NFκB potentiation (via CARD11 mutations, TNFR2 mutations) and NFAT enhancement (via PRKG1 loss) in SS creates a synergistic pro-survival, pro-proliferative signaling environment that mirrors constitutively active T-cell receptor signaling. Western blot analysis of CTCL cell lines confirms high levels of p-STAT3, p-ERK, p-JNK, and nuclear p50 NFκB1 across lines.

### F5: MAPK pathway mutations — BRAF K601E and MAPK1 E322K/E322A — activate ERK signaling in CTCL subtypes

**Finding:** Multiple MAPK pathway-activating mutations are identified across the cohort. A *BRAF* K601E mutation (a known activating allele) is present in one CD30-negative CTCL patient. Three *MAPK1* mutations affecting E322 (E322A in one MF patient; E322K in another MF patient) are identified; MAPK1 E322K is a previously reported activating allele, and both MAPK1 E322K and E322A are confirmed by Western blot to increase ERK1/2 phosphorylation in transduced cells. Additionally, *JAK3* V678L (previously described in T-prolymphocytic leukemia) co-occurs with a *SH2B3* loss-of-function mutation (Y273*) in one patient, and *STAT3* Y640F (activating) is found in two MF cases.

**Detail:** BRAF K601E is distinct from the canonical V600E hotspot found in melanoma and hairy cell leukemia; K601E activates BRAF but may have different drug sensitivity profiles relative to V600E. MAPK1/ERK2 E322K is a gain-of-function allele previously identified in HNSCC and CTCL (Choi et al., 2015). SH2B3 encodes a negative regulator of JAK kinases (LNK); its truncating mutation paired with a JAK3-activating allele creates dual-hit JAK pathway activation. Drug testing in CTCL cell lines shows JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, ruxolitinib) active in JAK3-V678L-expressing HUT78 cells, while NFκB inhibitors (bortezomib, Mi-2) are broadly active across all lines.

## Open Questions

- Do CARD11 linker-domain mutations in Sézary syndrome confer sensitivity to MALT1 inhibitors (ibrutinib, bortezomib) analogously to DLBCL CARD11 coiled-coil mutations?
- Can cGKIβ (PRKG1) loss-of-function be therapeutically targeted — e.g., through upstream cGMP modulators or by exploiting increased NFAT activation for immune-based strategies?
- What is the functional consequence of DNMT3A homozygous deletion in SS — does it cause global DNA hypomethylation and oncogenic gene activation similar to DNMT3A loss in AML?
- Does TET2 mutation in CTCL respond to hypomethylating agents (azacitidine/decitabine) or vitamin C (which restores TET activity), as proposed in myeloid malignancies?
- Is there a functional interaction between epigenetic regulator mutations (TET2, CREBBP) and chromosomal deletions (TP53, PTEN) — do they cooperate in tumor initiation or progression?
- Do BRAF K601E or MAPK1 E322K/E322A alleles sensitize CTCL tumors to MEK inhibitors, and how do they respond versus the constitutively active V600E allele?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=42 total; n=25 SS) limits statistical power for rare mutation discovery
- Control DNA from CD4-depleted blood for some SS patients may retain contaminating tumor lymphocytes, complicating somatic variant calling (addressed with 20% VAF threshold filter)
- No whole-genome sequencing — structural variants and noncoding mutations (e.g., TERT promoter, enhancer alterations) not characterized
- No RNA-seq — transcriptomic consequences of mutations and gene fusions not assessed
- Limited clinical annotation (treatment history, response data) precludes genotype-phenotype correlation with clinical outcomes
- CTCL cell lines used for drug testing may not fully represent primary Sézary syndrome biology
- Mutational heterogeneity within the 9 "other CTCL" cases may confound pathway-level analyses
