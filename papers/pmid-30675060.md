## Summary

Miller et al. (2019, *Nature*) performed MSK-IMPACT panel sequencing of cell-free DNA (ctDNA) from cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) of 85 glioma patients at MSKCC to evaluate CSF as a liquid biopsy for genomic profiling. Tumor-derived DNA was detected in 42/85 (49.4%) patients and closely mirrored the genomic landscape of matched tumor biopsies. CSF ctDNA positivity was strongly associated with tumor burden, radiographic progression, and a four-fold increased risk of death. The study demonstrated that CSF outperforms plasma for brain tumor ctDNA detection and enables non-invasive longitudinal monitoring of clonal evolution, including identification of new targetable alterations not present in the original tumor biopsy.

## Key Findings

### F1: CSF ctDNA Detected in ~Half of Glioma Patients, Outperforms Plasma

**Finding:** Tumor-derived DNA was detected by MSK-IMPACT in CSF from 42/85 (49.4%) glioma patients, while plasma ctDNA was undetectable in 16/19 (84%) patients with positive CSF.

**Detail:** Among the 19 patients for whom both CSF and plasma were tested, only 3 had any detectable plasma mutations, and 32 of the 35 plasma mutations came from a single hypermutated patient. The average variant allele fraction (VAF) in plasma was 0.58% versus 23.96% in CSF (Wilcoxon rank-sum p=5.82×10⁻¹¹). No oncogenic variants were found in CSF from 7 non-malignant neurological disease controls, confirming specificity. These data demonstrate that tumor DNA is shed directly into CSF rather than reaching it through plasma.

### F2: CSF ctDNA Positivity Is a Strong Independent Prognostic Factor

**Finding:** CSF ctDNA positivity was associated with a four-fold increased risk of death (HR=4.16, 95% CI 2.15–8.05, p=0.000024) in a multivariable Cox model adjusting for extent of resection, tumor burden, and IDH status.

**Detail:** Median overall survival (OS) from the time of CSF collection was 3.15 months (95% CI: 1.97–4.63) for ctDNA-positive patients versus 11.91 months (95% CI: 8.40–30.81) for ctDNA-negative patients (log-rank p=7.87×10⁻⁶). In the GBM IDH-wildtype subgroup alone, median OS was 2.04 months (ctDNA-positive) versus 9.89 months (ctDNA-negative, p=6.24×10⁻⁵). Importantly, 83% of ctDNA-positive patients (35/42) had no malignant cells by standard CSF cytopathology, confirming that molecular profiling is substantially more sensitive.

### F3: CSF ctDNA Positivity Correlates with Radiographic Disease Burden and Progression

**Finding:** CSF ctDNA positivity was significantly associated with radiographic tumor progression (p=0.0005), tumor burden by sum of product of diameters (SPD; p=1.7×10⁻⁶), and tumor spread toward the ventricular or subarachnoid space (p=0.02).

**Detail:** Median tumor burden (SPD) was 1,553 mm² in ctDNA-positive patients versus 373 mm² in ctDNA-negative patients. Worsening tumor was observed in 55% of ctDNA-positive versus 19% of ctDNA-negative patients. Absence of leptomeningeal, pial, or subependymal disease (brain-only) was present in only 14% of ctDNA-positive patients versus 37% of ctDNA-negative patients. No significant association was found with grade, disease duration, or prior therapy type.

### F4: CSF Genomic Landscape Mirrors Tumor Tissue with 100% Concordance in Subtype-Defining Alterations

**Finding:** Subtype-defining "truncal" alterations (IDH mutation, 1p/19q codeletion, TERT promoter mutation, TP53, and ATRX) were 100% concordant between CSF and matched tumor tissue in all 30 non-hypermutated pairs (10/10 LGG, 20/20 GBM).

**Detail:** The most common alterations in CSF ctDNA were TERT promoter mutations, TP53 coding mutations, IDH1 catalytic domain mutations, CDKN2A/CDKN2B deletions, EGFR amplification, and in-frame EGFRvIII deletion — mirroring the distribution seen in 553 MSK-IMPACT tumor biopsies. The median somatic mutation rate in CSF ctDNA was 4.90 mutations/Mb; after excluding hypermutated samples, it was 3.92/Mb in CSF and 3.94/Mb in matched tumor tissue, indicating near-identical overall mutational burden.

### F5: Median 81.7% Shared Mutation Rate Between CSF and Tumor; Much Lower in Hypermutated Cases

**Finding:** In non-hypermutated CSF/tumor pairs (n=30), the median shared mutation rate between CSF and matched tumor was 81.7% (range 0–100%). In hypermutated pairs (n=6), the median shared rate dropped to 19.6% (range 3–49%).

**Detail:** CSF samples collected within three weeks of tumor resection showed near-identical genetic profiles. Five patients had concurrent VP shunt placement (ventricular CSF) and lumbar puncture within three weeks; genomic profiles from these two anatomical sites were highly concordant (Pearson r²=0.966 in one hypermutated case). Clonal (trunk) mutations in tissue were almost always shared in CSF, whereas subclonal mutations were less likely to be shared. As collection intervals increased, divergence increased — particularly in growth factor receptor pathway genes.

### F6: Convergent Evolution in Growth Factor Receptor Pathways Detected by Serial CSF Profiling

**Finding:** Longitudinal CSF profiling revealed convergent evolution in growth factor receptor pathways, with later samples acquiring alterations in related genes/pathways while losing original alterations.

**Detail:** In patient #25 (GBM), an initial EGFR amplification and EGFR G719C mutation were replaced at recurrence by PDGFRA amplification and mutation, with no residual EGFR alteration — a classic example of convergent evolution. In patient #28, an initial PIK3CA E545K mutation was followed by MET and PDGFRA amplifications in a subsequent biopsy, and a CSF sample then retained MET amplification while gaining MYC amplification and losing PDGFRA amplification. In LGGs, truncal alterations (IDH1, TP53, ATRX) persisted throughout, but later samples showed additional mutations in glioma core pathways, consistent with branched evolution.

### F7: DNA Hypermutation in 12% of CSF ctDNA-Positive Patients from Prior Alkylator Therapy

**Finding:** 5/42 (12%) CSF ctDNA-positive glioma patients had markedly elevated mutation rates in CSF with the G:C→A:T transition signature of prior alkylating agent exposure; all five had received temozolomide.

**Detail:** Hypermutated CSF samples showed a dramatically higher mutation rate and a distinct mutational signature consistent with temozolomide-induced hypermutation. In one illustrative case (patient #36, GBM), the original tumor harbored 5 mutations; the recurrent tumor harbored 120 and the CSF 132 somatic SNVs. Hypermutated status was associated with lower concordance between CSF and tissue (median shared rate 19.6% vs. 81.7% in non-hypermutated cases), reflecting branched evolution driven by mutagenesis.

## Open Questions

- What is the earliest time point during glioma progression at which CSF ctDNA becomes detectable? Can it serve as an early warning of recurrence before radiographic changes?
- Can CSF ctDNA guide selection of targeted therapies (e.g., EGFR or PDGFRA inhibitors) in real time, and do CSF-identified alterations predict response?
- Would routine serial CSF profiling improve clinical outcomes compared to standard surveillance MRI alone?
- Why does ~50% of the glioma cohort shed no detectable tumor DNA into CSF even at symptomatic progression? What biological or anatomical factors determine shedding?
- Does the hypermutation signature in CSF predict differential response to subsequent immunotherapy or other agents targeting DNA damage repair?
- Can ultra-sensitive CSF assays detect minimal residual disease after gross total resection?

## Limitations

- Single-institution retrospective cohort (MSKCC); all patients had advanced, previously treated disease — not representative of newly diagnosed or low-burden glioma.
- CSF was collected for clinical indications (neurological symptoms), introducing selection bias toward patients with more symptomatic/advanced disease.
- ctDNA detected in only 49.4% of patients; the assay lacks sensitivity for patients with low tumor shedding or purely parenchymal (non-CSF-contacting) disease.
- Plasma cfDNA comparison was limited to 19 patients and used a different (more targeted, higher-sensitivity) assay, making direct sensitivity comparisons imperfect.
- MSK-IMPACT captures 410 cancer genes but not the full genome, so structural variants and alterations outside the panel are missed.
- Longitudinal serial CSF collections were available for only a subset of patients; evolution analyses are limited by sample availability and variable intervals between collections.
- No functional validation of newly identified targetable alterations (e.g., PDGFRA) in individual patient CSF; clinical utility of CSF-guided therapy selection remains to be prospectively tested.
