## Summary

The TCGA Network's comprehensive multi-platform molecular analysis of 333 primary prostate carcinomas (WES, SNP arrays, RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, methylation arrays, RPPA, low-pass WGS) establishes a molecular taxonomy in which 74% of tumors are assignable to seven genomically distinct subtypes defined by ETS gene fusions (*ERG* 46%, *ETV1* 8%, *ETV4* 4%, *FLI1* 1%) or point mutations (*SPOP* 11%, *FOXA1* 3%, *IDH1* 1%). Key additional findings include: DNA repair defects in 19% (BRCA2, CDK12, ATM, FANCD2, RAD51C) with direct therapeutic implications for PARP inhibitors; PI3K/MAPK pathway alterations in ~25% beyond PTEN deletion; SPOP- and FOXA1-mutant tumors exhibiting the highest androgen receptor (AR) transcriptional activity of all subtypes; IDH1-mutant prostate cancers representing the most hypermethylated subtype (exceeding even IDH1-mutant GBM and AML); and AR-V7 splice variants detectable in 50% of primary tumors ÔÇö an observation challenging the prior assumption that AR-V7 is exclusive to metastatic castration-resistant disease.

## Key Findings

### F1: Seven molecular subtypes cover 74% of primary prostate cancers ÔÇö ETS fusions dominate (53%), with SPOP, FOXA1, IDH1 mutations defining non-ETS subtypes

**Finding:** Integrative multi-platform clustering (somatic mutation, copy number, gene expression, DNA methylation) identifies seven molecularly distinct and mutually exclusive subtypes of primary prostate cancer, covering 74% of tumors: ERG fusions (46%), ETV1 fusions/overexpression (8%), ETV4 fusions (4%), FLI1 fusions (1%), SPOP mutations (11%), FOXA1 mutations (3%), and IDH1 mutations (1%). Within ETS-positive tumors, TMPRSS2 is the most common 5ÔÇ▓ fusion partner, but SLC45A3 and NDRG1 androgen-regulated partners also occur. The 26% of tumors lacking a defining alteration are heterogeneous ÔÇö some enriched for TP53/KDM6A/KMT2D mutations and arm-level CNA, others are genomically "quiet."

**Detail:** ETS fusions are largely mutually exclusive with each other and with SPOP mutations (except 4 cases with co-occurring SPOP + FOXA1 mutations, both clonal). SPOP-mutant tumors show distinctive SCNA profiles (CHD1 deletion, 6q/2q deletions), elevated DNA methylation, homogeneous gene expression, and frequent SPINK1 overexpression ÔÇö reinforcing SPOP as a cardinal subtype-defining lesion. ERG-positive tumors subdivide epigenetically into a hypermethylated subgroup (cluster 1, predominantly ERG-positive) and a moderately methylated group (cluster 3), revealing previously unappreciated heterogeneity within what was considered a single molecular subtype. Genomic comparison with 150 mCRPC samples (Robinson 2015) shows similar subtype distribution between primary and metastatic disease, but IDH1-mutant tumors are absent in the metastatic dataset.

### F2: DNA repair defects in 19% of primary prostate cancers ÔÇö BRCA2 (3%), CDK12 (1%), ATM, FANCD2, RAD51C ÔÇö providing early therapeutic rationale for PARP inhibitors

**Finding:** 19% of primary prostate cancers harbor inactivating alterations in DNA repair genes: *BRCA2* (3% ÔÇö germline and somatic truncating mutations plus focal homozygous deletions), *CDK12* (1% ÔÇö LOF mutations and deletions), *ATM* (nonsense and kinase-dead hotspot N2875), *FANCD2* (truncating mutation, homozygous deletions, heterozygous losses in 6%), and *RAD51C* (focal deletions 3%). Notably, heterozygous *BRCA2* losses (13q13.1) almost always co-occur with concurrent *RB1* loss (13q14.2) due to their proximity on chromosome 13q. This 19% frequency is remarkably consistent with the TOPARP-A trial (olaparib in mCRPC) finding that clinical responses occurred preferentially in tumors bearing DNA repair defects.

**Detail:** This was one of the first comprehensive characterizations of HRD prevalence in primary prostate cancer, predating the routine clinical adoption of BRCA testing and PARP inhibitors (olaparib FDA-approved for BRCA-mutated mCRPC in 2020, rucaparib in 2020). All six BRCA2 germline mutations identified were K3326*, a C-terminal truncating variant of debated functional impact but enriched prevalence in multiple tumor types. CDK12 inactivation specifically disrupts transcription-coupled DNA repair of large genes (including HR genes), providing a mechanistic rationale distinct from classic BRCA-type HRD. The co-deletion of BRCA2 and RB1 on 13q has implications for combinatorial targeting.

### F3: SPOP- and FOXA1-mutant tumors have the highest androgen receptor transcriptional activity of all prostate cancer subtypes ÔÇö AR-V7 detected in 50% of primary tumors

**Finding:** An AR activity score (based on 20 experimentally validated AR transcriptional target genes) reveals that *SPOP*-mutant tumors have significantly higher AR output than all other subtypes (p=1.1├ù10Ôü╗ÔüÂ vs. ERG-positive), and *FOXA1*-mutant tumors also show elevated AR activity (p=0.04). Notably, *AR* splice variants ÔÇö most prominently AR-V7 (truncated, ligand-binding domain-absent) ÔÇö are detectable at low levels by RNA-seq in 50% of primary prostate tumors and in benign tissue in some cases. AR-V7 expression in primary tumors was not associated with differential AR target gene expression or the seven genomic subtypes.

**Detail:** This finding that AR-V7 is present in 50% of primary treatment-naive tumors directly challenges the prior paradigm that AR-V7 is predominantly an mCRPC phenomenon linked to castration-resistance and antiandrogen resistance. The majority of AR splice forms detected used cryptic exon inclusion (not exon skipping) to create truncated proteins. The therapeutic implication is significant: if AR-V7 pre-exists in primary tumors at low levels, it may be selected for by androgen deprivation therapy, providing a pre-existing pool for rapid emergence of castration resistance. FOXA1 mutations in prostate (all histology-type-specific winged-helix domain missense mutations, not DNA-binding contact residues) likely disrupt chromatin cofactor interactions rather than direct DNA binding ÔÇö similar to FOXA1 mutations in lobular breast cancer.

### F4: IDH1-mutant primary prostate cancers define a distinct early-onset subtype with the highest genome-wide hypermethylation of any IDH1-mutant cancer ÔÇö more methylated than IDH1-mutant GBM or AML

**Finding:** *IDH1* R132 mutations occur in ~1% of primary prostate cancers, defining a novel subtype with distinct characteristics: association with younger age at diagnosis, absence of ETS fusions, wild-type *SPOP*, very low SCNA burden, and genome-wide DNA hypermethylation exceeding that of IDH1-mutant GBM and AML ÔÇö the two cancer types in which IDH1-driven methylation was first described. IDH1-mutant prostate tumors have the greatest number of epigenetically silenced genes of any prostate cancer subtype.

**Detail:** IDH1/2 mutations produce 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG), an oncometabolite that inhibits TET2 dioxygenases and histone demethylases, producing a CpG island methylator phenotype (CIMP). While this mechanism is well established in glioma and AML, the finding that IDH1-mutant prostate cancer shows even greater hypermethylation than these paradigm cancers is unexpected and may reflect tissue-specific differences in baseline methylation or 2-HG sensitivity. IDH1-mutant prostate cancers also show the fewest canonical driver lesions (no ETS fusions, no SPOP mutations, minimal copy-number alterations), suggesting a unique oncogenic trajectory through epigenetic mechanisms. Future studies with longer follow-up will be needed to determine if IDH1-mutant prostate cancers have distinct prognosis (analogous to IDH1-mutant gliomas having favorable prognosis) and whether IDH1 inhibitors (ivosidenib) have activity.

### F5: PI3K and MAPK pathway alterations in ~25% of primary prostate cancers ÔÇö beyond PTEN, rare but potentially actionable PIK3CA, PIK3CB, AKT1, HRAS, BRAF activating mutations identified

**Finding:** Beyond *PTEN* deletion/mutation (17%), the PI3K/MAPK signaling axis is altered in ~25% of primary prostate cancers through diverse low-frequency activating driver mutations: *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations (E545K, Q546K, N345I, C420R) in 6 tumors; *PIK3CB* E552K (paralogous to PIK3CA E545K) in 2 PTEN-null tumors; *AKT1* E17K (2 tumors) and D323Y (1, structurally adjacent to E17K); *HRAS* Q61R (3 tumors); *BRAF* kinase domain mutations (8 tumors ÔÇö L597R, K601E, G469A, none V600E); and two cases of other Ras-family small GTPase mutations (*RAC1* Q61R, *RRAS2* Q72L ÔÇö paralogous to KRAS Q61).

**Detail:** The "long tail" of low-frequency alterations collectively affects ~25% of primary prostate cancers and provides candidates for targeted therapy trials that were otherwise restricted to PTEN-loss patients. PIK3CB E552K co-occurring with PTEN homozygous deletion is particularly notable: PTEN-deleted tumors are PI3K╬▓-dependent (due to PI3K╬▒ feedback inhibition in PI3K╬▓), and combined PTEN loss + PIK3CB mutation may amplify PI3K output further. BRAF mutations in prostate cancer notably avoid the V600E hotspot ÔÇö instead clustering in non-V600E kinase domain hotspots (K601E, G469A) that confer sensitivity to MEK inhibitors rather than BRAF inhibitors. This has direct therapeutic implications: prostate cancer BRAF mutations may not respond to vemurafenib/dabrafenib but may respond to trametinib/cobimetinib.

## Open Questions

- Do IDH1-mutant primary prostate cancers have a favorable prognosis after radical prostatectomy (analogous to IDH1-mutant gliomas), and could IDH1 inhibitors (ivosidenib) have activity in IDH1-mutant prostate cancer?
- Does the detection of AR-V7 in 50% of primary hormone-naive tumors at low levels predict time to castration resistance, and should it inform adjuvant hormone therapy decision-making?
- Do the ~26% of "unclassified" primary prostate cancers require whole-genome sequencing to identify occult driver events (regulatory mutations, structural variants outside ETS/SPOP/FOXA1/IDH1)?
- Can DNA repair defects detected at primary prostatectomy (BRCA2, CDK12, ATM) guide earlier use of PARP inhibitors (before development of mCRPC), particularly in high-risk localized disease?
- Do non-V600E BRAF mutations in prostate cancer respond to MEK inhibitors (trametinib) better than BRAF inhibitors, and should prostate cancer BRAF mutations be histology-specifically treated?
- Does the co-deletion of BRCA2 and RB1 on chromosome 13q create a synthetic lethality that could be therapeutically exploited (e.g., CDK4/6 + PARP inhibition)?

## Limitations

- All tumors from radical prostatectomy for clinically localized disease; average follow-up only ~2 years ÔÇö insufficient for outcomes analysis given the long natural history of localized prostate cancer
- Single tumor focus per patient biopsied; prostate cancer is multifocal, and different foci may represent different molecular subtypes ÔÇö the dominant focus may not represent all disease biology
- Tumor cellularity challenge: prostate cancer biopsies often have low tumor content, requiring rigorous pathology QC (333 selected from 425 initially available) ÔÇö this selection may bias toward genomically simpler tumors
- No treatment history data (patients underwent initial prostatectomy); response to systemic therapy not assessable in this cohort
- RPPA data only for 152 of 333 samples; pathway activation at the protein level (e.g., pAKT, pERK) is incomplete and shows limited concordance with genomic alterations
- Six BRCA2 germline mutations were all K3326* ÔÇö a variant of debated functional significance; the true germline pathogenic BRCA2 variant frequency may be overestimated
- IDH1-mutant subtype (n=~3 tumors) is too small for robust statistical analysis of prognosis or therapeutic implications
- RNA-seq-based AR-V7 detection is less specific than protein-based assays (Oncotype DX AR-V7 nuclear detection); clinical relevance of primary tumor AR-V7 requires prospective validation
