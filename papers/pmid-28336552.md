## Summary

This study reports the prospective MSK-IMPACT sequencing experience in 860 patients with recurrent or metastatic lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD), with a focus on measuring the real-world clinical impact of comprehensive tumor profiling. Using OncoKB to stratify actionable alterations into four evidence levels, the study finds that 86.9% of patients harbored a potentially actionable event and 37.1% received a matched therapy. Clinical benefit from matched therapy was 52% overall (excluding standard-of-care Level 1 alterations). The use of matched therapy correlated strongly with the level of pre-existing clinical evidence (92% of Level 1 vs. 2% of Level 4 patients received matched therapy). Among tumors without known actionable mutations (Unknown Mitogenic Driver cohort, n=103), *STK11* and *KEAP1* are nominated as candidate oncogenic drivers. *KEAP1* mutations cluster at the Kelch domain–Nrf2 interaction interface, implicating loss of Nrf2 repression as a resistance/survival mechanism. This prospective study establishes the real-world utility — and limits — of broad tumor profiling in advanced LUAD.

## Key Findings

### F1: 86.9% of metastatic LUAD patients have potentially actionable alterations (OncoKB levels 1–4); EGFR (27%) and KRAS are most common

**Finding:** Potentially actionable somatic alterations were identified in 747/860 patients (86.9%). Consistent with prior studies, *EGFR* mutations (27%) and *KRAS* mutations were the most common and nearly universally mutually exclusive (p<0.0001). The MSK-IMPACT cohort showed a significantly higher proportion of *EGFR* mutant patients than TCGA (27% vs. 11%, p<0.001), likely due to referral bias (EGFR T790M clinical trials) and higher never-smoker proportion (32.2%). Oncogenic fusions in *ALK*, *ROS1*, and *RET*, *MET* exon 14 alterations, and *BRAF* V600E were each found in 1.7–3.8% of patients.

**Detail:** Less common actionable drivers were identified in 110 (12.8%) patients, including *NF1* truncating mutations (16 pts), *NRAS* activating mutations (10 pts), *MAP2K1/MEK1* mutations (6 pts), *ARAF* S214Y/P (2 pts), *RAF1* S257L (2 pts). PI3K pathway alterations as highest-level actionable finding in 3.7%: *PIK3CA* (17 pts), *PTEN* (6 pts), *TSC1/2* truncating (3 pts each), *AKT1* E17K (2 pts), *mTOR* S2215Y (1 pt). *BRCA1* inactivating (3 pts, 0.3%) and *BRCA2* truncating (8 pts, 0.9%) mutations were Level 2B based on PARP inhibitor approval in BRCA-mutant ovarian carcinoma. 5.5% (47/860) had *EGFR* T790M mutations — all post-EGFR-TKI therapy.

### F2: Matched therapy use correlates strongly with evidence level; clinical benefit 85%+ for Level 1 (EGFR/ALK/ROS1) alterations

**Finding:** 37.1% (319/860) of patients received a matched therapy guided by MSK-IMPACT results. Matched therapy use by level: Level 1 = 92%, Level 2A = 52%, Level 2B = 17%, Level 3 = 25%, Level 4 = 2% (p<0.0001). Clinical benefit was 84.8% for sensitizing EGFR inhibitors, 93.3% for ALK inhibitors, 84.6% for ROS1 inhibitors, and 76.5% for MET exon 14–directed therapy. Excluding standard-of-care Level 1 alterations, 14.4% (69/478) received matched therapy with 52% clinical benefit.

**Detail:** Level 2A alterations with high matched-therapy benefit: *MET* exon 14 (65.4% received MT; 76.5% benefit), *RET* fusions (53.3%; 72.7%), *BRAF* V600E (55.6%; 75%), wild-type *MET* amplification (16.7%; 50%). *KRAS*-mutant patients almost never received matched therapy (2/218; 0.9%). Among rare *EGFR* alleles: L861Q had significantly lower clinical benefit rate (43% vs. L858R; p=0.039), supporting evaluation of second/third-generation TKIs. *EGFR* exon 20 insertions (17 patients, Level 4 at time of study) were resistant to standard erlotinib, consistent with prior evidence. Level 2B patients (ERBB2, BRCA1/2, TSC1/2) — none with BRCA or TSC alterations received matched PARP or mTOR inhibitor therapy, despite approved drugs in other indications.

### F3: STK11 and KEAP1 nominated as candidate oncogenic drivers in tumors lacking known actionable mutations (Unknown Mitogenic Driver cohort)

**Finding:** 103 patients (12%) had tumors with no Level 1–4 actionable alteration (Unknown Mitogenic Driver, UMD). Compared to patients with Level 1–4 alterations, UMD tumors had significantly higher rates of *TP53*, *STK11*, *KEAP1*, *KMT2D*, and *PDGFRA* alterations (all p<0.05). In the UMD heavy-smoker subset, *STK11* (34%, p<0.05) and co-occurring *TP53*+*STK11* (27%) were especially enriched. *KEAP1* was altered in 34% (35/103) of UMD cases (10 truncations, 25 missense) with missense mutations clustering at the Kelch domain–Nrf2 interaction interface, consistent with loss of Nrf2 repression.

**Detail:** *KEAP1* loss activates Nrf2 (NRF2), upregulating cellular antioxidants and xenobiotic detoxification — conferring chemoresistance. Nrf2 inhibitors (luteolin, brusatol) have shown chemotherapy sensitization in preclinical studies. *STK11* loss impairs LKB1 → AMPK signaling and has been associated with resistance to immunotherapy in KRAS-mutant LUAD. Never-/former-light-smoker UMD cases were enriched for chromatin-modifying genes (*KMT2C*, *SETD2*, *CREBBP*) and HR genes (*MRE11A*, *BRCA2*) compared to Level 1–4 cases. UMD sample purity was not significantly different from Level 1–4 samples (mean 35% vs. 38%), ruling out low-purity artifact.

### F4: Multiple co-occurring actionable alterations identified in 239 patients; co-occurring EGFR+PIK3CA was most frequent pair (25 patients)

**Finding:** 239 patients had tumors with co-occurring targetable mutations; 46 patients had ≥3 concurrent Level 1–4 alterations. The most frequent pair of co-occurring Level 1–3 mutations was *EGFR* + *PIK3CA* (25 patients). Critically, *EGFR* and *KRAS* mutations co-occurred in only 2 patients (out of 214 EGFR-mutant); both had subclonal *KRAS* mutations (low variant allele frequencies) acquired after EGFR-TKI therapy. No patient was treated simultaneously targeting two actionable alterations.

**Detail:** The rarity of *EGFR*/*KRAS* co-occurrence confirms near-universal mutual exclusivity. The two co-occurring cases had *KRAS* at lower VAF than *EGFR*, consistent with subclonal KRAS-mutant populations emerging during TKI therapy. The frequent co-occurrence of *EGFR* with *PIK3CA* raises the question of whether PI3K pathway co-activation drives EGFR-TKI resistance. Nine of 17 *PIK3CA*-mutant patients with no higher-level driver had a co-occurring *KRAS* mutation (Level 4), suggesting PIK3CA may be a passenger rather than driver in KRAS-mutant context. The clinical challenge of co-occurring actionable alterations — which drug to target first — remains unresolved.

### F5: ERRFI1 (MIG6) loss identified as a novel mechanism of EGFR pathway activation in oncogene-negative LUAD

**Finding:** One UMD patient without an *EGFR* mutation had clinical benefit from erlotinib. Post-progression biopsy revealed an uncharacterized *ERRFI1* (ERBB receptor feedback inhibitor 1, MIG6) A143D missense mutation as the sole non-synonymous mutation. *ERRFI1* loss-of-function causes hyperactivation of EGFR and persistent MAPK signaling. *ERRFI1* mutations were identified in 6 (0.7%) patients in the entire cohort; in 4 cases co-occurring with EGFR or KRAS mutations.

**Detail:** Mouse models with *Errfi1* loss show tumor acceleration in *EGFR*-mutant backgrounds and gefitinib responsiveness, supporting the hypothesis that *ERRFI1* loss alone may confer EGFR pathway activation. This finding illustrates the capacity of broad MSK-IMPACT profiling to nominate rare but mechanistically plausible drivers that would be missed by standard hotspot testing. While this represents a single case report within the dataset, it highlights how non-hotspot alterations in negative-feedback regulators can create therapeutic vulnerabilities analogous to established oncogene drivers.

## Open Questions

- What proportion of KRAS-mutant LUAD patients (the largest oncogene-defined subset, with low matched-therapy use at the time of this study) benefit from now-approved KRAS G12C inhibitors (sotorasib, adagrasib)?
- Do co-occurring *KEAP1* mutations in *KRAS*-mutant LUAD predict resistance to KRAS G12C inhibitors, as suggested by later studies?
- Can *STK11*-altered LUAD be targeted directly (e.g., STE20-kinase inhibitors) or serve as a predictive biomarker for STK11/KEAP1-directed clinical trials?
- Does simultaneous targeting of two co-occurring actionable alterations (e.g., EGFR + PIK3CA) improve outcomes over sequential single-target approaches?
- What fraction of the UMD (oncogene-negative) cohort would be reclassified as actionable with genome-wide sequencing or newer OncoKB evidence levels?
- Are *ERRFI1* loss-of-function mutations in NSCLC functionally equivalent to sensitizing *EGFR* mutations for EGFR-TKI therapy, and can this be validated prospectively?

## Limitations

- Retrospective clinical outcome data; matched therapy use reflects clinical decisions made over a ~3-year period with rapidly evolving drug availability (some Level 2A agents not yet approved)
- No randomized comparison arm — observed clinical benefit rates include patient selection bias
- MSK-IMPACT panel covers >300 genes but not whole genome; some non-coding drivers (TERT promoter, intergenic fusions) not captured
- EGFR T790M prevalence inflated by referral bias to MSK for T790M-directed trials; not representative of general LUAD population
- UMD cohort analysis is hypothesis-generating; STK11/KEAP1 as oncogenic drivers requires functional validation and clinical trial testing
- Co-occurring alteration analysis limited to the MSK-IMPACT gene panel; complex rearrangements and copy number interplay not fully characterized
- No systematic collection of treatment toxicity data; clinical benefit binary (benefit/no benefit) rather than quantitative (PFS, OS)
