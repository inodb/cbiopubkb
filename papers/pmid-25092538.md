## Summary

MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing of 109 high-grade urothelial carcinoma of the bladder (UCB) tumors with matched germline identifies genomic predictors of recurrence-free survival (RFS) and cancer-specific survival (CSS) after radical cystectomy (RC). PIK3CA recurrent missense mutations associate with significantly improved RFS (HR=0.35, p=0.014) and CSS (HR=0.35, p=0.040), remaining significant after adjustment for pT and pN stage (HR=0.39, p=0.032). CDKN2A alterations independently associate with markedly worse RFS (HR=5.76, p<0.001) and CSS (HR=2.94, p=0.029). TP53 mutations (57%) are associated with extravesical and node-positive disease but lose independent prognostic significance after stage adjustment. Chromatin modifying gene (CMG) mutations are ubiquitous (83%) but not associated with outcomes. Data available in `blca_mskcc_solit_2014` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: PIK3CA recurrent missense mutations independently associate with improved RFS and CSS

**Finding:** Recurrent missense PIK3CA mutations (21% of cases, primarily activating helical domain mutations) are associated with significantly improved RFS (HR=0.35, p=0.014) and CSS (HR=0.35, p=0.040). The association with RFS persists in multivariable analysis controlling for pT and pN stage (HR=0.39, p=0.032). Analyzing PI3K/AKT pathway alterations as a group yields even stronger associations (RFS HR=0.34, p=0.006; CSS HR=0.29, p=0.01).

**Detail:** PI3K/AKT pathway alterations were found in 38 patients (35%), including PIK3CA mutations (21%), PTEN loss (6%), AKT1 mutations (2%), and TSC1 mutations (6%). Despite the favorable prognosis, patients with PIK3CA-mutant tumors still had 44% recurrence at 5 years, supporting the rationale for adjuvant trials of selective PI3K inhibitors. A similar favorable association between PIK3CA mutations and survival has been observed in breast cancer.

**Genes / cancer types:** PIK3CA, PTEN, AKT1, TSC1 · High-grade urothelial carcinoma of the bladder

### F2: CDKN2A alterations are independent predictors of poor RFS and CSS

**Finding:** CDKN2A alterations associate with markedly worse RFS (HR=5.76, p<0.001) and CSS (HR=2.94, p=0.029) in multivariable analyses, remaining significant after adjustment for pT and pN stage. This is the strongest genomic predictor of poor outcome identified in this cohort.

**Detail:** CDKN2A alterations regulate entry into S phase of the cell cycle. The cell cycle regulation pathway (including CDKN2A, RB1, CCND1) was altered in 46% of patients. The significantly worse outcome in CDKN2A-altered patients provides rationale for evaluating selective CDK4/6 inhibitors in this genomic subgroup. The association with outcome was independent of stage, suggesting a distinct biological mechanism beyond advanced disease at presentation.

### F3: TP53 mutations (57%) associate with advanced disease but not independently with outcomes

**Finding:** TP53 mutations are the most common alteration (57%), significantly more frequent in extravesical disease (69% vs. 32%, p=0.005) and lymph node-positive disease (77% vs. 56%, p=0.025). In univariable analysis, p53 pathway alterations associate with worse RFS (HR=1.92, p=0.048) and CSS (HR=2.94, p=0.013), but these associations are lost after adjustment for pT and pN stage.

**Detail:** TP53's association with worse outcomes is largely explained by its enrichment in locally advanced and node-positive disease. This suggests TP53 may drive aggressive locoregional disease rather than an independent mechanism of distant recurrence. The finding parallels observations in other solid tumors where TP53 mutations mark advanced stage but lose independent prognostic value after stage adjustment.

### F4: Chromatin modifying genes (CMGs) are ubiquitously mutated (83%) but not prognostic

**Finding:** CMG mutations occur in 83% of high-grade UCB patients — higher than previously reported, likely due to the deep sequencing approach (mean 579× coverage). However, no association between CMG mutations and RFS or CSS was found, suggesting CMG alterations may be necessary early events rather than drivers of clinical progression.

**Detail:** KDM6A was the most commonly mutated CMG (41%), with 41/45 mutations being truncating. ARID1A alterations (28%, enriched for truncating mutations) are mutually exclusive with SMARCA4 alterations, suggesting overlapping functionality. CREBBP (13%) and EP300 (15%) mutations are also mutually exclusive and predominantly truncating. Given ubiquity in nearly all UCB tumors, CMG mutations may represent a founder event in bladder carcinogenesis without further differentiation of aggressive phenotypes.

### F5: STAG2 mutations are not associated with clinical outcomes in this cohort

**Finding:** Unlike findings in prior studies (some showing worse survival, others improved survival with STAG2 loss), this study finds no association between STAG2 mutations and clinical outcomes after deep sequencing. STAG2, the cohesin subunit regulating sister chromatid cohesion, is validated using an orthogonal sequencing platform and shows high confirmation rate (99.3%) but no prognostic signal.

**Detail:** Prior studies of STAG2 in UCB produced contradictory results — one associating STAG2 alterations with worse survival, another with worse disease characteristics, and a third with improved outcomes. The lack of association here in a deeply sequenced cohort calls into question the prognostic value of STAG2 in high-grade UCB. The sample size limitation (n=89 for outcome analysis) may mask a real association.

## Open Questions

- Can the favorable prognostic signal of PIK3CA mutations in UCB be leveraged therapeutically with adjuvant PI3K inhibitors to reduce the 44% recurrence rate?
- Is the CDKN2A-driven poor prognosis targetable with CDK4/6 inhibitors, and should genomic stratification be incorporated into adjuvant trial design?
- Why do CMG mutations — present in 83% of high-grade UCB — fail to distinguish aggressive from indolent disease, and can specific CMG combinations carry prognostic signal?
- Can the cohort's findings be validated in larger UCB populations, including neoadjuvant chemotherapy-treated patients?
- What is the biological mechanism behind PIK3CA mutation conferring favorable outcomes when it encodes an oncogenic driver kinase?

## Limitations

- Single-institution retrospective study (n=109; n=89 for RC outcome analysis) — likely underpowered to detect associations for less common alterations
- Single-region biopsy per tumor; intratumoral heterogeneity may underestimate mutational diversity and influence outcome associations
- Targeted sequencing (MSK-IMPACT) captures only known cancer genes; structural variants, fusions, and less frequently mutated genes are not assessed
- DNA-based assay does not capture transcriptomic or epigenomic differences that may independently influence prognosis
- No neoadjuvant chemotherapy patients analyzed; findings may not generalize to the chemotherapy-treated population
