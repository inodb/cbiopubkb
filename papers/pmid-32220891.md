## Summary

This large clinical-grade sequencing study profiled 424 patients with metastatic castration-sensitive prostate cancer (mCSPC) using MSK-IMPACT to characterise the genomic underpinnings of clinical phenotypes (high-volume vs. low-volume disease; de-novo vs. recurrent metastases) and to identify pathway alterations associated with clinical outcomes. High-volume disease was associated with greater genomic instability (fraction genome altered) and enrichment of NOTCH, cell cycle, and epigenetic modifier pathway alterations. Alterations in *AR*, *TP53*, and the cell cycle and MYC pathways defined a poor-prognosis subgroup, while *SPOP* and WNT pathway alterations marked better prognosis. CDK12 alterations were enriched in de-novo metastatic disease. Data are available in cBioPortal as `prad_mcspc_mskcc_2020`.

## Key Findings

### F1: High-volume disease has higher genomic instability and inferior prognosis compared to low-volume disease

**Finding:** Patients with high-volume mCSPC (≥4 bone metastases or visceral metastases) developed castration resistance at 1.84× the rate (adjusted HR 1.84; 95% CI 1.40–2.41) and had 3.71× the mortality rate (adjusted HR 3.71; 95% CI 2.28–6.02) of low-volume patients. High-volume tumours had a significantly higher fraction genome altered (FGA; mean difference 4.6 percentage points; 95% CI 1.5–7.7), indicating greater chromosomal instability.

**Detail:** Median FGA was 32% (IQR 24–48%) across all 424 patients, reflecting the substantial copy-number complexity in mCSPC even before castration resistance. The association of FGA with disease volume but not timing of metastases suggests that chromosomal instability is a driver of metastatic burden rather than metastatic timing. Tumor mutation burden was low (median 2.6 mutations/Mb, IQR 1.8–4.4) across all subgroups, consistent with non-castration-resistant disease. Fifty percent of tumours (211/424) had at least one potentially actionable alteration annotated by OncoKB.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide copy number alteration burden · Metastatic castration-sensitive prostate cancer (mCSPC)

### F2: AR, TP53, cell cycle, and MYC pathway alterations define a poor-prognosis subgroup in mCSPC

**Finding:** Alterations in *AR*, *TP53*, the cell cycle pathway, and the MYC pathway were each associated with 1.6- to 5-fold higher rates of castration resistance and 2- to 4-fold higher mortality in multivariable models adjusting for all other pathways and clinical factors.

**Detail:** *AR* alterations were the single strongest predictor — tumours with AR alterations had 6–11 months shorter time to castration resistance over 36 months follow-up versus wild-type. Cell cycle pathway alterations (including CDK4/6 amplification, RB1 loss, CDKN2A deletion) were independently prognostic. TP53 alterations showed robust association with castration resistance progression but the association with overall survival was attenuated in multivariable models. MYC pathway alterations (predominantly MYC amplification on chromosome 8q) also independently predicted worse outcomes. These genomic risk factors stratify patients beyond clinical disease volume alone.

### F3: SPOP and WNT pathway alterations are associated with better prognosis in mCSPC

**Finding:** Alterations in *SPOP* and the WNT pathway were each associated with approximately 1.5-fold lower rates of castration resistance and better overall survival in multivariable models — indicating they mark a biologically distinct, more indolent subtype of mCSPC.

**Detail:** This finding is counterintuitive: WNT pathway alterations have been associated with worse outcomes in metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) in two smaller prior studies. In this mCSPC cohort, WNT pathway alterations (including CTNNB1 mutations and APC alterations) paradoxically correlate with better prognosis, suggesting that the prognostic impact of WNT signalling is disease-state dependent. SPOP mutations — which are enriched in a distinct genomic subtype of localised prostate cancer — similarly associate with better mCSPC prognosis, consistent with their association with higher AR pathway dependence and sensitivity to androgen-receptor targeted therapy. These findings have potential implications for treatment selection.

### F4: NOTCH, cell cycle, and epigenetic modifier pathways are enriched in high-volume metastatic disease

**Finding:** In high-volume mCSPC, NOTCH pathway alterations were 4.2 percentage points more common than in low-volume disease (95% CI 0.1–8.3); cell cycle and epigenetic modifier pathway alterations followed as the next-ranked candidates.

**Detail:** High-volume disease has long been recognised as a distinct poor-prognosis subgroup, historically without a genomic basis. This study identifies specific oncogenic pathway enrichments — most prominently NOTCH — as potential biological drivers of metastatic burden. NOTCH pathway alterations (primarily SPEN loss) have been proposed as determinants of docetaxel resistance, raising the possibility that NOTCH-altered, high-volume mCSPC patients might differ in their benefit from docetaxel-based intensification. No individual genes were significantly differentially altered by disease volume, indicating that pathway-level analysis is more informative than gene-level comparisons for clinical phenotype association in this setting.

### F5: CDK12 alterations are enriched in de-novo metastatic prostate cancer

**Finding:** *CDK12* alterations were 6.7 percentage points more common in de-novo metastatic disease compared to metastatic recurrences (95% CI 3.0–10.4; FDR 0.037) — the only gene-level finding to survive multiple-testing correction in the de-novo vs. recurrent metastasis comparison.

**Detail:** CDK12 encodes a cyclin-dependent kinase that regulates RNA polymerase II-mediated transcription elongation and DNA damage response. CDK12 loss causes a tandem-duplication genomic phenotype and focal copy-number gains, generating gene fusions and high neoantigen burden — a potential vulnerability for immunotherapy. The enrichment of CDK12 alterations in de-novo metastatic disease (which arises directly from high-grade, often Gleason 9–10 primaries) versus recurrent metastatic disease is consistent with CDK12 mutations marking an aggressively metastatic biology from the outset.

## Open Questions

- Does treatment intensification (e.g., abiraterone or enzalutamide added to ADT) differentially benefit patients based on AR, TP53, cell cycle, or SPOP alteration status in mCSPC?
- Why does WNT pathway alteration status appear to have opposite prognostic effects in mCSPC versus mCRPC — is this a disease-state transition effect or a cohort composition artefact?
- Do CDK12-altered de-novo metastatic tumours show increased response to immunotherapy (pembrolizumab) in the mCSPC setting, analogous to their proposed neoantigen-rich biology?
- Can the genomic risk stratification described here (AR + cell cycle + TP53 = high risk; SPOP + WNT = low risk) be prospectively validated in randomised clinical trial biomarker substudies (e.g., STAMPEDE, CHAARTED)?
- What is the clinical significance of the other 50% of tumours with PI3K, DNA repair, or RAS/MAPK alterations that showed null associations with prognosis in multivariable models?
- Are findings generalisable to non-academic, community-based mCSPC patient populations with different baseline demographics and treatment patterns?

## Limitations

- Single tertiary referral centre (MSK) cohort; subject to selection bias — only patients who underwent biopsy for clinical sequencing are included
- 88% of patients self-reported as white, limiting power to detect genomic differences by ancestry
- MSK-IMPACT is a targeted panel (341–468 genes), not whole-exome or whole-genome; rare mutations outside the panel are not captured
- Median follow-up of ~30 months; long-term survival associations may shift with extended follow-up
- De-novo vs. recurrent metastatic comparison is confounded by systematic differences in PSA at diagnosis, time from diagnosis, and ADT exposure — not fully correctable
- Pathway groupings are predefined (from Sanchez-Vega et al. 2018), which may mask individual-gene effects or over-aggregate biologically heterogeneous pathway alterations
