## Summary

Tang et al. developed a hybrid single-cell approach—combining brief clonal expansion with matched DNA/RNA sequencing—to genotype 133 individual melanocytes from 19 clinically normal skin sites across 6 donors. They found that UV-induced mutations dominate the mutational landscape of sun-exposed melanocytes, with average burdens of 7.9 mut/Mb (range <0.82–32.3 mut/Mb), and that intermittently sun-exposed skin accumulates more mutations than chronically sun-exposed skin. Pathogenic driver mutations (BRAF non-V600E, NRAS, NF1, RASA2, MAP2K1, and others) were detected in normal melanocytes, particularly near skin cancers, consistent with subclinical field cancerization. Phylogenetic analysis revealed that melanocytes spread through skin as clonally related fields invisible to the naked eye, offering a model for the de novo appearance of melanoma without a precursor nevus.

## Key Findings

### F1: UV Mutation Burden Varies Dramatically by Sun Exposure Pattern and Anatomic Site

**Finding:** Melanocytes from sun-shielded skin had far fewer mutations and negligible UV signature, while sun-exposed melanocytes were dominated by signature 7 (UV-induced C>T transitions). Counterintuitively, melanocytes from intermittently sun-exposed sites (back, limbs) had significantly higher mutation burdens than those from chronically sun-exposed sites (face), mirroring the anatomic distribution of melanoma incidence.

**Detail:** The mean mutation burden across all melanocytes was 7.9 mut/Mb (range <0.82–32.3 mut/Mb). ANOVA with linear mixed-effects models controlling for donor confirmed that sun exposure pattern significantly affects mutation burden (p = 4.43×10⁻⁴). Pairwise comparisons showed intermittently sun-exposed > chronically sun-exposed > sun-shielded. Sun-shielded melanocytes displayed minimal UV signature, while sun-exposed cells showed a pattern closely matching SBS signature 7 (UV-radiation). The back/limb vs. face paradox may reflect differences in mutation rate, DNA repair efficiency, or melanocyte turnover at those sites.

**Genes / cancer types:** `UV-SBS7` · Melanoma / normal skin

### F2: Elevated Mutation Burden in Melanocytes Adjacent to Skin Cancers

**Finding:** Melanocytes from donors with adjacent skin cancers showed markedly higher mutation burdens compared to those from cancer-free donors, demonstrating that the mutational load of normal perilesional skin reflects cumulative UV damage and skin cancer risk.

**Detail:** Donors 6 and 13, both with adjacent skin cancers, had melanocyte mutation burdens where more than half of individual cells exceeded the TCGA melanoma median. One paired donor-melanoma comparison showed comparable mutation burden between the tumor (bulk WGS) and its surrounding normal melanocytes, with no overlapping mutations—implying the tumor had not spread beyond excision margins. This contrasts with colorectal cancer, which exceeds surrounding normal tissue mutation burdens. The findings suggest that perilesional mutation burden could serve as a biomarker for cumulative UV damage and melanoma risk.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `MDM2` · Melanoma / normal skin (peritumoral)

### F3: Pathogenic MAPK Pathway Mutations Are Common in Normal Melanocytes

**Finding:** Twenty-nine pathogenic mutations across multiple oncogenic pathways were identified in 24 of 133 normal melanocytes, with MAPK pathway mutations predominating—including gain-of-function BRAF (non-V600E), NRAS Q61L, and MAP2K1 E203K, as well as loss-of-function mutations in NF1, CBL, and RASA2.

**Detail:** Notably, BRAF V600E—the most common MAPK mutation in melanocytic neoplasms and low-CSD melanomas—was absent; instead, weaker activators such as BRAF G466R and D594G were observed, consistent with their known biology as impaired kinase alleles requiring RAS activity to signal. The driver mutation spectrum mirrors high-CSD melanoma rather than low-CSD/nevus-associated melanoma, supporting a model in which normal melanocytes bearing weak MAPK mutations are poised to progress to high-CSD melanoma upon acquisition of secondary hits. CDKN2A, ARID2, PTEN, and DDX3X mutations were also found, suggesting that chromatin and cell-cycle alterations may cooperate with MAPK activation. TERT promoter mutations—prominent in overt melanoma—were conspicuously absent, suggesting they confer little selective advantage outside neoplastic contexts.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `NRAS`, `NF1`, `RASA2`, `MAP2K1`, `CBL`, `CDKN2A`, `ARID2`, `PTEN`, `DDX3X` · Melanoma / normal skin

### F4: Melanocytes Spread as Clonally Related Fields in Normal Skin

**Finding:** Phylogenetic analysis revealed nine pairs or trios of related melanocytes sharing somatic mutations, demonstrating that melanocytes can persist as clonal fields spread across macroscopically normal skin—invisible to the naked eye and predating clinical lesion formation.

**Detail:** Shared mutations in related cell pairs formed the trunk of phylogenetic trees, with private UV-signature mutations on individual branches, ruling out tissue culture artifacts (tissue culture rate: ~0.045 mut/Mb per 7 days vs. melanocyte mutation burdens orders of magnitude higher). Four of the nine clonal sets had a pathogenic mutation in the trunk, implicating that driver mutation in field establishment. The remaining fields may have formed through neutral drift or via undetected drivers. UV-induced mutations were detectable on both trunk and branches, confirming that field formation occurred in UV-exposed skin, not in culture. This clonal field model provides a mechanistic explanation for how melanomas can appear de novo without a visible precursor nevus.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `NF1`, `RASA2` (trunk mutations in clonal sets) · Melanoma / normal skin

### F5: Copy Number Alterations Are Rare in Normal Melanocytes Except Age-Related Sex Chromosome Loss

**Finding:** Autosomal copy number alterations were uncommon in normal melanocytes, consistent with the view that chromosomal instability is a late event in melanoma evolution; however, recurrent loss of the Y chromosome and the inactive X chromosome was frequently observed, reflecting a generalized aging phenomenon.

**Detail:** Copy number profiles across all 133 melanocytes showed only rare autosomal gains or losses, unlike overt melanomas which commonly harbor gains of 1q, 6p, 7, 8q and losses of 6q, 9p, 10q. Loss of the inactive X (in females) and mosaic Y loss (in males) were the most recurrent alterations, paralleling findings in normal aging blood. This rarity of autosomal copy number instability in pre-neoplastic melanocytes supports multi-step models in which chromosomal alterations occur after initial driver point mutations. CNVkit was used with matched DNA/RNA data and allelic imbalance confirmation to minimize false positives from amplification artifacts.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDKN2A` (9p loss absent), sex chromosomes · Melanoma / normal skin

### F6: Intra-Site Mutation Burden Heterogeneity Correlates with MDM2 Expression

**Finding:** Melanocytes from the same ~3 cm² skin patch showed mutation burdens varying by orders of magnitude, and differential expression analysis implicated MDM2 overexpression as a marker of high-burden cells, suggesting heterogeneity in p53-mediated DNA damage responses among co-located melanocytes.

**Detail:** Differential expression analysis (DESeq2) identified MDM2 as significantly overexpressed in melanocytes with elevated mutation burdens. MDM2 promotes rapid p53 degradation, raising the possibility that some melanocytes have reduced p53 activity, impairing DNA repair or damage-induced apoptosis and allowing greater mutation accumulation. An alternative explanation is variation in melanocyte residence times—low-burden melanocytes may have originated from or recently transited through UV-shielded niches such as hair follicles, analogous to repigmentation seen in vitiligo. Similar within-site heterogeneity was concurrently reported in bronchial epithelial cells of ex-smokers, suggesting this is a general feature of carcinogen-exposed normal epithelia.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MDM2`, `TP53` · Melanoma / normal skin

## Open Questions
- Do chronically sun-exposed sites (face) genuinely have lower mutation rates, or do melanocytes there turn over more rapidly, selecting for lower-burden cells?
- What fraction of normal skin melanocytes harbor pathogenic mutations, and how does this fraction change with age and UV exposure history?
- Do BRAF non-V600E and other weak MAPK mutations detected in normal melanocytes progress to melanoma, or do they represent evolutionary dead ends?
- How do clonal melanocyte fields interact with neighboring keratinocyte clones (TP53-mutant patches), and do these interactions shape melanoma risk?
- Can mutation burden of normal perilesional melanocytes serve as a clinical biomarker for melanoma surveillance or risk stratification?
- What is the role of melanocyte stem cells in hair follicles in replenishing UV-damaged epidermal melanocyte populations?

## Limitations
- Small cohort (6 donors, all European ancestry, ages 63–85), limiting generalizability across ancestries, ages, and Fitzpatrick skin types.
- Targeted gene panel (509 genes) used for most cells; WES performed on only 48 cells, so genome-wide mutation burden estimates rely on extrapolation for the majority of cells.
- Brief tissue culture period (7–14 days) could introduce a small number of culture-derived mutations, though the authors estimated <0.09 mut/Mb per 2-week period—negligible relative to observed burdens.
- Only 2 donors had adjacent melanoma tissue; the comparison of perilesional normal vs. tumor mutation burden is underpowered.
- Clonal expansion selects for melanocytes capable of growth in culture (~38% colony-forming efficiency), potentially biasing towards a subset of the true in vivo melanocyte population.
- No functional validation of the identified pathogenic mutations in normal melanocytes to confirm their biological activity or proliferative advantage in vivo.
