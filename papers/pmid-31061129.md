## Summary

Integrative whole-exome sequencing, RNA-seq, and central histopathology review of 444 metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) tumors from 429 patients enrolled at seven SU2C-PCF consortium sites, linked with longitudinal clinical outcomes including overall survival and time on treatment with first-line androgen receptor signaling inhibitors (ARSIs: abiraterone or enzalutamide). The cohort spans the clinical spectrum of mCRPC (ARSI-naïve, exposed, and taxane-exposed). Key finding: RB1 genomic alteration is the single strongest predictor of poor overall survival (CPE=0.768, P=0.002) and short time on ARSI therapy (CPE=0.818, P<0.001) in multivariate analysis, with a relative risk of 3.31 for survival and 6.56 for time on ARSI. Notably, PI3K pathway alterations and DNA repair gene mutations (BRCA2, BRCA1, ATM) were not significantly associated with ARSI outcomes in tissue-based analysis. AR alterations associate with shorter ARSI treatment duration but not overall survival. AR-V7 tumor expression does not predict outcomes, in contrast to circulating tumor cell (CTC)-based studies. Data available in `prad_su2c_2019` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: RB1 alteration is the strongest genomic predictor of poor outcome in mCRPC

**Finding:** Among 18 recurrent genomic alterations and pathways tested, only RB1 alteration was significantly associated with both shorter overall survival (CPE=0.768, univariate log-rank P=0.002) and shorter time on first-line ARSI (CPE=0.818, P<0.001) in 128 ARSI-naïve patients. In multivariate analysis, RB1 wild-type patients had a 3.31× greater OS (95% CI 1.64–6.67) and 6.56× longer time on ARSI than RB1-altered patients. RB1 loss score (expression-based) and the Cell-Cycle Progression (CCP) score were also significantly associated with survival and time on ARSI.

**Detail:** RB1 alteration may be underestimated by homozygous deletion analysis alone: RB1 loss also occurs epigenetically, through structural tandem duplications, and focally (detectable by IHC), suggesting that the true frequency and prognostic impact is likely higher than detected by WES alone. RB1 and TP53 co-alteration is strongly associated with neuroendocrine (NE) or small-cell features (as expected from mouse models and prior studies), but RB1 emerged as the dominant prognostic marker even in multivariate analyses controlling for histology, AR status, and NE expression score. Aneuploid chromosomal status (vs. diploid) was also independently associated with worse OS and shorter time on ARSI.

### F2: AR alterations associate with ARSI treatment resistance but not overall survival

**Finding:** AR genomic alterations (amplification and mutations) were detected at higher frequency post-ARSI exposure vs ARSI-naïve tumors, consistent with selection for AR-pathway resistance. AR alteration was significantly associated with shorter time on first-line ARSI (CPE=0.651, P=0.005), but not with overall survival from start of ARSI (P=0.658). AR splice variant 7 (AR-V7) expression in tumor tissue was not significantly associated with either time on ARSI or overall survival, in contrast to CTC-based studies.

**Detail:** AR genomic alterations (including amplification and activating mutations in the ligand-binding domain) were associated with increased AR transcriptional output (AR expression score). AR-V7 levels were elevated in post-taxane and post-ARSI samples, consistent with therapy-induced upregulation, but the absolute AR-V7 level in tumor tissue did not predict outcomes regardless of cutpoint. This discordance with CTC-based AR-V7 data may reflect that CTCs represent the most aggressive circulating disease compartment, or that tissue vs. liquid biopsy compartments sample different aspects of AR-pathway biology.

### F3: PI3K pathway and DNA repair gene alterations are not associated with ARSI outcomes in tissue-based analysis

**Finding:** Contrary to prior cfDNA-based studies, neither PI3K pathway alterations (PIK3CA, PTEN, PIK3CB, PIK3R1, AKT1; ~20–30% of patients) nor DNA repair gene mutations in BRCA2, BRCA1, and ATM were significantly associated with overall survival or time on first-line ARSI in this tissue-based WES cohort (all log-rank P>0.05).

**Detail:** This contradicts a landmark cfDNA study showing BRCA2/1/ATM alterations associated with response to ARSIs and with survival. Potential explanations: cfDNA profiling may detect only patients with more aggressive/advanced disease (cfDNA detection bias); homozygous deletion of BRCA2 (on chromosome 13q, 16 Mb from RB1) may be imprecisely captured in WES vs cfDNA; and tissue biopsy may sample earlier disease than circulating DNA. SPOP mutations were associated with longer time on ARSI (consistent with SPOP being a favorable early-stage driver), though not with overall survival. CDK12 alterations co-occurred with CCND1 and CDK4 amplification (suggesting potential CDK4/6 inhibitor sensitivity), but outcomes analysis was not performed.

### F4: Neuroendocrine prostate cancer is defined by combined TP53/RB1 loss plus transcriptomic NE score, but RB1 loss drives outcomes

**Finding:** Histopathologic NE/small-cell features were found in 11.2% of tumors and were enriched post-ARSI exposure (10.5%) vs ARSI-naïve (2.3%). Integrative analysis identified three expression clusters: (1) high-AR/low-NE (86% adenocarcinoma, largest group); (2) intermediate NE/AR; (3) high-NE/low-AR (74% had histologic NE features). TP53/RB1 co-alteration strongly associated with high-NE transcriptional score, but concordance was imperfect. NE transcriptional score was not significantly associated with ARSI outcomes in univariate analysis.

**Detail:** Approximately 3% of tumors had low AR and low NE scores ("double-low"), all histologically adenocarcinoma, suggesting a distinct biology not yet characterized. Some discordant cases (high-NE score but adenocarcinoma histology) displayed distinct nuclear features (pleomorphism, irregular nuclear membrane), potentially representing transitional states from adenocarcinoma to NE differentiation. The incomplete concordance between histology, transcriptomics, and genomics highlights the complexity of NE classification in mCRPC and suggests that multidimensional assessment is needed for accurate diagnosis.

### F5: Landscape of genomic co-occurrence reveals synthetic vulnerabilities and confirms prostate cancer subtypes

**Finding:** The large dataset enabled systematic co-occurrence and mutual exclusivity analysis. ETS family genes (ERG, ETV1) are mutually exclusive with SPOP and FOXA1, confirming distinct molecular subtypes. ERG and PTEN co-occur (confirming oncogenic synergy). TP53 and RB1 co-occur (NE pathway). CDK12 alterations co-occur with CCND1 and CDK4 amplification (suggesting CDK4/6 inhibitor vulnerability). BRCA2 and RB1 (both on 13q) trended toward co-occurrence but did not reach significance. RB1 and AR alterations showed mutual exclusivity trend.

**Detail:** The CDK12–CDK4/CCND1 co-occurrence is particularly actionable: CDK12 loss-of-function leads to focal tandem duplications and immunogenic neoantigen production, while CDK4/CCND1 amplification adds CDK4/6 pathway activation — together suggesting potential benefit from combined immune checkpoint blockade and CDK4/6 inhibition. The mutual exclusivity of RB1 and AR alterations could reflect that RB1 loss supports AR-independent survival, providing an alternative bypass of androgen signaling dependency that does not require AR pathway activation per se.

## Open Questions

- Can RB1 loss be reliably detected by IHC, cfDNA, or structural variant analysis in routine clinical practice, and would a composite RB1-loss biomarker improve prognostic accuracy beyond WES-based homozygous deletion?
- Why does AR-V7 tumor tissue expression fail to predict ARSI outcomes while CTC-based AR-V7 detection does? Is the discordance biological (CTC selection) or technical (assay sensitivity)?
- Does the CDK12 alteration + CDK4/CCND1 co-occurrence define a subgroup that benefits from combined CDK4/6 and immune checkpoint inhibition — and can this be tested in a prospective clinical trial?
- How frequently does ARSI treatment induce de novo RB1 loss vs. selecting for pre-existing RB1-deficient subclones, and can serial biopsies track this transition?
- What is the biological mechanism linking RB1 loss to ARSI resistance — is it primarily through NE transdifferentiation, cell-cycle bypass, or AR-independent transcriptional programs?

## Limitations

- Outcome analysis restricted to 128 patients initiating first-line ARSI therapy — this cohort may underpower detection of associations for less common alterations
- WES-based homozygous deletion may underestimate RB1 loss frequency; epigenetic, structural, and focal mechanisms are not fully captured
- Cross-sectional tissue profiling: each patient contributed one biopsy, missing longitudinal genomic evolution and spatial heterogeneity
- Tissue biopsy (vs. cfDNA) may undersample disease burden in heavily pre-treated patients with bone-dominant metastases
- RNA-seq available for only 332 of 444 tumors (323 patients), limiting the statistical power for transcriptomic association analyses
- Multivariate analysis included only 128 patients; associations with less common alterations may be underpowered
