## Summary

Whole-genome sequencing of 21 salivary adenoid cystic carcinomas (ACC) with validation in 81 additional cases (total n=102) reveals that MYBL1, a MYB family transcription factor, is rearranged in 35% of t(6;9)-negative ACCs via t(8;9) translocations producing MYBL1-NFIB fusions (12% of all ACCs). All MYBL1 rearrangements (fusions and truncations) disrupt the C-terminal negative regulatory domain while preserving the DNA-binding and transactivation domains, mimicking the mechanism of MYB activation in t(6;9)-positive ACCs. MYB and MYBL1 expression is mutually exclusive across tumors. Three novel molecular subclasses are defined: t(6;9)/MYB-NFIB (53%), t(8;9)/MYBL1-NFIB (14%), and non-MYB/MYBL1 tumors enriched for NOTCH1/SPEN mutations (33%). Data linked to `acyc_mda_2015` in cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: MYBL1-NFIB fusions occur in 35% of t(6;9)-negative ACCs via t(8;9) translocations

**Finding:** MYBL1 rearrangements were found in 5/12 t(6;9)-negative tumors by WGS; validated by FISH in 10/36 (27.8%) t(6;9)-negative tumors in the validation set; 8 had confirmed MYBL1-NFIB fusions. Overall, MYBL1-NFIB fusions represent 12% (12/102) of all ACCs, and 25% (12/48) of all t(6;9)-negative cases.

**Detail:** MYBL1-NFIB breakpoints map to MYBL1 introns 8, 9, 14, or 15 fused with NFIB exon 11 or 12 — only the last two exons of NFIB are incorporated. Multiple fusion transcripts from alternative splicing within the same tumor were observed (e.g., AC11: exon 8→exon 11 and exon 8→exon 12). All fusions were confirmed by RT-PCR and Sanger sequencing. An MYBL1-YTHDF3 fusion from an intrachromosomal rearrangement was identified in two additional cases lacking t(8;9). Three further cases had MYBL1 truncations (exon 9 or 10 breakpoints) with high MYBL1 expression but no NFIB partner.

**Genes / cancer types:** MYBL1, NFIB, YTHDF3 · Salivary adenoid cystic carcinoma

### F2: All MYBL1 rearrangements truncate the C-terminal negative regulatory domain, mirroring MYB activation mechanism

**Finding:** Every MYBL1 alteration identified — fusions (MYBL1-NFIB, MYBL1-YTHDF3), truncations, and the MYBL1-RAD51B intrachromosomal rearrangement — retains the DNA-binding domain (DBD) and transactivation domain (TAD) but removes or disrupts the C-terminal negative regulatory domain (NRD). This is the same structural principle that activates MYB in t(6;9)-positive ACCs.

**Detail:** MYBL1 is the MYB family member most structurally similar to MYB; both share conserved DBD, TAD, and C-terminal NRD domains. The NRD suppresses transcriptional activity; its loss constitutively activates MYBL1. High MYBL1 mRNA and (in exon 14/15 fusions) nuclear MYBL1 protein were confirmed in all rearranged cases by qRT-PCR, Western blotting, and IHC. Normal salivary gland was uniformly MYBL1-negative. These findings position MYBL1 as a constitutively activated oncogenic transcription factor in a substantial subset of ACCs.

### F3: MYB and MYBL1 expression are mutually exclusive; tumors lacking both are enriched for NOTCH1/SPEN mutations

**Finding:** A consistent inverse (mutually exclusive) relationship between MYB and MYBL1 mRNA expression was observed across 102 ACCs: t(6;9)/MYB-NFIB tumors express high MYB and low MYBL1; t(8;9)/MYBL1-NFIB tumors express high MYBL1 and low MYB. Tumors lacking both MYB and MYBL1 rearrangements (~33%) showed variable MYB expression and were enriched for NOTCH1 and SPEN mutations.

**Detail:** NOTCH1 and SPEN mutations were exclusively found in non-MYB/MYBL1 tumors by WGS mutational analysis. Other exclusive mutations in this non-fusion group include MUC16. MYB fusion group exclusive mutations include MUC12 and TFAP4; MYBL1 fusion group had MAP10 and OTOF mutations. CREBBP was the only gene mutated in both MYB-NFIB and non-fusion groups. This tripartite molecular classification (MYB-driven, MYBL1-driven, NOTCH/other) has potential implications for molecular-guided therapy in ACC.

### F4: Fusion transcript length (short vs. long) correlates with distinct gene expression programs in ACC

**Finding:** Unsupervised clustering of 13 MYB/MYBL1-fused tumors by expression array separated "long fusions" (breakpoint after MYB/MYBL1 exon 11) from "short fusions" (exons 8 or 9). GSEA revealed 19 gene sets enriched in long fusions (predominantly RNA processing and translation regulation) and 5 in short fusions (tissue development).

**Detail:** The biological distinction between long and short fusions suggests that the length of retained MYB or MYBL1 C-terminal sequence (even though the NRD is truncated in all cases) modulates transcriptional target specificity. Long fusions retain more exons and may recruit different co-regulators. These expression differences may explain heterogeneity in clinical behavior observed within MYB/MYBL1-fusion-positive ACCs and have implications for future therapeutic targeting of these fusions.

**Genes / cancer types:** MYB, MYBL1, NFIB · Salivary adenoid cystic carcinoma

### F5: 5'-NFIB fusions with non-MYB/MYBL1 partners may upregulate MYB via genomic proximity

**Finding:** Three t(6;9)-positive/MYB-NFIB-fusion-negative tumors harbor fusions of the 5' end of NFIB (exons 1–2) with XRCC4, PTPRD/NKAIN2, and AIG1. All three also have translocated genomic segments placed upstream of the MYB locus on chr6, and all express high MYB — suggesting that enhancer hijacking by NFIB regulatory sequences may activate MYB transcription in trans.

**Detail:** NFIB breakpoints in 5'-NFIB fusions cluster in intron 2 (vs. intron 10 in canonical MYB-NFIB fusions), placing the strong NFIB regulatory elements upstream of NFIB exon 2. One tumor (AC09) confirmed NFIB exon 2–XRCC4 exon 4 fusion by RT-PCR. All three patients with 5'-NFIB fusions were male in their early forties with distant metastatic disease. This enhancer hijacking mechanism provides a functional explanation for MYB activation in t(6;9)-positive/MYB-NFIB-fusion-negative ACCs.

## Open Questions

- Do MYBL1-NFIB fusions share the same oncogenic transcriptional targets as MYB-NFIB fusions, or do they activate distinct gene expression programs that could be therapeutically differentiated?
- Can NOTCH pathway inhibitors (e.g., gamma secretase inhibitors) specifically benefit the ~33% of ACCs enriched for NOTCH1/SPEN mutations that lack MYB/MYBL1 rearrangements?
- Does fusion breakpoint position (long vs. short) predict clinical behavior (recurrence, metastasis) within MYB/MYBL1-positive ACC, warranting its inclusion in pathologic reporting?
- Is the MYB activation via 5'-NFIB enhancer hijacking a more general mechanism applicable to other cancers with NFIB structural variants?
- What is the functional role of MYBL1 in normal salivary gland progenitor cells, and does its expression pattern define the cell of origin for MYBL1-driven ACCs?

## Limitations

- WGS discovery cohort was small (n=21); validation was performed by FISH/RT-PCR rather than WGS, potentially missing complex rearrangements
- Tumor specimens were collected over 30 years (1981–2011); heterogeneous tissue handling and storage may affect nucleic acid quality for some analyses
- Microarray expression data (Illumina HT-12) available from Array Express (E-MTAB-1397) but not high-depth RNA-seq; fusion transcript diversity may be underestimated
- Clinical outcome analyses showed MYB alteration associated with worse survival vs. MYBL1, but cohort size limits statistical power and multivariate adjustment
- No functional validation of MYBL1 oncogenic activity in cell lines or animal models reported in this study
- The MYBL1 antibody used for WB/IHC recognizes the C-terminal domain — detects only exon 14/15 fusions, not exon 8/9 fusions, limiting protein-level validation of short fusions
