## Summary

IMmotion150 is the first randomized phase 2 study (n=305) of atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1) alone or combined with bevacizumab (anti-VEGF) vs. sunitinib in treatment-naive metastatic RCC. PFS was not significantly improved in the ITT population, but the PD-L1+ subgroup showed a promising HR of 0.64 for atezolizumab + bevacizumab. Key discovery: TMB and neoantigen burden were NOT associated with PFS in mRCC, in contrast to other cancers. Instead, angiogenesis (Angio), T-effector/IFN-γ (Teff), and myeloid inflammation gene expression signatures strongly and differentially predict outcomes across treatments. PBRM1 mutation associates with sunitinib benefit. Bevacizumab may overcome myeloid-mediated resistance to atezolizumab.

## Key Findings

### F1: TMB and neoantigen burden are NOT associated with immunotherapy benefit in mRCC

**Finding:** In contrast to melanoma, NSCLC, and bladder cancer, tumor mutation burden (TMB), tumor neoantigen burden (TNB), indel load, and frameshift mutation burden (FMB) show no association with PFS or objective response in any of the three treatment arms in mRCC.

**Detail:** WES of 201 matched tumor-normal pairs: median TMB 30 (range 1–309); median TNB 8 (range 0–79); median indel load 3; median FMB 2. No apparent association between TMB or TNB and CR/PR vs. SD/PD in sunitinib (p=0.06), atezolizumab + bevacizumab (p=0.14), or atezolizumab monotherapy (p=0.93) arms. No correlation between TMB and intratumoral T-effector gene signature. Median TMB notably high in RCC (30 expressed) due to VHL/PBRM1–driven biology, but without the mutation signature features that drive immunogenicity in TMB-high cancers of other types.

**Genes / cancer types:** TMB, TNB, FMB (genomic metrics) · Metastatic clear-cell RCC

### F2: Angiogenesis gene signature predicts sunitinib benefit; its absence (AngiogLow) predicts atezolizumab + bevacizumab advantage

**Finding:** High expression of an angiogenesis (Angio) gene signature is strongly associated with improved ORR and PFS in the sunitinib arm; AngiogLow tumors derive superior PFS from atezolizumab + bevacizumab versus sunitinib.

**Detail:** In the sunitinib arm, Angio^High vs. Angio^Low: ORR 46% vs. 9%; PFS HR 0.31 (95% CI 0.18–0.55). No significant PFS difference between atezolizumab + bevacizumab and sunitinib in Angio^High subgroup. In Angio^Low subgroup, atezolizumab + bevacizumab improved PFS vs. sunitinib (HR 0.59; 95% CI 0.35–0.98). Angio^High tumors had higher CD31 IHC staining (p=4.19×10⁻²¹), confirming biological correlation with vascular density.

**Genes / cancer types:** Angiogenesis gene signature (VEGF-inducible genes) · Metastatic clear-cell RCC

### F3: T-effector/IFN-γ signature predicts atezolizumab + bevacizumab benefit; myeloid inflammation modifies immunotherapy response

**Finding:** High T-effector (Teff) gene signature expression associates with improved PFS and ORR with atezolizumab + bevacizumab. High myeloid inflammation within Teff^High tumors abrogates atezolizumab monotherapy benefit but not atezolizumab + bevacizumab benefit.

**Detail:** In atezolizumab + bevacizumab arm: Teff^High vs. Teff^Low: ORR 49% vs. 16%; PFS HR 0.50 (95% CI 0.30–0.86). Across arms: atezolizumab + bevacizumab outperformed sunitinib in Teff^High tumors (HR 0.55; 95% CI 0.32–0.95). Myeloid^High (within Teff^High): atezolizumab monotherapy PFS HR 3.82 (95% CI 1.70–8.60) worse than Teff^High/Myeloid^Low; atezolizumab + bevacizumab improved PFS vs. monotherapy in Teff^High/Myeloid^High (HR 0.25; 95% CI 0.10–0.60) — suggesting bevacizumab overcomes myeloid-mediated resistance. Teff signature strongly correlated with PD-L1 IC expression by IHC (p=3.26×10⁻²⁰) and CD8A intratumoral protein (p=1.26×10⁻²⁸).

**Genes / cancer types:** T-effector/IFN-γ gene signature, myeloid inflammation signature (IL-6, CXCL8, prostaglandins) · Metastatic clear-cell RCC

### F4: PD-L1 IC expression enriches for atezolizumab benefit, especially in the combination arm

**Finding:** PD-L1 expression on ≥1% of tumor-infiltrating immune cells (IC) identifies a subgroup (51% of enrolled patients) where atezolizumab + bevacizumab demonstrates substantially improved PFS vs. sunitinib (HR 0.64; 95% CI 0.38–1.08; median 14.7 vs. 7.8 months).

**Detail:** ITT PFS HR for atezolizumab + bevacizumab vs. sunitinib: 1.00 (95% CI 0.69–1.45; p not significant). PD-L1+ subgroup PFS HR: 0.64 (95% CI 0.38–1.08; median 14.7 vs. 7.8 months). PD-L1+ ORR: 46% (atezolizumab + bevacizumab) vs. 27% (sunitinib). Atezolizumab monotherapy PD-L1+ PFS HR vs. sunitinib: 1.03 (not improved). A dose-response trend observed: increasing PD-L1 cutoffs (≥5%) associated with larger atezolizumab + bevacizumab advantage. PD-L1 IC expression strongly correlated with Teff gene signature, validating IHC as a surrogate.

**Genes / cancer types:** PD-L1 (CD274) IHC · Metastatic clear-cell RCC

### F5: PBRM1 mutations associate with sunitinib benefit; VHL mutations are not predictive

**Finding:** Loss-of-function *PBRM1* mutations (44% of patients) are associated with improved PFS in the sunitinib arm (HR 0.38; 95% CI 0.20–0.73) and worse PFS with atezolizumab monotherapy versus sunitinib. *VHL* mutation (62%) shows no association with PFS in any arm.

**Detail:** PBRM1 mutants in sunitinib: HR 0.38 (95% CI 0.20–0.73) vs. PBRM1 wild-type. Atezolizumab monotherapy in PBRM1 mutants: HR 2.49 (95% CI 1.26–4.91) worse than sunitinib. Atezolizumab + bevacizumab improved vs. atezolizumab monotherapy in PBRM1 mutants (HR 0.42; 95% CI 0.22–0.82). Mean Angio gene signature expression higher in both VHL and PBRM1 mutants vs. non-mutants, suggesting VHL/PBRM1 loss drives angiogenic phenotype. VHL mutation frequency: 62%; no predictive signal detected.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PBRM1`, `VHL` · Metastatic clear-cell RCC

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can the three-signature model (Angio/Teff/Myeloid) be validated in the phase 3 IMmotion151 dataset and does it outperform PD-L1 IHC for treatment selection?
- Does bevacizumab overcome myeloid-mediated resistance broadly across cancer types (e.g., NSCLC, hepatocellular carcinoma) by reducing myeloid-derived suppressor cells?
- What drives high TMB in RCC without immunogenicity? Are there specific mutation signatures (non-SBS7/10/15) that generate nonfunctional neoantigens in RCC?
- Do PBRM1-mutant RCC tumors have distinct chromatin accessibility or gene expression programs that explain differential sensitivity to VEGF-targeted therapy?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Phase 2 trial sized for HR estimation, not statistical significance; primary endpoints not formally hypothesis-tested; p values reported for descriptive purposes only, not adjusted for multiple comparisons
- Biomarker analyses are exploratory; signature cutoffs (median-based) not prespecified and require prospective validation
- Gene expression profiling available in 263/305 patients (86%); WES in 201/305 (66%); not all biomarker analyses had full dataset
- No pre-specified adjustment for multiple comparisons across biomarker analyses — high risk of false-positive findings
- PBRM1 association with sunitinib benefit not powered for definitive conclusions (subgroup analysis)
- Trial population predominantly clear-cell histology (92–96% per arm); findings may not apply to non-clear-cell RCC
