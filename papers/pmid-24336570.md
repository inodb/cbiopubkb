## Summary

WES of 23 paired initial and first-recurrent grade II gliomas (and multi-region sampling in select cases) reveals that recurrent tumors are genomically distinct from their initial counterparts in a majority of patients. IDH1 R132H is the only mutation shared across all patient pairs, confirming it as the earliest clonal initiating event in low-grade astrocytic gliomagenesis. In 43% of cases, ≥50% of initial-tumor mutations were absent at recurrence, indicating branched clonal evolution from an early progenitor. Among 10 patients treated with temozolomide (TMZ), 6 developed hypermutated recurrences (31.9–90.9 mut/Mb) bearing a distinct TMZ-induced C>T signature at CpC/CpT dinucleotides; all six underwent malignant progression to GBM. These hypermutated recurrences harbored new driver mutations in the RB (RB1, CDKN2A) and AKT-mTOR (PIK3CA, PTEN, MTOR) pathways, indicating a TMZ-driven alternative evolutionary route to high-grade glioma. Full mutation data are deposited in the European Genome-phenome Archive (EGAS00001000579). Corresponding cBioPortal study: `lgg_ucsf_2014`.

## Key Findings

### F1: IDH1 mutation is the sole universal shared event — confirming it as the initiating clonal driver

**Finding:** IDH1 R132H was the only somatic mutation shared between the initial and recurrent tumor in every patient pair studied. In the most extreme case (patient 17), the initial and recurrent tumors shared only IDH1 R132H despite otherwise completely divergent exomes, and convergent evolution produced distinct TP53 and ATRX mutations in each tumor. This implicates IDH1 as the founding clonal mutation in all low-grade astrocytic gliomas.

**Detail:** Average of 33 somatic coding mutations per initial tumor; average 54% were shared at recurrence. IDH1 presence in all tumors including geographically distinct multi-region samples anchors the phylogenetic trunk. Patient 17 shared only IDH1 plus three non-coding somatic mutations, ruling out independent origin. IDH1 as a therapeutic target is therefore relevant to both initial and recurrent disease.

**Genes / cancer types:** IDH1 · Low-grade glioma (astrocytoma), GBM

### F2: Branched clonal evolution is common — recurrences often emerge from early-stage progenitor cells

**Finding:** Clonal evolution patterns ranged from linear (≥75% shared mutations; 4 patients) to branched (≤25% shared; 3 patients) with a continuum in between. In at least 43% of cases, ≥50% of initial-tumor mutations were absent at recurrence. This means that the genotype of the initial diagnostic biopsy frequently does not represent the cell population seeding recurrence, complicating precision therapy based on initial-tumor genomics alone.

**Detail:** Patient 17 had only IDH1 shared; patient 04 had three sequential recurrences, with the second recurrence branching from the initial tumor earlier than the first — demonstrating that branched and linear evolution can alternate within a single patient. Multi-region sampling confirmed that intratumoral heterogeneity explained only ~7% of apparently private mutations, so divergence is real rather than sampling artifact. Driver mutations in SMARCA4 were private to initial or recurrent tumor in 6/7 patients, suggesting SMARCA4 confers advantage only in the context of pre-existing early drivers.

**Genes / cancer types:** IDH1, TP53, ATRX, SMARCA4 · Low-grade glioma

### F3: TMZ chemotherapy induces a distinct hypermutation signature in 60% of treated patients

**Finding:** 6 of 10 TMZ-treated patients developed hypermutated recurrent gliomas (31.9–90.9 mut/Mb), bearing a characteristic C>T/G>A transition signature at CpC and CpT dinucleotides — the hallmark of TMZ-induced alkylating mutagenesis. >98.7% of these C>T transitions were classified as TMZ-associated (absent in the pre-treatment initial tumor). All 6 TMZ-hypermutated recurrences also acquired mutations in MMR pathway genes (not present initially) and showed MGMT promoter methylation, consistent with a known resistance mechanism.

**Detail:** TMZ-exposed tumors ranged from 0.2–4.5 mut/Mb (baseline) to 31.9–90.9 mut/Mb at recurrence. Multi-region sampling of untreated initial tumors confirmed that TMZ-associated mutations were not pre-existing at frequencies >1.7%. The C>G fraction was notably lower than the TMZ-induced C>T peak, distinguishing this from other signatures. MMR gene mutations acquired at recurrence (not at initial diagnosis) provide the mechanistic link: TMZ → MMR inactivation → hypermutation.

**Genes / cancer types:** MGMT, MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 (MMR) · Low-grade glioma, GBM

### F4: TMZ-induced hypermutation drives RB and AKT-mTOR pathway alterations and malignant progression to GBM

**Finding:** In each of the 6 hypermutated recurrences, TMZ-associated mutations affected genes in the RB pathway (RB1 splice site c.2520+1G>A; CDKN2A P114L) and the AKT-mTOR pathway (PIK3CA E542K; PTEN A121T and G165R; MTOR S2215F — a constitutively activating mutation). All 6 hypermutated recurrences progressed histologically to GBM. No RB or AKT-mTOR pathway mutations were detected in the untreated initial tumors or in non-hypermutated recurrences, establishing these as TMZ-driven events.

**Detail:** RB1 splice mutation (c.2520+1G>A) was confirmed to cause aberrant splicing, premature termination, and loss of C-terminal growth-suppression domain by RNA-seq. CDKN2A P114L prevents CDK4 inhibition and cell-cycle arrest; it recurs in other cancers and in familial melanoma germlines. MTOR S2215F validated in vitro as constitutively activating; adjacent tumor regions showed heterogeneous mTORC1 activity correlating with MTOR mutant allele frequency. Non-hypermutated recurrences progressing to GBM also acquired RB/AKT-mTOR changes, but through CNAs rather than TMZ-induced point mutations.

**Genes / cancer types:** RB1, CDKN2A, PIK3CA, PTEN, MTOR · GBM, low-grade glioma

### F5: BRAF V600E can be a subclonal, spatially restricted event that does not expand under therapy

**Finding:** In patient 18, BRAF V600E was present in only 3 of 6 geographically distinct initial-tumor regions and was undetectable in all 7 recurrence samples by both exome sequencing and droplet digital PCR. This subclonal BRAF-mutant clone did not expand despite the growth advantage BRAF V600E typically confers, contrasting with clonal sweeps observed in CLL.

**Detail:** The failure of BRAF V600E to expand underscores that driver mutations in isolation do not guarantee outgrowth — genetic context, microenvironmental pressures, and adjuvant therapy all modulate clonal fitness. This finding has practical implications: BRAF V600E detected in an initial glioma biopsy may be a transient subclonal event absent in recurrence, rendering targeted BRAF inhibition at recurrence ineffective.

## Open Questions

- What fraction of low-grade glioma patients treated with TMZ develop hypermutation, and are there germline or somatic predictors (beyond MGMT methylation) of this risk?
- Can IDH1 inhibitors prevent the branched evolution that seeds recurrence from early-stage progenitor cells, given that IDH1 is the single universal shared mutation?
- Do the RB1 and AKT-mTOR pathway mutations found in TMZ-hypermutated GBMs predict sensitivity to CDK4/6 inhibitors or mTOR inhibitors, and would combination therapy after hypermutation be effective?
- Why do some TMZ-treated patients (4 of 10) not develop hypermutation? Is this explained by MGMT methylation status, TMZ dose, or germline MMR variation?
- Does intratumoral spatial heterogeneity at initial diagnosis predict the degree of genetic divergence at recurrence, and can multi-region biopsy strategies improve precision therapy planning?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=23 patient pairs); findings need validation in larger paired glioma cohorts
- Only one recurrence sampled per patient in most cases; second and third recurrences (one patient) show evolution dynamics can shift
- WES at 125× covers coding regions only; non-coding driver events (e.g., TERT promoter) not captured
- TMZ treatment details (doses, cycles) not fully standardized across patients; confounds dose-response inferences for hypermutation
- Multi-region sampling performed in only 3–4 patients; extent of intratumoral heterogeneity in the broader cohort may be underestimated
