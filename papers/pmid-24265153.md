## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing (WES) of FFPE tumors from 45 BRAF-V600 metastatic melanoma patients treated with vemurafenib or dabrafenib monotherapy — paired pre-treatment and post-resistance biopsies from a multi-institutional German DeCOG consortium. Genetic resistance mechanisms were identified in 23/45 patients (51%). Known mechanisms (NRAS mutations 17.8%, BRAF amplification 8.9%, MAP2K1 mutations 15.6%) were confirmed; new clinical resistance mechanisms discovered include MAP2K2 (MEK2) mutations and focal MITF amplification. MEK1/2 mutations confer cross-resistance to MEK inhibitors but remain sensitive to ERK inhibitors in vitro. Multiple resistance mechanisms co-exist within single resistant biopsies in 3 patients. PI3K pathway alterations (PIK3CA, PTEN, PIK3R1) are frequent but their contribution to resistance is context-dependent. This study establishes the "long tail" model of RAF inhibitor resistance and demonstrates FFPE WES feasibility for clinical resistance studies.

## Key Findings

### F1: 51% of RAF inhibitor-resistant melanomas harbor a genetic resistance mechanism — NRAS, BRAF amplification, and MAP2K1 mutations most common

**Finding:** Among 45 patients, genetic alterations in known or putative RAF inhibitor resistance genes were identified in 23 (51%). Known resistance mechanisms confirmed: NRAS mutations (17.8%; 7 at Q61 + 1 at T58), BRAF amplification (8.9%), MAP2K1/MEK1 mutations (15.6%). Acquired NRAS mutations occurred exclusively in patients on therapy >12 weeks (p=0.04), consistent with selective pressure requiring prolonged treatment. MAPK pathway or downstream effector alterations (NRAS, BRAF, MAP2K1, MAP2K2, MITF, NF1) accounted for 44.4% (20/45) of patients.

**Detail:** The resistance landscape is dominated by MAPK pathway reactivation through multiple non-overlapping mechanisms. MAP2K1 mutations did not universally preclude clinical response — some were identified in pre-treatment tumors from patients who experienced clinical benefit (25–33 weeks on therapy), consistent with prior reports that MEK1 mutations do not always cause RAF inhibitor resistance. Additional alterations with less clear resistance relationships: PI3K pathway (PIK3CA, PTEN, PIK3R1), and genetic correlates of early resistance (HOXD8 nonsense mutation, RAC1 P29S in 3/14 early-resistance patients). The 49% without identified genetic mechanism likely harbor non-genetic resistance (BRAF alternative splicing, COT overexpression, RTK upregulation, HGF secretion — undetectable by WES).

### F2: Novel MAP2K2 (MEK2) mutations confer clinical RAF inhibitor resistance and cross-resistance to MEK inhibitors — but remain ERK inhibitor sensitive

**Finding:** Four MAP2K2 mutations (V35M, L46F, C125S, N126D) were identified in drug-resistant melanoma specimens. All four conferred resistance to both RAF (dabrafenib) and MEK (trametinib) inhibitors in A375 BRAF-V600E melanoma cells (10–>100-fold increased GI50). MEK2-C125S (homologous to MEK1-C121S, a known cross-resistance allele) produced the most profound resistance (>100-fold GI50 change). Critically, all MEK2 mutants remained sensitive to ERK inhibitor (VRT11E), identifying ERK inhibition as a therapeutic strategy to overcome MEK-based resistance.

**Detail:** MEK2 forms heterodimers with MEK1 to drive ERK phosphorylation downstream of RAF. The C125S mutation disrupts the allosteric pocket targeted by MEK inhibitors, causing constitutive MEK activation resistant to both RAF and MEK inhibition. Sustained MEK and ERK phosphorylation was maintained in the presence of drug concentrations that fully inhibited pERK in parental A375 cells. This is the first clinical report of MAP2K2 mutations as de novo resistance mechanisms to clinical RAF inhibition, expanding the MEK1/MEK2 resistance landscape beyond prior preclinical predictions.

### F3: Focal MITF amplification is a newly discovered clinical resistance mechanism — restoring oncogenic transcriptional output downstream of MAPK

**Finding:** In one patient lacking other known resistance mechanisms, a focal MITF amplification was identified specifically in the relapsed tumor (absent in pre-treatment). MITF (microphthalmia-associated transcription factor) is a melanocyte lineage master regulator and oncogene. Forced MITF overexpression in BRAF-V600E melanoma cells (WM266.4) conferred resistance to RAF (PLX4720), MEK (AZD6244), and ERK (VRT11E) inhibitors, with 30–80 fold increased GI50 values for PLX4720 in two additional cell lines (SKMEL19, UACC62).

**Detail:** MITF activity is normally regulated by MAPK signaling in melanocytes. Genomic amplification creates constitutively elevated MITF expression that bypasses MAPK pathway inhibition by restoring the transcriptional output downstream of ERK. This represents a qualitatively distinct category of resistance — transcriptional effector resistance — that confers cross-resistance to RAF, MEK, AND ERK inhibitors simultaneously, unlike purely kinase-based resistance mechanisms. This finding directly inspired subsequent work demonstrating MITF as a multifunctional oncogene in melanoma (Johannessen et al., Nature, co-submitted with this paper).

### F4: Intra-tumor heterogeneity of resistance — multiple independent resistance mechanisms co-exist within single biopsies

**Finding:** In 3 of 45 patients, multiple independent resistance mechanisms were identified within the same resistant tumor biopsy: (1) Patient 41: NRAS Q61R + NRAS T58I (mutually exclusive reads, suggesting distinct subclones) + MEK1-V60E; (2) Patient 08: acquired NRAS-Q61K + focal BRAF amplification; (3) Patient 02: MEK2 mutation + BRAF amplification (no pre-treatment sample). This demonstrates that BRAF-V600E melanomas can simultaneously elaborate multiple resistance mechanisms, a finding that was among the first systematic demonstrations of polyclonal resistance in any solid tumor.

**Detail:** The two distinct NRAS mutations in Patient 41 (Q61R and T58I, on mutually exclusive reads spanning both loci) represent either two trans mutations in the same resistant cell population or two distinct subclonal resistant populations — i.e., divergent clonal evolution under RAF inhibitor pressure. T58I is homologous to a germline KRAS Noonan syndrome allele, implying gain-of-function. This polyclonal resistance within a single biopsy implies that parallel therapeutic strategies — not sequential monotherapy — will be needed to achieve durable control. Non-somatic mechanisms (BRAF alternative splicing, COT overexpression) were not assessable by WES, almost certainly underestimating the true heterogeneity.

### F5: PI3K pathway alterations are frequent in RAF inhibitor resistance but are context-dependent — BRAF+PI3K combination may be therapeutic in PTEN-null tumors

**Finding:** PI3K pathway alterations (PIK3CA, PTEN, PIK3R1) are observed in resistant tumors in the absence of MAPK resistance mechanisms in multiple patients (e.g., Patient 48: pre-treatment PTEN-Y86fs → new acquired PIK3CA-H1047R; Patient 36: PTEN deletion in resistant tumor). However, some patients with pre-treatment PTEN mutations responded clinically (Patient 58: 18 weeks on therapy; Patient 1: 66 weeks, partial response). Combined BRAF (PLX4720) + PI3K (GDC0941) inhibition showed synergistic activity specifically in PTEN-null (A2058) but not PTEN-WT (A375) cells, suggesting a PTEN-context-dependent therapeutic opportunity.

**Detail:** 9 of 11 patients with PTEN missense mutations had mutations in the phosphatase domain, predicted loss-of-function. The model proposed is that PI3K pathway activation may augment tumor cell survival during RAF inhibitor treatment, creating a permissive state in which cells elaborate additional (non-genetic or genetic) proliferative resistance mechanisms — rather than directly causing resistance itself. RAC1-P29S was found in 3/14 early-resistance patients (p=0.026 for association with early resistance vs. sustained response), identified as a gain-of-function oncogene in melanoma in an orthogonal discovery, suggesting RAC1 marks a partially resistant pre-existing population.

## Open Questions

- Do MEK1/MEK2 resistance mutations predict cross-resistance to MEK inhibitors in combination RAF+MEK regimens (dabrafenib+trametinib), or does the RAFi component maintain partial activity?
- Can ERK inhibitors overcome both kinase-based (MEK1/2 mutations) and transcription factor-based (MITF amplification) resistance mechanisms simultaneously?
- What is the true clinical incidence of polyclonal resistance (multiple co-existing mechanisms) when assessed by multi-region biopsies or liquid biopsy rather than single-site tissue sampling?
- Does the presence of a pre-treatment RAC1-P29S mutation warrant alternative upfront combination therapy to prevent early progression?
- Can PTEN-null status (by IHC or sequencing) be used prospectively to select patients for BRAF+PI3K inhibitor combination trials?
- How does the resistance landscape in BRAF-V600E melanoma compare between RAF monotherapy (vemurafenib, dabrafenib) and combination RAF+MEK (dabrafenib+trametinib) therapy?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=45) from multiple European institutions; underpowered for rare resistance gene discovery (statistical significance not reached for many individual alterations)
- FFPE-based WES: degraded DNA quality limits sensitivity for low-VAF subclonal mutations; non-somatic resistance mechanisms (BRAF alternative splicing, COT/MAP3K8 upregulation, HGF secretion, RTK upregulation) not detectable by WES
- No matched RNA-seq or proteomics — expression-level resistance mechanisms systematically missed
- Serial biopsies not always from the same tumor focus; spatial heterogeneity in multi-lesion disease may confound pre/post comparisons
- Some "pre-treatment" biopsies may have been obtained from untreated lesions in a patient already developing resistance elsewhere
- RAC1 and HOXD8 resistance associations are exploratory and require functional and larger-cohort validation
- PI3K pathway variant classification: many PTEN missense mutations lacked experimental validation at time of publication; their functional status is uncertain
- Cohort predates combination RAF+MEK inhibitor standard of care; findings may not fully translate to the current clinical context where dabrafenib+trametinib is first-line
