## Summary

The flagship paper of the ICGC/TCGA Pan-Cancer Analysis of Whole Genomes (PCAWG) Consortium reports the integrative analysis of 2,658 whole-cancer genomes across 38 tumor types, representing the largest WGS pan-cancer resource assembled. Using uniformly processed data aligned across 13 compute clouds, the study catalogs the full landscape of somatic and germline variation — SNVs, indels, structural variants, retrotranspositions, and non-coding drivers. Key findings include: cancer genomes carry an average of 4–5 driver mutations; 91% of tumors have at least one identified driver; non-coding drivers are present in 25% of tumors (TERT promoter dominant); chromothripsis is frequently an early event in tumor evolution; telomere maintenance mechanisms differ by tissue of origin; and ~5% of PCAWG tumors have no identifiable driver. The dataset and computational pipelines are publicly available and serve as a foundational reference for the cancer genomics field.

## Key Findings

### F1: Cancer genomes average 4–5 driver mutations; 91% of tumors have at least one identified driver, but ~5% remain unexplained

**Finding:** Integrating coding and non-coding driver discovery across 2,658 whole-cancer genomes, the PCAWG compendium identifies an average of 4.6 driver mutations per tumor (2.6 driver point mutations + structural variants). 91% of tumors had ≥1 identified driver. However, 181 tumors (5.3% of the cohort, elevated in chromophobe RCC at 44% and pancreatic neuroendocrine at 22%) had no identifiable driver events, indicating that driver discovery is incomplete and some cancers may be driven by distinct mechanisms including chromosomal aneuploidy patterns.

**Detail:** The 'rank-and-cut' approach and driver compendium identified probable driver vs. passenger mutations in each genomic element. TP53 was the most frequently mutated driver: 954 tumors with driver TP53 mutations, 77% with biallelic inactivation (96% via somatic point mutation + somatic deletion). Non-technical causes of missing drivers include: undiscovered tissue-specific drivers (e.g., SETD2 in group-4 medulloblastoma identified by restricting testing to missing-driver cases), chromosomal aneuploidy patterns sufficient for tumorigenesis (chromophobe RCC, PNET), and carcinogen-specific or epigenetic drivers. Technical causes include panel-of-normals contamination by clonal hematopoiesis (affects JAK2 V617F in myeloproliferative neoplasms) and GC-bias at TERT promoter hotspots (~50% of tumors had sufficient depth).

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `TERT`, `SETD2`, `JAK2` · Pan-cancer (38 tumor types)

**Data availability:** `available`
> pancan_pcawg_2020 contains full mutation, CNA, SV, and expression data. Driver annotations and passenger classifications are available in the downloadable mutation data tables. Individual tumor driver landscapes are queryable by gene and tumor type.

**Tool support:** `native`
> cBioPortal Study View shows driver frequency by tumor type. OncoPrint displays co-occurring drivers across the cohort. TERT promoter mutations are included where called. Rank-and-cut driver scores require external data from PCAWG portal.

### F2: Non-coding driver point mutations affect 25% of PCAWG tumors; TERT promoter alone accounts for 9% — but individual enhancers/promoters are infrequent targets

**Finding:** Using newly discovered and known non-coding driver elements (promoters, enhancers), only 13% (785/5,913) of driver point mutations were non-coding in PCAWG. Despite this low proportion among all drivers, 25% of tumors carry at least one putative non-coding driver, with TERT promoter mutations accounting for one third of all non-coding driver point mutations (237/785; 9% of PCAWG tumors). Individual non-TERT enhancers and promoters are only infrequent targets.

**Detail:** Non-coding drivers are identified in a companion paper (PCAWG Drivers and Functional Interpretation group). Key non-coding elements: TERT promoter (most prevalent, especially in bladder, melanoma, hepatocellular), and a minority of other recurrently mutated non-coding elements. Proportional contribution differs by tumor type: breast adenocarcinoma and ovary adenocarcinoma are dominated by driver SVs (6.4 and 5.8 per tumor vs. 2.2 and 1.9 point mutations); colorectal adenocarcinoma and mature B-cell lymphoma are point-mutation-dominant (7.4 and 6.0 point mutations vs. 2.4 and 2.2 SVs). This has implications for clinical sequencing panel design — panels not capturing SV drivers systematically miss major driver classes in breast and ovarian cancers.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TERT` · Pan-cancer; particularly melanoma, bladder (BLCA), hepatocellular carcinoma (LIHC), glioblastoma

**Data availability:** `available`
> TERT promoter mutations are included in pancan_pcawg_2020 where called. Non-coding driver annotations from companion papers are available through the ICGC Data Portal and PCAWG Scout.

**Tool support:** `api`
> TERT mutations are queryable natively in cBioPortal. Full non-coding driver annotations require API access to the PCAWG data portal or download from UCSC Xena (pcawg.xenahubs.net). cBioPortal native UI does not display non-coding elements beyond mutations in annotated gene regions.

### F3: Chromothripsis is widespread (present in many tumor types) and is frequently an early event in tumor evolution — particularly in acral melanoma

**Finding:** PCAWG systematically characterized three major clustered mutational processes — chromothripsis, kataegis, and chromoplexy — across the entire cohort. Chromothripsis (tens-to-hundreds of simultaneous DNA breaks on one or a few chromosomes with near-random fragment stitching) is frequent and, in acral melanoma, precedes most somatic point mutations and affects several cancer-associated genes simultaneously, establishing it as an early driver event rather than a late genomic catastrophe.

**Detail:** Chromothripsis was detected across diverse tumor types. In acral melanoma, the timing analysis (via relative ordering of mutation accumulation on phylogenetic branches) places chromothripsis events at or before the emergence of the founding clone. Chromoplexy (co-occurring double-stranded breaks on different chromosomes repaired in shuffled chains) is prominent in prostate cancer. Kataegis (locally clustered strand-biased nucleotide substitutions, typically APOBEC or other cytidine deaminase signatures) co-localizes with SVs in many tumor types. These catastrophic events challenge simple clonal models and have implications for interpreting copy-number landscapes in clinical sequencing.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer, with emphasis on acral melanoma, prostate cancer (chromoplexy), pancreatic adenocarcinoma (kataegis)

**Data availability:** `available`
> Structural variant data including chromothripsis classifications are available in pancan_pcawg_2020 and via the Chromothripsis Explorer portal (compbio.med.harvard.edu/chromothripsis). PCAWG Scout provides per-patient circos plots.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> Structural variants are included in cBioPortal's pancan_pcawg_2020 but chromothripsis classification requires external analysis. The Chromothripsis Explorer web portal (linked from PCAWG) provides direct visualization. cBioPortal SV display is limited to gene fusion context; segment-level chromothripsis patterns require UCSC Xena or PCAWG portals.

### F4: Germline pathogenic variants affect 17% of PCAWG tumors; 4.5% show biallelic inactivation confirming cancer predisposition pathway activation

**Finding:** 17% of PCAWG patients carried rare germline protein-truncating variants (PTVs) in cancer-predisposition genes, DNA-damage response genes, or somatic driver genes. Biallelic inactivation (germline PTV + somatic alteration on the second allele) was confirmed in 4.5% of all patients, with 81% of these affecting known cancer-predisposition genes (BRCA1, BRCA2, ATM). This provides WGS-level validation of the two-hit hypothesis across 38 tumor types.

**Detail:** Among tumors with driver mutations in TP53 (n = 954), 77% had both alleles mutated; 96% of biallelic TP53 cases combined a somatic point mutation with somatic deletion of the other allele. For BRCA1/BRCA2 biallelic inactivation: germline PTVs combined with somatic LOH or somatic mutation. Age at diagnosis is correlated with most classes of somatic mutation (~190 SNVs/year, *P* = 0.02; ~22 indels/year, *P* = 5×10⁻⁵; ~1.5 SVs/year, *P* < 2×10⁻¹⁶ by linear regression), suggesting a strong contribution of clock-like mutational processes.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRCA1`, `BRCA2`, `ATM`, `TP53`, `CDKN2A` · Pan-cancer

**Data availability:** `available`
> Germline variant data (controlled-tier) are available via ICGC Data Portal and dbGaP for TCGA-derived samples. Open-tier somatic mutation and CNA data are in pancan_pcawg_2020 in cBioPortal. Germline-somatic biallelic analyses require controlled data access.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Somatic mutation and CNA co-occurrence (for biallelic analysis) is queryable via cBioPortal API. Germline PTVs require controlled-tier access through EGA/ICGC Data Portal. Direct germline-somatic joint queries are not natively supported in cBioPortal UI.

### F5: Telomere maintenance mechanisms and mutational burden correlate with tissue of origin and patient age, defining distinct cancer biology categories

**Finding:** Cancers with abnormal telomere maintenance (alternative lengthening of telomeres, ALT, or TERT upregulation) often originate from tissues with low replicative activity. Somatic mutation burden scales with patient age (~190 SNVs/year), with different age slopes for indels and SVs. These findings suggest that the background mutational landscape is shaped by the interplay between tissue-specific replication rates, environmental exposures, and repair defect history.

**Detail:** Telomere length was called from WGS data. Mechanisms of preventing telomere attrition differ across cancer types: TERT promoter mutation (9% of PCAWG), TERT amplification, ALT (associated with ATRX/DAXX mutations, particularly in gliomas, sarcomas). Age correlation with different variant classes: SNVs *P* = 0.02, indels *P* = 5×10⁻⁵, SVs *P* < 2×10⁻¹⁶. Broad correlation in mutation burden across different variant classes per patient even after controlling for purity/ploidy, suggesting shared underlying biology (possibly global repair defects or carcinogen exposure acting on multiple DNA damage types).

**Genes / cancer types:** `TERT`, `ATRX`, `DAXX` · Pan-cancer; particularly glioma, sarcoma, pediatric cancers (ALT); melanoma, urothelial, HCC (TERT promoter)

**Data availability:** `available`
> TERT mutations and ATRX/DAXX mutations are queryable in pancan_pcawg_2020. Telomere length estimates are available via UCSC Xena and PCAWG Scout. ALT status (from telomere content analysis) requires external datasets from companion PCAWG papers.

**Tool support:** `api`
> TERT, ATRX, DAXX mutation queries are natively supported. Telomere length data requires UCSC Xena (pcawg.xenahubs.net) or companion paper data downloads. Age-at-diagnosis vs. mutation burden scatter plots can be built via cBioPortal API or UCSC Xena.

## Open Questions

1. What drives the ~5% of PCAWG tumors with no identifiable driver? Are chromosomal aneuploidy patterns sufficient, or are there yet-undiscovered driver elements (non-coding, epigenetic, structural)?
2. Can TERT promoter mutation calling be improved in clinical sequencing pipelines given the GC-content–related coverage bias that caused false-negatives in ~50% of PCAWG samples?
3. For which tumor types does SV-dominant (rather than point-mutation-dominant) driver architecture most critically limit current clinical panel performance?
4. How can the PCAWG WGS compendium best be integrated with clinical MSK-IMPACT/TCGA exome data in cBioPortal to enable hybrid queries?
5. What fraction of chromothripsis events are actionable — i.e., simultaneous amplification of targetable oncogenes and deletion of tumor suppressors in a single catastrophic event?

## Limitations

- WGS data are primarily from primary tumors; metastatic representation is limited (173 cases).
- Mean sequencing depth (38–60×) is adequate for clonal variants but may miss low-frequency subclonal events.
- TERT promoter hotspot coverage was insufficient in ~50% of cases (GC-content bias), leading to underestimation of TERT driver frequency.
- JAK2 V617F was missed in 35 myeloproliferative neoplasms due to panel-of-normals contamination by clonal hematopoiesis.
- Non-coding driver discovery is still incomplete; this study focuses on known and newly significant elements but the full regulatory driver landscape remains to be defined.
- Germline data are controlled-tier and not uniformly accessible, limiting broad replication of germline-somatic joint analyses.
