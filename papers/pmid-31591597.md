## Summary

A biorepository of 65 patient-derived rectal cancer organoids (tumoroids) was established from 41 patients with primary, metastatic, and recurrent disease using routine biopsy forceps (77% success rate). These tumoroids retained 92% of oncogenic mutations from parent tumors and recapitulated individual patient responses to 5-FU, FOLFOX, and radiation ex vivo, with ex vivo chemosensitivity AUC correlating with patient progression-free survival (Spearman r=0.86, p=0.024). An endoluminal mouse model further demonstrated patient-specific in vivo chemotherapy response and anatomically correct metastasis patterns.

## Key Findings

### F1: Tumoroids retain 92% of oncogenic mutations from primary tumors

**Finding:** RC tumoroids faithfully recapitulate the mutational landscape of parent tumors, with 92% concordance of likely oncogenic mutations and 77% clonal concordance.

**Detail:** 31 tumoroid lines sequenced by MSK-IMPACT. Concordance of oncogenic mutations: 92% (range 0.66–1.00). Clonal oncogenic mutations also clonal in tumoroids: 77%. Top mutations in tumoroids mirror 287-patient MSK RC cohort: APC, TP53, KRAS, FBXW7. 2/41 patients (5%) harbored MMR-deficient tumors—similar to the 5–6% rate observed clinically. RAS mutant tumors: 62% of patients.

**Genes / cancer types:** `APC` · `TP53` · `KRAS` · `FBXW7` · Rectal cancer

**Data availability:** available
> rectal_msk_2019 contains sequencing and clinical data from the cohort.

**Tool support:** native
> Mutation landscape and top mutated genes viewable in OncoPrint and Mutations tab of rectal_msk_2019.

### F2: Ex vivo chemosensitivity correlates with patient progression-free survival

**Finding:** Area under the dose-response curve for 5-FU and FOLFOX in tumoroids strongly correlates with clinical PFS of the corresponding patients.

**Detail:** 21 tumoroids tested with 5-FU and FOLFOX (2–3 independent experiments each). Heterogeneous dose-response curves observed across lines. For 7 patients with available PFS endpoint: Spearman r=0.86, p=0.024 for both 5-FU and FOLFOX AUC vs PFS. Lower AUC (more sensitive) associated with longer PFS.

**Genes / cancer types:** Rectal cancer

**Data availability:** available
> Drug response and PFS data linked to rectal_msk_2019 study.

**Tool support:** native
> Survival data and clinical variables queryable in rectal_msk_2019; drug response correlation requires external analysis.

### F3: Ex vivo radiation sensitivity predicts clinical endoscopic radiation response

**Finding:** Tumoroids with high ex vivo radiation resistance (≥75th percentile AUC) corresponded to patients with minimal or no clinical response to radiotherapy, while sensitive tumoroids came from clinical responders.

**Detail:** 19 tumoroids irradiated ex vivo; heterogeneous responses observed. RC-MSK-023 (resistant, ≥75th percentile) recurred after CRT + surgery. RC-MSK-004 and RC-MSK-003 (resistant) showed no/minimal endoscopic response. RC-MSK-001, RC-MSK-025, RC-MSK-031 (≤25th percentile, sensitive) showed ≥50% circumference reduction or near-complete clinical response.

**Genes / cancer types:** Rectal cancer

**Data availability:** partial
> Clinical response data available through rectal_msk_2019; ex vivo radiation data not directly in cBioPortal.

**Tool support:** custom
> Clinical outcome data queryable; radiation response requires external ex vivo assay data.

### F4: KRAS mutant tumoroids recapitulate cetuximab resistance

**Finding:** KRAS-mutant RC tumoroids are resistant to the anti-EGFR antibody cetuximab, while KRAS-WT tumoroids are sensitive, faithfully reproducing the clinical biomarker relationship.

**Detail:** Proof-of-principle experiment with cetuximab (not standard RC treatment). KRAS mutant tumoroids demonstrated resistance; KRAS WT tumoroids demonstrated sensitivity. Consistent with clinical trial data for KRAS status predicting EGFR inhibitor response. Confirms cell-autonomous, biologically relevant response in the tumoroid model.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS` · Rectal cancer

**Data availability:** available
> KRAS mutation status available in rectal_msk_2019.

**Tool support:** native

### F5: Endoluminal mouse model recapitulates invasion, metastasis, and chemotherapy response

**Finding:** Human RC tumoroids engrafted into mouse rectal mucosa develop invasive adenocarcinoma and metastases (lung/liver) matching patient-specific tropism, and respond differentially to 5-FU/FOLFOX mirroring clinical outcomes.

**Detail:** 200,000 cells injected endoluminally into NSG mice; 100% engraftment by hEpCAM, 20–100% gross tumor formation by experiment. Progression to intramucosal adenocarcinoma by 16 weeks; invasive cancer by 22–30 weeks. Lung metastases from RC-MSK-001 (patient had lung mets); liver mets from RC-MSK-002 (patient had liver mets). RC-MSK-008 (aggressive, OS=1.1 yr): no response to 5-FU or FOLFOX in vivo (p=0.38 and p=0.80). RC-MSK-002 (less aggressive, OS=3.3 yr): significant response to 5-FU (p=0.0043) and FOLFOX (p=0.0028).

**Genes / cancer types:** Rectal cancer

**Data availability:** partial
> Clinical outcomes in rectal_msk_2019; in vivo mouse data not in cBioPortal.

**Tool support:** custom
> In vivo response data not in cBioPortal; clinical OS data queryable in rectal_msk_2019.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can tumoroid-based ex vivo chemosensitivity screening be validated prospectively to guide treatment choice in neoadjuvant RC therapy?
- Do genomic features (e.g., KRAS mutation type, TP53 status) predict ex vivo or in vivo treatment response in RC tumoroids?
- Can the endoluminal model be used to study mechanisms of complete clinical response to total neoadjuvant therapy?
- Do MSI-H RC tumoroids show differential sensitivity to immunotherapy in ex vivo assays?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Small n for PFS correlation (n=7 patients with mature PFS); larger prospective validation needed
- Tumoroid establishment predominantly epithelial; tumor microenvironment (stroma, immune cells) not recapitulated
- Engraftment efficiency variable (20–100%); not all tumoroids produced visible gross tumors in every experiment
- Ex vivo and in vivo assays require 6–12 weeks — potentially too slow for acute clinical decision-making
- Study uses NSG immunocompromised mice; immune component of chemoradiation response not modeled
