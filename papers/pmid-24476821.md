## Summary

TCGA integrative analysis of 131 high-grade muscle-invasive urothelial bladder carcinomas, profiled by WES, low-pass WGS (n=114), RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, RPPA, and Illumina 450K DNA methylation arrays. Key findings: 32 significantly mutated genes including 9 novel to any cancer; chromatin-regulatory genes are mutated more frequently in bladder cancer than in any other TCGA cancer type (89% of tumors have ≥1 chromatin gene mutation); four mRNA expression subtypes are identified (papillary-like, basal/squamous-like, and two intermediate); actionable targets are present in 69% of tumors — 42% via the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway and 45% via RTK/MAPK (including ERBB2/FGFR3). Novel findings include FGFR3–TACC3 activating fusions, APOBEC-signature mutagenesis, and viral integration (HPV16 into BCL2L1). Full data in `blca_tcga_pub` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Highest chromatin-gene mutation burden of any epithelial cancer in TCGA

**Finding:** Mutations in chromatin-regulatory genes are significantly enriched in bladder cancer compared with all other TCGA cancers. 89% of tumors have ≥1 mutation in a histone-modifying or chromatin-remodeling gene; 41% have ≥2. MLL2/KMT2D (27%), KDM6A (12%), ARID1A (13%), and EP300 are each significantly mutated, predominantly with loss-of-function truncating events.

**Detail:** MLL2 (H3K4 methyltransferase) mutation is a novel finding; KDM6A (H3K27 demethylase) mutations are mutually exclusive with MLL2, suggesting redundant downstream effects or synthetic lethality. MLL3, MLL, CREBBP, CHD7, and SRCAP each reach ≥10% mutation rates but do not pass MutSig significance thresholds. Network analysis links chromatin gene mutations to large-scale changes in transcription factor activity (FOXA2, SP1) converging on MYC/MAX regulation — suggesting de-differentiation as a consequence of chromatin disruption. This chromatin vulnerability is proposed as a target for bromodomain inhibitors.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLL2 (KMT2D), KDM6A, ARID1A, EP300, CREBBP, CHD7 · Urothelial bladder carcinoma

### F2: Four mRNA expression subtypes with distinct molecular and therapeutic implications

**Finding:** RNA-seq clustering identifies four expression clusters: Cluster I (papillary-like, FGFR3-altered, low miR-99a/100, HER2/ESR2 high), Cluster II (luminal-like, urothelial differentiation markers), Cluster III (basal/squamous-like, KRT5/KRT14/EGFR high, similar to basal-like breast cancer and squamous lung/HNSCC), and Cluster IV (mixed). Cluster I and II show features similar to luminal A breast cancer; Cluster III resembles basal-like breast cancer.

**Detail:** Cluster I is enriched for papillary morphology (P=0.0002), FGFR3 mutations and FGFR3–TACC3 fusions (P=0.0007), and reduced expression of miR-99a/100 (which suppress FGFR3). Clusters I and II express high HER2 protein and elevated ESR2 (estrogen receptor β), suggesting potential for hormone-targeted therapy (tamoxifen/raloxifene). Cluster III expresses cancer stem-cell markers (KRT14, KRT5) and may represent a more aggressive, dedifferentiated subtype. These subtypes corroborate independently published molecular subtypes of bladder cancer and are reproducible in external datasets.

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR3, ERBB2, GATA3, FOXA1, KRT14, KRT5, EGFR, ESR2 · Urothelial bladder carcinoma

### F3: FGFR3–TACC3 activating fusions and recurrent ERBB2 alterations as therapeutic targets

**Finding:** Recurrent intrachromosomal FGFR3–TACC3 in-frame activating fusions are identified in 3 of 131 cases by WGS; all three are confirmed to lead to constitutive FGFR3 kinase activation via predicted auto-dimerization. ERBB2 is altered (amplification, mutation, or fusion) in 9% of tumors; mutations in ERBB3 occur in 6%. Collectively FGFR3 alterations (mutation + fusion) affect 17% of tumors.

**Detail:** FGFR3 fusion breakpoints at intron 16 or exon 17 of FGFR3 and intron 10 of TACC3; all produce a fusion protein containing FGFR3 kinase domain and TACC3 C-terminal coiled-coil domain. ERBB2 amplification rates in bladder cancer approach those in HER2+ breast cancer, but bladder cancer shows a higher fraction of point mutations vs. amplification. Four ERBB2 translocations with different fusion partners were also found. ERBB2 and ERBB3 are potential targets for approved HER-family kinase inhibitors and antibodies.

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR3, TACC3, ERBB2, ERBB3 · Urothelial bladder carcinoma

### F4: PI3K/AKT/mTOR and RTK/RAS pathways each altered in ~42–45% of tumors; therapeutic targets in 69%

**Finding:** Integrated analysis identifies actionable PI3K/AKT/mTOR alterations in 42% and RTK/MAPK alterations in 44–45% of tumors, with PI3K and RTK alterations generally mutually exclusive. Specific events: PIK3CA activating mutations (17%), TSC1/2 mutation/deletion (9%), AKT3 overexpression (10%); FGFR3 alterations (17%), EGFR amplification (9%), ERBB2 alterations (9%), ERBB3 mutations (6%).

**Detail:** PIK3CA mutations cluster near E545 (helical domain). TSC1 truncating mutations (8%), 6 of which are homozygous, suggest mTOR inhibitor sensitivity. AKT3 overexpression is copy-number independent. TP53 pathway is inactivated in 76% of cases (49% TP53 mutation + 9% MDM2 amplification + 29% MDM2 overexpression; mutually exclusive). Cell-cycle pathway (p53/Rb axis) is altered in 93% — CDKN2A deletion 47%, RB1 mutation with reduced expression, CCNE1/E2F3 amplification. CDK4/CDK6 inhibitors and mTOR inhibitors are among the therapeutic candidates.

**Genes / cancer types:** PIK3CA, TSC1, TSC2, AKT3, FGFR3, EGFR, ERBB2, ERBB3, TP53, MDM2, RB1, CDKN2A, CCNE1 · Urothelial bladder carcinoma

### F5: APOBEC mutagenesis, novel SMGs, and viral integration as disease mechanisms

**Finding:** 51% of mutations in bladder cancer are Tp*C→(T/G), the APOBEC mutation signature, with APOBEC3B highly expressed in all tumors — implicating APOBEC-driven mutagenesis as a major mutational process. Novel significantly mutated genes (not previously reported in any cancer) include CDKN1A (p21), RXRA, ELF3, KLF5, FOXQ1, RHOB, PAIP1, BTG2, ZFP36L1. HPV16 integration into BCL2L1 found in 1 case with concurrent BCL2L1 amplification and overexpression.

**Detail:** 9 genes are bladder-specific novel SMGs: CDKN1A (truncating, CDK inhibitor), RXRA (7 of 12 mutations at S427F/Y hotspot, activates adipogenesis/lipid genes), ELF3, KLF5, FOXQ1, RHOB, PAIP1, BTG2, ZFP36L1. ERCC2 mutations (12%) are predominantly deleterious missense with putative dominant-negative effects; ERCC2-mutant tumors have fewer C>G mutations and trend toward higher mutation rate. NFE2L2 mutations (8%, Neh2 domain) activate oxidative stress response; TXNIP mutations (7%) are mutually exclusive — both dysregulate redox metabolism. STAG2 (11%) is X-linked cohesin complex component (inactivating mutations).

**Genes / cancer types:** APOBEC3B, CDKN1A, RXRA, ERCC2, NFE2L2, TXNIP, STAG2, RHOB, ELF3 · Urothelial bladder carcinoma

## Open Questions

- Which chromatin-modifying gene mutations (MLL2, KDM6A, ARID1A) predict response to bromodomain inhibitors or other epigenetic therapies?
- Can the FGFR3–TACC3 fusion serve as a companion diagnostic for FGFR inhibitors (erdafitinib) in muscle-invasive disease beyond non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer?
- Do RXRA S427F/Y hotspot mutations activate a druggable transcriptional program (adipogenesis/lipid metabolism)?
- Is the basal/squamous Cluster III subtype intrinsically resistant to standard cisplatin-based chemotherapy, as suggested for basal-like breast cancer vs. hormone-driven luminal cancers?
- Does APOBEC3B expression predict TMB and immunotherapy response independently of MSI?

## Limitations

- Only high-grade muscle-invasive tumors studied; findings do not directly apply to low-grade non-invasive papillary bladder cancer (where FGFR3 mutations are far more common)
- 131 samples is relatively small for subtype-level analyses; some SMG findings may lack power
- No outcome data (survival) available at time of analysis
- Viral integration analysis performed on only a subset with WGS (n=114); viral contribution may be underestimated
- APOBEC signature characterization precedes more refined signature decomposition methods (SBS2/SBS13 in COSMIC v3)
