## Summary

Comprehensive genomic analysis of 99 transitional cell carcinomas (TCCs) of the bladder by concurrent whole-genome sequencing (for CNAs), whole-exome sequencing (for somatic mutations), and RNA-seq (for 42 cases) reveals that sister chromatid cohesion and segregation (SCCS) pathway genes are the most distinctive novel finding — disrupted in 32% of TCCs through mutations in STAG2 (11%), ESPL1 (6%), NIPBL (4%), SMC1A (3%), and SMC3 (2%), and FGFR3-TACC3 gene fusions (5%). Among novel single genes, STAG2 ranks first in mutational significance and its alteration status independently predicts worse overall survival in both superficial and invasive subtypes. STAG2 alterations are associated with elevated tumor aneuploidy, consistent with its role in chromatid cohesion. The study also confirms broad chromatin remodeling gene involvement (58% of tumors) and maps extensive copy-number alterations including CDKN2A/B deletion in 50%.

## Key Findings

### F1: STAG2 is the top novel significantly mutated gene in TCC — truncating mutations in 11% plus 5% deletions and 23% promoter hypermethylation, associated with worse survival

**Finding:** STAG2, encoding the stromal antigen 2 cohesin complex subunit on the X chromosome, ranks first among 13 newly identified mutated genes in TCC by mutational significance (P = 8×10⁻¹¹). Eleven of 99 cases (11%) harbor STAG2 mutations, of which 9/11 are truncating (frameshift indels, nonsense, or splice-site), plus 5 additional cases with genomic STAG2 deletions, and 7/30 (23%) additional cases with promoter hypermethylation. Kaplan-Meier analysis shows significantly worse overall survival for patients with any STAG2 somatic alteration (P < 0.001), independent of disease subtype.

**Detail:** STAG2 is an X-linked gene, meaning a single hit effectively creates hemizygous loss in males, facilitating inactivation without the need for biallelic alterations. STAG2 encodes a component of the cohesin complex, which is essential for holding sister chromatids together from S phase through mitosis. Loss of STAG2 causes chromatid cohesion defects and aneuploidy — a finding previously demonstrated in Ewing sarcoma, glioblastoma, and melanoma, and confirmed here in TCC: STAG2-altered tumors had significantly higher aneuploidy scores (P = 0.01) than STAG2 wild-type tumors. The prognosis finding held in both superficial and invasive TCC subtypes, suggesting it is an independent predictor.

### F2: SCCS pathway alterations affect 32% of TCCs — bladder cancer is the first solid tumor with this level of SCCS gene disruption

**Finding:** Integrating mutations in STAG2 (16% including mutations + deletions), ESPL1 (6%), NIPBL (4%), SMC1A (3%), and SMC3 (2%), and the recurrent FGFR3-TACC3 fusion (5%), a total of 32/99 TCCs (32%) carry genetic alterations affecting sister chromatid cohesion and segregation. Tumors with SCCS pathway alterations showed significantly higher aneuploidy scores (P = 0.01) compared to SCCS wild-type tumors, confirming functional consequences of these alterations.

**Detail:** The SCCS process ensures accurate chromosome segregation during mitosis. ESPL1 encodes separase, the protease that cleaves cohesin to allow chromatid separation at anaphase — its mutation could prevent timely cohesin cleavage, causing cohesion persistence and segregation defects. NIPBL (Nipped-B-Like Protein) loads cohesin onto chromatin, and SMC1A and SMC3 are core structural cohesin subunits. The convergence of mutations across multiple SCCS components strongly argues for a functional role in TCC tumorigenesis rather than coincidental passenger events. Prior to this study, only rare or low-frequency SCCS gene alterations had been seen in other cancer types, making TCC unique in the prominence of this pathway.

### F3: FGFR3-TACC3 gene fusion identified by RNA-seq in 5% of TCCs — recapitulates glioblastoma fusion and causes mitotic segregation defects

**Finding:** RNA-seq of 42 sequenced tumors identified a recurrent in-frame FGFR3::TACC3 fusion in 2 cases (B59-3 and B100; 2/42, 5%). TACC3 encodes a microtubule-associated protein critical for spindle stability, making FGFR3-TACC3 fusions a mechanism of FGFR kinase activation that simultaneously impairs spindle assembly — a doubly oncogenic rearrangement. Both tumors showed markedly elevated TACC3 expression (outlier by RNA-seq), driven by the FGFR3 promoter in the fused transcript.

**Detail:** FGFR3 (at 4p16.3) and TACC3 (70 kb away) are frequently co-amplified in TCC; the fusion event appears to drive TACC3 overexpression via the FGFR3 promoter rather than genomic amplification per se. The same FGFR3-TACC3 fusion was previously described in glioblastoma multiforme where it induces mitotic and chromosomal segregation defects and triggers aneuploidy. This fusion also connects FGFR3 (one of the most commonly mutated genes in non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer) to the SCCS theme of the study. A GWAS-identified SNP at the FGFR3/TACC3 locus also associates with bladder cancer susceptibility, further linking this locus to bladder tumorigenesis.

### F4: Chromatin remodeling genes altered in 58% of TCCs — KDM6A (UTX) and ARID1A most frequent among 57+ affected cases

**Finding:** At least 57 of 99 TCCs (58%) harbored somatic mutations in chromatin remodeling genes — the single most commonly affected functional category in this tumor type. The most frequently mutated chromatin genes include KDM6A/UTX and UTY (histone demethylases, 30%), ARID1A and ARID4A (chromatin remodelers, 17%), KMT2A/B/E (MLL family histone methyltransferases, 16%), EP300 and EP400 (histone acetyltransferases, 15%), and SMARCA4/SMARCAC1 (SWI/SNF complex, 7%).

**Detail:** The breadth of chromatin remodeling gene involvement confirms prior work from the same group (2011 paper identifying chromatin remodeling in TCC) and suggests that epigenetic dysregulation is a fundamental oncogenic mechanism in urothelial cancer. KDM6A (UTX) is among the most significant mutated genes across the 37 identified, and notably it is an X-linked gene (like STAG2), again highlighting the importance of hemizygous X-linked gene inactivation in urothelial carcinogenesis. The SWI/SNF complex genes SMARCA4 and SMARCAC1 are altered in 7%, paralleling the SWI/SNF involvement seen in other epithelial cancers.

### F5: CDKN2A/B deletion in 50%, cell cycle pathway altered in 86% of TCCs — defining breadth of cell cycle disruption in urothelial cancer

**Finding:** WGS-based CNA analysis identified a deletion at 9p21 containing CDKN2A and CDKN2B in 50 of 99 tumors (50%), the most common focal deletion in the cohort. When considering all cell cycle alterations together (G1/S checkpoint: RB1 loss, MYC/CCND1/CCNE1/E2F amplifications, CDKN2A/B deletion; G2/M checkpoint: TP53/ATM/BRCA1/BRCA2/ATR/CREBBP/EP300 mutations, MDM2 amplification), 86 of 99 TCCs (86%) harbored alterations in cell cycle genes.

**Detail:** The 86% cell cycle alteration rate is among the highest of any tumor type analyzed by comprehensive genomics. G1/S disruptions include amplifications of MYC, CCND1, CCNE1, E2F1, and E2F3 — identified as novel amplified targets in this study. Particularly notable was the recurrent amplification of DHFR (dihydrofolate reductase) at 5q in 14% of tumors, which is a direct target of methotrexate — a clinically used bladder cancer treatment — suggesting that DHFR amplification may be a mechanism of methotrexate resistance. The ubiquity of cell cycle disruption (86%) suggests this pathway is essentially obligate for TCC development, though the specific mechanisms vary widely.

## Open Questions

- Does STAG2 alteration status (by IHC loss, mutation, or deletion) serve as a reliable biomarker of poor prognosis in large prospective TCC cohorts?
- Do STAG2-mutant TCCs show differential response to spindle-targeting agents (taxanes, vinblastine) given their underlying cohesion defects?
- What is the functional mechanism by which ESPL1 mutations (6% of TCCs) contribute to tumorigenesis — gain vs. loss of separase activity?
- Can the FGFR3-TACC3 fusion define a subset of TCCs responsive to FGFR inhibitors (erdafitinib now FDA-approved for FGFR3-mutant bladder cancer)?
- Does the high chromatin remodeling gene mutation rate (58%) predict response to EZH2 inhibitors, HDAC inhibitors, or immune checkpoint blockade?
- Is DHFR amplification a resistance mechanism to methotrexate-containing regimens (MVAC) in TCC, and can DHFR status guide chemotherapy selection?

## Limitations

- WGS coverage of only ~4× mean haploid depth is insufficient for sensitive somatic SNV detection; WES was the primary mutation discovery platform, limiting structural variant resolution
- Chinese population cohort from a single consortium; results may not fully generalize to Western populations with different environmental exposures and genetic backgrounds
- RNA-seq performed on only 42 of 99 tumors; fusion transcript detection (FGFR3-TACC3 and others) may be incomplete across the cohort
- STAG2 methylation was assessed in only 30 cases by bisulfite sequencing; full prevalence of epigenetic STAG2 silencing across the cohort is unknown
- Clinical follow-up data and treatment details are limited; survival analyses rely on overall survival without progression-free survival or treatment-specific response data
- Low WGS coverage means complex structural rearrangements, chromoplexy, and small SVs are underdetected compared to high-coverage WGS
- No single-cell data to address intratumoral heterogeneity of SCCS gene alterations and their clonal dynamics
