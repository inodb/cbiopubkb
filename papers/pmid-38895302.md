## Summary

This preprint combines a large epidemiological case-control analysis (32,315 dermatology patients, Northwestern University) with single-cell exome sequencing of 182 melanocytes from normal skin of 11 tanning bed users versus 15 controls to characterize how indoor tanning shapes the mutational landscape of skin cells. Epidemiologically, tanning bed use was associated with 2× increased melanoma risk (adjusted OR 2.0, 95% CI 1.38–2.98), dose-dependent risk increase with number of sessions, and redistribution of melanomas to body sites with low cumulative sun damage (trunk, lower extremities). Molecularly, melanocytes from tanning bed users have higher mutation burdens, a higher proportion of melanocytes with pathogenic driver mutations, and enrichment of mutational signature 11 — a signature with C>T changes (pyrimidine downstream of mutant site) distinct from the canonical UV signature 7. The authors propose that indoor tanning mutagenizes a broader field of melanocytes than natural sunlight by exposing body areas (lower back, trunk) that receive comparatively little natural UV, thereby creating a large reservoir of premalignant cells.

## Key Findings

### F1: Tanning bed use confers 2× adjusted risk of melanoma with dose-dependent relationship

**Finding:** In a case-control study of 32,315 dermatology patients, tanning bed users had a melanoma incidence of 5.1% vs. 2.1% in non-users (p<0.0001). Multiple logistic regression controlling for age, family history of melanoma, and sunburn history showed an adjusted OR of 2.0 (95% CI 1.38–2.98). A dose-dependent relationship between number of tanning sessions and melanoma risk was statistically significant (p<0.0001).

**Detail:** Tanning cohort was enriched for female sex, sunburn history, heavy sun exposure history, and family history of melanoma. Multiple primary melanomas were significantly more common in tanning bed users. Melanomas in tanning bed users arose more frequently on body sites with low cumulative sun damage (trunk, lower back, legs) — anatomical sites intensely exposed during tanning sessions but not in daily sun exposure. These patterns are reminiscent of familial melanoma (CDKN2A carriers), where a large field of melanocytes is at elevated risk due to a shared germline "hit."

### F2: Melanocytes from tanning bed users carry higher somatic mutation burdens across all anatomic sites

**Finding:** Single-cell exome sequencing of 182 normal-appearing skin melanocytes demonstrated that tanning bed users have significantly higher mutation burdens than controls (p=0.0128 in mixed-effects model adjusting for anatomic site and subject). The difference was most pronounced on the lower back — a site exposed during tanning but receiving comparatively little natural sunlight — where biopsy-level mutation burdens were significantly elevated (p<0.05). The upper back trended higher but did not reach biopsy-level significance, likely due to confounding from high natural UV exposure at that site.

**Detail:** Young tanning bed users (mean age 43.6 years) had more mutations than cadaver donors nearly twice their age (mean 78.3 years), a particularly striking comparison given the expectation that mutation burden increases with age from natural UV. Both control cohorts (clinic-matched controls and cadaver controls) had lower mutation burdens than tanning users. The cell-level mixed-effects model confirms the cohort effect independent of anatomic site. This data supports the hypothesis that tanning bed radiation induces persistent DNA damage beyond what natural sunlight delivers to the same body sites.

### F3: Mutational signature 11 is enriched in melanocytes from tanning bed users

**Finding:** Signature 11 — characterized by C>T mutations at sites with a pyrimidine immediately downstream (in contrast to signature 7, which has a pyrimidine upstream) — was the only mutational signature to reach statistical significance between tanning and control cohorts (p=0.0405 Wilcoxon rank-sum for proportion; p=0.00813 Poisson test for fraction of cells positive). Signature 7 (canonical UV-induced C>T) was the dominant signature in both cohorts but did not differ significantly between them.

**Detail:** Signature 11 was originally attributed to temozolomide treatment but in vitro studies show temozolomide induces a different profile. In COSMIC v3.4, signature 11 is most prevalent in melanoma — suggesting it may be a UV-variant rather than drug-related. The authors propose that the unique UVA-enriched spectrum from tanning beds (10–15× more UVA than natural sunlight in the UVA range) may account for signature 11. Reactive oxygen species (ROS) signatures, which UVA can induce, were not observed — possibly because ROS-induced mutations are masked by the dominant UV burden or do not reach mutagenic thresholds in vivo. The specificity of signature 11 enrichment to tanning users provides molecular evidence that tanning bed radiation has qualitatively distinct mutagenic effects from natural sunlight.

### F4: Tanning bed users have more melanocytes with pathogenic driver mutations, enriched for NF1 and MAPK pathway genes

**Finding:** 40 pathogenic mutations were found in 23 unique melanocytes across all donors. Melanocytes from tanning bed users were significantly more likely to harbor a pathogenic driver mutation than controls (p<0.05, Poisson test). Melanocytes on the upper back (highest natural sun exposure) were more likely to have pathogenic mutations than lower back cells, consistent with natural UV being the dominant mutagenic force at that site. Most driver mutations affected the MAPK signaling pathway, with NF1 loss-of-function being especially prevalent.

**Detail:** Having a high fraction of melanocytes with pathogenic mutations creates a large pool of cells one step closer to malignant transformation — directly analogous to the "first hit" in familial melanoma (CDKN2A germline mutation). Some melanocytes shared subsets of mutations indicating phylogenetic clonal fields; biopsies with high mutation burdens were more likely to contain such fields. Four biopsies had ≥2 melanocytes with shared somatic mutations, demonstrating clonal expansion of mutagenized melanocytes within the skin. This supports the field cancerization model: tanning beds increase both the number of premalignant precursor cells and the geographic extent of skin at risk.

### F5: Indoor tanning exposes ~1.5× more body surface area than typical outdoor sun exposure, explaining anatomic melanoma redistribution

**Finding:** Body sites receiving low cumulative sun damage (lower back, abdomen, buttocks, upper thighs) constitute approximately 1.5× the surface area of heavily sun-exposed sites. Tanning beds expose all these areas simultaneously, creating a dramatically expanded "at-risk" field. Melanomas in tanning bed users were significantly more common on low-sun-damage sites vs. non-tanners (p<0.05). This anatomic redistribution is mechanistically explained by the expanded field of mutagenized melanocytes observed in the molecular data.

**Detail:** The WHO classifies melanomas arising on skin with high vs. low cumulative sun damage as distinct biological entities. Tanning-bed-induced melanomas disproportionately fall in the low-sun-damage category — sites that would otherwise have modest UV exposure. Multiple primary melanomas, which were more common in tanning users, are consistent with a broad field of premalignant cells. The study compares this to hereditary melanoma (CDKN2A germline carriers), where the large field effect drives multiple primary tumors at young ages. The public health implication: tanning beds approximately double the body surface area at high risk of melanoma.

## Open Questions

- What is the specific UV wavelength or blend responsible for signature 11 enrichment in tanning bed users? Can experimental UVA-dominant irradiation reproduce signature 11 in cell culture models?
- Do tanning-bed-associated melanomas respond differently to checkpoint immunotherapy or targeted MAPK therapies compared to solar-UV-induced melanomas, given their distinct mutational signature profiles?
- Is the clonal expansion of NF1-mutant melanocytes in normal skin a generalizable precursor state, and can it be detected non-invasively (e.g., via liquid biopsy or Raman spectroscopy)?
- What are the long-term consequences of tanning bed exposure on germline-predisposed individuals (CDKN2A, BAP1, TERT carriers)? Is the risk multiplicative?
- Can the molecular findings inform public policy — e.g., by providing regulators with specific mutagenic evidence to strengthen tanning bed bans for minors or general population restrictions?

## Limitations

- Preprint (bioRxiv); not yet peer-reviewed as of date of record.
- Small molecular cohort (11 tanning donors, 15 controls), limiting statistical power for rare signatures.
- Cadaver control group (n=6) is nearly twice the age of tanning users (78.3 vs. 43.6 years), confounding age-matched comparisons of mutation burden.
- Upper back comparisons at biopsy level did not reach significance, likely underpowered given high background natural UV at that site.
- Self-reported tanning histories introduce recall bias; extremity of tanning history (>50 sessions) limits generalizability to moderate users.
- Germline mutation status of donors not fully characterized; subtle germline predisposition could inflate driver mutation findings.
