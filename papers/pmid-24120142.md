## Summary

Brennan et al. present a comprehensive multi-platform somatic landscape of glioblastoma (GBM) from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), characterizing 543 patients with WES (n=291), WGS (n=42), RNA-seq (n=164), RPPA (n=214), and copy-number/methylation arrays. The study identifies 71 significantly mutated genes, characterizes complex EGFR structural variants, and establishes TERT promoter mutations as a telomerase reactivation mechanism. Key translational findings include the restriction of MGMT methylation as a predictive biomarker to classical-subtype GBM and the demonstration that the survival advantage of proneural GBM is entirely conferred by the G-CIMP epigenetic phenotype.

## Key Findings

### F1: Comprehensive Somatic Mutation Landscape — 71 Significantly Mutated Genes

**Finding:** WES of 291 GBMs identified 71 significantly mutated genes (SMGs) by combined MutSig and InVEx analysis, including several novel genes not previously implicated in GBM.

**Detail:** A total of 21,540 somatic mutations were called (median 2.2 coding mutations/Mb). Previously reported SMGs confirmed include *PTEN*, *TP53*, *EGFR*, *PIK3CA*, *PIK3R1*, *NF1*, *RB1*, *IDH1*, and *PDGFRA*. Novel SMGs include *LZTR1* (mutated in 10 samples, co-deleted in 5/6 at 22q), *ATRX* (6%), *SPTA1* (9%), *GABRA6* (4%), and *KEL* (5%). *BRAF* V600E was found in 5/291 (1.7%) GBMs. Chromatin modifier genes (CMGs) were mutated in 46% of the cohort, with CMG mutations mutually exclusive (MEMo p=0.0008).

### F2: Three Core Signaling Pathways Dysregulated in Nearly All GBMs

**Finding:** Integration of mutation and copy-number data shows that three canonical pathways — RTK/RAS/PI3K, p53, and Rb — are cumulatively disrupted in 89%, 85%, and 79% of GBMs, respectively.

**Detail:** At least one RTK alteration was found in 67.3% of cases: *EGFR* (57.4%), *PDGFRA* (13.1%), *MET* (1.6%), *FGFR2/3* (3.2%). PI3K pathway alterations occurred in 89.6% of tumors (*PIK3CA*/*PIK3R1* mutations in 25.1%, *PTEN* mutations/deletions mutually exclusive with PI3K mutations, p=0.0047). p53 pathway disruption (85.3%) occurred via *TP53* mutation/deletion (27.9%), *MDM1/2/4* amplification (15.1%), or *CDKN2A* deletion (57.8%). Rb pathway alterations (78.9%) included *RB1* mutation/deletion (7.6%), *CDK4/6* amplification (15.5%), and *CDKN2A* deletion. Alterations within each pathway were mutually exclusive by MEMo analysis.

### F3: EGFR Is Altered in 57% of GBMs Through Multiple Overlapping Mechanisms

**Finding:** EGFR is the most complex oncogenic target in GBM, with point mutations, focal amplification, aberrant splicing (EGFRvIII, Δ12–13, Δ14–15), C-terminal deletions, and fusion transcripts all occurring in the same cohort.

**Detail:** Overall, 57% of GBMs showed at least one EGFR alteration (mutation, rearrangement, aberrant splicing, or focal amplification). EGFRvIII (exon 2–7 deletion, exon 1–8 junction) was highly expressed (≥10% transcript allelic fraction, TAF) in 11% and at low level (≥1% TAF) in 19% of cases, confirmed by nCounter and RT-PCR. C-terminal EGFR deletions were present at ≥10% TAF in 3.7% and at ≥1% TAF in an additional 9%. The novel EGFR Δ12–13 variant was detected in 28.7% (likely a splice variant, lacking concordant DNA deletion in 14/15 WGS cases). EGFR-SEPT14 was the most recurrent fusion transcript (6/164 RNA-seq samples). 38.4% harbored a genomic rearrangement or point mutation expressed at ≥10% TAF.

**Genes / cancer types:** `EGFR`, `SEPT14`, `SEC61G` · Glioblastoma (GBM)

### F4: TERT Promoter Mutations Drive Telomerase Reactivation in Primary GBM; ATRX Mutations Define ALT in Secondary GBM

**Finding:** TERT promoter mutations (C228T or C250T) are present in 21/25 (84%) of primary GBMs with adequate WGS coverage and are associated with elevated TERT mRNA; ATRX-mutant GBMs (which lack TERT promoter mutations) use alternative lengthening of telomeres (ALT).

**Detail:** Among 25 GBMs with sufficient WGS coverage of the TERT promoter, C228T was found in 15 cases and C250T in 6 cases — mutually exclusive with each other. The four TERT-wildtype cases all harbored *ATRX* mutations, and were also IDH1- and TP53-mutant, consistent with secondary GBM. TERT promoter-mutant tumors showed elevated TERT RNA expression relative to ATRX-mutant tumors. IDH1 mutations were present in 28/423 (6%) patients (R132H predominant), and all 13 IDH1-mutant GBMs with WES had concurrent TP53 mutation; ATRX mutations co-occurred frequently. G-CIMP methylation was present in 42/532 (7.9%) of cases, all associated with IDH1/G-CIMP+ status (OS: 38.3 vs. 12.7 months, p=8.3e-9).

### F5: Molecular Subtypes — Survival Advantage of Proneural GBM Is Entirely Conferred by G-CIMP

**Finding:** The survival benefit of proneural-subtype GBM is attributable solely to the G-CIMP epigenetic phenotype, not to proneural transcriptomic identity itself; non-G-CIMP proneural GBMs have outcomes similar to or worse than other subtypes.

**Detail:** Four transcriptomic subtypes (proneural, neural, classical, mesenchymal) were assigned to the full 543-patient cohort. Six DNA methylation clusters (M1–M6 plus G-CIMP) were identified from 396 samples. G-CIMP-positive (M5) tumors were enriched in the proneural subtype and had significantly better survival (OS 38.3 months vs. 12.7 months for non-G-CIMP). Non-G-CIMP proneural GBMs showed unfavorable early outcomes similar to mesenchymal GBM. Proneural G-CIMP tumors were diagnosed at younger age (41 vs. 56 yrs). ATRX mutations and MYC amplifications were enriched in G-CIMP+. CDK4 and SOX2 amplifications enriched in non-G-CIMP proneural. Classical subtype showed broad amplification of chromosomes 19 and 20.

### F6: MGMT Methylation Is a Predictive Biomarker Only in Classical-Subtype GBM

**Finding:** MGMT promoter methylation (present in 48.5% of the cohort) predicts treatment response and survival benefit only in the classical GBM subtype, not in proneural, mesenchymal, or neural subtypes.

**Detail:** MGMT DNA methylation was estimated in 359 patients; 174 (48.5%) were methylated. G-CIMP cases showed higher MGMT methylation (79% vs. 46% for non-G-CIMP). In subtype-stratified analysis, MGMT status distinguished responders from non-responders only in classical GBM (n=96; p=0.01). No significant MGMT survival effect was observed in proneural (p=0.57), mesenchymal (p=0.62), or neural (p=0.12) subtypes. This finding has direct clinical implications, suggesting that MGMT testing may be most informative when combined with molecular subtyping.

### F7: FGFR3-TACC3 Fusions and Oncogenic Gene Fusions Identified by RNA-seq

**Finding:** RNA-seq of 164 GBMs identified 228 fusion transcripts in 106 samples, including the actionable FGFR3-TACC3 inversion in 2 cases and recurrent EGFR fusions (EGFR-SEPT14 in 6 cases).

**Detail:** A total of 48 interchromosomal and 180 intrachromosomal fusion transcripts were detected; ~37% were in-frame. The FGFR3-TACC3 fusion (an oncogenic inversion previously shown to activate FGFR signaling) was found in 2 samples; FGFR3 or TACC3 focal amplification was present in 14/537 (2.6%) of copy-number profiles, suggesting amplification as a surrogate marker for the rearrangement. EGFR-SEPT14 was the most recurrent fusion (6/164 samples). WGS of 42 samples detected 238 high-confidence somatic rearrangements (49 interchromosomal, 125 intrachromosomal, 64 intragenic), with recurrent intragenic events in EGFR (n=12), CPM (n=3), and PRIM2 (n=3).

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR3`, `TACC3`, `EGFR`, `SEPT14`, `MDM2`, `CDK4` · Glioblastoma (GBM)

## Open Questions

- What is the functional significance of low-level EGFRvIII expression (1–10% TAF) in GBM cells, and does it confer clinical resistance through paracrine mechanisms?
- Are there alternative mechanisms driving the G-CIMP phenotype in the 7 IDH1-wildtype / IDH2-wildtype G-CIMP+ cases?
- Do co-occurring RTK alterations (e.g., concurrent EGFR + PDGFRA in 42% of PDGFRA-altered tumors) represent intratumoral heterogeneity, and how does this impact targeted therapy design?
- What is the functional role of the novel SMG LZTR1 in GBM pathogenesis?
- Can proteomic pathway activity (RPPA) better stratify patients for targeted therapy than genomic alteration status alone, given the non-linear relationship between genomic and proteomic signals?
- Does BRAF V600E in the 1.7% of GBMs predict response to BRAF inhibitors (e.g., vemurafenib) as in melanoma?

## Limitations

- TERT promoter mutation analysis was limited to 25/543 patients (those with sufficient WGS coverage), limiting statistical power for TERT-related analyses.
- WGS was performed in only 42 samples, restricting comprehensive structural variant characterization to a small subset of the cohort.
- TCGA selection criteria favor primary GBM, resulting in underrepresentation of IDH1-mutant (secondary) GBM (6%) relative to population-based series.
- Proteomics (RPPA) was performed on 214 samples only, and unsupervised clustering failed to yield clear proteomic subtypes, limiting cross-platform integration.
- Single time-point, bulk tumor analysis cannot resolve intratumoral heterogeneity or temporal evolution of alterations under treatment pressure.
- Fusion transcript and splice variant analyses (EGFRvIII, Δ12–13, FGFR3-TACC3) were limited to the 164 samples with RNA-seq data.
