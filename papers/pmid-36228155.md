## Summary

This MSK study comprehensively characterizes the somatic mutational landscape of gallbladder carcinoma (GBC) using targeted NGS (MSK-IMPACT) in 233 patients (244 samples) treated at Memorial Sloan Kettering from 2014–2021. The most common oncogenic alterations were in TP53 (63%) and the RTK-RAS and Cell Cycle pathways, with ERBB2, KRAS, and CTNNB1 identified as potential novel drivers. One third of patients harbored clinically actionable alterations, and SMAD4 and STK11 mutations independently predicted poor survival in metastatic disease. The study supports broad tumor sequencing as standard of care in GBC and distinguishes GBC from cholangiocarcinoma at the molecular level.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: TP53 and Cell Cycle Pathway Dominate the GBC Mutational Landscape
- **Finding**: TP53 alterations (63%) and Cell Cycle pathway genes (CDKN2A 21%, MDM2 amplification 11%, RB1 3.5%) are the most prevalent oncogenic events in GBC, together altered in 74% and 46% of samples respectively.
- **Detail**: All TP53 alterations are loss-of-function. MDM2 amplification occurs in 11% of tumors. Pathway-level analysis using the Sanchez-Vega canonical pathway framework showed TP53 pathway altered in 74%, Cell Cycle in 46%.
- **Genes**: TP53, CDKN2A, CDKN2B, MDM2, RB1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: tumor suppressor loss, cell cycle dysregulation, oncogenesis

### Finding 2: ERBB2 Is a Recurrent and Actionable Target
- **Finding**: ERBB2 is altered by amplification (10%) or oncogenic mutation (6%) in approximately 16% of patients, making it the leading actionable target in GBC. Twenty-four patients received HER2-directed therapy; 10 responses were observed.
- **Detail**: ERBB2 amplification is classified as OncoKB Level 3B (standard-of-care targeted therapy in another indication). The most frequent oncogenic ERBB2 mutation is p.S310F/Y. Ten patients received HER2-directed therapies (trastuzumab, pan-HER2 TKIs) with clinical responses in some.
- **Genes**: ERBB2, ERBB3
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: actionability, targeted therapy, HER2 amplification, mutation hotspot

### Finding 3: CTNNB1, KRAS, and PIK3CA Are Novel Driver Genes in GBC
- **Finding**: Oncodrive clustering identified CTNNB1 (6%, hotspot p.S45F/P), KRAS (7%, hotspot p.G12A/C/D/R), and ERBB2 as clustered gain-of-function driver genes. PIK3CA (9%) and CTNNB1 alterations were more frequent in tumors without cholelithiasis.
- **Detail**: These GOF mutations define two oncogenic programs — β-catenin/Wnt (CTNNB1) and RTK-RAS (KRAS, ERBB2) — and are supported by studies showing CTNNB1 and KRAS as initiating events during gallbladder adenoma-to-carcinoma progression. PIK3CA hotspot mutations (p.E545K, p.H1047R, etc.) are actionable via PI3K/AKT inhibitors.
- **Genes**: CTNNB1, KRAS, PIK3CA, ERBB2
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: driver gene, oncogenic clustering, pathway activation, actionability

### Finding 4: SMAD4 and STK11 Mutations Are Independent Prognostic Markers in Metastatic GBC
- **Finding**: In metastatic patients, SMAD4 alterations (23%) and STK11 mutations (7%) independently associated with shorter overall survival in multivariate Cox regression (STK11: HR 3.76, p=0.004; SMAD4: HR 2.11, p=0.012).
- **Detail**: Neither gene correlated with OS in primary tumor patients. The association was independent of clinicopathologic variables. STK11 alterations have been associated with resistance to checkpoint inhibitors in other cancers; two STK11-mutated patients in this cohort did respond to checkpoint blockade.
- **Genes**: SMAD4, STK11
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: prognosis, survival, TGF-beta signaling, LKB1/STK11, treatment resistance

### Finding 5: 35% of Patients Have Clinically Actionable Alterations
- **Finding**: 82 patients (35%) harbored at least one OncoKB Level 1, 3A, or 3B actionable alteration. One patient had an LMNA::NTRK1 fusion (Level 1; FDA-approved entrectinib/larotrectinib). 18% of metastatic patients received biomarker-directed or trial therapy.
- **Detail**: Actionable alterations included ERBB2 amplification (n=24), PIK3CA hotspot mutations (n=21), BRCA1/2 mutations (n=9), ATM (n=5), CDK12 (n=3), TSC1/2 (n=5), KRAS p.G12C (n=2, Level 3A in biliary cancer trials), and rare fusions (FGFR3::TACC3, ROS1::PARK2, RET fusions). Multiple patients had 2–5 co-occurring actionable events.
- **Genes**: ERBB2, PIK3CA, BRCA1, BRCA2, ATM, CDK12, TSC1, TSC2, KRAS, NTRK1, FGFR3, RET, ROS1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: actionability, precision oncology, clinical trial matching, off-label therapy

### Finding 6: Histologic Subtype–Specific Molecular Alterations
- **Finding**: RB1 loss-of-function mutations are enriched in small cell neuroendocrine carcinomas; NOTCH pathway GOF variants are enriched in carcinomas with squamous differentiation (26% vs. 8% in adenocarcinomas).
- **Detail**: These associations mirror patterns in other cancer types (RB1 in small-cell NSCLC and esophageal carcinoma; NOTCH in squamous cancers) and suggest shared molecular programs across histologies. Cell Cycle pathway alterations were more common in non-adenocarcinoma tumors (e.g., 83% in small cell NEC vs. 42% in adenocarcinoma).
- **Genes**: RB1, NOTCH1, NOTCH3, CDKN2A, CDKN2B
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: histologic subtype, molecular classification, neuroendocrine, squamous differentiation

### Finding 7: GBC Is Molecularly Distinct from Cholangiocarcinoma
- **Finding**: GBC rarely harbors IDH1 mutations, BAP1 alterations, or FGFR2 fusions — hallmarks of intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma — indicating distinct cell of origin and etiology. No Salmonella or Helicobacter reads were detected in the tumor microbiome despite being established GBC risk factors.
- **Detail**: MSI-High tumors were found in only 3% of patients. ~10% had high TMB. Among 12 checkpoint inhibitor–treated patients, 42% had responses (including 3 MSI-High and 1 MSS/TMB-high). Metastatic samples showed higher microbiome diversity than primary tumors but no oncogenically relevant pathogen signatures.
- **Genes**: IDH1, BAP1, FGFR2
- **Data availability**: partial
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: differential diagnosis, biliary cancer, cholangiocarcinoma, microbiome, MSI, TMB, immunotherapy

## Open Questions

1. Do GBC molecular landscapes differ substantially in high-incidence geographic regions (Chile, India, South America) where cholelithiasis and infectious exposures are more prevalent — and could those differences yield distinct therapeutic targets?
2. Can SMAD4 and STK11 alteration status be prospectively used to stratify patients in clinical trials, particularly given their association with immune checkpoint inhibitor resistance?
3. What is the optimal HER2-directed therapeutic strategy (antibody, TKI, ADC) for ERBB2-amplified vs. ERBB2-mutant GBC, and do these patient subgroups have different outcomes?
4. Does the tumor microbiome play a causal role in GBC carcinogenesis, or is the composition of off-target sequencing reads primarily a reflection of the tissue environment?

## Limitations

1. Single-institution cohort (MSK, predominantly US Caucasian patients), limiting generalizability to populations from GBC-endemic regions where distinct molecular subtypes may predominate.
2. Small numbers of neuroendocrine carcinoma histologic subtypes (n=11) reduce statistical power to identify subtype-specific molecular alterations.
3. Cholelithiasis data was missing in ~49% of primary samples, limiting analysis of the cholelithiasis–molecular alteration association.
4. Retrospective treatment data: biomarker-directed therapy was not uniformly applied, and response rates are based on physician assessment rather than standardized criteria, precluding definitive efficacy conclusions.
