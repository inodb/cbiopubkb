## Summary

This paper systematically characterizes the clonal dynamics of human breast cancer patient-derived xenografts (PDXs) at single-cell resolution. Thirty xenograft lines from 55 patients were established and serially propagated in NSG/NRG mice; 15 were analyzed by deep whole-genome shotgun sequencing (WGSS), SNP6.0 arrays, and targeted deep amplicon sequencing covering thousands of somatic SNV positions. The central finding is that clonal selection on initial engraftment is universal — all 15 cases showed non-neutral dynamics — ranging from extreme selective expansion of initially minor (<5%) clones to moderate polyclonal engraftment. Post-engraftment clonal dynamics are inversely related to the severity of initial selection: tumors with strong initial bottlenecks are more stable during serial passage, while those with moderate initial selection continue evolving. Single-cell multiplexed sequencing (210 nuclei across two cases) directly validated bulk PyClone predictions, resolving cascading subclonal phylogenies. Critically, the same clonal expansion patterns were reproduced when the same tumor was transplanted into multiple mice, demonstrating deterministic genomic fitness rather than stochastic drift.

## Key Findings

### F1: Clonal selection on engraftment is universal across all 15 breast cancer PDX lines

**Finding:** In every one of the 15 breast cancer xenograft lines analyzed, initial engraftment induced non-neutral clonal dynamics, regardless of ER/HER2 subtype or whether the starting material was primary tumor or pleural effusion.

**Detail:** Between 6.5–32.1% of WGSS-detected SNVs were present at or below detection threshold in the patient tumor but highly prevalent in the xenograft (expansion). Conversely, 0.2–19.4% of SNVs were detectable in tumor but lost in xenograft (contraction). PyClone cellular prevalence estimates confirmed the presence of mutation clusters that shifted dramatically between tumor and xenograft. The severity ranged from extreme (SA494, SA495, SA530, SA532, SA533 — minor clone expands to dominate) to moderate polyclonal engraftment (SA493, SA501, SA531). Copy number architecture (TITAN analysis) showed concordant dynamics: a xenograft-specific deletion encompassing `TP53` was found in SA500 coincident with SNV cluster dynamics.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` (one case); pan-genomic SNV clusters used as clonal marks · Breast cancer (all subtypes: ER+, ER−, HER2+ and TN; primary and metastatic)

### F2: Post-engraftment clonal dynamics inversely correlate with initial selection severity

**Finding:** Xenografts that underwent strong initial clonal selection (minor clone expansion to dominance) show relatively stable clonal architecture during subsequent serial passages, whereas xenografts with moderate initial selection exhibit ongoing, pronounced clonal evolution over time.

**Detail:** Cases like SA494, SA495, SA530, SA532, SA533 underwent extreme bottlenecks at initial transplant; subsequent passages were relatively stable with the dominant clone maintained. In contrast, SA501 showed moderate initial selection but dramatically ongoing dynamics across passages X1–X4, including the gradual expansion of a cluster 3 genotype and decline of cluster 5, culminating in near-complete dominance of a genotype E clone (accumulating 5 sequential mutations) by passage 4. Polyclonal sub-structure can re-emerge even after an initial bottleneck (SA493, SA494, SA495, SA500, SA531), suggesting that initial selection does not preclude subsequent clonal diversification.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-genomic SNV clusters · Breast cancer PDX (ER+, ER−, TN; primary and metastatic)

### F3: Single-cell sequencing directly validates bulk PyClone clonal genotype predictions

**Finding:** Multiplexed single-cell targeted sequencing of 210 nuclei from two cases (SA494 and SA501) confirmed that PyClone mutation clusters derived from bulk population sequencing accurately represent major clonal genotypes, resolving them to single-cell genotypes with high confidence.

**Detail:** SA494 (extreme selection): Bayesian phylogenetic inference on 62 tumor + 58 passage-4 xenograft nuclei recovered two discrete clades bearing mutually exclusive allele sets, confirming expansion of a <5% originating clone to xenograft dominance. SA501 (complex dynamics): 90 nuclei from passages X1, X2, X4 revealed 5 cascading genotypes (A→B→C→D→E), each accumulating successive PyClone cluster mutations in a branching phylogeny. The relative abundance of single-cell genotypes across passages mirrored bulk PyClone cellular prevalence predictions (Genotype E absent at X1/X2, dominant at X4). This is the first single-cell validation of PyClone mutation clusters as markers of major clonal genotypes in a PDX system.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-genomic somatic SNVs as clonal markers · Breast cancer PDX (SA494 ER+; SA501 mixed subtype)

**Data availability:** `not-available`

### F4: Clonal dynamics are reproducible in replicate transplants — deterministic fitness, not drift

**Finding:** In 4 out of 5 tested cases, transplanting the same tumor population into multiple independent mice produced parallel clonal dynamics: the same mutation clusters expanded or contracted across replicate animals, indicating that genomic genotypes confer reproducible fitness advantages rather than stochastic selection.

**Detail:** SA501 showed expansion of cluster 3 (and decline of cluster 5) reproduced in replicate mice at passage 2, 3, and 4 independently. SA535 showed reproducible clonal expansion in both NSG and NRG mouse strains. SA532 showed reproducible expansion in 3/7 replicates at X2. SA429 showed replicated dynamics at X2. Only SA496 showed less reproducible evolution. Pearson correlations of per-SNV cellular prevalences between same-passage replicates ranged from 0.91–0.94 for SA429, SA501, SA532, SA535 (each inferred independently). This strongly implies pre-existing clonal fitness hierarchies — not de novo mutagenesis — as the primary driver of PDX clonal selection.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-genomic SNV clusters · Breast cancer PDX (SA429, SA496, SA501, SA532, SA535)

**Data availability:** `available`

### F5: Mutational processes (substitution spectra) are maintained post-engraftment

**Finding:** Nucleotide substitution patterns in patient tumor and matched xenograft passages are broadly comparable, indicating that the mutational processes active in the original tumor are maintained in the xenograft, despite strong clonal selection.

**Detail:** Comparison of WGSS-derived trinucleotide substitution profiles between originating tumors and xenograft passages showed consistent patterns across the 15 tumor–xenograft series. SNV counts (range 4.3–27.7 × 10³ genome-wide; 57–1,040 in coding regions), CNA/LOH (34–67% of genome), and SV burdens were consistent with published breast cancer whole-genome studies. Chromothripsis was observed in SA429 and breakage-fusion-bridge cycles in SA429 and SA494, and these complex events were conserved across passages. This supports the use of PDX as biologically faithful models for studying mutational processes and drug response.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-genomic; chromothripsis regions in SA429; BFB cycles SA429/SA494 · All breast cancer subtypes

**Data availability:** `available`

## Open Questions

1. Which specific genomic features (gene mutations, copy number changes, epigenetic marks) confer the fitness advantage that drives reproducible clonal selection — and can these be identified prospectively?
2. Do the clones that engraft preferentially represent cancer stem-cell-like populations, and does engraftment fitness correlate with clinical metastatic potential?
3. How do targeted therapies applied to PDX models alter clonal dynamics versus the natural selection observed here, and does initial clonal composition predict drug response?
4. Can the PDX clonal bottleneck be exploited to enrich rare treatment-resistant subclones for functional studies?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=15 for WGSS analysis); observations may not generalize to the full spectrum of breast cancer subtypes and mutational contexts.
- Immunodeficient mouse models (NSG/NRG) lack an intact immune system, which may systematically alter selection pressures compared to patients.
- Histopathological and imaging characteristics remain broadly stable despite dramatic clonal changes — PDX may not model immune-edited or immune-excluded tumor microenvironments.
- PyClone clusters rely on bulk population averages; rare clones below 5% cellular prevalence may be missed in population sequencing (addressed by single-cell for SA494/SA501 only).
- The deterministic fitness basis is inferred from reproducibility; precise molecular mechanisms driving fitness remain unresolved.
