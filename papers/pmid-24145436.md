## Summary

First comprehensive whole-genome and whole-exome sequencing study of mantle cell lymphoma (MCL), sequencing 29 primary cases (WGS: n=4, WES: n=29) plus 6 MCL cell lines, with validation by targeted sequencing in an independent cohort of 172 MCL patients. The study identifies 25 significantly mutated genes, including known drivers (ATM, CCND1, TP53) and novel hits (WHSC1/NSD2, MLL2, MEF2B, BIRC3, TLR2, NOTCH2). Chromatin modifier mutations (WHSC1, MLL2, MEF2B) are identified for the first time in MCL in 14% of cases. NOTCH1/2 mutations are found in 9.5% of MCL and mark a highly aggressive subgroup with 3-year OS of 24% vs. 63%. Paired analysis of simultaneous and sequential MCL samples reveals subclonal heterogeneity at diagnosis and dynamic clonal evolution at disease progression — with some mutations representing early trunk events (ATM, CCND1) and others late events (TP53, WHSC1, BIRC3). Data available in `mcl_idibips_2013` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: 25 significantly mutated genes identified; ATM, CCND1, TP53 most frequent and differentially distributed by MCL subtype

**Finding:** WES of 29 MCL identified 25 significantly recurrently mutated genes. ATM was mutated in 41% of all cases (12/29), exclusively in SOX11-positive MCL (55% in SOX11+ vs. 0% in SOX11-negative; P=0.023). CCND1 mutations occurred in 35% (10/29), predominantly in SOX11-negative/IGHV-mutated MCL (86% vs. 18%; P=0.03). TP53 mutations were present in 28% (8/29) and equally distributed across MCL subtypes. All 29 tumors harbored at least one mutation in these 25 genes.

**Detail:** CNA analysis (SNP array) detected chromosomal copy number alterations in 90% of cases (26/29), with an average of 11.1 CNAs per altered case. SOX11-expressing MCL showed significantly more CNAs than SOX11-negative MCL (mean 13 vs. 2; P=2.1×10⁻⁵). The five patients with indolent MCL (no treatment needed, median 55 months) had significantly fewer somatic mutations (mean 11 vs. 25; P=3.4×10⁻⁵) and fewer CNAs (mean 2 vs. 12; P=0.001) than patients requiring treatment, establishing genomic complexity as a correlate of clinical aggressiveness.

**Genes / cancer types:** ATM, CCND1, TP53, CDKN2A · Mantle cell lymphoma

### F2: Novel chromatin modifier mutations (WHSC1/NSD2, MLL2, MEF2B) in 14% of MCL

**Finding:** Three chromatin-modifying genes were recurrently mutated in MCL for the first time: WHSC1 (also known as NSD2/MMSET) in 10% (13/130 by targeted sequencing), MLL2 in 14% (4/29 by WES), and MEF2B in 3.2% (6/187). These mutations were almost exclusively found in SOX11-expressing, IGHV-unmutated MCL. Together, chromatin modifier mutations affected 10/29 (34%) of WES cases.

**Detail:** WHSC1 mutations (E1099K and T1150A, clustered in the conserved SET domain exons 18-19) are the same residues targeted by overexpression in plasma cell myeloma via t(4;14). Expression analysis showed 236 genes differentially expressed in WHSC1-mutated MCL, with 81% upregulated — overlapping proliferation and cell-cycle gene signatures. This resembles the gain-of-function effect of WHSC1 overexpression in PCM, suggesting an activating role for the point mutations. MLL2 mutations were predominantly truncating; MEF2B carried a recurrent hotspot (p.K23R) matching mutations found in DLBCL and follicular lymphoma.

**Genes / cancer types:** WHSC1 (NSD2), MLL2 (KMT2D), MEF2B · Mantle cell lymphoma

### F3: NOTCH1 and NOTCH2 mutations mark highly aggressive MCL with dismal prognosis

**Finding:** NOTCH2 mutations (PEST domain truncating) were found in 5.2% (9/172) of MCL and NOTCH1 truncating mutations in 4.6% (8/172). Combined NOTCH1/2 alterations occurred in 9.5% of cases (only 1 patient had both). NOTCH1/2-mutated MCL had dramatically shorter survival: 3-year OS of 24% vs. 63% in NOTCH1/2 wild-type (P=3.4×10⁻⁴). NOTCH mutations were strongly enriched in blastoid/pleomorphic MCL (67% vs. 13%; P=1.3×10⁻⁵). In bivariate analysis, NOTCH2 (HR 3.5; P=0.017) and TP53 (HR 2.4; P=0.003) mutations were independent risk factors for OS.

**Detail:** NOTCH2-mutated MCL showed 841 differentially expressed genes (FDR <5%), with 42% upregulated — enriched in cell-cycle and metabolic pathways, and overlapping NOTCH activation signatures in lymphoid cells. NOTCH1 and NOTCH2 mutations were mutually exclusive in nearly all cases (only 1 overlap), suggesting they provide similar selective advantages. These findings parallel NOTCH1 mutations in aggressive CLL and NOTCH2 mutations in splenic marginal zone lymphoma, establishing Notch pathway activation as a shared mechanism of lymphoma aggressiveness.

**Genes / cancer types:** NOTCH1, NOTCH2, TP53 · Mantle cell lymphoma

### F4: Clonal heterogeneity at diagnosis between topographic sites demonstrated by paired sequencing

**Finding:** Analysis of 6 paired simultaneous samples (peripheral blood and lymphoid tissue from the same patient) revealed that 4 of 6 cases had identical mutations at both sites (single major clone), while 2 cases (M023 and M026) showed topographic subclonal heterogeneity — a shared founding clone plus site-specific subclones. Eight of the 25 recurrent driver genes (including ATM and CCND1) were present at similar allelic frequencies in both subclones, indicating they were early clonal events.

**Detail:** Kataegis (regional hypermutation clusters) was observed in 3 of 4 WGS cases, most frequently in IGHV-mutated MCL, around the 11q13 t(11;14) breakpoint, Ig gene loci, and deleted 9p21.3 region. The same kataegis pattern was stable between diagnosis and progression in case M003, suggesting it is an early, stable genomic event. ATM, CCND1, MLL2, and KCNC2 were identified as predominantly clonal/early events; TP53, WHSC1, BIRC3, and ABCA3 appeared in only one of two subclones, marking them as later events.

**Genes / cancer types:** ATM, CCND1, TP53, WHSC1, BIRC3, MLL2 · Mantle cell lymphoma

### F5: Treatment-driven clonal evolution at progression: eradication and outgrowth of subclones

**Finding:** Sequential paired sampling (diagnosis vs. progression) in two patients demonstrated distinct patterns of clonal evolution. Patient M003 (indolent, 3+ years stable disease before progression) showed accumulation of new mutations (16% increase in whole-genome mutations), 20 acquired CNAs, and chromothripsis at chromosomes 4 and 12 at progression. Patient M002 (chemotherapy-treated, relapsed 3 years later) showed eradication of the major initial clone and emergence of a new subclone — consistent with treatment-driven selection of a pre-existing minor clone.

**Detail:** BIRC3 mutations were observed as late events: absent at diagnosis in two cases that acquired the mutation in post-treatment samples, sometimes accompanied by acquisition of an 11q22.1–q24.2 deletion. NOTCH1 was found in only one of two synchronous samples in two cases, underscoring its subclonal nature. Together, these findings show that MCL evolution is not linear and that treatment actively shapes the clonal landscape — a critical consideration for monitoring relapsed/refractory MCL.

**Genes / cancer types:** BIRC3, TP53, NOTCH1, WHSC1 · Mantle cell lymphoma

## Open Questions

- Can NOTCH1/2 mutations be exploited therapeutically with gamma-secretase inhibitors in aggressive MCL, and do they predict response to novel targeted agents (BTK inhibitors, BCL-2 inhibitors)?
- Do WHSC1 (NSD2) mutations in MCL confer sensitivity to EZH2 inhibitors (given the inverse H3K36/H3K27 methylation relationship) as suggested by the PCM analogy?
- What is the functional role of BIRC3 loss — does it confer resistance to apoptosis induction by standard chemotherapy or novel agents such as ibrutinib?
- Does the minimal somatic mutation burden in indolent/SOX11-negative MCL represent a sufficiently stable disease state to guide watchful waiting versus early treatment?
- Is chromothripsis at disease progression a mechanism of catastrophic genomic instability that could be targeted, or does it represent a byproduct of other genomic stresses?

## Limitations

- Small discovery cohort (n=29 WES); while validated in 172 cases, the validation was limited to specific targeted genes rather than the full exome
- Validation was performed by Sanger sequencing of selected hotspot regions rather than deep NGS, potentially missing low-frequency variants
- Copy number analysis (SNP array) and somatic mutation calling (WES) were performed separately and integration was not fully harmonized
- No transcriptomic data integrated; gene expression observations for WHSC1 and NOTCH2 are based on subset analyses (8–19 cases)
- Treatment data and clinical outcomes were not systematically captured for the full validation cohort, limiting survival analyses to subsets
