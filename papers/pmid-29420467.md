## Summary

SUMMIT is a global, multi-histology basket trial (NCT01953926) evaluating neratinib — an irreversible pan-HER tyrosine kinase inhibitor — in 141 patients with HER2-mutant (n=125) or HER3-mutant (n=16) solid tumors across 21 cancer types. This landmark study demonstrated that neratinib efficacy is governed jointly by tumor lineage and specific mutant allele — not by mutation presence alone. Greatest activity was observed in HER2-mutant breast cancer (ORR₈ 32%), with responses also in cervical, biliary, and salivary gland cancers. Lung (predominantly exon 20 insertions) and colorectal/bladder cancers showed no responses. No responses were seen in HER3-mutant tumors. Co-mutations in TP53 and concurrent HER3 mutations trended toward lack of benefit. The study establishes a paradigm for how histology-specific resistance mechanisms contextualize genomically-driven therapy and has directly informed neratinib's FDA approval in HER2-mutant breast cancer.

## Key Findings

### F1: Neratinib efficacy in HER2-mutant cancer is tumor-type dependent: breast (ORR 32%), biliary/cervical (responses), lung/bladder/colorectal (no responses)

**Finding:** Among 125 HER2-mutant patients, objective responses were observed in breast (ORR₈ 32%; 8/25 responses), biliary tract, cervical, and salivary gland cancers; no RECIST responses were seen in bladder (n=16), colorectal (n=12), or in the majority of lung (n=26; 1 response). The breast cancer cohort met its primary efficacy endpoint; lung, colorectal, and bladder cohorts did not. Breast cancer patients were all HER2 non-amplified at enrollment, confirming this is a mutation-driven (not amplification-driven) signal.

**Detail:** The breast cohort (n=25) included both ER+ (30% response rate, 6/20) and ER− (40%, 2/5) patients. In cervical cancer (n=5), all patients had extracellular domain mutations (S310); the single responder had S310F. In biliary tract cancer, responses were observed in kinase domain missense mutations. In lung cancer, where HER2 exon 20 insertions predominate, only 1 patient (with a kinase domain missense mutation, L755S) responded — mirroring the known resistance of EGFR exon 20 insertions to 1st/2nd-generation EGFR inhibitors. Despite low ORR, median PFS in lung was 5.5 months with 6 patients remaining on therapy >1 year, suggesting disease control without RECIST response. The lineage-specific resistance is attributed to differential expression of feedback signaling pathways and co-occurring alterations across cancer types.

### F2: Neratinib efficacy varies by HER2 mutant allele: kinase domain missense mutations respond across tumor types; exon 20 insertions respond only in breast

**Finding:** Among 86 patients with broad genomic profiling, responses were observed across all HER2 mutation classes in neratinib-sensitive tumor types (breast, biliary, cervical), but within tumor types with modest sensitivity (lung), only kinase domain missense mutations responded. In tumor types with lineage-based resistance (bladder, colorectal), no responses were seen regardless of allele or domain. Hotspot mutations at S310 (extracellular), L755, V777, Y772_A775dup (kinase), and G778_P780dup (kinase insertion) were enriched among responders.

**Detail:** Among 31 unique HER2 mutations enrolled: kinase domain missense (66%), extracellular domain (26%), transmembrane/juxtamembrane (8%). In-frame exon 20 insertions (22% of patients) — predominantly in lung cancer — were the most treatment-resistant class overall, though 2 breast cancer patients with exon 20 insertions responded. Among 15 patients with non-hotspot HER2 mutations, only 1 responded (a complex breast cancer insertion L755_E757delinsS). This suggests that non-hotspot positions are unlikely oncogenic drivers and unlikely to be sensitizing alleles. Co-amplification of the mutant ERBB2 allele (15 patients, 17%) did not confer additional benefit over mutation alone (p=0.50), suggesting amplification and mutation drive sensitivity through the same mechanism.

### F3: Co-mutations in *TP53* and concurrent HER2+HER3 mutations trend toward lack of clinical benefit

**Finding:** In 86 patients with broad profiling and dichotomized clinical benefit (≥24 weeks stable disease or partial response), co-mutations in TP53 (nominal p=0.018) and concurrent HER3 mutations (nominal p=0.064) were enriched in the no-benefit group. Co-mutation of HER2 and HER3 was observed in 9/86 patients across multiple tumor types (breast n=3, bladder n=2, gastroesophageal n=2, colorectal n=1, pancreatic n=1) — no patient with dual HER2+HER3 mutation achieved clinical benefit.

**Detail:** Cell cycle pathway alterations (primarily driven by TP53 mutations) were significantly associated with lack of clinical benefit (p=0.043). RTK/RAS/RAF pathway co-activation also trended toward worse outcome (p=0.060). High TMB (≥13.8 mut/Mb; 17/86 patients, 20%) was enriched in non-responders (24% of no-benefit vs. 5% of benefit group, p=0.10), consistent with the hypothesis that high TMB tumors may harbor HER2 mutations as passengers rather than drivers. Clonality of the HER2 mutation was assessed in 74 patients: 95% were clonal, and none of 4 patients with subclonal HER2 mutations achieved clinical benefit, supporting clonality as a prerequisite for response. Statistical significance for most co-mutation analyses was lost after multiple hypothesis correction due to small subgroup sizes.

### F4: HER3-mutant cancers do not respond to single-agent neratinib despite preclinical oncogenic activity

**Finding:** None of the 16 patients with HER3-mutant tumors (including colorectal n=5, bladder n=2, biliary n=2, and others) achieved an objective response to neratinib. Despite preclinical data suggesting HER3 mutations drive constitutive signaling via wild-type HER2, single-agent pan-HER inhibition produced no clinical benefit in HER3-mutant cancers in this heavily pretreated population.

**Detail:** HER3 mutations enrolled were all missense variants clustered in the extracellular furin-like and receptor domains (11 unique mutations); 75% were at known mutational hotspots. The absence of responses contrasts with in vitro data suggesting oncogenic activity of HER3 mutations (dependent on wild-type HER2 for signaling). Possible explanations include: tumor type composition (colorectal and bladder predominated — the same lineages resistant in HER2-mutant disease), confounding co-alterations, or insufficient neratinib-mediated HER2 suppression to block HER3-driven signaling. Concordance of HER3 mutation calls between local and central testing was 75% (6/8), slightly lower than HER2 (95%), which may also affect patient selection.

### F5: Concordance of local vs. central HER2 mutation testing was 95%, validating distributed genomic eligibility screening for basket trials

**Finding:** Among 99 patients eligible for concordance analysis (excluding those where local and central tests were identical), 95% (69/73) had concordant HER2 mutation calls between central (tissue and/or plasma) and local sequencing. Central testing identified one locally reported mutation (V773M) as a germline polymorphism — that patient had progressive disease. The 4 discordant cases may reflect tumor heterogeneity over time (sample age >3 years in 2 cases).

**Detail:** This high concordance rate supports the use of locally performed sequencing for basket trial eligibility, reducing the barrier to enrollment and accelerating trial accrual for rare mutations. Plasma ctDNA concordance was evaluated for 21 patients via liquid biopsy; all patients with discordant HER2 results had progressive disease (median PFS 43 days), emphasizing that genomic eligibility predicts for therapeutic benefit when mutations are true positives. This finding has practical implications for decentralized genomic-eligibility precision oncology trials.

## Open Questions

- Why do bladder and colorectal cancers with HER2 kinase domain missense mutations — which activate constitutive HER2 signaling in vitro — fail to respond to neratinib in vivo?
- What is the mechanism by which concurrent HER3 mutation abrogates benefit from HER2 inhibition?
- Would HER3-mutant cancers respond to antibody-drug conjugates (e.g., patritumab deruxtecan, HER3-DXd) that do not require kinase inhibition?
- Do exon 20 HER2 insertions in lung cancer respond to poziotinib or other exon 20 insertion-specific inhibitors as EGFR exon 20 insertions do to mobocertinib/amivantamab?
- Can biomarkers (TP53 co-mutation, TMB, clonality) be prospectively validated to refine patient selection for neratinib in HER2-mutant cancers?

## Limitations

- Basket trial design with heterogeneous cohorts limits statistical power for allele- and tumor-type-specific analyses, particularly for rare tumor types
- Heavy prior therapy burden (50% ≥3 prior lines) may mask therapeutic activity attributable to resistance mechanisms from prior treatments
- Broad profiling for co-mutation analysis was available only in 86/141 patients (61%), limiting representativeness
- Multiple hypothesis testing corrections reduce significance of co-mutation associations due to small sample sizes
- HER3-mutant cohort (n=16) is underpowered for definitive efficacy conclusions; tumor type composition (colorectal/bladder predominant) confounds interpretation
