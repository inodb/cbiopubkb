## Summary

Pan-cancer analysis of microsatellite instability (MSI) using MANTIS software on whole-exome sequencing data from 11,139 tumor-normal pairs across 39 cancer types (TCGA, TARGET, and external cohorts). MSI was detected in 27 of 39 cancer types, with an overall prevalence of 3.8%. Beyond the well-known Lynch syndrome–associated tumor types (endometrial, colorectal, gastric), the study identified significant and previously under-recognized MSI in adrenocortical carcinoma (4.3%), cervical squamous cell carcinoma (2.6%), and mesothelioma (2.4%). MSI-H tumors in ACC and CESC showed significantly higher mutational burden than MSS counterparts. The findings support expanding clinical MSI testing beyond colorectal and endometrial cancers to identify additional patients who may benefit from PD-1 immune checkpoint inhibitors. Data accessible across TCGA pan-cancer atlas studies on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: MSI detected in 27/39 cancer types; 3.8% overall prevalence with wide disease-specific variation

**Finding:** MSI was present in 27 of 39 cancer types analyzed. Disease-specific prevalence ranged from 31.4% in endometrial carcinoma to 0.25% in glioblastoma multiforme. Among the 27 MSI-positive types, 12 had >1% prevalence and more than one MSI-H tumor. Twelve cancer types showed no detectable MSI.

**Detail:** The four highest-prevalence types—endometrial carcinoma, colon adenocarcinoma, gastric adenocarcinoma, and rectal adenocarcinoma—are all Lynch syndrome–associated. MSI was more frequent in colon adenocarcinoma (19.7%) than rectal adenocarcinoma (5.7%), consistent with prior literature. Of 2,530 loci analyzed, 22 high-performing loci were identified as particularly discriminatory across ≥5 cancer types; neither Bethesda nor Promega panel loci were among them, suggesting that standard PCR-based panels may be suboptimal for pan-cancer MSI detection.

### F2: Novel significant MSI in ACC (4.3%), CESC (2.6%), and mesothelioma (2.4%)

**Finding:** Three cancer types not previously well-characterized for MSI showed statistically clear bimodal MANTIS score distributions distinguishing MSI-H from MSS tumors: adrenocortical carcinoma (4/92 cases, 4.3%), cervical squamous cell carcinoma/endocervical adenocarcinoma (8/305 cases, 2.6%), and mesothelioma (2/83 cases, 2.4%). These represent new candidate cancer types for expanded immunotherapy eligibility evaluation.

**Detail:** Kernel density estimation of MANTIS scores confirmed distinct MSI-H and MSS populations in all three types. Prior studies of MSI in ACC have primarily implicated Lynch syndrome as a risk factor for familial ACC, but NGS-based pan-cancer MSI calling had not previously been applied. Active clinical trials of immune checkpoint inhibitors were underway or enrolling in all three tumor types at the time of publication (NCT02673333 for ACC; NCT02635360 for CESC; NCT02784171, NCT02991482 for MESO).

### F3: MSI-H ACC and CESC tumors are significantly hypermutated vs. MSS counterparts

**Finding:** Somatic variant calling (MuTect) in ACC, CESC, and MESO confirmed that MSI-H tumors within each cohort had markedly elevated mutational burden. MSI-H ACC averaged 1,157 somatic mutations versus 216 in MSS ACC (P=0.01). MSI-H CESC averaged 5,675 versus 639 in MSS CESC (P=0.003). MESO showed a ~7-fold increase in MSI-H vs. MSS (982 vs. 142; P=0.10, not significant).

**Detail:** 64% of MSI-H cases across ACC, CESC, and MESO harbored at least one predicted deleterious somatic mutation in MMR or proofreading polymerase genes (MSH2, MSH6, MLH1, PMS2, EXO1, POLD1, POLE), compared to 7% of MSS cases. Mutational signature analysis using deconstructSigs (27 Alexandrov signatures) did not reveal statistically significant differences in signature prevalence between MSI-H and MSS tumors within each cohort, likely due to the small number of MSI-H cases.

### F4: Heterogeneity of MSI patterns across tumor types; candidate loci for a pan-cancer NGS MSI panel

**Finding:** Among 2,530 exome-proximal microsatellite loci, only 22 demonstrated high discriminatory power (difference score > 0.75) in at least 5 cancer types and had ≥50% coverage in those cohorts. These did not overlap with the standard clinical Bethesda or Promega PCR MSI panels, indicating that existing PCR-based panels designed for colorectal cancer may perform suboptimally in other cancer types.

**Detail:** The heterogeneity in MANTIS scores and discriminatory loci performance across tumor types underscores that MSI manifests differently at the genomic level depending on cancer type. The 22 high-performing loci represent a potential foundation for a targeted NGS-based pan-cancer MSI panel—an approach now embodied in clinical assays such as MSIsensor and Foundation Medicine's MSI detection module. MANTIS requires a matched normal to call MSI reliably; tumor-only calling would require separate calibration.

### F5: Expanded clinical MSI testing supports immunotherapy triage across all solid tumor types

**Finding:** Among 9,569 non-colorectal/non-endometrial/non-gastric tumors analyzed, 77 (0.8%) were MSI-H. Though individually rare in most cancer types, these patients represent an unexplored population that may benefit from pembrolizumab or other PD-1 inhibitors, which received tissue-agnostic FDA approval for MSI-H/dMMR tumors in 2017. The study supports a paradigm shift from organ-specific to biomarker-driven MSI testing across all solid tumors.

**Detail:** Prior to this study, clinical MSI testing was recommended only for colorectal and endometrial cancers. The FDA's subsequent approval of pembrolizumab for any MSI-H/dMMR solid tumor validated the clinical utility of pan-cancer MSI detection. The MANTIS tool used here achieved 97% sensitivity and 99% specificity versus PCR in prior validation work across six cancer types, making it suitable for discovery in new cancer types. Total MSI-H cases across non-CRC/UCEC/STAD accounted for a substantial absolute number of patients who could benefit from immunotherapy.

## Open Questions

- Can the 22 high-performing MSI loci identified here form the basis of a validated tumor-only pan-cancer NGS MSI panel for clinical use?
- Does MSI-H in ACC and CESC originate primarily from Lynch syndrome germline mutations, somatic MLH1 hypermethylation, or other mechanisms?
- What is the prognostic significance of MSI-H in cancer types beyond colorectal, endometrial, and gastric (e.g., ACC, CESC, MESO)?
- Do MSI-H mesothelioma cases respond to PD-1 inhibitors with similar durable responses as observed in colorectal MSI-H tumors?
- Can matched-normal–free MSI calling algorithms (e.g., MANTIS in tumor-only mode) reliably detect MSI across these rare cancer types in the clinic?

## Limitations

- MESO MSI-H vs. MSS mutational burden difference did not reach statistical significance (P=0.10), possibly due to the small number of MSI-H MESO cases (n=2)
- Mutational signature analysis pooled all MSI-H cases within each tumor type, masking individual-level signature heterogeneity; per-sample analysis lacked statistical power
- DLBCL data were aligned to hg19 rather than hg38 due to a GDC contamination issue, and MSI calling used a different locus set (2,539 vs. 2,530)
- No clinical outcome or treatment data were linked; prognostic and predictive value of MSI-H in non-CRC/UCEC types cannot be assessed from this dataset alone
- External cohorts (CLL, CTCL, NPC, CHOL) used different capture protocols and aligners than TCGA, potentially introducing platform-related biases
