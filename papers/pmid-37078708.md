## Summary

This retrospective study evaluated 132 patients with nodal peripheral T-cell lymphoma (PTCL) treated with CHOP-based curative-intent chemotherapy and sequenced by MSK-IMPACT (up to 500 genes), characterizing both clinical and molecular predictors of survival. Among all somatic genetic aberrations tested, only *TP53* mutations (16% overall; 28% of PTCL-NOS, 33% of ALK⁻ ALCL) and *TP53*/17p deletions independently predicted inferior progression-free survival (PFS) on multivariate analysis (HR 3.1, p=0.005 and HR 4.1, p=0.03, respectively), with a median PFS of 4.5 months for *TP53*-mutant PTCL vs. 10.5 months for *TP53* wild-type. *CDKN2A* deletions (7%), though rare, were the only genetic alteration correlating with inferior overall survival (median OS 17.6 vs. 56.7 months). ASCT consolidation in first remission was the strongest predictor of superior PFS and OS.

## Key Findings

### F1: TP53 mutations independently predict inferior PFS in PTCL treated with CHOP-based chemotherapy

**Finding:** *TP53* mutations were found in 16% (21/132) of all PTCL patients and were the only somatic genetic aberration independently associated with inferior PFS on multivariate analysis (HR 3.1, 95% CI 1.4–6.8; p=0.005). Median PFS was 4.5 months for *TP53*-mutant PTCL (n=21) vs. 10.5 months for *TP53* wild-type (n=111). *TP53*/17p deletions (7/132, 5%) also independently predicted inferior PFS (HR 4.1, 95% CI 1.1–15.0; p=0.03).

**Detail:** *TP53* mutations were unevenly distributed across subtypes: highest in PTCL-NOS (28%; 10/36) and ALK⁻ ALCL (33%; 5/15), and much rarer in AITL (3%; 2/62). This subtype-specific distribution is consistent with the distinct oncogenic mechanisms underlying each PTCL subtype. The very short median PFS of 4.5 months for *TP53*-mutant PTCL indicates that most patients do not achieve durable disease control with standard CHOP-based therapy, suggesting these patients may be better suited for novel induction strategies, alternative consolidation, or clinical trials. Notably, *TP53* mutation status was not associated with inferior OS, raising the possibility that salvage therapies (including allo-SCT) may partially rescue outcomes.

### F2: CDKN2A deletions are rare (7%) but predict markedly inferior overall survival in PTCL

**Finding:** *CDKN2A* deletions were found in 7% (9/132) of PTCL patients and were the only genetic aberration independently associated with inferior OS on multivariate analysis (HR 12.1, 95% CI 2.8–52.0; p<0.001). Median OS was 17.6 months for *CDKN2A*-deleted PTCL vs. 56.7 months for patients without *CDKN2A* deletions.

**Detail:** Despite their rarity, *CDKN2A* deletions confer a dramatically adverse prognosis, with a median OS less than 18 months — nearly four-fold shorter than those without the deletion. CDKN2A encodes the p16INK4A and p14ARF tumor suppressors, which regulate both CDK4/RB and MDM2/p53 pathways. *CDKN2A* deletions were especially prevalent in MEITL (3/4 cases, 75%), a rare and aggressive PTCL subtype, where co-occurrence with *SETD2* mutations was also observed. This finding suggests *CDKN2A* deletion may identify a biologically distinct high-risk PTCL subset warranting consideration of CDK4/6 inhibitors or alternative therapeutic strategies.

### F3: Subtype-specific mutation landscapes: AITL dominated by TET2/RHOA/DNMT3A/IDH2; PTCL-NOS by TP53/TET2/PLCG1

**Finding:** The most common mutations across all 132 PTCL patients were *TET2* (52%), *RHOA* (30%), *DNMT3A* (19%), *TP53* (16%), and *IDH2* (11%). Subtype-specific patterns were pronounced: AITL was dominated by *TET2* (82%), *RHOA* (53%), *DNMT3A* (31%), and *IDH2* (23%); while PTCL-NOS showed *TP53* (28%) and *TET2* (28%) as co-leaders, followed by *PLCG1* (17%) and *STAT5B* (11%).

**Detail:** In AITL, 71% (36/51) of *TET2*-mutant cases harbored >1 *TET2* mutation and 88% of *RHOA* mutations were the G17V hotspot, consistent with prior AITL genomics literature. All *IDH2* mutations in AITL affected the R172X hotspot in exon 4. The high prevalence of *TET2* mutations in AITL (82%) supports a model where *TET2*-driven clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) precedes T-cell transformation. *RHOA* G17V, a loss-of-function mutation specific to AITL/TFH-type PTCL, activates TCR signaling through interaction with VAV1 and downstream PI3K/AKT. The PTCL-NOS mutation landscape reflects greater oncogenic heterogeneity with potential actionable targets in *PLCG1* and *STAT5B*.

### F4: ASCT consolidation in first remission is the strongest treatment-related predictor of superior PFS and OS

**Finding:** Receipt of ASCT consolidation in first remission was independently associated with superior PFS (HR 0.3, 95% CI 0.1–0.5; p<0.001) and OS (HR 0.1, 95% CI 0.04–0.4; p<0.001) on multivariate analysis. In the CCD cohort, 37/87 (43%) received ASCT in first remission; 50/132 (38%) in the entire cohort.

**Detail:** The strong ASCT benefit persisted in multivariate analysis after adjusting for stage, bone marrow involvement, and genetic risk factors. This is consistent with prior nonrandomized prospective trials and retrospective analyses showing ASCT advantage over historical control therapies. Importantly, neither the use of etoposide-containing regimens (CHOEP/EPOCH, 45%) nor brentuximab vedotin-containing regimens (12%) correlated with significantly different PFS outcomes in this cohort, suggesting these chemotherapy intensification strategies may not obviate the need for ASCT consolidation in eligible patients.

### F5: TP53 mutation status does not correlate with inferior OS, suggesting salvage opportunities exist for TP53-mutant PTCL

**Finding:** Despite *TP53* mutations strongly predicting inferior PFS (median 4.5 vs. 10.5 months), *TP53* alterations did not correlate with inferior OS in univariate or multivariate analysis. In contrast, *CDKN2A* deletions (HR 12.1, p<0.001) and ECOG PS ≥2 (HR 19.2, p=0.003) were the genetic and clinical predictors of inferior OS, respectively.

**Detail:** The disconnect between *TP53*'s impact on PFS (strong negative effect) vs. OS (none) is clinically important. It suggests that although *TP53*-mutant PTCL progresses rapidly under CHOP, some patients are successfully salvaged by second-line therapies and potentially allo-SCT at relapse, restoring OS to a level comparable to *TP53* wild-type patients. This contrasts with AML where *TP53* mutations confer globally inferior survival with standard therapy. Prospective studies should evaluate whether upfront allo-SCT or novel induction strategies in *TP53*-mutant PTCL can improve both PFS and OS.

## Open Questions

- Should *TP53*-mutant PTCL patients be enrolled in novel induction therapy trials or routed directly to allo-SCT after limited induction?
- Does *CDKN2A* deletion predict sensitivity to CDK4/6 inhibitors in PTCL?
- Can CHIP-associated *TET2* mutations be used to identify AITL precursor states before lymphoma development?
- Do *IDH2* R172 mutations predict response to enasidenib (IDH2 inhibitor) in AITL/TFH-type PTCL?
- Is the lack of *TP53* impact on OS due to effective salvage therapies, or patient selection biases in this retrospective cohort?
- What genetic features predict benefit from ASCT consolidation vs. non-ASCT strategies in PTCL?

## Limitations

- Retrospective single-institution study; selection bias from patients managed at MSK may limit generalizability
- 34% of patients were missing ≥1 baseline clinical prognostic variable; the CCD cohort (n=87) is smaller than ideal
- Some sequencing performed at relapse (25% in entire cohort), not at diagnosis; clonal evolution may have altered the mutation landscape
- Small subgroup sizes for rare PTCL subtypes (MEITL n=4, ALK+ ALCL n=6) limit subtype-specific conclusions
- No centralized treatment assignment; heterogeneity in salvage regimens limits OS interpretation
- No germline sequencing to distinguish somatic from germline *TP53* alterations
- Median follow-up of 26 months may be insufficient to assess long-term OS differences
