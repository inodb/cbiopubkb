## Summary

Proof-of-principle prospective study of cfDNA-based disease monitoring in 44 newly diagnosed endometrial cancer (EC) patients at MSK, using paired tumor sequencing (MSK-IMPACT, 468 genes) and high-sensitivity liquid biopsy (MSK-ACCESS, 129 genes, UMI-based molecular barcoding). Serial plasma samples were collected pre-operatively, post-operatively, and every 6 months for up to 24 months. Key findings: cfDNA levels correlate with surgical stage; pre-operative ctDNA is detected in 22% of patients (primarily advanced-stage and aggressive-histology cases); 92% of tumor-matched mutations are detected at baseline cfDNA; and serial ctDNA tracking during follow-up mirrors disease progression and treatment response with a lead time over clinical detection in 2/6 recurrent patients. Baseline and post-surgery ctDNA positivity are independently associated with significantly reduced progression-free survival (p<0.001 and p=0.014, respectively), establishing cfDNA as a prognostic and monitoring biomarker in EC.

## Key Findings

### F1: cfDNA concentration correlates with surgical stage and histologic aggressiveness in endometrial cancer

**Finding:** EC patients with stage III/IV disease had significantly higher pre-operative cfDNA concentrations than stage I patients (Wilcoxon rank sum test, p=0.028). Median cfDNA was 2.68 ng/mL for stage IA, 4.05 ng/mL for stage IB, 4.78 ng/mL for stage III, and 11.61 ng/mL for stage IV. cfDNA concentrations were numerically (though not statistically, p>0.1) higher in aggressive histologic subtypes — carcinosarcoma and high-grade carcinomas — compared to endometrioid EC.

**Detail:** Total cfDNA concentration (not tumor-derived fraction) was used as the first-level biomarker, measured from pre-operative blood draws. The correlation with stage is consistent with findings in other tumor types and likely reflects tumor burden/volume and/or vascular invasion. The lack of statistical significance for histologic subtype is attributable to small sample sizes in aggressive subtype groups (carcinosarcoma n=6, high-grade carcinoma n=4). MRI-based staging (performed in 34/44 patients) aligned with final surgical stage in 29/34 cases, supporting MRI as an imaging correlate of cfDNA burden. The most commonly mutated genes in primary tumors (by MSK-IMPACT): TP53 (52%), PTEN (50%), PIK3CA (50%), ARID1A (36%), FBXW7 (29%).

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, PTEN, PIK3CA, ARID1A, FBXW7 · Endometrial carcinoma (endometrioid, serous, carcinosarcoma)

### F2: MSK-ACCESS detects 92% of tumor-informed mutations at baseline pre-operative cfDNA, with 100% concordance at follow-up

**Finding:** Of 38 somatic mutations identified in primary tumors that were also covered by the MSK-ACCESS panel, 35 (92%) were detected in baseline/pre-operative cfDNA and 38 (100%) were detected at follow-up timepoints. Clonal mutations in primary tumors were all detected in cfDNA when present in the MSK-ACCESS panel. The tumor-informed genotyping approach (requiring ≥2 duplex consensus reads at known somatic mutation sites) achieved high sensitivity for ctDNA detection.

**Detail:** MSK-ACCESS employs unique molecular identifiers (UMIs/molecular barcoding) enabling duplex sequencing at high depth (median 25,611× raw, 1,340× duplex coverage). The high sensitivity is enabled by the tumor-informed ("genotyping") approach — querying specific positions known to be mutated in the matched tissue sample — rather than de novo variant calling. One exception was case CD28 (stage IB mixed EC with isolated tumor cells), where clonal CDKN2A and TERT promoter mutations were absent at baseline but detected later, while a subclonal TP53 mutation was detected — suggesting sub-threshold tumor fraction at baseline for clonal mutations in this case. Baseline ctDNA was detected in 8/36 (22%) patients with sequencing data; all 8 had advanced-stage disease, high tumor volume, and/or aggressive histology. None of the low-grade stage I endometrioid EC patients had detectable baseline ctDNA.

### F3: ctDNA tracks disease progression and therapy response in serial samples with lead time over clinical detection in 2 patients

**Finding:** In 6/25 patients (24%) with serial cfDNA sequencing, ctDNA was detected during follow-up. Changes in mutant allele fractions (MAFs) and ctDNA fraction in serial plasma samples closely mirrored disease progression and treatment response. In 2 of the 6 recurrent patients, ctDNA elevation preceded clinically detected recurrence, demonstrating a lead-time advantage for cfDNA-based surveillance.

**Detail:** The ctDNA fraction varied from 0.7% to 2.0% in most baseline-positive cases, with two outlier cases: CD22 (stage IVB serous carcinoma, ctDNA fraction 5.8%) and CD23 (stage IVB carcinosarcoma with 2,608 cm³ tumor burden, ctDNA fraction 74.6%) — both patients died within 31 months. Spatial heterogeneity between primary tumor and extra-uterine sites was observed in 4 stage III/IV cases with cfDNA and extra-uterine biopsy data. In case CD20, PIK3CA and KRAS mutations subclonal in the primary tumor became clonal in sentinel lymph nodes, and were detected in cfDNA — suggesting liquid biopsy captures the dominant metastatic clone. In case CD22, an ARID1A truncating mutation was shared across the primary tumor, multiple extra-uterine sites, and cfDNA, establishing it as the trunk mutation. cfDNA was generally more representative of the combined mutation landscape across all tumor sites than any single extra-uterine biopsy.

### F4: Baseline and post-surgery ctDNA positivity are significantly associated with reduced progression-free survival

**Finding:** Presence of ctDNA at baseline (pre-operatively) was significantly associated with reduced PFS (p<0.001). Post-surgery ctDNA positivity was also significantly associated with reduced PFS (p=0.014, landmark analysis at 51 days post-surgery). Of 44 EC patients, 11 developed recurrent or persistent disease over a median follow-up of 33 months (range 0.3–44 months).

**Detail:** Landmark analysis at 51 days post-surgery (the longest post-surgery cfDNA collection interval) accounts for the time-dependent nature of post-surgery cfDNA measurement, ensuring the association is not confounded by the timing of blood draw. The strong prognostic signal from baseline ctDNA (p<0.001) reflects enrichment of ctDNA-positive cases among advanced-stage, high-volume, and aggressive-histology tumors — all established adverse prognostic factors. Post-surgery ctDNA positivity (p=0.014) provides additional value beyond surgical stage alone, as post-operative ctDNA clearance presumably reflects completeness of surgical resection and tumor biology. These data position cfDNA as a potential stratification biomarker for adjuvant therapy decisions in EC, especially in identifying high-risk patients among those with early-stage disease.

### F5: cfDNA captures the combined mutation landscape of multi-site disease better than any single tissue biopsy

**Finding:** In 4 stage III/IV patients with matched primary tumor, extra-uterine site (lymph nodes, peritoneal implants, fallopian tube), and cfDNA data, liquid biopsy at baseline captured mutations from across all tumor sites, including mutations present only in extra-uterine metastatic sites but absent in the primary tumor. In contrast, individual extra-uterine biopsies reflected only local clonal composition.

**Detail:** In case CD20, PIK3CA, KRAS, and ARID1A mutations present across both the primary and extra-uterine lymph nodes were all detected in cfDNA. Site-specific mutations (AGO2, FOXA1) were restricted to two sentinel lymph nodes and detected in cfDNA, suggesting shedding from those sites. In case CD26, two different PIK3CA hotspot mutations were present — one in the primary tumor only, one shared across primary, extra-uterine, and cfDNA — and a TP53 splice site restricted to the primary tumor was detected in cfDNA, demonstrating the sensitivity of the assay. These findings establish cfDNA as a spatially comprehensive tumor sampling tool superior to single-site biopsy for detecting the full mutational landscape of multi-site disease, with implications for treatment selection and resistance monitoring.

## Open Questions

- Can post-surgery ctDNA positivity guide adjuvant therapy decisions in EC, particularly in identifying high-risk early-stage patients who would benefit from chemotherapy or radiation beyond standard clinical staging?
- Is the detection sensitivity of MSK-ACCESS sufficient for early-stage EC (stage IA endometrioid), where ctDNA levels are below current detection thresholds in all tested patients?
- Do POLE-ultramutated or MSI-H EC subtypes have distinct cfDNA kinetics (e.g., higher baseline mutation burden in plasma) that require adjusted interpretation?
- Can longitudinal cfDNA monitoring detect the emergence of resistance-conferring mutations (e.g., PIK3CA, TP53) that would guide therapy switching in recurrent EC?
- Would a broader cfDNA panel (e.g., MSK-IMPACT 468 genes rather than 129-gene MSK-ACCESS panel) detect additional clinically relevant mutations currently missed due to panel overlap limitations?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=44) with only 11 recurrences; statistical power for subgroup analyses (e.g., by histologic subtype or molecular subtype) is limited
- Enrichment of aggressive histologic subtypes beyond their population prevalence (14% carcinosarcoma vs. typical ~5%) limits generalizability to a population-based EC cohort
- The 129-gene MSK-ACCESS panel covers only a subset (129/468) of MSK-IMPACT genes; mutations in the 339 non-overlapping genes cannot be tracked in cfDNA
- COVID-19 pandemic impacted some 18- and 24-month blood draws, potentially affecting recurrence detection at those timepoints
- The study is a proof-of-principle without a control arm; prospective randomized trials are needed to demonstrate clinical utility of cfDNA-guided management
