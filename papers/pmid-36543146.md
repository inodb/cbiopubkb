## Summary

This prospective MSK study profiled 1,313 patients with bladder urothelial carcinoma by MSK-IMPACT, performed whole exome sequencing (WES) of 22 primary-metastasis pairs, and expanded to 148 targeted-sequenced matched primary-metastatic pairs to characterize genomic concordance. An additional 45–123 patients had plasma cell-free DNA (cfDNA) profiling via MSK-ACCESS. The study reveals that bladder cancer is characterized by early branched evolution leading to high lesion-to-lesion genomic heterogeneity: on average, only 42% of mutations are shared between primary and metastatic sites (WES), and 23% of potentially actionable alterations (FGFR3, PIK3CA, TSC1, ERBB2) are discordant between primary and metastatic tumors. ARID1A mutations, when discordant, are exclusively found in metastatic samples (16% discordant rate among ARID1A-mutant cases). cfDNA analysis identifies 20% of targetable mutations not detected in any tumor specimen, suggesting it is complementary to tumor tissue for guiding therapy. Data are available in cBioPortal as `paired_bladder_2022`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Actionable genomic alterations are discordant in 23% of primary-metastatic urothelial cancer pairs

**Finding:** Among 119 matched primary-metastatic MSK-IMPACT pairs, the concordance for known/likely oncogenic alterations is 85% overall. However, 23% of potentially actionable mutations in *FGFR3*, *PIK3CA*, *TSC1*, and *ERBB2* are discordant between primary and metastatic sites — meaning profiling only the primary tumor would miss actionable targets in the metastatic lesion (or vice versa) in nearly 1 in 4 patients.

**Detail:** Specific discordance rates: ERBB2 38%, PIK3CA 27%, TSC1 17%, FGFR3 9%. FGFR3 discordance is clinically critical given FDA-approved erdafitinib for FGFR2/3-altered metastatic urothelial carcinoma. In patients with discordant FGFR3 status, the actionable alteration was exclusive to the metastatic sample in both cases with discordant FGFR3, both of whom had received intervening systemic therapy. This suggests archival primary tissue will fail to detect actionable mutations in up to 35% of metastatic bladder cancer patients.

### F2: ARID1A mutations are acquired during metastatic progression and are exclusive to metastatic sites when discordant

**Finding:** In both WES (3 cases) and MSK-IMPACT (16% of ARID1A-mutant pairs) analyses, ARID1A mutations — when discordant — are exclusively found in the metastatic tumor, never only in the primary. Multi-region resampling of discordant primary tumors and manual BAM review confirm absence of subclonal ARID1A mutations in primary samples.

**Detail:** ARID1A mutation frequency increases with tumor stage: 14% in low-grade non-invasive, 19% in high-grade non-invasive, 25% in high-grade invasive, and 28% in metastatic urothelial carcinoma, consistent with a late-arising driver role in metastatic progression. ARID1A is an SWI/SNF component and has been proposed as a biomarker of ICI sensitivity (immune checkpoint) and EZH2 inhibitor sensitivity, making its selective metastatic acquisition particularly relevant for treatment planning.

### F3: Bladder cancer exhibits early branched evolution with low primary-metastasis mutational concordance (~42%)

**Finding:** WES of 22 primary-metastasis pairs reveals that on average only 42% of all somatic mutations are shared (range 6–84%), despite similar tumor mutational burdens (TMB) between sites (p=0.1, Wilcoxon). Phylogenetic analysis shows an early-branched evolution pattern with large numbers of private mutations at both primary and metastatic sites.

**Detail:** The concordance of shared mutations correlates weakly with time between specimen collection (R²=0.17, p=0.053), and excluding patients with intervening treatment does not change this. Dominant mutational signatures are similar between primary and metastatic lesions, indicating similar mutagenic processes but parallel evolution from an early common ancestor. This branched architecture, rather than a linear model, underpins the high discordance of actionable alterations.

### F4: Plasma cfDNA identifies 20% of targetable alterations not detected in any tumor biopsy

**Finding:** Among 45 patients with matched primary tumor, metastatic tumor, and cfDNA, 20% of targetable mutations (OncoKB levels 1–4) were identified exclusively by cfDNA analysis and not by either tumor biopsy. Expanding to 123 patients with any tumor + cfDNA: 60% of targetable alterations were concordant, 17% exclusive to cfDNA only, and 23% exclusive to tumor.

**Detail:** A dramatic case illustrates convergent FGFR3 evolution: cfDNA detected 3 oncogenic FGFR3 hotspot mutations and an FGFR3-TACC3 fusion, while the metastatic biopsy detected only one. On-treatment cfDNA monitoring identified acquired FGFR3 resistance mutations (N540S, K650E, V553M) in a patient on erdafitinib, including a novel putative gatekeeper mutation. This demonstrates cfDNA's complementary role for both baseline targetable alteration detection and acquired resistance monitoring.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR3`, `FGFR3-TACC3` (fusion) · Urothelial carcinoma

**Data availability:** `partial`

### F5: Chromatin-modifying gene mutation frequencies vary by grade and stage in bladder cancer

**Finding:** Among the 1,313 MSK-IMPACT-profiled patients, chromatin-modifying gene mutations are common but show differential enrichment by stage: TP53 (q<0.001) and RB1 (q<0.001) are more frequent in high-grade/metastatic tumors, while FGFR3 (q<0.001) and PIK3CA (q=0.04) are more frequent in low-grade/stage tumors. ARID1A is the only chromatin modifier more commonly mutated in high-grade invasive and metastatic samples.

**Detail:** The frequency of chromatin-modifying gene mutations (KDM6A, ARID1A, CREBBP, etc.) suggests that many arise early as initiating events in benign-appearing urothelium (field effect) and are thus commonly shared between primary and metastatic tumors. The stage-specific patterns of TP53/RB1 and FGFR3/PIK3CA suggest distinct molecular pathways for muscle-invasive vs. non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer.

## Open Questions

- Should FGFR3 testing for erdafitinib eligibility use metastatic (fresh) biopsy rather than archival primary tissue? Should cfDNA be added as a standard co-test?
- Does ARID1A acquisition during metastatic progression functionally drive invasion/metastasis, and can its selective presence in metastases be leveraged for therapeutic targeting?
- Are FGFR3-TACC3 fusions reliably detectable by cfDNA, and how do acquired resistance mutations evolve clonally under FGFR inhibitor pressure?
- Does early branched evolution in bladder cancer explain the lower success rate of targeted therapies in bladder cancer vs. lung cancer?
- Can multi-region or liquid biopsy profiling improve patient selection for precision oncology approaches in metastatic urothelial carcinoma?

## Limitations

- Variable time intervals between primary and metastatic biopsy collection and heterogeneous intervening treatments introduce confounding in concordance analyses
- Analysis is limited to DNA-level alterations; epigenetic, transcriptomic, and tumor microenvironment differences between primary and metastatic sites are not captured
- cfDNA-tumor interval variability may contribute to apparent discordance in some cases
- WES pairs (N=22) are relatively small for statistical power; larger paired WES studies are needed to confirm subclonal ARID1A dynamics
- cfDNA MSK-ACCESS covers only 129 genes, potentially missing discordant alterations in non-covered genomic regions
- Retrospective design with enrichment for advanced/metastatic patients may not reflect typical localized bladder cancer populations
