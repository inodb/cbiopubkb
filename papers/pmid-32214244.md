## Summary

Comprehensive reanalysis of 18,116 TCGA samples across 33 cancer types for microbial reads (bacteria, archaea, viruses), using Kraken k-mer matching and SHOGUN alignment-based pipelines. After extensive decontamination (removing up to 92.3% of data in the most stringent settings), cancer-type-specific microbial signatures persisted in both tissue and blood. Machine learning (stochastic gradient boosting) models trained on normalized microbial abundance data distinguished one cancer type vs. all others and tumor vs. normal with high AUROC in most cancer types. Blood-based microbial DNA (mbDNA) signatures remained discriminatory even for stage Ia–IIc cancers and tumors without genomic alterations detectable by commercial ctDNA panels (Guardant360, FoundationOne Liquid). An independent plasma cell-free validation cohort (n=169: 100 cancer patients with prostate, lung, or melanoma; 69 HIV-negative healthy controls) demonstrated cancer-vs-healthy and cancer-type-vs-cancer-type discrimination using plasma-derived cell-free microbial nucleic acids under gold-standard microbiology controls. The TCGA studies linked in cBioPortal provide the host genomic context for these samples but not the microbial data itself.

## Key Findings

### F1: Unique microbial signatures exist in tissue and blood for most cancer types, surviving extensive decontamination

**Finding:** After normalizing 7.2% non-human TCGA reads with Voom-SNM and applying three levels of in silico decontamination (up to 92.3% data removed), stochastic gradient boosting ML models achieved high AUROC for one-cancer-type-vs-all-others discrimination across 33 cancer types and tumor-vs-normal comparisons in 15 cancer types. Performance persisted across independent Kraken and SHOGUN pipelines and across independent sequencing centers.

**Detail:** Of 6.4×10¹² total TCGA reads, 7.2% were non-human; 35.2% of those were bacterial/archaeal/viral, with 12.6% resolved at genus level by Kraken. Batch effects (sequencing center, platform) dominated raw PCA but were substantially corrected by Voom-SNM normalization, after which "disease type" explained the largest variance component. Models were validated by: (i) random split of TCGA into two independent halves with cross-testing; (ii) single-center analyses (HMS for WGS, UNC for RNA-Seq); (iii) genomic alignment-filtered Kraken data; (iv) SHOGUN on 13,517 samples. Models relying on spiked pseudo-contaminants (identified retrospectively) were flagged as potentially unreliable (DLBC, MESO).

### F2: Known microbial cancer associations validated — Fusobacterium in GI cancers, HPV in cervical/HNSCC, HBV in liver, EBV in gastric

**Finding:** Ecologically expected cancer-microbe associations were confirmed as a biological validation of the pipeline: Fusobacterium genus was enriched in all GI cancers vs. blood-derived normals (P≤3.3×10⁻¹¹); Alphapapillomavirus (HPV) was the top discriminatory feature for CESC and was differentially abundant in HPV-positive vs. HPV-negative HNSCC; Orthohepadnavirus (HBV) was enriched in hepatitis-B-positive LIHC; Lymphocryptovirus (EBV) was enriched in EBV-classified STAD molecular subtype.

**Detail:** For COAD solid-tissue normal samples (n=70), SourceTracker2 trained on HMP2 body-site data identified stool as the primary contributor (mean 20.17% fractional contribution), confirming gut origin of part of the community. SKCM primary tumors did not show significant stool contribution, as expected. Importantly, Helicobacter pylori was not differentially enriched in STAD primary tumors vs. adjacent normals (P=0.72), consistent with H. pylori's known role as a field effect carcinogen rather than an intratumoral microbe. Microcystis (hepatotoxic cyanobacterium) was the top feature for LIHC discrimination, a novel finding.

**Genes / cancer types:** (Fusobacterium, Alphapapillomavirus, Orthohepadnavirus, EBV/Lymphocryptovirus, Faecalibacterium) · COAD, CESC, HNSCC, LIHC, STAD

### F3: Blood-based microbial DNA signatures discriminate cancer types including early-stage (Ia-IIc) tumors

**Finding:** ML models trained on TCGA whole-blood WGS microbial profiles (not plasma) achieved high cancer-type discrimination. Critically, blood mbDNA models retained discriminatory power for stage Ia–IIc cancers specifically, and for patients whose tumors showed no detectable alterations on Guardant360 (covering ~70% of patients) or FoundationOne Liquid ctDNA assays. This suggests mbDNA may be complementary to ctDNA for early-stage cancer detection.

**Detail:** A new set of ML models was built excluding stage III/IV blood samples to test stage Ia-IIc discrimination — performance remained high for most cancer types tested. For ctDNA-negative patients (those without ≥1 targetable alteration on commercial panels), mbDNA models still separated cancer types, though limited by the retrospective and whole-blood (vs. plasma) nature of TCGA samples. CESC, KIRP, and LIHC models potentially incorporated pseudo-contaminants, flagging them as less trustworthy. The mbDNA approach is distinct from ctDNA: it measures non-human microbial nucleic acids rather than human tumor-derived DNA.

### F4: Plasma cell-free microbial DNA discriminates cancer from healthy and cancer type from cancer type in independent validation

**Finding:** In an independent validation cohort (100 stage III-IV cancer patients: 59 prostate, 25 lung, 16 melanoma; 69 HIV-negative healthy controls), plasma cell-free mbDNA under gold-standard microbiology controls discriminated cancer vs. healthy (bootstrapped AUROC high; 500-iteration 70/30 splits) and cancer type vs. cancer type by leave-one-out ML. Prostate vs. lung and prostate vs. melanoma were highly separable; melanoma showed lower performance, consistent with TCGA blood results.

**Detail:** This validation used plasma-derived cell-free DNA (cfDNA) — the clinically standard liquid biopsy matrix — rather than whole blood, processed in a single batch with single library prep method to minimize technical variation. Negative blanks, DNA concentration normalization, and the same Kraken+Voom-SNM pipeline as TCGA were applied. Age and sex normalization was required (permutation tests confirmed confounding). SHOGUN-based reanalysis showed concordant performance. The SKCM cohort (n=16) was too small for reliable pairwise discrimination at full cohort size, but subsampled PC and LC cohorts matched to SKCM size remained separable (mean AUROC=0.891). This is the first prospective demonstration of cancer discrimination using plasma cell-free microbial nucleic acids with full contamination controls.

### F5: Decontamination is essential but introduces tradeoffs — stringent filtering may remove true biological signals

**Finding:** A multi-tiered decontamination framework identified 377 putative contaminant genera (including 283 in silico from DNA concentration correlation + 94 from reagent "blacklist"). Removing all putative contaminants discarded up to 92.3% of total reads. Within-cancer-type comparisons (tumor vs. normal) often maintained or improved performance with stringent decontamination; cancer-vs-cancer comparisons that depend on tissue-site-specific microbiome knowledge degraded with over-decontamination.

**Detail:** The decontamination pipeline used two orthogonal approaches: (i) correlation of taxon read fractions with DNA/RNA concentration (inverse correlation = contaminant signal) across 8 sequencing centers separately; (ii) manual "blacklist" of genera commonly found in negative reagent blanks. Notably, 19.1% of the reagent blacklist genera were also identified by the data-driven approach. Pathobionts (e.g., Mycobacterium) were manually re-allowed after literature review. Pseudo-contaminants spiked into the dataset tracked through the pipeline confirmed that true biological signals were mostly preserved. The COAD solid-tissue normal body-site attribution analysis showed that progressive decontamination improved ecological plausibility up to a threshold, then abolished it — demonstrating the biological cost of over-filtering.

## Open Questions

1. Does blood mbDNA originate from intratumoral microbes, gut bacterial translocation, or circulating microbial DNA from distant sites — and does the source affect diagnostic utility?
2. Can plasma cell-free microbial signatures discriminate early-stage (Ia–IIc) cancers in prospective, controlled clinical studies with matched healthy controls?
3. Are the discriminatory microbial genera ecologically meaningful (i.e., residing within tumors or tumor microenvironment) or statistical associations driven by systemic host immune changes altering commensal communities?
4. How does mbDNA-based discrimination perform across different racial/ethnic populations, given known microbiome diversity?
5. Can microbial signatures be combined with ctDNA or protein biomarkers to create a multi-analyte early cancer detection assay?

## Limitations

- TCGA blood samples used whole blood or buffy coat (not plasma) — not directly comparable to clinical ctDNA assays which use plasma.
- It is impossible to determine from sequencing data alone whether detected mbDNA comes from live microbes, dead bacteria, or host cells with intracellular bacteria.
- Retrospective TCGA samples lack gold-standard microbiology controls; contamination cannot be fully excluded despite rigorous in silico decontamination.
- Genus-level taxonomic resolution limits biological mechanistic interpretation; species/strain-level identification would be more informative.
- The melanoma plasma validation cohort (n=16) was too small for reliable discrimination; larger prospective cohorts are needed.
- Stage III-IV cancers in the plasma validation study may not represent the early-detection clinical scenario where mbDNA diagnostics would be most impactful.
- The 2022 Gihawi et al. reanalysis raised concerns about potential residual human read contamination in the TCGA microbial assignments, with active methodological debate ongoing.
