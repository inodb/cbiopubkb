## Summary

This 2012 study performed whole-exome sequencing (WES) on 55 primary DLBCL tumor-normal pairs (49 analyzable after quality filtering) to map the landscape of somatic mutations. Using the MutSig algorithm, the authors identified 58 significantly mutated genes at FDR <0.1, including known drivers (MYD88, CARD11, EZH2, CREBBP) and newly implicated genes (MEF2B, MLL2, BTG1, GNA13, ACTB, P2RY8, PCLO, TNFRSF14). A key mechanistic finding is that BCL2 point mutations in BCL2/IgH-rearranged patients arise from AID-mediated somatic hypermutation (SHM) at the IgH locus — predominantly synonymous, with strong negative selection against BCL2 loss-of-function in functional BH domains. Finally, a statistical approach to prioritize functionally relevant but low-frequency mutations identified KRAS, BRAF, and NOTCH1 as candidate drivers in a subset of patients. This study is the founding genomic characterization of the `dlbc_broad_2012` cBioPortal study.

## Key Findings

### F1: 58 significantly mutated genes in DLBCL, including 8 not previously linked to DLBCL

**Finding:** MutSig analysis of 49 DLBCL exomes identified 58 significantly mutated genes (q1 ≤0.1), validating known drivers and adding 8 newly implicated genes: MEF2B, MLL2 (KMT2D), BTG1, GNA13, ACTB, P2RY8, PCLO, and TNFRSF14.

**Detail:** Mean nonsynonymous mutation rate 3.2/Mb — higher than CLL or AML (<1/Mb) and multiple myeloma (1.3/Mb), consistent with DLBCL's GC origin and AID activity. Known top drivers: MYD88 (MyD88 L265P dominant; NF-κB signaling), CARD11 (BCR signaling), EZH2 (Y641 mutations; epigenetic), CREBBP (acetyltransferase). Novel drivers include GNA13 (G-protein α subunit; GC retention/B-cell migration), TNFRSF14 (HVEM; B-cell immune escape), MEF2B (transcription factor), and ACTB (cytoskeleton). MutSig applied three normalization factors: sample-specific mutation rate, nonsynonymous:synonymous ratio, and median DLBCL gene expression — improving specificity over simple frequency counting.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MYD88`, `CARD11`, `EZH2`, `CREBBP`, `MEF2B`, `MLL2`, `BTG1`, `GNA13`, `ACTB`, `P2RY8`, `PCLO`, `TNFRSF14` · DLBCL

### F2: BCL2 point mutations in BCL2/IgH-rearranged DLBCL are driven by AID-mediated somatic hypermutation with strong negative selection against loss of function

**Finding:** BCL2 point mutations co-occur specifically with BCL2/IgH rearrangements and are primarily synonymous, strongly enriched in WRCY AID-target motifs — consistent with off-target AID activity from the IgH locus. Nonsynonymous BCL2 mutations cluster outside the functionally critical BH domains, indicating negative selection against BCL2 loss-of-function.

**Detail:** Comprehensive analysis of WRCY motif enrichment at BCL2 mutation sites confirms AID as the mutational mechanism. Synonymous vs. nonsynonymous BCL2 mutation ratio is elevated compared to background, indicating purifying selection against coding changes in BH1-4 domains. This is an important mechanistic distinction: BCL2 is not a classical loss-of-function tumor suppressor in DLBCL — rather, SHM maintains BCL2 protein function while creating a high somatic mutation burden at the locus. The finding clarifies why BCL2 mutations in DLBCL do not impair anti-apoptotic function.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BCL2` (and IgH locus) · DLBCL (BCL2/IgH-rearranged subset)

### F3: MLL2 (KMT2D) and other chromatin modifiers are recurrently mutated, indicating widespread epigenetic dysregulation in DLBCL

**Finding:** MLL2 (KMT2D, a histone H3K4 methyltransferase) is significantly mutated in DLBCL (identified with EZH2 and CREBBP). Together, these three epigenetic regulators suggest that epigenetic dysregulation — affecting H3K4/K27 methylation and histone acetylation — is a core oncogenic mechanism.

**Detail:** EZH2 Y641 mutations (gain-of-function, causing H3K27 hypermethylation) were previously established; this paper adds MLL2 truncating mutations (loss of H3K4 methyltransferase activity) and CREBBP loss-of-function (reduced H3 acetylation). The co-occurrence of activating (EZH2) and inactivating (MLL2, CREBBP) chromatin modifier mutations creates opposing epigenetic pressures, likely converging on silencing of B-cell differentiation and activation programs. This pattern motivates EZH2 inhibitor development (tazemetostat now FDA-approved for EZH2-mutant follicular lymphoma and DLBCL).

**Genes / cancer types:** `EZH2`, `MLL2` (`KMT2D`), `CREBBP` · DLBCL

### F4: GNA13, TNFRSF14, and P2RY8 implicate GC B-cell egress and immune escape as novel mechanisms in DLBCL

**Finding:** Three newly identified significantly mutated genes — GNA13 (Gα13, sphingosine-1-phosphate receptor signaling), TNFRSF14 (HVEM, inhibitory immune receptor), and P2RY8 (purinergic receptor) — share a common role in germinal center B-cell retention and immune evasion, defining a novel oncogenic module distinct from BCR/NF-κB signaling.

**Detail:** GNA13 mutations cluster in functionally important domains; GNA13 loss impairs GC B-cell egress (S1P-mediated). TNFRSF14/HVEM mutations disrupt co-inhibitory signaling (BTLA, CD160 axis), enabling immune escape. P2RY8 constrains GC B-cell movement and proliferation; mutations may allow GC cells to proliferate unchecked. These genes define a pathway distinct from the ABC/GCB cell-of-origin model. BTG1 (anti-proliferative adaptor) and PCLO (synaptic scaffold, involved in CREB pathway) were also newly implicated.

**Genes / cancer types:** `GNA13`, `TNFRSF14`, `P2RY8`, `BTG1`, `PCLO` · DLBCL

### F5: Statistical prioritization identifies KRAS, BRAF, and NOTCH1 as low-frequency but likely driver mutations in subsets of DLBCL

**Finding:** Using a complementary MutSig algorithm variant (q2) designed to detect functionally relevant mutations at conserved or clustered sites regardless of frequency, KRAS, BRAF, and NOTCH1 were identified as candidate drivers in small subsets of DLBCL patients — nominating RAS/MAPK and NOTCH signaling as additional DLBCL oncogenic pathways.

**Detail:** q2 algorithm identifies mutations at COSMIC database positions and functionally conserved residues even in single patients. KRAS G12 and BRAF V600 hotspot mutations found in DLBCL, supporting RAS/MAPK pathway activation. NOTCH1 mutations (often truncating in the PEST domain, as in CLL and T-ALL) implicate Notch signaling in a subset of DLBCLs. These findings have therapeutic implications: BRAF V600E in DLBCL could confer sensitivity to BRAF inhibitors (vemurafenib). This represents an early example of statistically rigorous low-frequency driver discovery.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `BRAF`, `NOTCH1` · DLBCL (subsets)

## Open Questions

- Do GNA13, TNFRSF14, and P2RY8 mutations define a distinct molecular subtype (e.g., overlapping with the EZB or ST2 genetic subtypes described in later LymphGen classifications)?
- Can BCL2/SHM mutation patterns (synonymous enrichment, WRCY motif) serve as a diagnostic or prognostic biomarker for BCL2/IgH-driven DLBCL independent of FISH?
- Are the BRAF V600E-mutant DLBCL patients identified here sensitive to BRAF/MEK inhibition — and how do co-occurring BCR pathway mutations modulate response?
- How does GNA13 loss interact with S1P receptor modulator treatments (fingolimod) — and does this alter lymphoma cell homing or survival?
- Does NOTCH1 mutation in DLBCL correlate with Notch pathway activation signatures and sensitivity to gamma-secretase inhibitors?

## Limitations

- Small discovery cohort (n=49 analyzable); power to detect genes mutated in <5% of patients is limited.
- Six samples excluded for high stromal contamination — selection bias toward high-purity tumors, potentially underrepresenting some microenvironment-rich subtypes.
- Cell-of-origin (ABC vs. GCB) subtype not uniformly available for all patients; subtype-specific driver enrichment analysis was limited.
- WES does not capture structural variants (translocations, BCL2/IgH), requiring orthogonal FISH data.
- AID motif analysis is correlative; direct functional validation of BCL2 SHM mechanism was not performed in this study.
- 2012 study predates comprehensive cell-of-origin molecular classifications (LymphGen); some novel gene findings have since been reclassified into known subtypes.
