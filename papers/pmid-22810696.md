## Summary

Landmark TCGA genome-scale analysis of 276 primary colorectal carcinomas using WES (n=224), low-pass WGS (n=97), SNP CNA arrays, DNA methylation, mRNA, and miRNA profiling. Key findings: 16% of CRCs are hypermutated (three-quarters MSI-H, one-quarter via somatic MMR/POLE mutations); non-hypermutated colon and rectal tumors are genomically indistinguishable despite different anatomy; WNT pathway alterations affect >93% of all tumors; and 24 significantly mutated genes are catalogued including novel targets SOX9, FAM123B, and ARID1A. New actionable findings include ERBB2 amplification (4%), IGF2 amplification (7%), and NAV2–TCF7L1 fusions. MYC transcriptional activation is a near-universal downstream event. Full data available in `coadread_tcga_pub` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Two hypermutation classes with distinct biology — MSI-H/CIMP and somatic MMR/POLE

**Finding:** 16% of CRCs are hypermutated (>12 mutations/Mb). Three-quarters have MSI-H with MLH1 promoter methylation and CIMP; one-quarter have somatic mutations in MLH1, MLH3, MSH2, MSH3, MSH6, PMS2, or POLE without MSI-H. These two classes follow different oncogenic trajectories.

**Detail:** MSI-H/CIMP tumors show near-diploid genomes, right colon enrichment, BRAF V600E mutations, and markedly elevated frameshift mutation rates in mononucleotide repeat-containing genes (50× vs. non-hypermutated). Somatic MMR/POLE tumors are the six cases with the highest overall mutation burdens and lack CIMP. TP53 mutations are less frequent in hypermutated tumors (20% vs. 60%) and APC is less commonly mutated (51% vs. 81%). Hypermutated cancers have a better prognosis; mutation rate may be a superior prognostic indicator to MSI status alone.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, POLE, MLH3, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, BRAF, TP53, APC · Colorectal carcinoma

### F2: Colon and rectal tumors are genomically indistinguishable in non-hypermutated cases

**Finding:** Among non-hypermutated tumors, colon and rectal cancers show no significant differences in copy-number alteration patterns, DNA methylation profiles, mRNA expression, or miRNA profiles. This supports treating them as a single genomic entity for molecular analysis and potentially for systemic therapy decisions.

**Detail:** Hypermutated tumors are enriched in the right/ascending colon (three-quarters of hypermutated cases). Right colon tumors also show higher hypermethylation than left colon or rectal tumors — possibly reflecting their midgut embryonic origin. Among non-hypermutated tumors from all sites, genomic patterns are uniform, supporting merged analysis and shared therapeutic trials.

### F3: WNT pathway alteration is near-universal; 16 distinct altered genes identified

**Finding:** WNT signaling is altered in 93% of non-hypermutated and 97% of hypermutated CRCs. Beyond APC (mutated/deleted in ~80%) and CTNNB1 (activating mutations), recurrently altered WNT genes include SOX9 (novel frameshift/nonsense, 9 cases), FAM123B/WTX (X-linked negative regulator, loss-of-function), TCF7L2 (deletion/mutation in 12%), FBXW7, AXIN2, DKK family, and FZD10 (overexpressed up to 100× in 17% of cases).

**Detail:** SOX9 has not previously been described as a cancer driver; all nine mutations in non-hypermutated CRC are frameshift or nonsense, indicating loss of function. SOX9 is transcriptionally repressed by WNT signaling and its protein facilitates β-catenin degradation — forming a feedback loop. FAM123B mutations are almost all loss-of-function, consistent with its role as a negative WNT regulator. MYC transcriptional activation (and repression of MYC targets) is near-universal by PARADIGM analysis, confirming MYC as a central effector downstream of WNT deregulation.

### F4: IGF2 amplification (7%) and ERBB2 amplification (4%) as novel actionable targets

**Finding:** A recurrent 100–150 kb focal amplification at 11p15.5, found in 7% of CRCs, targets IGF2 and miR-483. IGF2 amplification/overexpression (including 15% without amplification) is nearly mutually exclusive with PIK3CA/PIK3R1 mutations and PTEN deletion, placing it in the PI3K signaling axis. ERBB2 amplification (4%, 17q21.1) is separately identified as a drug target.

**Detail:** IGF2 overexpression is up to 100-fold above normal in some cases; miR-483 (embedded in IGF2) is co-amplified and co-expressed. INS and TH in the same locus are not overexpressed, supporting IGF2/miR-483 as the functional targets. IRS2 (chromosome 13, frequently gained) encodes the IGF2–IGF1R–PI3K adaptor; IRS2 overexpression is mutually exclusive with IGF2 overexpression (P=0.04), suggesting a common PI3K-activating axis. ERBB2 amplification has precedent for trastuzumab sensitivity from breast and gastric cancer trials.

**Genes / cancer types:** IGF2, miR-483, IRS2, ERBB2, PIK3CA, PIK3R1, PTEN · Colorectal carcinoma

### F5: Key pathway co-occurrence: RAS+PI3K alterations co-occur in one-third of tumors; dual inhibition needed

**Finding:** 55% of non-hypermutated CRCs have alterations in KRAS, NRAS, or BRAF (mutually exclusive within MAPK). Co-occurrence of RAS/MAPK alterations with PI3K pathway alterations (PIK3CA/PIK3R1 mutation or PTEN deletion) occurs in one-third of tumors (P=0.039), suggesting that monotherapy targeting either pathway may be insufficient and that dual inhibition strategies should be tested.

**Detail:** ERBB family receptor alterations (mutations or amplifications) are present in 13% of non-hypermutated and 53% of hypermutated tumors, with recurrent ERBB2(V842I) and ERBB3(V104M) mutations. TGF-β pathway alterations affect 27% of non-hypermutated and 87% of hypermutated cases (TGFBR1/2, ACVR2A, ACVR1B, SMAD2/3/4). TP53 biallelic inactivation in 59% of non-hypermutated cases; ATM and TP53 mutations show trend toward mutual exclusivity.

## Open Questions

- Does the near-universal MYC transcriptional activation identify a therapeutic vulnerability for direct or indirect MYC inhibition strategies?
- Can the IGF2/IRS2/PI3K axis be effectively targeted in the ~22% of CRCs (7% amplified + 15% overexpressed without amplification) that overexpress IGF2?
- What is the biological significance of miR-483 co-amplification with IGF2?
- Do ERBB2 amplification or recurrent ERBB2/ERBB3 mutations in CRC predict trastuzumab response as they do in breast and gastric cancers?
- Why are most hypermutated CRCs concentrated in the right/ascending colon, and what does this imply for screening and treatment strategies?

## Limitations

- Prospective collection without survival data available at time of analysis; aggressiveness surrogates (stage, lymph node status) used instead
- Low-pass WGS performed on only a subset (n=97); structural variant detection incomplete for the full cohort
- mRNA expression profiling used Agilent arrays (not RNA-seq) for the primary expression analysis, with RNA-seq added subsequently
- DNA methylation used HumanMethylation27 arrays, which cover a limited fraction of CpG sites compared to later 450K/850K arrays
- Batch effects between sample collections were detected but not corrected computationally
