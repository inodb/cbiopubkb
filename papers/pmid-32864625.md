## Summary

A phase I/II trial (NCT01870505) of alpelisib (PI3Kα inhibitor) plus aromatase inhibitor in 51 patients with HR+, HER2− PIK3CA-mutant metastatic breast cancer (MBC) achieved a clinical benefit rate of 52% and ORR of 19%, with benefit exclusively in *PIK3CA*-mutant patients. Longitudinal ctDNA and tumor sequencing identified *PTEN* loss-of-function alterations in 25% of evaluable patients (pre- and post-treatment) and expanding *ESR1* activating mutations as the two primary resistance mechanisms. These data establish *PTEN* loss and *ESR1* mutations as clinically actionable resistance biomarkers for alpelisib-based therapy.

## Key Findings

### F1: Clinical activity is restricted to PIK3CA-mutant patients

**Finding:** Clinical benefit was observed exclusively in *PIK3CA*-mutant patients (CBR 57.5%, 23/40), with zero clinical benefit in the six evaluable *PIK3CA* wild-type patients (p = 0.025).

**Detail:** The overall CBR was 52% (23/44 patients; 95% CI 37–68%). ORR was 19% (6/31 patients with measurable disease; 95% CI 7–37%), including 5 partial responses and 1 complete response. Median TTF for all patients was 14 weeks (range 2–169). PIK3CA mutation distribution followed the expected pattern for HR+ MBC with kinase domain (H1047R/L/Y) and helical domain (E545K/A, E542K) mutations predominating.

### F2: PTEN loss-of-function is a recurrent mechanism of alpelisib resistance

**Finding:** *PTEN* loss-of-function alterations were identified in 25% of evaluable patients (8/32 ctDNA specimens), both pre-existing (3 patients with rapid progression) and acquired post-treatment (5 patients without pre-treatment PTEN loss).

**Detail:** Three patients with pre-treatment PTEN loss (missense A126S, truncating R130*, or homozygous deletion confirmed in tumor tissue) all had rapid disease progression (no clinical benefit). Five patients acquired *PTEN* alterations post-treatment (including 2 truncating mutations, 4 previously established loss-of-function missense mutations, and 1 homozygous deletion) with no evidence of these alterations in pre-treatment ctDNA or tumor tissue. The co-occurrence of clonal *PIK3CA* mutation with *PTEN* loss (n=2 patients at baseline) was uniformly associated with no clinical benefit and early progressive disease (week-8 radiographic assessment).

### F3: ESR1 activating mutations are associated with intrinsic resistance to alpelisib plus AI

**Finding:** Activating *ESR1* mutations (D538G, Y537S) conferring AI resistance were identified exclusively in baseline tumor samples of non-responding patients and were significantly associated with lack of clinical benefit (p = 0.0067).

**Detail:** Six patients had baseline *ESR1* mutations (D538G, n=3; Y537S, n=3), all with no clinical benefit. No patients with clinical benefit had baseline *ESR1* mutations. In serial ctDNA analysis, *ESR1* and *PIK3CA* were the two genes with significantly increasing variant allele fractions (VAF) between pre- and post-treatment samples (both q = 0.0278, Wilcoxon signed-rank). ESR1 Y537S conferred AI resistance confirmed in MCF7 xenograft models.

### F4: Maculopapular rash is the primary dose-limiting toxicity of alpelisib plus AI

**Finding:** Maculopapular rash was the most common grade 3 adverse event, occurring in 33% of patients (17/51), and was more prevalent with continuous vs. intermittent dosing schedules.

**Detail:** Grade 3 rash occurred in 50% (7/14) of patients on continuous dosing arms (A, B) vs. 35% (13/37) of patients on intermittent dosing arms (C, D). The 5-days-on/2-days-off schedule (Arm D) established an MTD of 350 mg alpelisib with daily exemestane. The overall DLT rate was lower on intermittent (64%, 9/14 continuous) vs. continuous dosing. No grade 4 toxicities observed; no treatment-related deaths. Rash onset typically within 9–15 days and responded to alpelisib interruption plus antihistamines/steroids.

### F5: Clonality of PIK3CA mutation does not reliably predict clinical benefit

**Finding:** The majority of *PIK3CA* mutations were clonal (70%, 31/39 mutations in 32 evaluable pre-treatment tumors), but the association between *PIK3CA* clonality and clinical benefit was inconclusive due to limited statistical power (p = 0.37).

**Detail:** Three of five patients with only subclonal *PIK3CA* mutations had no clinical benefit, but sample sizes were too small to draw firm conclusions. ctDNA concordance with tumor NGS was high: 89% (34/38) of tumor *PIK3CA* mutations were detectable in ctDNA pre-treatment, validating ctDNA as a reliable sampling method.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Would prospective *PTEN* screening (tumor or ctDNA) before alpelisib + AI selection reduce non-beneficial exposures?
- Does co-occurring *PIK3CA* + *PTEN* loss predict resistance to all PI3K inhibitors, or only isoform-selective agents?
- Can serial ctDNA *ESR1* VAF kinetics (rising on AI) predict futility of adding alpelisib vs. switching to SERD?
- Is there a threshold PIK3CA allele frequency or clonal fraction below which alpelisib benefit is diminished?
- Do patients with acquired *PTEN* loss benefit from subsequent mTOR inhibitor–based salvage therapy?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Small trial (n=51); insufficient power for many subgroup analyses including PIK3CA clonality and PTEN co-occurrence.
- Non-randomized phase I/II design; no control arm; CBR/ORR cannot be attributed to alpelisib vs. AI alone.
- ctDNA panel covers only 73 genes; may miss some resistance mechanisms outside the targeted panel.
- Single-site needle biopsies may not capture spatial tumor heterogeneity; ctDNA partially addresses this.
- ESR1 association analysis (n=6 with mutations) is underpowered despite statistical significance.
