## Summary

In 430 patients with microsatellite-stable (MSS) unresectable metastatic colorectal cancer (mCRC) profiled by MSK-IMPACT and treated with first-line chemotherapy at MSK, APC mutation location — not simply APC mutation presence — is the key genomic determinant of outcome. N-terminal APC mutations (truncating before amino acid 1400, retaining ≤2 of 3 β-catenin-binding 20R repeats) associate with significantly longer OS and PFS, while C-terminal APC mutations (truncating after aa 1400, with weaker Wnt activation) associate with worse outcomes and enrich for concurrent KRAS, BRAF, and PI3K pathway alterations. Contrary to prior hypotheses, DDR pathway alterations — whether germline or somatic — do not associate with survival or predict platinum sensitivity in MSS mCRC, and large-scale state transition (LST) scores do not indicate homologous recombination deficiency in this cohort.

## Key Findings

### F1: N-terminal APC mutations associate with significantly better OS and PFS in MSS mCRC

**Finding:** In MSS unresectable mCRC (n=430), only APC and BRAF alteration frequencies significantly varied by duration of chemotherapy response. Multivariate analysis confirmed APC mutation associates with longer PFS (HR 0.68, p<0.001) and OS (HR 0.56, p<0.001) compared to no Wnt pathway alteration or alterations in other Wnt genes. The benefit is specifically driven by N-terminal APC mutations (truncation before aa 1400).

**Detail:** APC cutpoint analysis across 50-aa intervals: survival differences first emerged at aa 600 and became statistically significant at aa 1400. N-terminal side APC mutations: significantly longer OS and PFS vs. C-terminal or no APC alteration. Dual N-terminal mutations had similar outcomes to single N-terminal mutations; outcomes with dual C-terminal or mixed N+C mutations matched single C-terminal mutations. Survival differences by APC site persisted after adjusting for primary tumor sidedness. BRAF alterations (right-sided enrichment) associated with shorter OS in parallel analysis.

### F2: C-terminal APC mutations are enriched for concurrent RAS and PI3K pathway alterations, explaining poorer outcomes

**Finding:** CRCs with C-terminal APC mutations (weak Wnt activation) show selective pressure for additional mitogenic alterations: KRAS/BRAF alterations in 72% and 13% vs. 46% and 2% for N-terminal APC cases; PIK3CA alterations in 28% vs. 11%; PTEN alterations in 12% vs. 3% (all p<0.001 or q<0.01). About three-quarters of C-terminal APC tumors carry at least one additional mitogenic pathway activation.

**Detail:** Mechanistic hypothesis: "just right" Wnt activation (N-terminal APC mutations fully abrogate β-catenin regulation without additional pathway co-drivers) is sufficient for transformation, while distal/C-terminal APC mutations produce suboptimal Wnt signaling that drives selection for concurrent RAS/PI3K pathway activation. This multi-pathway co-activation makes C-terminal APC tumors more aggressive. Tumor sidedness: N-terminal APC mutations more common in left-sided colon/rectal primaries; C-terminal mutations more common in right-sided colon tumors (consistent with right-sided enrichment for KRAS/BRAF/PIK3CA).

### F3: DDR pathway alterations do not associate with survival or predict oxaliplatin sensitivity in MSS mCRC

**Finding:** DDR pathway alterations were not associated with PFS or OS across the cohort, nor with PFS in patients receiving oxaliplatin-containing first-line chemotherapy (HR 1.1, 95% CI 0.7–1.6, p=0.74). LST scores did not vary significantly between CRCs with or without somatic or germline DDR pathway alterations, and no patient had an LST score >15 (a proposed HRD cut-off).

**Detail:** DDR pathway alterations were enriched in MSI-H mCRC (consistent with known biology) but were not enriched in a functionally meaningful way in MSS tumors. LST analysis per chromosome arm showed no evidence of genomic scarring from homologous recombination deficiency. A previous finding that BRCA alterations in non-BRCA cancer types often lack biallelic loss (second allele retention) is consistent: DDR alterations in MSS CRC may be co-passengers without functional consequence. Germline DDR alterations analyzed separately also showed no survival association.

### F4: Highly activating Wnt alterations (CTNNB1 mutations, RNF43 alterations) also associate with poor prognosis and co-occur with RAS pathway

**Finding:** CTNNB1 (β-catenin) activating mutations and RNF43 alterations (ligand-dependent Wnt dependency) both associate with poor prognosis in MSS mCRC, and both commonly co-occur with RAS pathway alterations (63% with CTNNB1, 71% with RNF43 alterations). This further supports the model that suboptimal Wnt activity selects for additional oncogenic co-drivers.

**Detail:** CTNNB1 activating mutations cause constitutive β-catenin nuclear translocation (very high Wnt) and poor outcomes despite this; RNF43 loss-of-function leads to ligand-dependent Wnt signaling — a distinct activation mode from APC loss. Both these non-APC Wnt alterations phenocopy C-terminal APC behavior in terms of co-occurring RAS pathway activation and outcomes. This broadens the concept from APC-specific to general Wnt pathway activation quality: incomplete or excessive Wnt activation both appear to select for additional mitogenic pathway activation.

### F5: APC mutation site provides a clinically actionable prognostic biomarker independent of standard prognostic genes

**Finding:** Multivariate models incorporating established prognostic genes (BRAF, KRAS) and Wnt pathway genes (APC, AMER1, CTNNB1, RNF43) confirm N-terminal APC alteration as an independent predictor of longer OS (HR 0.56) and PFS (HR 0.68). This effect is independent of primary tumor sidedness — a known prognostic factor in mCRC.

**Detail:** The multivariate model included: Wnt pathway gene status (APC N-terminal, APC C-terminal, AMER1, CTNNB1, RNF43), KRAS, BRAF, and tumor sidedness. N-terminal APC remained independently significant (p<0.001 for both OS and PFS). The prognostic effect of APC mutation site on outcomes is mechanistically distinct from the prognostic effect of BRAF or RAS mutations and survives mutual adjustment. This makes APC mutation site a candidate for routine reporting in mCRC sequencing results alongside KRAS, BRAF, and MSI status.

## Open Questions

- Does N-terminal APC mutation status predict benefit from specific chemotherapy regimens (e.g., FOLFOX vs. FOLFIRI vs. CAPOX) beyond general first-line chemotherapy?
- Can APC mutation site be incorporated into prognostic nomograms for mCRC to improve patient stratification in clinical trials?
- Does the co-mutation pattern (N-terminal APC + TP53 vs. C-terminal APC + KRAS) define distinct tumor biology that can be targeted (e.g., KRAS G12C inhibitors specifically in C-terminal APC/KRAS co-mutant tumors)?
- Does the DDR-negative finding extend to newer DDR-targeted agents (e.g., PARP inhibitors in BRCA-altered CRC patients with co-occurring biallelic loss)?
- How does microsatellite instability status interact with APC mutation site — do MSI-H CRCs with N- vs. C-terminal APC differ in ICI response?
- Are the survival differences by APC mutation site reproduced in the TCGA CRC cohort (TCGA coadread) which is available in cBioPortal?

## Limitations

- Retrospective, single-institution study (MSK) in a highly selected patient population treated with first-line chemotherapy; results may not generalize to community practice
- MSK-IMPACT is a targeted panel, not WGS/WES; structural variants in APC (e.g., large deletions) not comprehensively captured
- Only 430 MSS mCRC patients — subgroup analyses (e.g., stratified by KRAS allele or tumor sidedness) are underpowered
- LST scores are imperfect HRD surrogates (not equivalent to genome-wide scar signatures like HRD-sum); absence of HRD by LST does not completely rule out functional HRD
- Short Letter format limits reporting detail; full methods, supplementary data, and forest plots for DDR subgroups are limited
- Study predates widespread KRAS G12C inhibitor availability; impact of APC mutation site on novel targeted therapy outcomes not assessed
