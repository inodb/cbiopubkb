## Summary

The definitive study establishing genomic concordance between matched primary and metastatic CRC using next-generation sequencing. Analysis of 69 matched primary-metastasis-normal trios with MSK-IMPACT (230 cancer genes, mean 692× depth) demonstrates 100% concordance for KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF, and 93% concordance for all significantly mutated genes in CRC. The small residual discordance is partially explained by intra-tumor heterogeneity (multi-region sequencing resolved 17/46 discordant mutations). Importantly, in KRAS/NRAS/BRAF-WT tumors, metastasis-specific RTK-RAS activating events (MAP2K1 Q56P, EGFR amplification) are occasionally found, with potential clinical implications. WGS of 4 patient trios validates the targeted sequencing results. The study provides a foundational clinical justification for basing anti-EGFR therapy eligibility decisions on primary tumor sequencing in most CRC patients.

## Key Findings

### F1: KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF mutations are 100% concordant between primary and metastatic CRC tumors

**Finding:** Of 434 total non-synonymous somatic mutations detected, no discordant mutations were observed in KRAS, NRAS, or BRAF across 69 matched pairs. Among all TCGA-defined significantly mutated genes in non-hypermutated CRC (n=247 mutations), concordance was 93% (229/247), with 79% of all mutations shared overall.

**Detail:** The 18 private mutations among significantly mutated genes involved APC (n=7), PIK3CA (n=5), SMAD4 (n=3), and TP53 (n=3). The majority of private APC mutations (5/7) were secondary mutations in cases that already shared a clonal APC mutation—consistent with ongoing, subclonal APC inactivation. One primary-specific APC mutation was detectable at low frequency in the metastasis by comparative analysis. Another was lost in the metastasis via chromosomal deletion (phenotypically equivalent loss). WGS of 4 trios validated all IMPACT-detected mutations and revealed no additional mutations in the 230 profiled genes, supporting the equivalency of targeted sequencing and WGS for clinically actionable detection.

### F2: Multi-region sequencing reveals subclonal heterogeneity explains most discordant mutations

**Finding:** Sequencing of 97 additional spatially separate regions from 22 patients (46 discordant mutations) resolved 17/46 (37%) discordant mutations—including 12/22 (55%) mutations originally detected only in the metastasis but subsequently found to be subclonal in the primary tumor. This suggests the reported concordance rate (79% overall, 93% for significantly mutated genes) is itself an overestimate of true discordance.

**Detail:** Multi-region sampling demonstrates that apparent primary-metastasis discordance largely reflects inadequate sampling of intra-tumor heterogeneity within the primary tumor. Convergent phenotypic evolution was observed: two patients harbored independent mutations in the same gene (TP53: R248Q vs. Y163*; PIK3CA: E542K vs. E545K)—indicating strong selection for inactivation of the same gene by parallel routes. Four of the private PI3K pathway events were found in additional spatial regions, confirming subclonal status. No significant difference in concordance between tumors with different primary locations (right colon, left colon, rectum) or resection timing (concurrent vs. subsequent).

### F3: Metastasis-specific RTK-RAS activating events emerge in KRAS/NRAS/BRAF-WT tumors

**Finding:** In 3 patients lacking KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF mutations, RTK-RAS pathway activating events private to the metastasis were identified: (1) MAP2K1 (MEK1) Q56P oncogenic mutation (confirmed to hyperactivate ERK signaling by transfection assay, equivalent to known activating mutations); (2) EGFR high-level amplification (confirmed by FISH) in one patient with only 7p polysomy in the primary; (3) SMAD3 missense mutation in the transcriptional regulation domain + SMAD4 mutation in a third patient.

**Detail:** MAP2K1 A106T (a second private metastatic MEK1 mutation) did not affect signaling by western blot, establishing functional hierarchy within detected variants. The EGFR high-level amplification was metastasis-specific; FISH of the primary showed only 7p polysomy without focal amplification. These findings demonstrate that in RAS/RAF-WT tumors, additional RTK-RAS activating events can arise specifically in metastases, representing occult drivers that would be missed if only the primary tumor were profiled. Two SMAD4 pathway mutations co-occurring in one patient illustrate parallel TGF-β pathway inactivation in the metastatic clone.

### F4: Prior chemotherapy reduces apparent tumor heterogeneity between primary and metastatic tumors

**Finding:** Among concurrently resected tumor pairs, chemonaive patients were more likely to have discordant (private) mutations (22/28, 79%) compared to pre-treated patients (11/24, 46%; chi-square p = 0.01). Chemonaive tumors also had a higher total mutation count (mean 6.6 vs. 5.8, p = 0.1). Primary tumors were larger in chemonaive patients (mean 5.2 vs. 4.2 cm, p = 0.02). Among patients with subsequently resected metastases (metastasis received treatment alone), primary-only mutations were more common than in patients with concurrently resected, pre-treated tumors (64% vs. 25%, p = 0.03).

**Detail:** Proposed explanations: general tumor debulking and decreased heterogeneity from effective treatment eliminate heterogeneous subclones, reducing apparent discordance. This implies that chemotherapy acts as an evolutionary filter, selecting for clones present in both primary and metastatic sites. The concordance benefit of chemotherapy suggests that pre-treatment tumor sequencing may underestimate clonal complexity at the time of clinical decision-making, while post-treatment sequencing may overestimate concordance. No disease-specific survival difference was observed between patients with concordant vs. discordant mutation profiles.

### F5: Targeted sequencing (230 genes) is sufficient as a clinical diagnostic surrogate for WGS in CRC

**Finding:** WGS of 4 patient trios (two concordant, two discordant by IMPACT) confirms all IMPACT-detected mutations (≥5% allele frequency) and reveals no additional mutations in the 230 IMPACT-profiled genes. Concordance by IMPACT is a reliable surrogate for genome-wide concordance: the two IMPACT-concordant patients showed 80–83% overall shared mutations by WGS, and the two IMPACT-discordant patients showed only 25–38% shared genome-wide mutations.

**Detail:** IMPACT-concordant patients (14 and 54) showed 86–87% concordance in non-synonymous protein-coding mutations and 91% in loss-of-function mutations. WGS identified a novel RASA1 double-nonsense mutation (C372* and R679*, mutually exclusive with KRAS/NRAS) in IMPACT-concordant patient 14—a negative RAS regulator altered in 2% of TCGA CRC; addition of RASA1 capture probes to subsequent IMPACT version was prompted by this finding. IMPACT-discordant patients (3 and 19) harbored multiple loss-of-function mutations private to metastasis (6 and 9, respectively) and COSMIC-recurrent metastasis-specific events—consistent with genuine molecular divergence, not sampling artifact.

## Open Questions

- In the KRAS/NRAS/BRAF-WT patients with metastasis-specific MAP2K1 or EGFR amplification, would anti-EGFR therapy (cetuximab/panitumumab) or MEK inhibitor have clinical activity if the metastasis had been biopsied first?
- Does the chemotherapy-induced reduction in apparent heterogeneity translate to improved primary tumor representativeness for clinical molecular profiling decisions in the current era of multi-drug regimens and anti-EGFR therapies?
- With the substantial expansion of MSK-IMPACT from 230 to 505 genes, do larger gene panels reveal additional concordance patterns or identify more metastasis-specific events in PI3K and other pathways?
- Can serial liquid biopsy (ctDNA) track the clonal evolution from primary to metastatic CRC in real time, capturing events like MAP2K1 or EGFR amplification that arise specifically in metastases?
- Do discordant cases (those with 25–38% shared mutations at WGS level) represent biologically distinct disease from concordant cases in terms of treatment response or survival?

## Limitations

- All patients are microsatellite-stable—findings may not apply to MSI-H CRC where hypermutation and immune-related evolution are dominant
- No anti-EGFR therapy exposure prior to resection; this is the key limitation for interpretation, since anti-EGFR selection of RAS subclones is known and would reduce concordance
- Sample size (n=69) limits power to detect low-frequency discordant events at specific genes
- Single-institution cohort (MSK); enriched for surgical candidates at a tertiary center
- WGS validation limited to 4 patients—while results validate IMPACT, the discordant cases may be over-represented in WGS selection
- FFPE tissue used for additional multi-region samples; quality variability may inflate apparent discordance
- Long time intervals between primary and metastatic resection could introduce temporal divergence not present in concurrent resections
