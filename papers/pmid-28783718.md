## Summary

The MET500 cohort: the first comprehensive integrative sequencing study of 500 adult patients with metastatic solid tumors across 30+ primary cancer types and 22+ biopsy sites (Mi-Oncoseq Program, University of Michigan). All cases underwent paired tumor-normal WES (180× tumor / 120× normal, mean 62% tumor content) and RNA-seq (868 libraries). Most prevalent somatically altered genes: TP53 (53.2%), CDKN2A (16%), PTEN (15.8%), RB1 (13.6%), PIK3CA (13.4%), AR (12.6%), KRAS (10.2%). Mutation burden is significantly elevated in metastases vs. primary TCGA tumors for most cancer types. 12.2% of patients harbor pathogenic germline variants, 75% in DNA repair genes. Gene fusion analysis identified putative driver fusions in 39.8% of cases including 8 novel pathogenic fusions. Transcriptome profiling reveals two metastatic subtypes (proliferative and EMT-like) and enables immune microenvironment characterization. Data available in `metastatic_solid_tumors_mich_2017` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Mutation burden is significantly elevated in metastases vs. primary tumors, especially in low-burden cancer types

**Finding:** For the majority of cancer types in the MET500, the number of somatic mutations is significantly higher in metastases compared to matched primary TCGA cohorts. This enrichment is most pronounced in tumor types with low primary-stage mutation burden, such as prostate and adrenal cancer.

**Detail:** The most frequently altered tumor suppressors across all cancers are TP53 (53.2%), CDKN2A (16%), PTEN (15.8%), and RB1 (13.6%). Tumor suppressors are altered across many cancer types (pan-cancer), while oncogenes tend to be cancer type-specific (e.g., AR in prostate, GNAS in pancreatic). The increase in mutation burden in metastases is accompanied by increased frequency of the most widely mutated genes. Prostate cancer showed the most dramatic enrichment from primary to metastatic stage. Average 7.8 biologically relevant alterations per mCRPC case.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, CDKN2A, PTEN, RB1, PIK3CA, AR, KRAS · Pan-cancer (metastatic solid tumors)

### F2: 12.2% of metastatic patients harbor pathogenic germline variants; 75% in DNA repair genes

**Finding:** 63 presumed pathogenic germline mutations (PPGMs) were identified in 61 patients (12.2%) across 18 genes. 75% involve DNA repair pathway genes: MUTYH (16%), BRCA2 (14%), CHEK2 (14%), BRCA1 (8%). The odds of any PPGM in metastatic cancer significantly exceed ExAC population rates (OR=3.00, P=1×10⁻¹³).

**Detail:** Outside DNA repair, PPGMs in APC (9.5%), MITF (8%), and HOXB13 (5%) were also identified. Of 61 PPGM carriers, 30 (49%) had a somatic second-allele aberration (LOH or somatic mutation) in the same gene, meeting criteria for biallelic inactivation and molecular evidence of pathogenicity. Germline-enriched genes in MET500 vs. ExAC: BRCA1, BRCA2, APC, CHEK2, MITF, MLH1, NBN, RB1. High germline DNA repair variant rate supports routine germline testing and genetic counseling for all metastatic cancer patients, regardless of primary cancer type.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRCA1, BRCA2, CHEK2, MUTYH, ATM, APC, MITF, HOXB13, MLH1, NBN, RB1 · Pan-cancer metastatic

### F3: Driver gene fusions in 39.8% of metastatic cases; 8 novel actionable fusions identified

**Finding:** RNA-seq analysis of 868 libraries from 496 patients identified putative pathogenic fusions in 39.8% of cases. 138 were activating fusions; 103 were inactivating tumor suppressor fusions. Novel fusions involving targetable kinases (FGFR, BRAF, ALK) were identified with novel partners extending actionability to new cancer types.

**Detail:** Activating fusions classified as DNA-binding (n=88), protein kinases (n=29), and signal transducers (n=21). The most commonly inactivated tumor suppressor genes via fusion are NF1 (n=18), TP53 (n=11), PTEN (n=11), and RB1 (n=6). Eight novel pathogenic fusions identified include activated FGFR, BRAF, and ALK fusions with new partners; GREB1-NR4A3 in uterine leiomyosarcoma; POC5-PRKD1 in polymorphous low-grade adenocarcinoma; CIC-CITED1 in high-grade sarcoma; two NOTCH fusion classes with different gamma-secretase inhibitor sensitivity. ETS fusions remain prevalent (56.7%) in prostate cancer.

**Genes / cancer types:** NF1, TP53, PTEN, RB1, FGFR1, BRAF, ALK, NOTCH2, NR4A3, PRKD1 · Pan-cancer (uterine sarcoma, tongue carcinoma, high-grade sarcoma, prostate)

### F4: Two transcriptional subtypes of metastatic cancer — proliferative and EMT-like — with distinct hallmarks

**Finding:** Unsupervised transcriptomic analysis of MET500 patients identifies two principal metastatic subtypes: a proliferative subtype (associated with increased metabolism and systemic stress response) and an EMT-like subtype (associated with inflammatory signatures). These subtypes are mutually exclusive and weakly associated with primary tissue of origin or biopsy site.

**Detail:** 25 non-redundant experimental "meta-signatures" were computationally derived from bulk RNA-seq; hierarchical clustering reveals four canonical cancer hallmarks: immune response, EMT, proliferation, and metabolism. Metastatic tumors show global increase in proliferation, stress-response, and metabolism vs. normal tissues. EMT and immune response can be either up- or down-regulated. Metastatic samples show significant dedifferentiation vs. primary tumors, with tissue-specific gene expression maintained only partially, and liver biopsies are the only biopsy site showing distinct transcriptional segregation. The mutual exclusivity between proliferative and EMT gene sets is stronger in metastases than primary tumors, suggesting these states are more entrenched in advanced disease.

**Genes / cancer types:** (Transcriptome-wide) · Pan-cancer metastatic

### F5: Immune microenvironment landscape in metastatic cancer; immunologically active patients have higher PD-L1, mutational burden, and CD8+ T-cells

**Finding:** A RNA-seq-based leukocyte infiltration score (MImmScore) demonstrates that immune infiltration is strongly determined by tumor type (preserved from primary to metastatic stage) and identifies immunologically distinct subtypes within each tumor type. Immunologically active patients show elevated PD-L1, higher mutational burden, and higher predicted immunotherapy response scores.

**Detail:** Kidney cancer, lung cancer, and melanoma remain immune-infiltrated at metastatic sites; breast and prostate cancer are less immunogenic in both primary and metastatic settings. Unsupervised clustering reveals TIL composition differences: M2 macrophages highly prevalent (immunosuppressive); M1 largely absent; a small CD8+ T-cell-dominant cluster (TIL-5) identified. T-cell clonality (TCRβ deep-sequencing) correlates with total T-cell number and inversely with Treg/cytotoxic T-cell ratio. Highly mutated samples have more infiltrating T-cells. Three immunological categories defined: silent, partially active, fully active (based on TIL-5/APC-1/Tcell-1 cluster membership). Active patients have significantly higher PD-L1, HLA, granzyme expression, and a melanoma-derived immunotherapy response score.

**Genes / cancer types:** CD274 (PD-L1), CD80, CD86, CD28, HLA genes, GZMB · Pan-cancer metastatic

## Open Questions

- What fraction of the 12.2% germline pathogenic variant carriers in MET500 had prior genetic counseling, and how many had actionable findings that changed management?
- Do the two metastatic transcriptional subtypes (proliferative vs. EMT-like) have distinct sensitivities to specific systemic therapies beyond immunotherapy?
- Can the RNA-seq-based MImmScore and T-cell clonality profiling reliably predict checkpoint inhibitor benefit across diverse tumor types?
- What drives the stronger mutual exclusivity between proliferative and EMT states in metastatic tumors vs. primary tumors — is this therapy-induced or intrinsic to metastatic biology?
- Why do liver biopsies cluster transcriptionally distinct from other metastatic sites — organ microenvironment or tumor selection?

## Limitations

- No matched primary-metastatic tumor pairs from individual patients; inter-patient comparisons with TCGA primary cohorts confounded by patient selection and treatment history
- Demographics skewed: 92% Caucasian, 51.6% male; limited generalizability to non-white populations
- RNA-seq performed on two platforms (poly(A)+ and exome-capture) with potential batch effects; cross-platform normalization applied
- MImmScore and immune subtype analysis not validated prospectively for immunotherapy response prediction
- CUP cases (5%) were assigned by expression-based tissue-of-origin analysis; primary site misclassification possible
