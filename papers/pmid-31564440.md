## Summary

First translational study demonstrating that hyperpolarized (HP) [1-¹³C]pyruvate MRI can non-invasively quantify real-time pyruvate-to-lactate conversion in human prostate cancer and correlate it with Gleason grade and underlying molecular mechanisms. Twelve pre-prostatectomy prostate cancer patients (5 imaged twice, 17 total injections) underwent HP MRI followed by whole-mount pathology correlation. The study showed that HP lactate signal increases significantly with Gleason grade, that monocarboxylate transporter 1 (MCT1/SLC16A1) is the rate-limiting driver of this signal (confirmed by IHC and TCGA RNA-seq), and that PTEN loss is associated with the highest lactate levels. These results establish a mechanistic link between actionable genomic alterations and non-invasive metabolic imaging biomarkers.

## Key Findings

### F1: HP lactate increases significantly with prostate cancer Gleason grade

**Finding:** The maximum lactate-to-total-carbon ratio (Lac_max) was significantly lower in normal prostate tissue than in Gleason grade 3 tumor regions (p=0.0001) and grade ≥4 regions (p<0.0001), establishing HP lactate as a non-invasive imaging biomarker of tumor aggressiveness.

**Detail:** Spatial distribution of Lac_max maps co-localized with cancer regions identified by a pathologist on matched whole-mount histopathology slides. While lactate increased with higher Gleason grades, the study's sample size (12 patients) was insufficient to establish quantitative thresholds for distinguishing low- from high-grade tumors. Additionally, HP lactate did not correlate strongly with standard clinical markers (PSA, prostate volume, total tumor volume; R²<0.26 for best correlate, PSA), suggesting that metabolic flux provides orthogonal information beyond conventional clinical parameters.

### F2: HP MRI is reproducible within patients across test/retest injections

**Finding:** Five patients received two HP pyruvate injections (4 within 1 hour; 1 on a separate day), and time-to-max pyruvate (p=0.24) and time-to-max lactate (p=0.78) did not differ significantly between injections after arterial input function correction, establishing technical reproducibility of HP MRI in humans.

**Detail:** Pyruvate delivery time-to-max across patients ranged from 11.2–33.5 s post-injection (21.8±5.4 s), reflecting inter-patient perfusion differences. After correcting for arterial input function (internal pudendal artery), the range narrowed to 6.2–12.4 s (10.1±2.4 s), showing a bimodal distribution separated by the 4.9 s temporal resolution of the acquisition. Similarly, Lac_max did not differ significantly between test and retest injections (p=0.2). This reproducibility is comparable to variability reported in large ¹⁸F-FDG PET studies, validating HP MRI for longitudinal treatment monitoring.

### F3: MCT1 (SLC16A1) is the rate-limiting transporter driving HP lactate signal in prostate cancer

**Finding:** MCT1 protein expression by IHC correlated with HP lactate signal (Lac_max 60% higher in high-MCT1 vs. low-MCT1 regions; p=0.028), and TCGA RNA-seq data confirmed significant MCT1 and LDHA upregulation in prostate cancer versus matched normal tissue.

**Detail:** A threshold of ≥30% MCT1-positive staining per ROI was used to classify high vs. low MCT1 expression (Lac_max: 0.38±0.3 vs. 0.23±0.04). In the TCGA prostate cancer cohort, MCT1 (SLC16A1) and LDHA — the enzyme catalyzing lactate dehydrogenase forward reaction — were both significantly upregulated in cancer versus adjacent normal tissue. MCT1 facilitates pyruvate uptake from blood into the tumor cell, making it the rate-limiting step for HP pyruvate-to-lactate conversion. MCT4, which exports lactate, was not the primary determinant. Elevated MCT1 has been shown to associate with poor prognosis and high mortality in multiple cancers.

**Genes / cancer types:** SLC16A1 (MCT1), LDHA · Prostate adenocarcinoma

### F4: PTEN loss is associated with the highest HP lactate levels, linking genomics to metabolic imaging

**Finding:** Among regions with actionable somatic mutations identified by MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing, PTEN loss (2 homozygous, 3 heterozygous) was associated with the highest Lac_max values (P=0.059), while TMPRSS2-ERG fusion did not affect HP lactate (P=0.730).

**Detail:** PI3K pathway activation via PTEN loss is known to upregulate anaerobic glycolysis and lactate production. The near-significant trend (p=0.059) despite only 5 PTEN-loss ROIs demonstrates the potential of HP MRI to image the metabolic consequences of specific genomic alterations non-invasively. PTEN loss is associated with aggressive, high-Gleason-grade prostate tumors, and PI3K/AKT pathway activation downstream of PTEN loss may drive MCT1 upregulation and enhanced glycolytic flux. The absence of a TMPRSS2-ERG effect suggests this common prostate cancer fusion does not fundamentally reprogram pyruvate metabolism.

**Genes / cancer types:** PTEN, TMPRSS2-ERG · Prostate adenocarcinoma

### F5: HP MRI detects prostate lesions missed on conventional anatomic MRI

**Finding:** In at least one patient, HP lactate signal was co-localized with a pathologically confirmed prostate cancer lesion that was not visible on conventional T2-weighted or diffusion-weighted MRI, demonstrating that metabolic imaging provides orthogonal, complementary information.

**Detail:** Anterior apical prostate tumors — which are common, challenging to biopsy by standard transrectal approaches, and difficult to visualize on conventional MRI — represent a clinically important unmet need. The HP lactate signal in this case was detected by a region confirmed as tumor on whole-mount pathology but undetectable on standard multi-parametric MRI. This proof-of-concept case highlights the potential for HP MRI to improve detection of anteriorly located or small lesions, potentially guiding targeted biopsy and focal therapy.

## Open Questions

- Can HP MRI quantitatively distinguish Gleason grade 3 from grade ≥4 tumors with sufficient sensitivity and specificity at higher spatial resolution to guide focal therapy planning?
- Does MCT1 inhibition suppress HP lactate signal in vivo, and could this be used to monitor MCT1-targeted therapy response?
- Will HP pyruvate MRI prove useful for detecting biochemical recurrence or treatment response to androgen deprivation therapy or chemotherapy in larger prospective studies?
- Do patients with homozygous vs. heterozygous PTEN loss show a dose-dependent relationship with HP lactate signal that could distinguish these clinically distinct groups?
- Can HP MRI be integrated with multi-parametric MRI to create a combined staging tool that predicts pathological outcomes without tissue biopsy?

## Limitations

- Very small cohort (12 patients, 17 injections) limits statistical power for subgroup analyses (e.g., PTEN loss: only 5 ROIs)
- Spatial resolution (1×1×1.5 cm³ voxels) causes partial-volume effects when comparing to whole-mount pathology; smaller lesions are not reliably resolved
- HP MRI is technically demanding (requires specialized polarizer infrastructure, GMP-grade hyperpolarized agents, real-time acquisition) limiting generalizability
- Single time point imaging without longitudinal follow-up; no treatment monitoring data presented
- PTEN loss association with HP lactate did not reach statistical significance (p=0.059), requiring larger validation cohorts
