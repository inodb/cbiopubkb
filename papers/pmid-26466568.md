## Summary

Whole-genome sequencing of 56 neuroblastomas (high-risk n=39; low-risk n=17) with matched normals, extended by FISH and targeted sequencing in 161 additional tumors (total n=217), discovers recurrent genomic rearrangements at chromosome 5p15.33 that cluster 50 kb upstream of *TERT* in 31% of high-risk neuroblastomas. These rearrangements occur in a mutually exclusive fashion with *MYCN* amplification and *ATRX* mutations, and all converge on telomere lengthening as the defining molecular feature of high-risk neuroblastoma. Rearrangements juxtapose the *TERT* locus to strong super-enhancer elements translocated from other chromosomes, inducing massive chromatin remodeling and transcriptional activation of *TERT* without altering the core promoter. *TERT* rearrangements predict especially poor clinical outcome (5-year OS: 41±16%), comparable to *MYCN*-amplified tumors (54±10%). Together, TERT rearrangements, MYCN amplification, and ATRX mutations account for the large majority of high-risk neuroblastomas and provide a mechanistic molecular taxonomy of this deadly pediatric disease.

## Key Findings

### F1: Recurrent 5p15.33 rearrangements activate TERT in 31% of high-risk neuroblastomas, mutually exclusive with MYCN amplification and ATRX mutation

**Finding:** WGS identified recurrent genomic rearrangements clustering within a 100 kb region at 5p15.33, consistently 50 kb upstream of the *TERT* transcription start site, in 12/39 (31%) high-risk neuroblastomas. These rearrangements were absent in low-risk tumors (P=0.01) and occurred in a mutually exclusive fashion with *MYCN* amplifications (n=10) and *ATRX* mutations (n=7) (P=0.008). In the extended cohort (n=217), TERT rearrangements were present in 28 cases (13%), 27 of which were high-risk — including 22/65 (34%) MYCN-non-amplified high-risk neuroblastomas.

**Detail:** The structural diversity of rearrangements was large: balanced rearrangements, translocations with single copy number gain, focal high-level amplifications, and chromothripsis of chromosome 5 in 2 cases. Chromosomal partners were scattered across chromosome 5 (7 cases) and other chromosomes (5 cases). Despite this diversity, all rearrangements consistently brought foreign genomic sequences (carrying strong enhancer elements) into proximity with the *TERT* locus. The *TERT* coding sequence and core promoter were never directly disrupted. Identical rearrangements were detected in 4 tumor pairs at initial diagnosis and relapse, confirming stability during disease progression.

### F2: TERT rearrangements juxtapose the TERT locus to super-enhancer elements, causing massive chromatin remodeling

**Finding:** ChIP-seq in 3 TERT-rearranged tumors revealed strong H3K27ac and H3K4me1 peaks in the translocated regions immediately adjacent to rearrangement breakpoints, consistent with active super-enhancer elements. In all 3 cases, rearrangements juxtaposed *TERT* to enhancer clusters ranked among the strongest in the tumor genome. These features were absent in tumors lacking *TERT* rearrangements or *MYCN* amplification.

**Detail:** Active promoter marks (H3K4me3, H3K27ac) and transcription elongation marks (H3K36me3) spread to the *TERT* locus in TERT-rearranged cases, whereas the same locus was broadly marked by the repressive Polycomb mark H3K27me3 in control high-risk tumors. DNA methylation analysis showed consistent CpG hypermethylation across the *TERT* locus in TERT-rearranged and MYCN-amplified tumors, including a site (cg11625005) proximal to the core promoter previously associated with a disabled repressive element. This is a striking example of the "enhancer hijacking" mechanism — where structural rearrangements place proto-oncogenes under the control of tissue-inappropriate strong enhancers — later described broadly in cancer.

### F3: TERT mRNA is massively upregulated by rearrangements (not copy number gain), driving enzymatic telomerase activity

**Finding:** *TERT* mRNA expression was 92-fold higher in TERT-rearranged tumors than in low-risk tumors (RNA-seq), and significantly higher than MYCN-amplified tumors (P=0.028). Critically, TERT expression did not correlate with TERT copy number in rearranged tumors, and monoallelic TERT expression was detected in 5 evaluable tumors — indicating that the rearranged allele alone drives expression. Neuroblastoma cell lines with TERT translocations or MYCN amplification showed both elevated TERT mRNA and significantly increased enzymatic telomerase activity by TRAP assay, compared with cell lines lacking these alterations.

**Detail:** The mechanism is repositioning-driven transcriptional de-repression, not copy number amplification. Normal somatic cells maintain *TERT* in condensed, transcriptionally silent chromatin at 5p15.33; rearrangements break this silencing by bringing strong enhancers into the *TERT* neighborhood. The distance of the breakpoint from the *TERT* TSS did not affect expression levels, suggesting that enhancer activity operates at range. MYCN-amplified tumors activate *TERT* transcriptionally through a different mechanism (MYCN directly binds *TERT* promoter E-boxes), but both converge on the same phenotype of constitutive telomerase activity.

### F4: Three mutually exclusive telomere maintenance pathways define high-risk neuroblastoma subtypes with distinct outcomes

**Finding:** Integrating WGS, expression, and telomere length data reveals three mutually exclusive pathways of telomere lengthening in high-risk neuroblastoma: (1) TERT rearrangements → constitutive telomerase activation → short telomeres; (2) MYCN amplification → transcriptional TERT activation → short telomeres; (3) ATRX mutation → alternative lengthening of telomeres (ALT) → long telomeres. High-risk tumors lacking all three had long telomeres and evidence of ALT in cell lines. Low-risk tumors had telomere lengths similar to matched normals, suggesting inactive telomere maintenance.

**Detail:** ATRX-mutated tumors and high-risk tumors without TERT/MYCN had abundant telomere repeats by WGS telomere ratio analysis and telomere FISH. ALT was confirmed in ATRX-mutant cell line CHLA-90 and in 2 additional high-risk lines. 5-year overall survival: TERT-rearranged 41±16%, MYCN-amplified 54±10%, other high-risk 79±8%, low-risk/intermediate 98±2%. TERT rearrangements were independently prognostic for overall survival in multivariable Cox regression adjusting for stage and MYCN status. This three-pathway model suggests that targeting telomere maintenance (e.g., with telomerase inhibitors) may be broadly relevant in high-risk neuroblastoma regardless of mechanism.

### F5: TERT rearrangements are independent prognostic markers and potential therapeutic targets in high-risk neuroblastoma

**Finding:** In multivariable Cox regression for overall survival in patients ≥18 months at diagnosis (n=125), TERT rearrangement status was an independent poor prognostic factor after adjusting for stage and MYCN status. The 5-year event-free survival was significantly worse for TERT-rearranged patients (P=0.038) than MYCN-non-amplified high-risk patients without TERT rearrangements. Together, TERT rearrangements, MYCN amplification, and ATRX mutations identify molecular drivers in the large majority of high-risk neuroblastomas.

**Detail:** The identification of TERT as a unifying functional target — active telomerase in ~55% of high-risk cases (TERT-rearranged + MYCN-amplified), ALT in most remaining high-risk — motivates telomerase inhibitor development for neuroblastoma. Imetelstat (GRN163L) and other TERT-targeting agents have been explored in adult cancers; neuroblastoma may be a compelling pediatric indication given the high frequency of telomerase activation. FISH and targeted sequencing of the 5p15.33 locus offer feasible diagnostic methods for identifying TERT-rearranged cases in clinical samples.

## Open Questions

- Can telomerase inhibitors (e.g., imetelstat) achieve clinical activity in TERT-rearranged or MYCN-amplified neuroblastoma?
- Do ATRX-mutant neuroblastomas (ALT mechanism) respond differently to genotoxic chemotherapy than telomerase-active tumors?
- What specific enhancer elements are translocated to the TERT locus, and do they define tissue-of-origin dependencies?
- Can TERT rearrangement status be reliably detected in cfDNA or bone marrow aspirates for non-invasive monitoring?
- Does TERT rearrangement status predict response to anti-GD2 immunotherapy or ALK inhibitors in MYCN-non-amplified high-risk disease?
- Are TERT rearrangements acquired at disease onset or present in the earliest tumor progenitors?

## Limitations

- WGS discovery cohort limited to 56 tumors (39 high-risk); validation used FISH/targeted sequencing rather than WGS
- ChIP-seq performed in only 3 TERT-rearranged primary tumors; limited generalizability of chromatin remodeling data
- RNA-seq available for only a subset (n=54 total across subgroups) — expression analyses underpowered for subgroup comparisons
- Telomerase activity measured in cell lines, not primary tumors; results may not fully reflect in vivo biology
- FISH-based validation cannot detect all structural variant classes (e.g., cryptic inversions within chromosome 5)
- German patient cohort treated per GPOH protocols; may not generalize to other treatment regimens
- Survival analyses include patients across multiple decades (1991–2014) with evolving treatment standards
- ATRX ALT mechanism confirmed functionally in only one ATRX-mutant primary-derived cell line (CHLA-90)
