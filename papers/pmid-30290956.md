## Summary

This retrospective study at MSKCC compared 288 patients with primary (de novo) versus secondary (progressed from NMIBC) muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC) treated with cisplatin-based neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NAC) and radical cystectomy. Using MSK-IMPACT and TCGA whole-exome sequencing across 479 chemotherapy-naïve specimens, the study found that secondary MIBC has significantly lower pathologic response rates and worse survival after NAC, and that *ERCC2* missense mutations—which sensitize tumors to cisplatin—are enriched in primary MIBC (12% vs 1.3%). Data are publicly available via cBioPortal (blca_msk_tcga_2020).

## Key Findings

### F1: Secondary MIBC has lower pathologic response rate to cisplatin NAC

**Finding:** Patients with secondary MIBC (progressed from prior NMIBC) achieved pathologic response (≤pT1N0) at significantly lower rates than primary MIBC after cisplatin-based NAC.

**Detail:** Pathologic response rate was 26% in secondary MIBC vs 45% in primary MIBC (p = 0.02, univariable). On multivariable analysis adjusted for age, sex, and T stage, secondary MIBC remained independently associated with lower response (OR = 0.4 [95% CI 0.18–0.84], p = 0.02). Notably, no secondary MIBC patient achieved pT0 at cystectomy compared to 15% of primary MIBC patients.

### F2: ERCC2 missense mutations are enriched in primary MIBC

**Finding:** Likely deleterious *ERCC2* missense mutations—which predict cisplatin sensitivity—occur significantly more often in primary MIBC than secondary MIBC, in both discovery and validation cohorts.

**Detail:** In the retrospective discovery cohort: 10.9% (36/330) primary vs 1.8% (1/55) secondary (p = 0.044). Validated prospectively: 15.7% (12/70) vs 0% (0/24) (p = 0.033). In the combined cohort: 12% (48/400) vs 1.3% (1/79) (p = 0.004). Mutations clustered near or within conserved helicase domains of ERCC2, consistent with loss of nucleotide excision repair (NER) function and heightened cisplatin sensitivity.

**Genes / cancer types:** ERCC2 · Urothelial carcinoma / MIBC

### F3: Secondary MIBC has significantly worse survival outcomes after NAC

**Finding:** Secondary MIBC patients treated with NAC had significantly worse recurrence-free survival (RFS), cancer-specific survival (CSS), and overall survival (OS) compared to primary MIBC patients.

**Detail:** On univariable analysis: RFS (p < 0.001), CSS (p = 0.004), OS (p = 0.006). Multivariable analysis (adjusted for age, sex, T stage, adjuvant chemo) confirmed worse RFS (p = 0.007) and OS (p = 0.048). Critically, secondary MIBC patients treated with NAC fared *worse* than those treated with cystectomy alone (CSS p = 0.002), suggesting cisplatin NAC provides no benefit—and possible harm—in this group. Time to cystectomy was 5.8 months for secondary vs 1.7 months for primary MIBC patients (p < 0.001).

### F4: ERCC2 mutations associate with higher tumor mutation burden

**Finding:** *ERCC2*-mutant tumors have significantly higher tumor mutation burden (TMB) than ERCC2 wild-type tumors, and primary MIBC has higher median TMB than secondary MIBC by targeted sequencing.

**Detail:** By WES: ERCC2-mutant median 14.7 mut/Mb (IQR 8.5–23.2) vs wild-type 5.5 mut/Mb (IQR 3.2–9.5), p < 0.001. By targeted sequencing: 25.0 mut/Mb (19.9–29.4) vs 8.5 (6.1–15.8), p < 0.001. Primary MIBC had higher median TMB than secondary MIBC by targeted sequencing (11.2 vs 7.7 mut/Mb, p = 0.012), though not confirmed by WES alone.

**Genes / cancer types:** ERCC2 · Urothelial carcinoma / MIBC

### F5: FGFR3, STAG2, and TSC1 alterations enriched in secondary MIBC; KMT2D in primary

**Finding:** In the combined exploratory genomic analysis, several genes showed differential alteration frequencies between primary and secondary MIBC, pointing to distinct oncogenic pathways.

**Detail:** *KMT2D* alterations were enriched in primary MIBC (23% vs 10%, p = 0.013). Inactivating alterations in *STAG2* (7.8% vs 17%, p = 0.014) and *TSC1* (5.5% vs 14%, p = 0.007) were more frequent in secondary MIBC. RTK/MAPK pathway alterations were found in 42% of secondary MIBC specimens, with *FGFR3* (18%) and *ERBB2* (15%) among the most commonly altered. *TP53* pathway and cell cycle regulatory gene alterations were detected in 61% and 65% of secondary MIBC, respectively. No alteration remained significant after FDR correction.

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR3, STAG2, TSC1, KMT2D, ERBB2, TP53, RB1 · Urothelial carcinoma / MIBC

### F6: Molecular subtypes (basal/luminal) do not explain primary vs secondary MIBC differences

**Finding:** Unsupervised clustering of TCGA gene expression data showed no enrichment of basal or luminal molecular subtypes in primary versus secondary MIBC, indicating that the clinical differences are independent of RNA-based intrinsic subtyping.

**Detail:** Two previously published gene classifier sets were applied to the TCGA discovery cohort. Neither luminal nor basal subtypes were differentially distributed between primary and secondary MIBC, though the cohort size limited statistical power. The worse cisplatin response in secondary MIBC thus cannot be attributed to a higher proportion of intrinsically chemoresistant (luminal) subtypes.

### F7: Secondary MIBC patients may benefit from upfront cystectomy or clinical trials

**Finding:** Given the lack of benefit from cisplatin NAC in secondary MIBC, the authors suggest upfront radical cystectomy or enrollment in targeted therapy trials (e.g., FGFR inhibitors) as preferred strategies.

**Detail:** Secondary MIBC patients treated with NAC had worse CSS compared to cystectomy alone (p = 0.002), and a significantly delayed time to cystectomy (5.8 vs 1.7 months, p < 0.001) may worsen micrometastatic spread in this chemorefractory population. The high rate of *FGFR3* alterations (18%) in secondary MIBC suggests potential for FGFR-targeted neoadjuvant trials. The authors also hypothesize that prior BCG immunotherapy may select against *ERCC2*-mutant clones (higher TMB, more NER-deficient), explaining their absence in secondary MIBC.

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR3, ERCC2 · Urothelial carcinoma / MIBC

## Open Questions

- Does *ERCC2* mutation status prospectively predict NAC response in MIBC well enough to change treatment decisions (ongoing SWOG1314/Alliance 017301 trials)?
- Do *ERCC2*-mutant NMIBC subclones get selectively eliminated by BCG immunotherapy, and is TMB/neoantigen burden a mechanistic link?
- Can secondary MIBC patients with *FGFR3* alterations benefit from neoadjuvant FGFR inhibitors as an alternative to cisplatin-based NAC?

## Limitations

- Retrospective clinical cohort with selection bias; secondary MIBC patients often referred from outside institutions with variable prior NMIBC management.
- Molecular subtype analysis was limited by cohort size, potentially underpowered to detect subtype-based differences between primary and secondary MIBC.
- Somatic DDR gene analysis was the primary focus; germline, epigenetic, and transcriptomic contributors to cisplatin sensitivity in DDR-wild-type patients were not assessed.
