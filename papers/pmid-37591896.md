## Summary

Largest single-institution study of resected and sequenced (MSK-IMPACT) NSCLC brain metastases (BM): 233 patients with comprehensive clinical annotation, including 47 matched primary tumor (PT) and 42 extracranial metastasis (EM) samples. BM specimens showed higher TMB (median 8.8 vs. 5.8 mut/Mb), higher fraction genome altered (FGA), and more chromosomal instability than PT or EM. *CDKN2A/B* deletions were enriched in BM (34%) vs. PT (13%; p=0.003). Cell cycle pathway alterations were enriched in BM vs. PT (56% vs. 32%; p=0.004). In paired analyses, BM-BM pairs showed high genomic concordance while BM-PT/EM pairs showed divergent evolution. Key clinical-genomic correlates: *EGFR* alterations (especially non-canonical) enriched in leptomeningeal disease (LMD); *MYC* amplification in multifocal regional progression. Data available in `bm_nsclc_mskcc_2023` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: BM specimens have higher TMB, FGA, and chromosomal instability than primary or extracranial metastases

**Finding:** Brain metastases from NSCLC have significantly higher tumor mutational burden (median 8.8 vs. 5.8 mut/Mb vs. extracranial; p=0.00766), higher fraction genome altered (BM vs. EM: p=2.765e-06; BM vs. PT: p=2.273e-07), and broader chromosomal instability than PT or EM specimens. Whole-genome duplication events were numerically highest in LMD patients.

**Detail:** These findings confirm that BM specimens represent a molecularly distinct and genomically advanced state of NSCLC, consistent with branched evolution from a common ancestor with subsequent divergence in the intracranial niche. Higher TMB in BM compared to extracranial sites suggests ongoing mutagenic processes or clonal selection during CNS colonization. The elevated FGA implies widespread copy number remodeling as a feature of successful brain colonization. These broad genomic features are available in cBioPortal for cohort-level exploration.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide TMB, FGA, CNA · NSCLC brain metastasis (adenocarcinoma, squamous, other)

### F2: *CDKN2A/B* deletions and cell cycle pathway alterations are specifically enriched in NSCLC brain metastases

**Finding:** *CDKN2A/B* alterations were present in 34% of BM specimens vs. 13% of PT samples (p=0.003, q=0.04). At the pathway level, cell cycle pathway alterations were enriched in BM vs. PT (56% vs. 32%; p=0.004, q=0.041). These findings replicated in the LUAD-only subgroup. Approximately 50% of patients had cell cycle CNA alterations that were non-overlapping and mutually exclusive, suggesting cell cycle disruption is a near-essential prerequisite for brain colonization.

**Detail:** Loss of *CDKN2A* inactivates the RB pathway by releasing CDK4/6 from inhibition, promoting uncontrolled G1-S progression. CDK4/6 inhibitors are approved for breast cancer and are in trials for NSCLC; their activity in BM patients with cell cycle alterations is an active research question. The mutual exclusivity of different cell cycle CNAs across patients further supports the hypothesis that any route to cell cycle deregulation is sufficient for CNS progression. This is the most robust genomic hallmark of NSCLC BM identified in this study.

**Genes / cancer types:** CDKN2A, CDKN2B, RB1, CDK4, CDK6 · NSCLC brain metastasis (LUAD predominantly)

### F3: Non-canonical *EGFR* mutations are enriched in leptomeningeal disease and persist despite maximal EGFR-directed therapy

**Finding:** Among LUAD patients with BM, those who developed leptomeningeal disease (LMD) as first intracranial failure were significantly more likely to have *EGFR* alterations in their BM specimens (45% vs. 21%; p=0.044) compared to non-progressors. Many of these were non-canonical *EGFR* mutations (L861Q, G719A/S, A755G, N771\_H773dup) that persisted across serial tissue and CSF specimens despite EGFR TKI therapy and radiation.

**Detail:** Detailed patient vignettes illustrate persistence of atypical *EGFR* mutations through treatment with erlotinib, osimertinib, and even subsequent proton craniospinal irradiation. *EGFR* T790M acquired resistance mutations appeared and resolved, but the atypical drivers remained constant. The authors propose that non-canonical EGFR mutations confer partial resistance to osimertinib and inherent tropism for the leptomeninges — an environment metabolically distinct from brain parenchyma — and that this combination predisposes to LMD as a dominant failure pattern in patients with otherwise good brain control and longer survival. Acquired *HLA-B* mutations were seen as private BM alterations potentially mediating immune evasion.

**Genes / cancer types:** EGFR (L861Q, G719A/S, N771_H773dup, exon 19 del), HLA-B · NSCLC brain metastasis (LUAD)

### F4: *MYC* amplification associates with multifocal regional progression; *RB1* loss with local failure

**Finding:** *MYC* amplifications were significantly more common in BM patients who later developed multifocal regional progression compared to those with local progression (22% vs. 0%; p=0.023). *RB1* deletions and *NKX3-1* alterations were associated with local disease progression (p=0.022 and p=0.044, respectively). MYC pathway alterations were significantly enriched in patients with LMD (p=0.013) and both types of regional progression.

**Detail:** Mouse models of brain metastasis have shown *MYC* overexpression promotes tumor dissemination by protecting against oxidative stress in the brain microenvironment. The association of *RB1* loss with local failure is counterintuitive — one might expect *RB1*-deficient tumors to be more radiosensitive — but concurrent alterations may confer radiation resistance. *NKX3-1* alterations are well-described in prostate cancer metastasis but less characterized in NSCLC. These genomic biomarkers, if validated prospectively, could inform risk-stratified surveillance and therapy selection after BM resection.

**Genes / cancer types:** MYC, RB1, NKX3-1, NKX2-1, NF1 · NSCLC brain metastasis (LUAD)

### F5: BM-BM pairs show high genomic concordance; BM-PT/EM pairs show branched evolution with private BM alterations

**Finding:** In 7 patients with two independent resected BM specimens, genomic concordance was high — in contrast to BM-PT and BM-EM pairs, which showed many alterations unique to each compartment. In paired BM-PT/EM comparisons, clonal alterations in *TP53*, *KRAS*, and *EGFR* were consistently shared, while many additional alterations were compartment-private. Patients who developed BM late (as a progression event after PT/EM biopsy) had higher private BM mutation rates than patients presenting with synchronous BM at diagnosis.

**Detail:** This pattern supports a model in which BM seeds are derived from a common clonal ancestor with the primary/extracranial disease, but then diverge significantly during brain colonization. Once established, BMs evolve in a locally constrained, parallel manner (hence BM-BM concordance). Private BM alterations in *HLA-B* — present in several patients with treatment-failure BM — may represent immune evasion mechanisms specific to the immunosuppressed brain tumor microenvironment. Acquired *EGFR* T790M resistance was identified in one BM specimen and retained in a subsequent PT biopsy, demonstrating intracranial-to-systemic clonal propagation.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, KRAS, EGFR, HLA-B · NSCLC brain metastasis (LUAD, multiple histologies)

## Open Questions

- Can CDK4/6 inhibitors exploit the near-universal cell cycle pathway enrichment in NSCLC BM, either as monotherapy or combined with EGFR TKIs?
- Do non-canonical *EGFR* mutations identify a patient subgroup requiring more aggressive upfront CNS-directed therapy (e.g., craniospinal irradiation) to prevent LMD?
- Are *HLA-B* deletions in BM specimens a generalizable immune evasion mechanism, and do they predict resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors in the intracranial setting?
- What metabolic or microenvironmental features of CSF enable *EGFR*-mutant clones to colonize and persist in the leptomeningeal space specifically?
- Does *MYC* amplification predict benefit from whole-brain RT or proton craniospinal irradiation for multifocal regional progression?

## Limitations

- Highly selected cohort: only patients with large, symptomatic BM requiring surgical resection; excludes smaller asymptomatic BMs managed with SRS alone
- MSK-IMPACT targeted panel only; non-coding elements, transcriptional programs, and lesser-known somatic events not captured
- Retrospective design with selection bias; clinical outcomes limited to intracranial progression classification rather than standardized response criteria
- Small matched-pair subsets (n=2 synchronous BM-PT pairs); evolution analyses underpowered
- HLA-B and other private BM findings are exploratory and require validation in independent cohorts
