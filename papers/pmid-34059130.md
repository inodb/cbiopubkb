## Summary

This study from MSK establishes a tumor fraction-guided triaging strategy for cell-free DNA (cfDNA) profiling in 118 metastatic solid tumor patients. Using cf-IMPACT (a 410-gene plasma adaptation of MSK-IMPACT) combined with shallow whole genome sequencing (sWGS) to estimate cfDNA tumor fraction (z-score), the authors demonstrate that tumor fraction estimation from sWGS can triage negative cfDNA results toward either ultra-deep sensitive re-sequencing (MSK-ACCESS, for low tumor fraction samples) or broader whole exome sequencing (WES, for high tumor fraction samples with no detectable mutations by cf-IMPACT). This three-platform strategy identified mutations in 76% of all patients (90/118), including actionable alterations in 25%. The study provides a framework for sequentially applying cfDNA platforms based on biology rather than testing all patients with a single assay. The paired tumor and plasma genomic data are accessible in cBioPortal as mixed_cfdna_msk_2020.

## Key Findings

### F1: cf-IMPACT identifies somatic mutations in 72% of patients with matched tumor data, with high concordance at high tumor fraction

**Finding:** cf-IMPACT identified somatic mutations in 55/76 (72%) patients for whom MSK-IMPACT tumor profiling data were available. Concordance between plasma and tumor mutational profiles, and agreement in TMB values, was significantly higher in samples with high cfDNA tumor fraction (z-score ≥5) compared to low tumor fraction samples, establishing that plasma tumor fraction is a key determinant of cfDNA assay performance.

**Detail:** Among samples with z-score ≥5 (high tumor fraction), plasma–tumor mutation concordance was significantly higher — plasma detected the majority of clonal tumor mutations also found in MSK-IMPACT tissue profiling. In low tumor fraction samples (z-score <5), many expected mutations were not detectable in plasma, explaining false-negative cfDNA results. TMB estimates from cf-IMPACT correlated with tumor MSK-IMPACT TMB estimates at high tumor fractions. For the 42 patients without tumor sequencing data, cf-IMPACT identified mutations in 16/42 (38%) — a lower detection rate reflecting the heterogeneous nature of this group.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `KRAS`, `PIK3CA`, `AR`, `BRCA1`, `BRCA2`, `EGFR`, `ERBB2` · Breast, prostate, urothelial, NSCLC, melanoma, testicular cancers (metastatic)

### F2: sWGS-based tumor fraction estimation (z-score) guides triaging of negative cfDNA results to the most appropriate follow-up assay

**Finding:** Plasma samples with no mutations detected by cf-IMPACT were triaged using sWGS z-score: low tumor fraction (z-score <5) samples were re-analyzed with MSK-ACCESS (ultra-deep, sensitive, fewer genes) while high tumor fraction (z-score ≥5) samples were re-analyzed with WES (broader genomic coverage). This biology-driven triage strategy maximized mutation detection efficiency and minimized unnecessary costly testing.

**Detail:** Of 47 samples with no alterations detected by cf-IMPACT, 29 had low tumor fraction (z-score <5) and sufficient material for MSK-ACCESS, which revealed somatic mutations in 14/29 (48%) — demonstrating that shallow WGS tumor fraction estimation prevented false interpretation of these as truly negative (likely reflecting low tumor shed requiring a more sensitive assay). Separately, 5 samples with no cf-IMPACT alterations but high tumor fraction (z-score ≥5) were analyzed by WES — identifying mutational signatures and potential oncogenic drivers in all 5 cases, suggesting that these tumors had genomic alterations outside the cf-IMPACT 410-gene panel. This framework maximizes actionable information from each blood sample.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide (WES) and targeted panel (MSK-ACCESS) · Pan-cancer metastatic

**Data availability:** `partial`

### F3: Clinically actionable alterations identified in 25% of all patients via plasma profiling, including FDA-approved drug targets

**Finding:** Across the entire 118-patient cohort, cf-IMPACT analysis of plasma revealed mutations in 71/118 (60%) patients overall, with clinically actionable alterations identified in 30/118 (25%) patients — including therapeutic targets of FDA-approved drugs. The combined three-platform approach (cf-IMPACT + MSK-ACCESS + WES) identified mutations in 90/118 (76%) of all patients.

**Detail:** Actionable alterations included ERBB2 amplification, PIK3CA mutations (breast cancer), EGFR mutations (NSCLC), AR amplification/mutation (prostate cancer), KRAS mutations, BRCA1/2 mutations (HRD), and others with matched approved or investigational therapies. The 25% actionability rate from plasma alone — without tissue biopsy — is clinically meaningful given that re-biopsy is often not feasible in metastatic patients. The additional 16% detection gain from MSK-ACCESS/WES triaging brought the overall actionability rate higher, demonstrating that the three-platform strategy adds clinical value beyond a single-assay approach.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ERBB2`, `PIK3CA`, `EGFR`, `KRAS`, `AR`, `BRCA1`, `BRCA2`, `TP53`, `PTEN` · Breast, prostate, urothelial, NSCLC (metastatic)

### F4: MSK-ACCESS ultra-deep sequencing rescues mutation detection in 48% of low tumor fraction, cf-IMPACT-negative samples

**Finding:** Of 29 cf-IMPACT-negative, low tumor fraction (z-score <5) plasma samples with sufficient material for MSK-ACCESS analysis, 14/29 (48%) yielded detectable somatic mutations — demonstrating the clinical utility of ultra-deep sequencing for patients with low circulating tumor DNA who would otherwise be classified as "liquid biopsy negative."

**Detail:** MSK-ACCESS uses an ultra-deep sequencing approach (targeting ~500 genes at >3,000× coverage) with duplex sequencing to reduce sequencing error, enabling detection of mutations at allele frequencies below 1% — substantially lower than cf-IMPACT's ~2–5% de novo detection limit. The 48% rescue rate suggests that nearly half of "negative" plasma tests in metastatic patients are technical false negatives due to insufficient tumor shedding, not genuine absence of tumor mutations. Identifying these patients with low tumor fraction also has biological relevance: low tumor shed in metastatic disease may indicate favorable prognosis or deep treatment response.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer targeted panel · Metastatic solid tumors (breast, prostate, urothelial, NSCLC, melanoma)

### F5: WES of high tumor fraction, cf-IMPACT-negative plasma identifies mutational signatures and novel drivers outside targeted panels

**Finding:** Five patients with no alterations detected by cf-IMPACT but high cfDNA tumor fraction (z-score ≥5) underwent plasma WES, which identified mutational signatures (e.g., APOBEC, HRD, MMR-deficiency signatures) and potential oncogenic driver alterations in genes outside the cf-IMPACT 410-gene panel in all 5 cases — demonstrating that high tumor fraction with a negative targeted panel result indicates a genuinely altered tumor genome with alterations in unsequenced regions.

**Detail:** This finding is clinically important: high tumor fraction with a negative targeted panel result should not be interpreted as "no actionable alterations" but rather as "alterations likely present outside the panel's scope." WES in these cases revealed structural variants, fusions, and mutations in cancer genes not covered by cf-IMPACT. Mutational signatures identified included APOBEC (relevant for immunotherapy selection), BRCA-related HRD signatures (relevant for PARP inhibitor selection), and MMR deficiency signatures (relevant for pembrolizumab). This highlights the clinical trade-off between panel breadth and sequencing depth, and supports the complementary use of WES when targeted panels are uninformative in high-tumor-fraction samples.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide (WES) · Pan-cancer metastatic (5 patients)

**Data availability:** `not-available`

## Open Questions

- Can sWGS tumor fraction (z-score) estimation be standardized across institutions with different sequencing platforms and normal panel compositions?
- Does low cfDNA tumor fraction in a metastatic patient reliably indicate favorable prognosis, treatment response, or simply anatomical inaccessibility of tumors to cfDNA shedding?
- What is the optimal cfDNA tumor fraction threshold (z-score cutoff) for triaging to MSK-ACCESS vs. WES, and does this threshold differ by cancer type or disease site?
- Can cfDNA TMB (from cf-IMPACT) be used interchangeably with tissue TMB for immunotherapy selection, particularly in patients who cannot provide tissue biopsies?
- Does serial cfDNA profiling with sWGS tumor fraction monitoring enable earlier detection of treatment resistance or disease progression compared to imaging?
- How does spatial tumor heterogeneity (different metastatic sites shedding different clones) affect cfDNA mutation profiles and actionability assessments?

## Limitations

- Single-institution study (MSK); patient population enriched for patients already receiving clinical-grade MSK-IMPACT tumor profiling
- Small cohort (n=118) with heterogeneous cancer types limits statistical power for cancer-type-specific conclusions
- WES triage applied to only 5 patients with high tumor fraction + cf-IMPACT negative; underpowered to draw robust conclusions
- sWGS z-score estimated from copy-number alterations only; may underestimate tumor fraction in tumors with few copy-number changes (e.g., diploid tumors)
- cf-IMPACT designed for 1% VAF detection at known sites; de novo mutation detection requires 2–5% VAF — both thresholds may miss low-frequency subclonal mutations
- Fragment size analysis as a secondary TF estimation method (cited in related literature) not used in all samples
- Clinical outcomes (response to actionable alterations detected, progression-free or overall survival) not reported; actionability is theoretical rather than empirically linked to treatment benefit
- MSK-ACCESS panel composition and cf-IMPACT panel differ; direct mutation concordance comparisons across tiers may introduce platform-specific biases
