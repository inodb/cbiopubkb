## Summary

Landmark prospective clinical sequencing study of 150 metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) patients from an eight-institution consortium (SU2C-PCF). All cases underwent whole exome sequencing (WES), matched germline sequencing, and transcriptome sequencing from fresh biopsies of bone (28.7%), lymph node (42%), liver (12.7%), and other soft tissue (16.7%). 89% of mCRPC cases harbored a clinically actionable alteration. AR pathway was aberrant in 71.3%, DNA repair (BRCA1/BRCA2/ATM) in 19.3%, PI3K pathway in 49%, WNT pathway in 18%. TP53 and AR mutations are significantly enriched in mCRPC vs. primary prostate cancer. Germline BRCA2 pathogenic mutations found in 5.3% of cases. Data available in `prad_su2c_2015` on cBioPortal, establishing a foundational precision oncology framework for mCRPC.

## Key Findings

### F1: 89% of mCRPC harbor a clinically actionable alteration; AR pathway aberrant in 71%

**Finding:** Nearly every mCRPC case (89%) harbors at least one potentially actionable somatic or germline alteration. 63% of cases have direct AR alterations. Even excluding AR, 65% harbor actionable events in PI3K (49%), DNA repair (19%), RAF kinases (3%), CDK inhibitors (7%), and WNT (5%) pathways. An average of 7.8 biologically relevant aberrations per case was identified.

**Detail:** AR pathway alterations (107/150, 71.3%) include AR amplification, point mutations at known resistance hotspots (T878A for flutamide agonism; W742C for bicalutamide agonism; L702H for glucocorticoid agonism), splice variants including AR-V7, and alterations in FOXA1, NCOR1/NCOR2 (negative regulators), SPOP, and ZBTB16 (novel recurrent homozygous deletion in 5%, chr11q23). Novel ZBTB16 homozygous deletion activates MAPK signaling. AR enhancer region amplification and novel AR mutations not seen in primary prostate cancer were also identified.

**Genes / cancer types:** AR, FOXA1, NCOR1, NCOR2, SPOP, ZBTB16 · mCRPC

### F2: DNA repair pathway aberrant in 19.3% — BRCA2/BRCA1/ATM biallelic loss predicts PARP inhibitor benefit

**Finding:** 19.3% of mCRPC patients have biallelic loss of BRCA2, BRCA1, or ATM, creating a large patient subset predicted to benefit from PARP inhibitor therapy. BRCA2 biallelic loss alone occurs in 12.7% (including 5.3% with germline pathogenic variants plus somatic second hit). Clinical responses to PARP inhibition in concurrent trials correlated with BRCA2/ATM biallelic loss.

**Detail:** BRCA2 inactivation mechanisms include somatic point mutation plus LOH, and homozygous deletion. ~90% of BRCA2-altered cases show biallelic loss. CDK12, FANCA, RAD51B, RAD51C also altered. MLH1/MSH2 structural alterations drive 3 of 4 hypermutated cases. Biallelic BRCA2 loss was enriched in mCRPC relative to primary prostate cancer, with 5.3% germline BRCA2 pathogenic alleles substantially higher than primary cancer rates — warranting routine germline testing in mCRPC. Affected individuals with BRCA2/ATM biallelic loss in the companion olaparib trial (Mateo et al. 2014) achieved clinical benefit.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRCA2, BRCA1, ATM, CDK12, FANCA, RAD51B, RAD51C, MLH1, MSH2 · mCRPC

### F3: PI3K pathway altered in 49%; novel PIK3CB mutations and fusions in PTEN-deficient context

**Finding:** The PI3K signaling pathway is the second most commonly altered pathway in mCRPC (49%). Beyond canonical PTEN loss and PIK3CA mutations/amplifications, this study identifies first-in-mCRPC activating mutations in PIK3CB at positions analogous to canonical PIK3CA hotspots. PIK3CA/B fusions also drive overexpression.

**Detail:** PIK3CB mutations appear predominantly in PTEN-deficient cases, consistent with biochemical evidence that PTEN-null cancers depend on PIK3CB rather than PIK3CA. PIK3CA amplification (focal amplification at 3q) results in overexpression. Gene fusions involving TBL1XR1-PIK3CA and ACPP-PIK3CB both cause outlier expression of the respective catalytic subunits. AKT1 p.E17K activating mutations also identified. Patients with PIK3CB-specific mutations/amplification achieved durable responses (>1 year) to PIK3CB-specific inhibition in a companion trial. CCND1 amplifications (9%) provide a CDK4 inhibitor hypothesis.

**Genes / cancer types:** PTEN, PIK3CA, PIK3CB, AKT1, CCND1, CDKN2A, CDKN1B, CDK4 · mCRPC

### F4: WNT pathway in 18%; novel RSPO2 fusions and APC/RNF43/ZNRF3 alterations predict porcupine inhibitor sensitivity

**Finding:** WNT pathway alterations occur in 18% (27/150) of mCRPC, including novel R-spondin (RSPO2) gene fusions, recurrent APC mutations, and alterations in RNF43 and ZNRF3 (recently described in colorectal and endometrial cancer). RSPO2 fusion carriers are predicted to respond to porcupine inhibitors.

**Detail:** GRHL2-RSPO2 fusion drives outlier RSPO2 overexpression. CTNNB1 hotspot activating mutations and APC loss-of-function mutations (mutually exclusive with each other and with RNF43/ZNRF3) occur. RNF43 and ZNRF3 are negative regulators of WNT ligand presentation at the cell surface; their loss can be reversed by porcupine inhibitors (blocking WNT ligand secretion). Combined RNF43/ZNRF3/RSPO2-altered cases represent ~6% of mCRPC. These alterations had not been described in clinical mCRPC biopsies before this study.

**Genes / cancer types:** APC, CTNNB1, RNF43, ZNRF3, RSPO2 · mCRPC

### F5: TP53 and AR selectively enriched in mCRPC vs. primary prostate cancer; RAF kinase fusions in 3%

**Finding:** Comparing mCRPC to 440 primary prostate cancer exomes, TP53 (q<0.001) and AR (exclusively mutated in mCRPC) are the most selectively enriched in metastatic castration-resistant disease. RAF kinase fusions (BRAF, RAF1) were identified in 3% of mCRPC cases, suggesting MEK inhibitor candidacy in this subset.

**Detail:** No genes were selectively mutated in primary prostate cancer vs. mCRPC. KMT2D, APC, BRCA2, and GNAS are also enriched. The 4.4 mutations/Mb average rate (vs. ~2/Mb primary) reflects accumulated genomic damage under treatment selection. 60% of cases harbored driver gene fusions (ETS fusions in 56.7% as the most common class). Novel ETS fusions to FLI1, ETV4, and ETV5 beyond canonical ERG identified. BRAF/RAF1 fusions in 3% warrant pan-RAF or MEK inhibitor investigation. Actionable pathogenic germline variants found in 8% of patients, indicating need for routine germline counseling.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, AR, KMT2D, APC, BRCA2, BRAF, RAF1, ERG, FLI1, ETV4, ETV5 · mCRPC

## Open Questions

- Which specific AR mutations (T878A, W742C, L702H, novel) predict resistance to which second-generation antiandrogen therapy (abiraterone vs. enzalutamide vs. darolutamide)?
- Does the BRCA2 biallelic loss rate of 12.7% apply comparably to the current post-docetaxel, post-abiraterone/enzalutamide mCRPC population?
- Can R-spondin fusions in mCRPC predict clinical responses to porcupine inhibitors currently in clinical trials?
- What fraction of patients with PIK3CB mutations respond to PIK3CB-selective inhibition — is this dependent on concurrent PTEN deficiency?
- Does treatment-related longitudinal sampling reveal additional AR mutations or NEPC transformation not captured in this cross-sectional analysis?

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design with single biopsy per patient; longitudinal samples would better capture clonal evolution and resistance mechanisms
- Bone biopsies (28.7%) have lower tumor content and sequencing success rate than soft tissue; potential selection bias toward accessible metastatic sites
- WES (not WGS); large structural variants and non-coding driver elements not captured
- AR-V7 splice variant analysis from RNA-seq lacked clinical outcome correlation at time of publication (treatment response data not yet collected)
- Small cohort for pathway subgroup analysis; some WNT/RAF/CDK associations underpowered
