## Summary

This study provides the first comprehensive multi-platform genomic characterization of small-cell lung cancer (SCLC), analyzing 80 human SCLCs including 36 primary tumor-normal pairs, 17 matched cell line pairs, 4 additional primary tumors, and 23 cell lines — plus whole-genome sequencing of one pair. Key platforms: exome sequencing, genome-wide copy-number analysis (n=56), and whole-transcriptome RNA-seq. The study identifies 22 significantly mutated genes, discovers *SOX2* amplification in ~27% of SCLC, validates SOX2 as a driver (shRNA knockdown reduces proliferation), identifies a recurrent *RLF-MYCL1* fusion oncogene, and characterizes the SCLC mutation spectrum dominated by tobacco-associated G-to-T transversions. This was a landmark early SCLC genomic study that established SOX2 as an SCLC oncogene and defined the overall mutational landscape.

## Key Findings

### F1: SOX2 is amplified in ~27% of SCLC and is a functional oncogene (driver by shRNA dependency)

**Finding:** *SOX2* at chromosome 3q26.33 is focally amplified (copy number ≥4) in ~27% (15/56) of SCLC samples, making it one of the most frequently amplified genes in this cancer. SOX2 expression is elevated in most SCLC vs. adjacent normal. shRNA-mediated SOX2 suppression blocks proliferation in SOX2-amplified SCLC lines, confirming oncogenic dependency.

**Detail:** RNA-seq confirmed elevated SOX2 expression in amplified and non-amplified SCLC samples vs. adjacent normal. FISH validation in an independent cohort of 110 primary SCLC cases showed SOX2 protein expression strongly correlates with gene copy number and with clinical stage (higher composite IHC score in advanced stages). SOX2 was previously known as a driver in lung and esophageal squamous cell carcinoma; this study extended its role to SCLC. Multiple SOX family genes (SOX3, SOX4, SOX5, SOX6, SOX9, SOX11, SOX14, SOX17) carry somatic mutations that are mutually exclusive, suggesting convergent SOX pathway dysregulation.

### F2: TP53 and RB1 are near-universally inactivated in SCLC; defining genomic hallmarks confirmed at scale

**Finding:** *TP53* (75–90%) and *RB1* (60–90%) inactivating mutations or deletions are the most prevalent alterations in SCLC, confirmed in this comprehensive cohort as previously reported. Copy-number loss at the RB1 and RASSF1/FHIT loci is recurrent. In contrast to NSCLC, no KRAS mutations were detected in any SCLC sample.

**Detail:** Both genes were among the 22 significantly mutated genes in this study. The consistent absence of KRAS mutations and the rare occurrence of EGFR, BRAF, and KRAS mutations distinguish SCLC from NSCLC genomically and explain the lack of benefit from EGFR/KRAS-targeted therapies in SCLC. Previously identified alterations — MYC family amplification, EGFR/BCL2 amplification, RASSF1A/PTEN/FHIT loss — were also confirmed. PTEN mutations found in 2–4%.

### F3: Recurrent RLF-MYCL1 fusion identified by RNA-seq; silencing reduces proliferation

**Finding:** RNA sequencing identified multiple fusion transcripts in SCLC, including a recurrent *RLF-MYCL1* fusion. Silencing *MYCL1* (L-Myc) in SCLC cell lines harboring the *RLF-MYCL1* fusion decreased cell proliferation, suggesting oncogenic dependency.

**Detail:** This functional validation (analogous to the SOX2 shRNA data) positions MYCL1 as an SCLC oncogene in the subset carrying this fusion. MYCL1 is a MYC family member already known to be amplified in some SCLC; this fusion represents a structural alteration activating it. The *RLF-MYCL1* fusion is potentially detectable by RNA sequencing and could serve as a biomarker for MYCL1-dependent SCLC.

### F4: Tobacco-associated mutation signature dominates; 22 significantly mutated genes including chromatin modifiers and kinases

**Finding:** The dominant mutation pattern in SCLC is G-to-T transversions (consistent with tobacco carcinogen exposure), followed by G-to-A and A-to-G transitions. 22 significantly mutated genes were identified (FDR ≤10%), including novel SCLC genes: chromatin-modifying enzymes (EP300, MLL2, TRRAP, RUNX1T1), kinases (STK38, LRRK2, PRKD3, CDK14), Ras regulators (RAB37, RASGRF1, RASGRF2), and Notch/Hedgehog pathway members (NOTCH1/2/3, SMO).

**Detail:** Mean non-synonymous mutation rate was 5.5/Mb (excluding one hypermutated sample with 2,953 mutations). 98% of identified protein-altering variants were novel vs. COSMIC at the time of publication. Mutations cluster in PI3K pathway (PIK3CA, AKT1-3, MTOR, RPS6KA2/6), the mediator complex (MED12/12L/13/13L/15/24/25/27/29), and glutamate receptor family members. KIT mutation at codon 761 (activating change previously reported in mast cell disorder) identified. CDKN2A and PTEN hotspot mutations also found.

### F5: New copy-number loss genes (KIF2A, CNTN3) and confirmed MYC/SOX4/KIT amplifications; copy-number landscape defined

**Finding:** Genome-wide copy-number analysis of 56 SCLC samples confirmed recurrent losses at RB1, RASSF1, FHIT and amplifications at MYC, SOX4, KIT. Novel copy-number loss genes KIF2A (mitotic kinesin) and CNTN3 (contactin 3) were identified as not previously known to be altered in SCLC. SOX2 amplification at 3q26.33 is the most clinically significant novel amplification.

**Detail:** GISTIC analysis identified significant recurrent amplicons and deletions. SOX4 amplification (separate from SOX2) also confirmed. The novel losses of KIF2A and CNTN3 may contribute to chromosomal instability and SCLC biology, respectively, though functional relevance was not experimentally validated in this study.

## Open Questions

- Can SOX2 amplification be used as a patient selection biomarker for SOX2-directed therapeutic strategies in SCLC?
- Are RLF-MYCL1 or other MYCL1-activating fusions enriched in a clinically distinct SCLC subset?
- Do the chromatin modifier mutations (EP300, MLL2, TRRAP) predict sensitivity to epigenetic therapies (BET inhibitors, EZH2 inhibitors, HDAC inhibitors)?
- What is the functional role of mediator complex mutations (MED12/13 family) in SCLC, given that MED12 mutations in other cancers affect CDK8-associated transcriptional regulation?
- Do SOX family mutation subtypes (SOX3/4/6/9/11/14/17) represent distinct SCLC molecular subgroups with differential prognosis or treatment sensitivity?
- How does the SCLC mutational landscape in 2012 (predominantly cell lines and primary surgical specimens) compare to more recent ctDNA-based or biopsy-based SCLC cohorts?

## Limitations

- Small primary tumor-normal pair cohort (n=36 for exome; n=56 for CNA); limited statistical power for rare mutations
- Majority of cell lines in analysis; cell line biology may not fully represent primary tumor landscape
- SCLC is typically not resected (treated with chemotherapy); surgical specimen bias — resected tumors may be earlier-stage or atypical SCLC
- No survival or clinical outcome data linked to genomic features in this cohort
- RNA-seq and WGS performed on only a subset of samples; fusion detection not comprehensive
- Study predates systematic SCLC transcriptional subtype classification (SCLC-A/N/P/Y based on ASCL1/NEUROD1/POU2F3/YAP1)
