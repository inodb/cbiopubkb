## Summary

This landmark integrative genomic study characterized 207 soft-tissue sarcoma samples across seven major subtypes (dedifferentiated liposarcoma, myxoid/round-cell liposarcoma, pleomorphic liposarcoma, leiomyosarcoma, GIST, myxofibrosarcoma, synovial sarcoma) using DNA sequencing of 722 genes in 47 tumor/normal pairs, SNP-array copy-number profiling (n=207), and gene expression arrays (n=149). Frequently mutated genes included *TP53* (17% of pleomorphic liposarcomas), *NF1* (10.5% of myxofibrosarcomas, 8% of pleomorphic liposarcomas), and *PIK3CA* (18% of myxoid/round-cell liposarcomas). Chromosome 12q amplification was near-universal (~90%) in dedifferentiated liposarcoma, with functional RNAi screens identifying *CDK4* and *YEATS4* as essential amplified drivers, providing direct therapeutic targets in a cancer type with no prior known actionable alterations.

## Key Findings

### F1: PIK3CA hotspot mutations in 18% of myxoid/round-cell liposarcomas associate with AKT activation and inferior survival

**Finding:** *PIK3CA* was mutated in 13/71 (18%) of myxoid/round-cell liposarcomas (MRCs), with mutations clustering in the helical domain (E542K, E545K) and kinase domain (H1047L, H1047R). Patients with *PIK3CA* mutations had significantly shorter disease-specific survival than those with wild-type *PIK3CA* (p=0.036, log-rank test).

**Detail:** This was the first report of *PIK3CA* mutations in a mesenchymal cancer. Helical-domain mutations (E545K) were associated with increased Akt phosphorylation (Ser473 and Thr308) and downstream phosphorylation of PRAS40 and S6 kinase, while kinase-domain mutants (H1047R) did not show similar Akt activation, potentially due to higher PTEN levels compensating for PI3K activity. A single MRC case harbored homozygous *PTEN* deletion with high Akt phosphorylation. These data suggest a subset of MRC patients could benefit from PI3K/mTOR pathway inhibitors in clinical trials.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PIK3CA`, `PTEN` · Myxoid/round-cell liposarcoma

### F2: NF1 inactivated by point mutations and genomic deletions in myxofibrosarcoma and pleomorphic liposarcoma

**Finding:** Diverse *NF1* alterations were identified across two sarcoma subtypes: 7 point mutations (including 2 known germline pathogenic variants: R304* and Q369*) in myxofibrosarcoma (10.5%) and pleomorphic liposarcoma (8%), plus 12 genomic deletions at the *NF1* locus. Biallelic inactivation was evident in some tumors, with heterozygous mutations accompanied by deletion of the wild-type allele and reduced expression.

**Detail:** Prior to this study, somatic *NF1* alterations were associated only with malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors (MPNSTs) and GISTs in neurofibromatosis patients. This study established *NF1* as a somatic tumor suppressor in sporadic sarcomas. The co-occurrence of point mutations and genomic deletions (biallelic hits), together with reduced mRNA expression, is consistent with classic two-hit tumor suppressor inactivation. Loss of NF1 function activates RAS/MAPK and mTOR signaling, providing a rationale for mTOR inhibitor therapy in NF1-deficient sarcomas. This finding parallels concurrent reports of NF1 alterations in lung cancer and glioblastoma.

### F3: Chromosome 12q amplification in ~90% of dedifferentiated liposarcomas; CDK4 and YEATS4 identified as essential amplified oncogenes

**Finding:** Focal and broad amplification of chromosome 12q13-q32 was the most prevalent alteration in any subtype (~90% of dedifferentiated liposarcomas). An shRNA screen of 385 amplified-region genes in three dedifferentiated liposarcoma cell lines identified 99 genes whose knockdown decreased proliferation, with *CDK4* and *YEATS4* validated as the most therapeutically relevant essential oncogenes.

**Detail:** *CDK4*, a known oncogene and cell-cycle regulator, was the most strongly overexpressed amplified gene in dedifferentiated liposarcoma relative to normal fat. Sustained shRNA knockdown of *CDK4* inhibited proliferation in two of three cell lines, and pharmacological CDK4/6 inhibition with PD0332991 (a selective CDK4/CDK6 inhibitor in clinical trials) induced G1 arrest. *MDM2* knockdown also impaired proliferation with sustained knockdown. *YEATS4* (GAS41), a transcriptional repressor of the p53 network frequently co-amplified with *MDM2*, was also validated as essential; the data suggest that *YEATS4* and *MDM2* may cooperatively repress p53 in dedifferentiated liposarcoma, with potential consequences for MDM2 antagonist (Nutlin-based) therapies.

### F4: TP53 mutations restricted to pleomorphic liposarcoma subtype; found in 17% of cases

**Finding:** *TP53* was the most frequently mutated gene in pleomorphic liposarcomas (4 mutations in 24 samples, 17%), but strikingly was not found mutated in any of the other six sarcoma subtypes sequenced. Mutation types included missense (C135F, T155I) and splice site alterations.

**Detail:** The subtype-specificity of *TP53* mutations is notable given that TP53 pathway disruption is one of the most universal cancer alterations. Its restriction to pleomorphic liposarcomas — one of the highest-grade and most complex karyotype sarcoma subtypes — suggests that TP53 inactivation may cooperate with genomic complexity to drive this subtype. In contrast, the simpler-karyotype subtypes (MRC, synovial sarcoma, GIST) showed no TP53 mutations, consistent with their pathognomonic translocations or activating mutations being primary drivers sufficient for oncogenesis. This finding has implications for the differential diagnosis and molecular subclassification of sarcomas.

### F5: Sarcoma subtypes with complex vs. simple karyotypes show distinct patterns of genomic complexity; myxofibrosarcoma and pleomorphic liposarcoma are molecularly similar

**Finding:** SNP-array copy-number profiling revealed striking differences between sarcoma subtypes: myxoid/round-cell liposarcoma, synovial sarcoma, and GIST had nearly normal karyotypes, while dedifferentiated and pleomorphic liposarcoma, leiomyosarcoma, and myxofibrosarcoma exhibited complex, highly rearranged karyotypes. Notably, myxofibrosarcoma and pleomorphic liposarcoma had strikingly similar genomic profiles, suggesting they may be a single molecular entity.

**Detail:** Only the four complex-karyotype subtypes harbored significant copy-neutral loss-of-heterozygosity (LOH), consistent with mitotic recombination contributing to tumor suppressor gene inactivation. These findings have important taxonomic implications: the observed molecular convergence of myxofibrosarcoma and pleomorphic liposarcoma — two historically classified as distinct entities — raises the possibility of reclassifying them together. This distinction between simple (translocation-driven) and complex (chromosomal instability-driven) sarcomas has important prognostic implications and reflects fundamentally different mechanisms of oncogenesis.

## Open Questions

- Do *PIK3CA*-mutant myxoid/round-cell liposarcomas respond to PI3K or mTOR inhibitors in prospective clinical trials?
- Are *NF1*-deficient sarcomas (without germline neurofibromatosis) sensitive to mTOR or MEK inhibitors?
- What is the mechanism by which *YEATS4* and *MDM2* co-amplification suppresses TP53 signaling, and does this predict Nutlin sensitivity?
- Can molecular convergence of myxofibrosarcoma and pleomorphic liposarcoma guide development of shared therapeutic strategies?
- What additional driver genes in the non-sequenced portions of the genome contribute to complex-karyotype sarcoma biology?
- Does the CDK4/6 inhibitor benefit in dedifferentiated liposarcoma require RB1 proficiency, as seen in breast cancer?

## Limitations

- Sequencing limited to 722 genes in only 47 tumor/normal pairs; whole-exome and whole-genome sequencing would reveal additional driver mutations
- Extended genotyping of the 160 additional tumors was limited to known mutation sites, not discovery of novel mutations
- Sample sizes within individual subtypes are small (e.g., n=21-50 per subtype), limiting statistical power for rare-event discovery
- shRNA functional screen performed in only 3 dedifferentiated liposarcoma cell lines; results may not generalize to all tumors of this subtype
- No matched germline sequencing for all samples; somatic vs. germline status of some variants uncertain
- Absence of validated survival analyses for most genomic features due to limited follow-up data
- Study predates TERT promoter mutation analysis, next-generation sequencing standard of care, and RNA-seq; fusion transcripts may have been missed
