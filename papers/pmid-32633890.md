## Summary

This proof-of-concept study demonstrates the feasibility of detecting somatic RB1 mutations in plasma cell-free DNA (cfDNA) from 10 pediatric patients with advanced intraocular unilateral retinoblastoma, a disease where tumor biopsy is contraindicated and eye enucleation rates have fallen below 10% due to improved intra-arterial chemotherapy. Using MSK-IMPACT to profile enucleation specimens and a custom RB1-targeted cfDNA panel (~1530× unique coverage), tumor-guided genotyping detected 10 of 13 expected RB1 mutations in plasma (8/10 patients; median VAF 4.9%), while de novo calling identified 7 of 13 mutations (6/10 patients) without prior tumor information. The findings support cfDNA as a non-invasive platform for RB1 mutation characterization to guide genetic counseling and clinical management.

## Key Findings

### F1: Tumor-guided cfDNA genotyping detects RB1 mutations in 80% of retinoblastoma patients

**Finding:** Using known tumor RB1 mutation status to guide cfDNA analysis, somatic RB1 mutations were detectable in plasma from 8 of 10 patients (10 of 13 expected mutations).

**Detail:** Median VAF in cfDNA 4.9% (range 0.7%–12.6%). Two patients not detected: P16 showed sub-threshold supporting reads (5/5666 and 2/2286 for two mutations); P19 showed no detectable cfDNA signal (only patient who received prior chemotherapy). Total plasma cfDNA yields 5.5–27.4 ng (mean 17.3 ng); sequenced to ~1530× unique coverage. Technical replicates in 5 samples showed high reproducibility (Pearson r²=0.993).

### F2: De novo RB1 mutation calling without tumor information detects mutations in 60% of patients

**Finding:** Without using prior tumor sequencing data, de novo variant calling in cfDNA identified RB1 mutations in 6 of 10 patients (7 of 13 expected mutations) with high stringency criteria.

**Detail:** De novo criteria: VAF >0.5% with ≥10 mutant allele reads; ≥2× the matched buffy coat VAF; >mean + 2 SD across 9 unmatched buffy coats; manual IGV review. Two additional variants detected de novo (VAF 1.56% and 0.99%) not confirmed in matched tumors—possibly false positives, mosaic variants from a retinal subpopulation, or sub-detection-threshold tumor mutations (0 reads/280–741 total in IMPACT data). Technical replicates concordant for de novo calls.

### F3: cfDNA RB1 detection distinguishes somatic from germline disease, enabling non-invasive genetic counseling

**Finding:** Identifying somatic RB1 mutations exclusively in cfDNA (not buffy coat) definitively confirms non-heritable disease, avoiding unnecessary germline surveillance and reducing examinations under anesthesia.

**Detail:** Each cfDNA mutation was genotyped simultaneously in the patient's matched buffy coat and 9 unmatched buffy coats to filter germline and background error. Somatic confirmation requires VAF in plasma higher than matched buffy coat and higher than mean+2SD across unmatched normals. 10%–15% of unilateral retinoblastoma patients carry germline RB1 mutations; identifying somatic-only disease eliminates need for extra cancer screening and reduces family anxiety and surveillance burden.

### F4: Cohort includes 3 patients who progressed to metastatic retinoblastoma, supporting cfDNA as a potential disease monitoring tool

**Finding:** Three of the 10 patients developed metastatic disease, suggesting the cohort captures a high-risk subset and that pre-enucleation cfDNA could serve as a baseline for future metastasis monitoring.

**Detail:** 3/10 patients developed metastatic disease (higher than general retinoblastoma population, reflecting that MSKCC enucleation patients have advanced disease by selection bias, since >95% of MSKCC retinoblastoma patients receive vision-sparing therapy). The metastasis-prone subset was identifiable by advanced staging features (optic nerve involvement, choroidal involvement, vitreous seeding). Pre-treatment cfDNA RB1 VAF was detectable in all 3 metastatic patients by tumor-guided genotyping.

### F5: MYCN amplification and RB1-wildtype retinoblastoma not represented; RB1 is the universal target

**Finding:** All 10 tumors in this cohort harbored biallelic RB1 mutations as the primary driver; MYCN-amplified, RB1-wildtype cases (a recognized small subset) were not included.

**Detail:** MSK-IMPACT identified somatic RB1 mutations in all 10 enucleation specimens. Germline RB1 testing results varied: some patients had confirmed heterozygous germline mutations (e.g., P01: c.1072C>T exon 11), mosaic deletions (P11: del24-26), or negative germline testing (indicating somatic biallelic hits). A small subset of retinoblastoma (~2%) is driven by MYCN amplification without RB1 alteration; this cohort does not represent that subgroup. Custom panel targeted only RB1 exons plus SNP regions for copy-number inference.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can plasma cfDNA RB1 VAF levels be used to monitor minimal residual disease or early metastatic relapse after intra-arterial chemotherapy (vision-sparing treatment)?
- Would error-corrected sequencing (UMI-based) or larger cfDNA input volumes improve sensitivity below the current ~0.5% VAF floor, enabling detection in lower tumor burden patients?
- Can aqueous humor or vitreous cfDNA outperform plasma in local disease staging while plasma monitors systemic disease?
- Does pre-treatment plasma cfDNA VAF correlate with response to intra-arterial chemotherapy and risk of enucleation?
- Can cfDNA detect RB1 mutations in the ~90% of MSKCC patients who receive vision-sparing therapy and do not undergo enucleation?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Very small cohort (n=10); proof-of-concept only; insufficient statistical power for correlative analyses.
- De novo calling detected only 54% of expected mutations (7/13); sensitivity insufficient for clinical implementation without tumor-guided genotyping.
- One patient (P19) received prior chemotherapy, potentially reducing circulating tumor DNA shedding; RB1 not detectable in this patient.
- Custom panel limited to RB1 exons; cannot detect MYCN amplification or other genomic alterations relevant to prognosis.
- Two potential false-positive de novo calls could not be verified due to insufficient remaining sample.
- Background error rate at <1% VAF not systematically characterized in healthy donor controls.
