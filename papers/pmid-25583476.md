## Summary

WES of 78 gastric adenocarcinomas from northern Chinese patients (Tianjin Medical University Cancer Institute), plus WGS of two cases with multi-region sampling (3 primary + 2 lymph node metastases each), and targeted sequencing validation in 216 additional cases — 294 total patients with comprehensive clinical follow-up. Clonality analysis (SciClone) defines two GC subtypes: high-clonality (HiC, >4 clone clusters) associated with TP53 mutation, C>G signature, older age, and markedly shorter survival (adjusted HR 4.69); and low-clonality (LoC, ≤4 clusters) associated with ARID1A mutation, younger age, and longer survival. Novel actionable findings include NRG1 and ERBB4 co-mutation in ~10% of GC (Lapatinib-targetable) and BRCA2 mutations (8%) as a predictor of longer survival in patients receiving platinum-based chemotherapy. Data available in `egc_tmucih_2015` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: HiC vs. LoC clonality subtypes are independent prognostic classifiers in gastric cancer

**Finding:** SciClone clonality analysis identified a bimodal distribution of clone cluster numbers, defining two subtypes: HiC (>4 clusters; n=9 in WES cohort) with significantly shorter survival, and LoC (≤4 clusters; n=68) with longer survival (log-rank P=0.02). HiC remained an independent prognostic factor after adjustment for age, sex, stage, Lauren subtype, TP53, and ARID1A mutation (adjusted HR 4.69; 95% CI 1.62–13.6; P=0.0043). HiC was associated with older age, TP53 mutation, and a higher C>G transition fraction; LoC with younger age and ARID1A mutation.

**Detail:** 92.3% of all mutations were subclonal (11,768 protein-coding subclonal SNVs across cohort; median 106 subclonal mutations/sample). The HiC subtype had a significantly smaller C>T transversion fraction (45% vs. 52%) and greater C>G transition fraction (25% vs. 9%, P=0.002). The poor prognosis of HiC may reflect escape of drug-resistant subclones under single-agent therapy. DGIdb analysis of HiC tumors identified targetable mutations in multiple clones per patient (e.g., ALK, ABL2, SMAD4, NRG1, KRAS), suggesting combination therapy would be required for curative intent.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, ARID1A · Gastric adenocarcinoma

### F2: WGS phylogenetics reveals metastases arise from a single primary subclone, not the dominant primary clone

**Finding:** Multi-region WGS of two GC cases (3 primary + 2 LN metastasis samples each) showed considerable divergence among the three primary tumor regions per case. In each case, only one primary-tumor region was closely related to the two lymph node metastases (which were highly similar to each other), indicating metastases were seeded by a single ancestral subclone rather than the bulk primary tumor. Drug-target mapping (DGIdb) showed different actionable targets in different tumor regions, requiring multi-drug strategies for all clones.

**Detail:** Pt1 had 4,082 nonsilent mutations including MSH6, TGFBR2, KDM5A, and MLL4 mutations (mismatch repair/chromatin), consistent with its high mutation rate. Pt2 had only 287 nonsilent mutations, with SETBP1 p.S944N (known myeloid mutation), amplification of AKAP9 and CDK6. CACNA1D p.V529G was mutated in both cases — an intriguing recurrence. Both patients had extensive CNA (>500 Mb affected) and thousands of structural breakpoints. Pt1 may benefit from PI3K + CDK7 + Notch inhibition; Pt2 from AURKC + CDK6 inhibitors.

**Genes / cancer types:** MSH6, TGFBR2, KDM5A, SETBP1, CDK6, CACNA1D · Gastric adenocarcinoma (metastatic)

### F3: NRG1 and ERBB4 mutations define a novel, Lapatinib-targetable axis in ~10% of gastric cancer

**Finding:** ERBB4 was significantly mutated in both the discovery (7/78 cases) and validation (13/216 cases) cohorts. NRG1 (the ERBB4 ligand) was also mutated: 5 discovery + 16 validation cases. Combined, NRG1 or ERBB4 was mutated in 34 of 294 GC cases (11.6%), with 3 samples having mutations in both genes. NRG1 and ERBB4 mutations were mutually exclusive of other ERBB/NRG family mutations (P=0.02 by permutation). ERBB4 kinase domain mutations (7/21, 33%) and receptor domain mutations (4/21, 20%) were represented. The NRG1 EGF-like domain was a hotspot.

**Detail:** ERBB4 p.R50C was shared with melanoma. ERBB4 activates both ERBB4 and PI3K-AKT signaling; it can be inhibited by Lapatinib (dual EGFR/ERBB2 kinase inhibitor). Lapatinib was in Phase III trials for ERBB4-mutant melanoma (NCT01264081) at time of publication. NRG1 overexpression previously shown to activate ERBB signaling and confer Lapatinib sensitivity. ERBB4 and NRG1 mutations were also found in the TCGA GC cohort (Russian/Hong Kong populations), extending the finding beyond Chinese GC. Mutual exclusivity with other ERBB/NRG members supports the axis as a functional unit.

### F4: BRCA2 mutations (8%) are associated with significantly longer survival in platinum-treated gastric cancer

**Finding:** BRCA2 mutations were found in 5/78 WES cases and 12/216 targeted cases (17 total from Tianjin), plus 28/289 TCGA GC cases. When Tianjin and TCGA cohorts were pooled (n=569; 45 BRCA2 mutation carriers), mutation carriers had significantly longer survival (log-rank P=0.03). After adjustment for age, stage, and population, BRCA2 mutation remained a significant independent predictor (P=0.05; HR=0.37; 95% CI 0.13–0.96). No BRCA1 mutations were found. Most BRCA2 mutations were missense (unlike nonsense/frameshift in breast/ovarian cancer).

**Detail:** The proposed mechanism parallels ovarian cancer: BRCA2-deficient cells have reduced capacity to repair platinum-induced DNA damage, improving response to the platinum-doublet/triplet regimens standard for GC. The predominance of missense (rather than frameshift/nonsense) BRCA2 mutations in GC is notable and their functional significance as deleterious mutations was not fully validated, but in silico prediction supports damaging effect. GC is the third most common cancer in BRCA2-mutation carriers after breast and ovarian, making this finding clinically relevant.

### F5: Histologic subtype and anatomic location have distinct mutational correlates in Chinese gastric cancer

**Finding:** CDH1 somatic mutations (21/294 cases, 7.1%) were strongly enriched in diffuse-type GC, and 8/21 were loss-of-function. RHOA mutations were also enriched in diffuse GC, consistent with other reports. Wnt pathway mutations showed moderate enrichment in intestinal-type GC. CDH1-mutated cases had shorter survival; Wnt pathway-mutated cases had longer survival (log-rank P=0.04). RHOA-mutated cases did not show shorter survival like CDH1-mutated cases, suggesting RHOA-driven and CDH1-driven diffuse GCs are clinically distinct. Anatomic enrichments were population-specific (not recapitulated in TCGA).

**Detail:** 13 genes showed significant enrichment in specific anatomical locations (antrum, body, cardia) within the Chinese cohort; 7 remained significant with validation. None of these anatomical enrichments were observed in TCGA (Russian/Hong Kong patients), suggesting population-specific or dietary contributions to mutation distributions. Cardia GC was enriched for CDH1 mutations; antrum for CSMD3 and DST, among others. This highlights the importance of ethnicity-matched cohorts for identifying population-relevant drivers.

## Open Questions

- Can the HiC/LoC clonality classification be assessed from routine clinical sequencing data (targeted panels) rather than WES, enabling prospective use as a prognostic biomarker?
- Do ERBB4 and NRG1 mutations in GC predict Lapatinib or other ERBB-directed therapy response, and is a clinical trial warranted in this 10% molecularly defined population?
- Are BRCA2 mutations in GC truly loss-of-function in the majority of cases (given the predominance of missense variants), and can HRD assays (genomic scar scores) identify platinum-sensitive GC more broadly?
- Why does RHOA mutation associate with diffuse-type histology but not with the inferior survival seen in CDH1-mutated diffuse GC — do RHOA-driven tumors have distinct biology or clinical trajectories?
- Can the population-specific anatomical enrichments (e.g., CDH1 in cardia GC in Chinese) be linked to distinct dietary exposures or H. pylori strain differences?

## Limitations

- HiC subtype comprised only 9 cases in the WES cohort; survival finding requires validation in a prospective, larger dataset
- Multi-region WGS limited to 2 patients; generalizability of metastatic seeding conclusions is uncertain
- Median follow-up of 25.1 months for WES cohort is short for definitive survival analysis in GC
- Most BRCA2 mutations were missense; functional validation as loss-of-function is incomplete
- Clonality analysis by SciClone may be sensitive to sequencing depth and tumor purity; the HiC/LoC boundary (cluster number = 4) was empirically defined
- ERBB4/NRG1 findings need prospective clinical trial validation; Lapatinib efficacy in ERBB4-mutant GC is not established
