## Summary

This pan-cancer whole-exome sequencing study harmonized 249 clinically annotated tumors across six cancer types (melanoma, NSCLC, bladder, HNSCC, anal cancer, sarcoma) treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4). All tumors are microsatellite stable (MSS). The study identifies multiple genomic correlates of ICI response beyond tumor mutational burden (TMB): clonal mutation architecture, specific driver gene mutations (PIK3CA, KRAS, PBRM1 enriched in responders; EGFR enriched in non-responders), mutational signatures (APOBEC, smoking), interferon-γ pathway copy number losses, SWI/SNF complex truncations, and HLA-restricted neoantigen repertoire. The study documents the inter-relatedness of these features and the statistical sample sizes needed to robustly detect individual gene associations. Data are available in cBioPortal as `mixed_allen_2018`.

## Key Findings

### F1: TMB associates with ICI response but with substantial overlap; clonal mutation burden is a stronger predictor

**Finding:** Higher TMB in CR/PR vs. PD patients (p<0.0005) across all cancer types, but distributions overlap substantially (AUC=0.66 as single variable). Clonal nonsynonymous mutational burden is a stronger predictor than total TMB (p=0.00005 for CR/PR vs. PD), and high intratumoral heterogeneity (>50% subclonal mutations) strongly predicts non-response (p=0.0014).

**Detail:** Patients with stable disease had intermediate TMB, and SD patients with long OS had higher mutational burdens than those with short OS. ABSOLUTE was used to infer clonality. The findings confirm that the quality (clonality) of mutations matters as much as the quantity for predicting ICI benefit in MSS tumors.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer (melanoma, NSCLC, bladder, HNSCC) · All MSS tumors

### F2: Driver gene mutations in PIK3CA, KRAS, and PBRM1 enrich in responders; EGFR mutations in non-responders

**Finding:** Clonal driver alterations in *PIK3CA*, *KRAS*, and *PBRM1* are enriched in CR/PR patients (p<0.05), while clonal hotspot mutations in *EGFR* are enriched in PD (p<0.05). *KRAS* and *PIK3CA* associations persist after controlling for TMB.

**Detail:** *PIK3CA* CR/PR cases included melanoma, HNSCC, anal, and bladder cancers — not lung — suggesting cancer-type context matters. *KRAS* mutations occurred across multiple CR/PR cancer types. *EGFR*-mutant tumors had high subclonal proportions, low TMB, and over-representation in never-smokers, forming a biologically coherent ICI-resistant profile. None of these associations passed FDR correction, reflecting the need for larger cohorts (~300 patients for 10% prevalence variants).

**Genes / cancer types:** `PIK3CA`, `KRAS`, `PBRM1`, `EGFR` · Melanoma, NSCLC, bladder, HNSCC

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `native`
> OncoPrint and Mutations tab allow querying individual gene mutations with response annotations. Survival and response tabs support association queries.

### F3: Mutational signatures (APOBEC, smoking, UV) are inter-related with TMB, clonality, and ICI response

**Finding:** In NSCLC, smoking-associated mutations (Signature 4) correlate with high TMB, low intratumoral heterogeneity, and enrichment in CR/PR. In HNSCC and bladder cancer, APOBEC signatures (S2/S13) associate with higher TMB and greater CR/PR likelihood (p=0.019). In melanoma, dominant mutational signature explains much of the variance in TMB; after stratification by signature, TMB alone no longer predicts response within groups.

**Detail:** This finding suggests that in melanoma, TMB may serve as a proxy for underlying mutagenic biology rather than being independently causal in driving ICI response. The APOBEC signature association in bladder/HNSCC may partly explain *PIK3CA* hotspot enrichment in responders, as APOBEC is linked to *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations. These inter-relations highlight the complexity of parsing individual predictors.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer (NSCLC, melanoma, bladder, HNSCC)

**Data availability:** `available`
> Mutation data available in `mixed_allen_2018`; signature decomposition results not directly displayed in cBioPortal UI but can be derived from mutation files.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> Mutational signature analysis requires external tools (e.g., SigProfiler, COSMIC signature fitting). cBioPortal mutation data downloadable for this purpose; results not natively rendered in portal.

**Concepts:** #mutational-signatures #APOBEC #smoking #ICI-biomarker #MSS #clonality

### F4: Interferon-γ pathway copy number losses and SWI/SNF truncations associate with ICI resistance

**Finding:** Copy number alterations disrupting IFN-γ signaling genes are enriched in PD vs. CR/PR (19/123 vs. 3/70; p=0.019). Homozygous *PTEN* deletion occurs exclusively in PD patients (N=4; p=0.30 trend). Truncating mutations in SWI/SNF complex genes (*ARID1A*, *PBRM1*, *SMARCA4*, *SMARCA2*) are enriched in CR/PR, with *PBRM1* LOF particularly notable.

**Detail:** IFN-γ pathway CNAs include losses of JAK1, JAK2, IFNGR1, and amplifications of PD-L1/PD-L2. CDK4/MDM2 amplifications (12q) were enriched in PD. The SWI/SNF finding is consistent with the PBRM1 result in ccRCC (PMID29301960) and suggests chromatin remodeling complex disruption may be a pan-cancer ICI sensitizer.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PTEN`, `PBRM1`, `ARID1A`, `SMARCA4`, `JAK1`, `JAK2` · Pan-cancer

**Data availability:** `available`
> CNA and mutation data available in `mixed_allen_2018`. IFN-γ pathway gene status queryable.

**Tool support:** `native`
> CNA data in `mixed_allen_2018` accessible via Mutations and CNA tabs. Pathway-level analysis available via Pathways tab. OncoKB provides SWI/SNF gene annotations.

**Concepts:** #IFN-gamma #PTEN #SWI-SNF #PBRM1 #resistance #ICI-biomarker #CNA

### F5: HLA-restricted neoantigens and antigen presentation features contribute to response heterogeneity

**Finding:** HLA-restricted neoantigen features (including specific HLA alleles and neoantigen binding affinity) contribute to selective ICI response beyond TMB, though these associations are complex and require large cohorts to validate.

**Detail:** The study applied neoantigen prediction pipelines (MHC binding affinity) to WES mutation data and inferred patient HLA types. Alterations in antigen presentation machinery (B2M, HLA loci) were uncommon overall but present in some non-responding tumors. The study highlights that even in MSS tumors, the immunopeptidome composition (which neoantigens are presented) matters independently of mutational count.

**Genes / cancer types:** `B2M`, HLA loci · Pan-cancer

**Data availability:** `not-available`
> HLA typing and neoantigen binding predictions are not standard cBioPortal fields. B2M mutation data is available but HLA-restricted neoantigen analysis requires external tools.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> HLA typing (POLYSOLVER, OptiType) and neoantigen prediction (pVACseq, NetMHCpan) require external pipelines applied to mutation data downloaded from cBioPortal.

## Open Questions

- Which single genomic feature (or combination) provides the highest positive predictive value for ICI benefit in individual MSS tumor subtypes?
- Do KRAS and PIK3CA mutations drive ICI sensitivity through immune-intrinsic mechanisms (e.g., altered cytokine signaling), or are they proxies for mutagenic processes?
- Can SWI/SNF complex disruption (PBRM1, ARID1A, SMARCA4) be validated as a pan-cancer ICI sensitizer in prospective biomarker-enriched trials?
- What sample sizes are needed to robustly validate rare (1%) response-associated genomic variants with proper FDR correction?
- How should intratumoral heterogeneity be measured and weighted in multi-analyte ICI response prediction models?

## Limitations

- Retrospective, multi-cohort study with heterogeneous treatment regimens (anti-PD-1, anti-CTLA-4, combination) potentially confounding genomic associations
- Sample sizes insufficient for most individual gene associations to survive FDR correction; many findings are hypothesis-generating
- All tumors are microsatellite stable by design; MSI-H tumors excluded, limiting generalizability to the full cancer spectrum
- HLA and neoantigen analyses limited by computational complexity and lack of standardized pipelines across contributing studies
- Tumor purity variability (10–97%) introduces noise in CNA and clonality estimates despite correction
- Cancer type representation is uneven (melanoma N=151 vs. bladder N=27), which may bias pan-cancer findings toward melanoma biology
