## Summary

First whole-genome sequencing (WGS) characterization of soft tissue myoepithelial carcinomas (MECs), supplemented by WES and RNA-seq, in two male patients with metastatic disease (ages 27 and 37 at diagnosis) spanning 12 tumor samples total. The study confirms the defining biology of MECs — low tumor mutational burden (mean 1.35 mut/Mb), gene rearrangements involving the 22q11-12 region, and absence of actionable point mutations in known cancer genes — while revealing for the first time the complex structural variant (SV) underpinnings of these events. Patient 1 harbors a novel ASCC2::GGNBP2 fusion and biallelic SMARCB1/NF1 deletion arising from a single >30-way biallelic chromoplexy event targeting chromosomes 17, 19, and 22. Patient 2 carries a known EWSR1::KLF15 fusion alongside ELK4 amplification arising from a double-minute chromosome. WGS provides mechanistic insights invisible to targeted sequencing or WES, establishing MECs as a gene fusion–driven cancer type shaped by complex structural rearrangements.

## Key Findings

### F1: Both MECs have low TMB and no actionable point mutations — consistent across primary and all metastatic samples

**Finding:** Whole-exome sequencing of 12 samples (6 per patient, spanning primary and metastatic/recurrent sites) demonstrates uniformly low tumor mutational burden (mean 1.35 mut/Mb, range 0.43–2.98), with no Tier 1 or Tier 2 cancer gene variants actionable by current standards. This low TMB is stable from primary tumor to all metastatic sites for both patients, suggesting it is an intrinsic feature of MEC biology rather than an artifact.

**Detail:** The only variants in annotated cancer genes found by WES were a likely-damaging POLE missense mutation (p.E830G) in three Patient 2 samples, a NUMA1 mutation, and LARP4B mutation, all predicted with low confidence for functional relevance. The absence of actionable alterations in known cancer genes — including in KRAS, BRAF, PIK3CA, TP53, RB1, and others — is consistent with the published case reports of individual MECs showing similarly sparse point mutation landscapes. This points to structural rearrangements rather than point mutations as the primary oncogenic mechanism, underscoring the inadequacy of panel sequencing alone for comprehensive MEC characterization.

### F2: Patient 1 harbors a novel ASCC2::GGNBP2 fusion arising from a >30-way biallelic chromoplexy event that simultaneously deletes SMARCB1 and NF1

**Finding:** WGS and RNA-seq of Patient 1's tumor (rhabdoid morphology, INI-1 loss) revealed a novel ASCC2 (Chr 22)::GGNBP2 (Chr 17) in-frame fusion transcript, validated by RT-PCR and Sanger sequencing. WGS analysis using JaBbA and gGnome revealed that this fusion, along with biallelic deletion of SMARCB1 (Chr 22q) and NF1 (Chr 17q), arose from a single complex cluster of >30 rearrangement junctions spanning chromosomes 17, 19, and 22 — consistent with a biallelic chromoplexy event where both parental homologs were simultaneously rearranged.

**Detail:** The chromoplexy cluster targeted break ends within 150 kbp of each other and was deconvolved into six complex derivative chromosomes. Allelic copy number analysis showed only focal LOH across the three affected chromosomes, consistent with rearrangement of both parental alleles rather than sequential events. SMARCB1 was additionally targeted by a simple deletion junction that may have occurred before or after the chromoplexy, explaining its biallelic loss. The ASCC2::GGNBP2 fusion is novel — GGNBP2 encodes a zinc-finger DNA-binding protein (ZNF403) whose knockdown impairs anchorage-independent growth; ASCC2 is a transcriptional coactivator with potential functional similarity to EWSR1 and FUS. The zinc-finger 3′ partner pattern mirrors other MEC fusions (EWSR1::KLF15, EWSR1::ZNF444, FUS::KLF17).

### F3: Patient 2 harbors a confirmed EWSR1::KLF15 rearrangement — supporting a distinct MEC molecular subgroup with characteristic pathology

**Finding:** WGS and RNA-seq of Patient 2's tumor (clear cell/small cell morphology, INI-1 retained, strong S100) identified an EWSR1 (exon 8)::KLF15 (exon 2) in-frame fusion, confirmed by FISH and RNA-seq. This fusion has previously been reported in six MECs and one benign myoepithelial tumor, all in children/young adults — making Patient 2 (age 37) an older outlier. Pathological features match prior EWSR1::KLF15 cases, including biphasic morphology, small round cell appearance, focal clear cells, diffuse S100 expression, and retained INI-1, supporting that EWSR1::KLF15 MECs form a distinct pathological subgroup.

**Detail:** EWSR1 rearrangements occur in ~50% of MECs overall, with numerous 3′ partners (POU5F1, PBX1, PBX3, ZNF444, KLF17, KLF15, ATF1). KLF15 (Krüppel-Like Factor 15) is a zinc-finger transcription factor; the EWSR1::KLF15 fusion likely creates an aberrant transcription factor fusing the transactivation domain of EWSR1 to the DNA-binding domain of KLF15. Patient 2 additionally showed high-level (>10 copy) ELK4 amplification, arising from a complex nested focal duplication pattern on Chr 1q consistent with a double-minute extrachromosomal circular DNA — a novel finding not previously described in MEC. ELK4 is an ETS family transcription factor, and its cis-splicing with SLC45A3 drives oncogenic expression in prostate cancer.

### F4: COSMIC Signature 3 (HR deficiency) enriched in both MEC genomes despite absence of detectable HR pathway gene mutations

**Finding:** Mutational signature analysis revealed that a substantial fraction of SNVs in both patients matched COSMIC Signature 3, associated with homologous recombination (HR) deficiency: 36.8% of SNVs in MEC1 and 17.4% in MEC2. Despite systematic evaluation of somatic alterations in known HR pathway genes (BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, RAD51, etc.), no high-confidence HR gene mutations were identified in either tumor.

**Detail:** The presence of Signature 3 without a detectable HR gene mutation is a recognized phenomenon in some cancers where HR deficiency may arise from epigenetic silencing, promoter hypermethylation, or RAD51-pathway dysfunction not captured by somatic mutation analysis. In MECs, the HR deficiency signature may alternatively reflect an as-yet-uncharacterized mechanism related to the structural rearrangement burden — complex SVs could arise from replication stress or DSB repair defects that also generate an Sig3-like mutational footprint. Whether Sig3-positive MECs respond to PARP inhibitors or platinum chemotherapy remains to be tested.

### F5: WGS reveals complex structural variant landscape (chromoplexy, double minutes, chromothripsis) invisible to WES — expanding knowledge of MEC biology

**Finding:** Despite near-diploid genomes and low TMB, both MEC tumors harbored rich spectra of structural variants by WGS: both simple events (translocations, duplications, deletions, inversions) and complex patterns (chromoplexy, templated insertion chains, double minutes). Patient 1's chromoplexy cluster comprised a >30-junction biallelic event; Patient 2's ELK4 amplification arose from a nested focal high-copy duplication pattern consistent with a double minute. These complex events were entirely invisible to WES, which failed to detect the mechanistic basis of even the known pathological findings (SMARCB1 loss, EWSR1 rearrangement context).

**Detail:** Soft tissue sarcomas are known to be enriched in complex SVs; this study extends that observation to MECs. The chromoplexy in Patient 1 — where a single punctuated event rearranged both parental alleles across three chromosomes — is unprecedented in MECs and raises fundamental questions about the pre-conditions necessary for such events. The analogy to Ewing sarcoma (where Anderson et al. showed most EWSR1::ERG fusions arise via chromoplexy) is notable: MECs similarly appear to use complex rearrangements rather than simple translocations to generate their defining gene fusions. This argues that WGS, not WES or targeted sequencing, is the appropriate platform for MEC genomic characterization.

## Open Questions

- Is the ASCC2::GGNBP2 fusion a recurrent driver in MECs lacking EWSR1 rearrangement — particularly those with rhabdoid features and INI-1 loss?
- What is the functional consequence of the ASCC2::GGNBP2 fusion protein — does ASCC2 act as a transcriptional coactivator analogous to EWSR1's role in other sarcoma fusions?
- Does Signature 3 (HR deficiency) in the absence of detectable HR mutations indicate PARP inhibitor or platinum sensitivity in MECs?
- How common is biallelic chromoplexy (targeting both parental alleles simultaneously) in other soft tissue sarcomas, and what genomic preconditions favor this catastrophic event?
- Does the EWSR1::KLF15 MEC subgroup represent a distinct disease entity with a unified clinical behavior justifying separate classification?
- Could ELK4 amplification (via double minute) in Patient 2 be targeted therapeutically, analogous to extrachromosomal DNA amplification strategies being explored in glioblastoma?

## Limitations

- Extremely small cohort (n=2 patients, 12 samples): all findings are exploratory and cannot be considered as frequency estimates for MECs generally
- WGS performed at relatively low coverage (29× tumor, 25× normal for P1; 22× and 15× for P2) — insufficient for confident detection of low-allele-frequency subclonal mutations and some categories of SVs
- Patient 2 WGS was performed on FFPE material, which introduces DNA damage artifacts affecting SV calling accuracy
- No functional validation of the novel ASCC2::GGNBP2 fusion — whether it is a driver or a bystander event of the chromoplexy remains speculative
- The clinical significance of Signature 3 in MECs cannot be tested for therapeutic implications without a clinical trial or larger cohort with treatment response data
- Comparative genomic analysis with rhabdoid tumors, Ewing sarcoma, and other EWSR1-rearranged tumors not performed at the WGS level — molecular overlap remains poorly characterized
- BRM (SMARCA2) alternating expression pattern observed by IHC but not explained at the genomic level; the mechanism of this heterogeneous loss awaits investigation
