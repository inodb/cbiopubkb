## Summary

Expanded Beat AML functional genomic resource combining ex vivo drug sensitivity testing with genomic, transcriptomic, and clinical annotations across a cumulative cohort of 805 AML patients (942 specimens, spanning 10 years of collection). This study harmonizes Waves 1+2 (published 2018) with Waves 3+4 (new), demonstrating strong cross-cohort concordance (89% for mutation-drug associations, 98% for expression module-drug correlations). Key findings: AML cell maturation state broadly governs drug response and can conditionally modify mutation-drug associations; 73 drugs correlate with at least one cell-type signature; the single gene PEAR1 is among the strongest predictors of overall survival in young AML patients, performing equivalently to the 17-gene LSC17 signature. Data available at `aml_ohsu_2022` on cBioPortal and through the Vizome interactive browser.

## Key Findings

### F1: Cross-cohort concordance validates the Beat AML platform as a robust functional genomic resource

**Finding:** Waves 3+4 serve as an independent validation cohort for findings from Waves 1+2. Mutation frequencies, overall survival distributions, and ex vivo drug response profiles are highly concordant between cohorts (Pearson r=0.965 for drug AUC; 89% concordance for mutation-drug associations; 98% for expression module-drug correlations).

**Detail:** De novo AML frequency was slightly lower in Waves 1+2 (49%) vs Waves 3+4 (58%), the only notable clinical difference. RNA-seq was harmonized using conditional quantile normalization (CQN) parameters learned from Waves 1+2 and applied to Waves 3+4, yielding near-complete overlap in WGCNA eigengene distributions across 13 modules. 65 new mutation-drug associations reached significance in the combined cohort that were not significant in Waves 1+2 alone (27% increase), demonstrating the value of increased sample size.

**Genes / cancer types:** FLT3, NPM1, TP53, RUNX1, ASXL1 · Acute myeloid leukemia

### F2: AML cell maturation state governs drug response broadly across 73 inhibitors

**Finding:** Using six cell-type gene expression scores derived from single-cell RNA-seq signatures (HSC-like, Progenitor-like, GMP-like, Promonocyte-like, Monocyte-like, cDC-like), 73 drugs showed response significantly correlated with at least one cell-type. Drugs cluster into three groups: active against primitive states, active against differentiated states, or broadly active.

**Detail:** BCL2 inhibitors (e.g., venetoclax) and CDK4/6 inhibitors show stronger efficacy against less differentiated (HSC-/Progenitor-like) states. BET and MEK inhibitors show better efficacy against more differentiated (Monocyte-like) tumors. Venetoclax and panobinostat (HDAC inhibitor) are extreme outliers with inverse response patterns. Mutation-to-cell-type associations include: TP53/BCOR/SF3B1 → HSC-like enrichment; NPM1 → Promonocyte-like; RAS → Monocyte-like; RUNX1 → both HSC-like and cDC-like (suggesting co-option of disparate states).

**Genes / cancer types:** FLT3, NPM1, TP53, RAS, RUNX1, BCOR, SF3B1 · AML

### F3: Cell maturation state conditionally modifies mutation-drug associations (FLT3-ITD/sorafenib example)

**Finding:** Several drug-mutation associations are conditional on AML cell maturation state. The canonical FLT3-ITD/sorafenib sensitivity relationship is substantially modified by the Monocyte-like score: FLT3-ITD cases with a high Progenitor-like score respond well to sorafenib, while a prominent Monocyte-like signal confers resistance despite FLT3-ITD.

**Detail:** Numerous instances of significant interactions between cell-type score and mutational/cytogenetic events were identified, mostly involving the Monocyte-like score. PIK family inhibitors show strong activity against NPM1-mutated cases with high cDC-like scores, but resistance in NPM1-mutated cases with high HSC-like scores. These conditional effects highlight that mutation-based biomarkers alone are insufficient to predict drug response without accounting for differentiation state. This has immediate implications for patient stratification in clinical trials.

**Genes / cancer types:** FLT3, NPM1, RAS · AML

**Data availability:** `available`

### F4: Treatment-refractory genetic subtypes (TP53, STAG2, RUNX1) sensitive to PIK family inhibitors

**Finding:** Aggregating drugs into 25 families by shared targets reveals unexpected associations: TP53, STAG2, and RUNX1 mutations — all associated with poor prognosis and treatment resistance — show significantly greater sensitivity to phosphatidylinositol kinase (PIK) family inhibitors. N/KRAS mutations confer greater sensitivity to CDK inhibitors.

**Detail:** FLT3-ITD with Type III RTK inhibitors and RAS with MEK (STE7) inhibitors are expected associations confirmed at scale. The PIK inhibitor sensitivity of poor-prognosis subtypes (TP53, STAG2, RUNX1) is a novel finding with potential therapeutic implications, since these subtypes have very limited standard-of-care options. Drug family aggregation increases statistical power relative to single-drug analysis, enabling detection of previously underpowered associations.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, STAG2, RUNX1, FLT3, RAS, KRAS, NRAS · AML

### F5: PEAR1 is a single-gene predictor of overall survival in AML, equivalent to LSC17, especially in young patients

**Finding:** PEAR1 (platelet endothelial aggregation receptor 1, also known as JEDI/MEGF12) is the hub gene of WGCNA module 3, which is one of the strongest predictors of poor overall survival. PEAR1 expression is strongly correlated with an HSC-like malignant cell state and predicts outcome in young AML patients (<45 years) equivalently to the established 17-gene LSC17 signature.

**Detail:** Module 3 (203 genes) is among the strongest predictors of poor outcome in young patients and TP53-wild-type middle-aged patients in a conditional inference forest model. PEAR1 is the highest kME (hub connectivity) gene in module 3, far exceeding all others. PEAR1 expression is significantly elevated in Adverse vs. Favorable ELN 2017 risk groups (older patients), in transformed AML, and in genetic subtypes with poor prognosis (TP53, RUNX1, ASXL1, SRSF2). Validated in two independent cohorts (TCGA AML, Malani et al.). PEAR1 surface expression on AML patient tumor cells was confirmed by flow cytometry, suggesting a potential therapeutic target.

**Genes / cancer types:** PEAR1, TP53, RUNX1, ASXL1, NPM1, CEBPA · AML

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `native`

## Open Questions

- Does PEAR1 causally drive HSC-like tumor state, or is it a downstream marker of a broader transcriptional program?
- Can PEAR1 surface expression be exploited therapeutically (antibody-drug conjugate, bispecific antibody) without disrupting normal platelet aggregation?
- Are the PIK inhibitor sensitivities of TP53/STAG2/RUNX1-mutant AML reproducible in vivo and in the context of standard AML induction regimens?
- Does the cell-type maturation state at diagnosis predict clinical response to venetoclax+azacitidine (now standard of care) in the same way as ex vivo BCL2 inhibitor sensitivity?
- What is the biological explanation for RUNX1-mutant AML showing enrichment for both the most primitive (HSC-like) and most differentiated (cDC-like) states simultaneously?

## Limitations

- Ex vivo drug sensitivity testing may not perfectly predict in vivo response due to absence of microenvironment and pharmacokinetic factors
- Beat AML cohort is predominantly anthracycline-era treatment; PEAR1 prognostic significance has not been validated in the venetoclax/azacitidine era
- Non-Caucasian patients underrepresented (N=13 African Heritage); PEAR1 expression differences across ethnicities not fully powered
- Cell-type score deconvolution is bulk RNA-seq-based; single-cell resolution would provide more precise estimates
- Drug panel tested varies across patient specimens; not every drug was tested in every patient
