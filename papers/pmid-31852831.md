## Summary

Comprehensive genomic and phenotypic characterization of 96 Mayo Clinic glioblastoma patient-derived xenografts (PDX), the largest annotated PDX collection at time of publication. PDX were established primarily from IDH-wildtype GBM (n=93) with 47% engraftment rate for grade IV gliomas. Integrated multi-omic profiling including WES (n=83), RNA-seq (n=68), and genome-wide methylation (n=76) shows that the panel broadly captures GBM molecular heterogeneity — gene expression subtypes (classical 46%, mesenchymal 31%, proneural 23%), methylation classes (LGm4/5/6), and MGMT promoter methylation status. EGFR alterations are frequent (45%) and preserved during engraftment; PDGFRA amplification is notably absent in all PDX. MGMT promoter methylation predicts temozolomide response in orthotopic models, and patient-PDX treatment response correlates significantly (ρ=0.53). All data and PDX models are openly shared via cBioPortal (study `gbm_mayo_pdx_sarkaria_2019`).

## Key Findings

### F1: PDX panel broadly captures GBM molecular heterogeneity, with exception of PDGFRA amplification

**Finding:** The 96-PDX panel recapitulates common GBM driver alterations at frequencies comparable to TCGA: TERT promoter mutations (86%), CDKN2A homozygous deletion (70%), EGFR alterations (45%), PTEN loss/mutation (48%), TP53 mutations (36%), MDM2 amplification (10%), NF1 mutations (17%), and RB1 pathway inactivation (16% RB1 + CDK4/6 amplification). However, PDGFRA amplification, present in ~15% of human GBM, is absent from all PDX lines, suggesting a selection bottleneck or pressure to lose this amplification during xenograft engraftment.

**Detail:** EGFR alterations are diverse: high-level amplification (n=35), SNVs (n=12 lines), and exon deletion variants EGFRvIII (n=11, Δexon2–7), EGFRvII (n=2), C-terminal deletions (n=2), and a novel Δexon6–7 (n=1). All exon deletion variants occur in EGFR-amplified PDX. Despite absent PDGFRA amplification, 29% and 24% of PDX show strong and moderate PDGFRA protein expression, suggesting PDGFRA expression without copy number amplification. Three rare fusions identified: FGFR3-TACC3 (3 PDX), PTPRZ1-MET, CHTOP-NTRK1 — representing potentially targetable alterations.

### F2: MGMT promoter methylation predicts temozolomide response in orthotopic PDX models

**Finding:** MGMT promoter methylation (present in 45–55% of PDX depending on assay) is strongly associated with response to temozolomide (TMZ) and concurrent RT/TMZ in orthotopic xenograft experiments, but does not correlate with response to radiation therapy alone. This recapitulates the clinical biomarker utility of MGMT methylation in GBM patients.

**Detail:** 37 PDX lines were treated with RT, TMZ, concurrent RT/TMZ, or bevacizumab. Median survival ratios relative to placebo: RT=1.6, TMZ=2.5, RT/TMZ=2.8. MGMT methylated (M) PDX showed significantly greater benefit from TMZ and RT/TMZ versus MGMT unmethylated (U) PDX. Bevacizumab showed survival ratio >1.3 in 23% of PDX (7/30). Patient-PDX treatment response correlation coefficient ρ=0.53 for standard therapies. MGMT methylation was concordant between clinical testing and PDX in 19 of 20 matched pairs. These pre-clinical findings directly informed a biomarker enrichment strategy for the Alliance Phase II/III trial of veliparib + TMZ.

### F3: Gene expression subtypes reflect GBM heterogeneity; classical enriched for EGFR amplification

**Finding:** RNA-seq of 68 orthotopic PDX reveals classical (46%), mesenchymal (31%), and proneural (23%) expression subtypes consistent with the three-subtype GBM classification. Classical subtype is significantly enriched for EGFR-amplified PDX (78% of classical vs. 29% mesenchymal and 7% proneural), while mesenchymal subtype is enriched for NF1 alterations.

**Detail:** Expression subtype assignment uses an updated classification scheme excluding the neural subtype. PCA of RNA-seq data shows grouping by expression subtype. Recurrent fusion transcripts include FGFR3-TACC3 (3 lines), FGFR3-TRIM54, FGFR3-CALCOCO1, PTPRZ1-MET, and CHTOP-NTRK1, the latter two representing therapeutic targets in clinical development. Methylation profiling by EPIC array assigned PDX to TCGA pan-glioma classes LGm4 (46%), LGm5 (44%), and LGm6 (10%), largely IDH-wildtype classes, with 3 IDH-mutant PDX in LGm1.

### F4: Patient-PDX genomic concordance is high; clonal selection occurs at low frequency

**Finding:** WES of 24 matched patient-PDX pairs shows high concordance for driver alterations: EGFR amplification is preserved in 100% of amplified patient-PDX pairs confirmed by both WES and FISH. In only 4 of 24 pairs are notable discordances observed, characterized by clonal selection events (e.g., subclonal PDGFRA amplification lost, subclonal MYCN amplification selected) rather than de novo alterations.

**Detail:** Private mutations in either patient or PDX are predominantly low VAF, consistent with pre-existing subclonal heterogeneity rather than new mutations during engraftment. In GBM110, MYCN amplification was subclonal in patient (0–27% of cells by FISH) but clonal in all PDX cells, a clear positive selection event. In GBM159, PDGFRA amplification (45–75% subclonal in patient) was lost in PDX, a negative selection event. GBM181 showed hypermutation phenotype from MSH6 mutation in both patient and PDX. Two PDX showed CDKN2A homozygous loss compared to single-copy loss in the patient, consistent with LOH at engraftment. These changes reflect the known intra-tumoral heterogeneity of GBM and the selective pressure of the xenograft microenvironment.

### F5: Recurrent-tumor PDX capture hypermutation and therapy-related genetic changes

**Finding:** PDX derived from recurrent GBM (n=28) show similar overall mutation burdens to primary-tumor PDX, but four recurrent PDX harbor multiple MSH6 mutations and high mutation burden consistent with TMZ-induced hypermutation. Recurrent-specific alterations LTBP4, IGF1R, and PRDM2 are identified in proportions matching published recurrent GBM data (14%, 7%, 7%).

**Detail:** MSH6 mutation-driven hypermutation in recurrent GBM is a well-characterized consequence of TMZ therapy (TMZ is a methylating agent and MSH6 repairs O6-methylguanine). The GBM154 PDX is a particularly instructive case: established from recurrence 10 years after initial LGG oligodendroglioma diagnosis; retained TERT promoter, CHEK2, and 1p/19q co-deletion from the original tumor but lost IDH1 p.R132H, acquired a new IDH1 p.F397L mutation — an example of IDH loss on high-grade transformation. Recurrent PDX engraftment rate (42%) is slightly lower than primary (50%), possibly reflecting therapy-related necrosis in post-treatment specimens.

## Open Questions

- Why is PDGFRA amplification systematically lost during PDX engraftment, and can this be prevented (e.g., through modified engraftment protocols or co-culture with relevant stromal cells)?
- Do the recurrent fusion transcripts (FGFR3-TACC3, PTPRZ1-MET, CHTOP-NTRK1) predict response to cognate targeted inhibitors across the PDX panel?
- Can MGMT methylation thresholds established in PDX pre-clinical trials be directly translated to clinical trial patient enrichment strategies?
- How does extended serial passage affect the genetic fidelity and drug response characteristics of these PDX, particularly for subclonal alterations?
- Would restoration of an intact immune microenvironment (e.g., humanized mouse models) alter the survival benefit of bevacizumab or enable immunotherapy studies?

## Limitations

- PDX lack an intact immune system, precluding immunotherapy studies and potentially affecting drug delivery and tumor microenvironment
- PDGFRA amplification systematically absent from PDX despite ~15% prevalence in patient GBM, limiting translational studies for this alteration
- Methylation profiling may be confounded by contaminating mouse stromal DNA and higher passage numbers
- Serial PDX passage may introduce additional copy number changes not captured in this cross-sectional characterization
- Engraftment rate of 36% overall (47% for grade IV) means a substantial fraction of patient tumors, potentially enriched for specific molecular subtypes, could not be modeled
