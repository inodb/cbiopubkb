## Summary

This study sequenced 240 matched tumor/normal pairs from high-risk (stage 4) neuroblastoma patients older than 18 months, using whole exome sequencing (WES; 221 cases), whole genome sequencing (WGS; 18 cases), and RNA-seq (10 cases) as part of the NCI TARGET initiative. The study identified a remarkably low somatic mutation rate (median 0.60 mutations/Mb) and few recurrently mutated genes, with *ALK* (9.2%), *PTPN11* (2.9%), *ATRX* (9.6% including deletions), *MYCN* point mutations (1.7%), and *NRAS* (0.83%) as the key drivers. Enriched germline variants in *ALK*, *CHEK2*, *PINK1*, and *BARD1* suggest a larger role for predisposition than previously recognized. The paucity of recurrent druggable somatic mutations challenges precision oncology strategies reliant on DNA sequencing alone.

## Key Findings

### F1: Low somatic mutation burden in high-risk neuroblastoma

**Finding:** High-risk neuroblastoma has one of the lowest somatic mutation rates observed in any cancer type, with a median of 0.60 mutations per megabase (0.48 non-silent). Across 240 cases, a total of 5,291 candidate somatic mutations were detected in 3,960 genes, with a median of 18 exomic mutations per tumor.

**Detail:** The mutation frequency is comparable to medulloblastoma and hematopoietic malignancies, twice that of pediatric rhabdoid cancer, and far below adult solid tumors. No correlation was observed between mutation frequency and age at diagnosis (p=0.28, Spearman) or other clinical variables. Two hypermutated outlier tumors (7.27 and 4.29 mutations/Mb) harbored DNA repair gene defects (*MLH1* and *DB1*). Mutation context (transition/transversion ratios) did not implicate environmental mutagenesis, and no pathogenic viral sequences were detected.

### F2: ALK kinase domain mutations are the most frequent somatic driver

**Finding:** *ALK* was the most significantly mutated gene, with somatic missense mutations in 9.2% of cases (22/240), all restricted to the kinase domain at 7 unique sites. *ALK* mutation was the only significantly mutated gene associated with inferior overall survival (p=0.0103).

**Detail:** All 22 *ALK* mutations were missense, with no loss-of-function events, consistent with an activating oncogenic role. Activating *ALK* mutations were not associated with *MYCN* amplification (p=0.28). Contrary to a prior report, p.Phe1174 mutations were not enriched in *MYCN*-amplified cases (p=0.53). Two germline *ALK* variants were also identified: the pathogenic p.Arg1275Gln (known familial neuroblastoma allele) and the likely benign p.Ile1250Thr. *ALK* is a directly targetable receptor tyrosine kinase with FDA-approved inhibitors (crizotinib, lorlatinib) active in ALK-driven cancers.

### F3: ATRX loss-of-function alterations define an older-onset subgroup mutually exclusive with MYCN amplification

**Finding:** *ATRX* was altered in 9.6% of cases (6 loss-of-function mutations plus 17 multi-exon deletions), defining a distinct genomic subgroup enriched in adolescents and young adults that does not overlap with *MYCN* amplification.

**Detail:** Loss-of-function *ATRX* mutations included 3 missense and 3 truncating events; focal multi-exon deletions accounted for the majority (17 cases). *ATRX* alterations were mutually exclusive with *MYCN* amplification (p<0.05) and significantly enriched in older patients (p=0.0021). *ATRX* encodes a chromatin remodeling helicase involved in alternative lengthening of telomeres (ALT); its loss may confer a distinct mechanism of genomic instability. ### F4: Recurrent MYCN point mutation (p.Pro44Leu) independent of amplification

**Finding:** Four cases without *MYCN* amplification harbored an identical *MYCN* p.Pro44Leu (p.Phe44Leu) activating point mutation, representing a novel mechanism of MYCN activation distinct from gene amplification.

**Detail:** All four cases had regional single-copy gain of chromosome 2p, with three showing allelic gain of the mutant allele. In one case with RNA-seq data, the mutant allele was expressed at twice the level of wild-type. The Pro44Leu variant scores as functional by PolyPhen2 (0.971), SIFT (0), MutationTaster (1.0), and AlignGVGD (C65). The residue is conserved across the MYC superfamily (pfam01056). ### F5: RAS/MAPK pathway mutations, including PTPN11 and NRAS

**Finding:** Beyond *ALK*, additional RAS/MAPK pathway genes were recurrently mutated: *PTPN11* in 2.9% (7/240) and *NRAS* in 0.83% (2/240) of cases. Pathway-level enrichment analysis identified 8 of 12 significantly enriched gene sets implicating RAS/MAPK signaling components.

**Detail:** All 7 *PTPN11* mutations were missense at previously reported hotspot sites (COSMIC), consistent with gain-of-function SHP2 activation. No pathogenic germline *PTPN11* variants were detected. The 2 *NRAS* mutations were identified only by restricting MutSig analysis to COSMIC genes, reflecting borderline statistical significance given the cohort size. These RAS/MAPK pathway mutations are potentially targetable (MEK inhibitors, SHP2 inhibitors). However, the low individual gene frequencies suggest that genomic profiling of individual tumors may miss most patients with pathway activation.

### F6: Enrichment of pathogenic germline variants in ALK, CHEK2, PINK1, and BARD1

**Finding:** Systematic germline analysis identified significant enrichment of rare pathogenic variants in *ALK*, *CHEK2*, *PINK1*, and *BARD1* in neuroblastoma cases compared to 1,974 ESP controls, suggesting inherited predisposition plays a larger role than previously recognized.

**Detail:** *ALK* p.Arg1275Gln (the most common familial neuroblastoma allele) was confirmed. Three *CHEK2* variants (p.Arg145Trp, p.Arg181His, p.Arg180His) destabilize the protein and are established cancer predisposition alleles not previously reported in neuroblastoma. Two *PINK1* variants (p.Leu437Pro, p.Arg279His) associated with Parkinson disease were found; *PINK1* is known to be regulated by MYC proteins. Two *BARD1* loss-of-function variants (p.Arg112*, p.Arg641*) support rare variant contributions at a locus already implicated by GWAS. *TP53* p.Pro219Ser (Li-Fraumeni) and a *PALB2* splice-site variant were also identified. These findings suggest a polygenic germline architecture in neuroblastoma predisposition.

### F7: Structural rearrangements cluster near MYCN/NBAS locus; no chromothripsis detected

**Finding:** WGS of 19 cases identified a median of 41.5 chromosomal breakpoints per case (range 29â€“143), with *NBAS* (adjacent to *MYCN* on 2p) the most rearranged geneâ€”11 distinct events in 3 *MYCN*-amplified casesâ€”but no recurrent fusion transcripts were detected.

**Detail:** Of 83 rearrangements affecting 97 genes, 22 had RNA-seq support; 11q;17q translocations were found in 3/19 cases. Complex local rearrangements around *MYCN* and *NBAS* were more consistent with an episomal amplification model than chromothripsis. No chromothripsis was detected in any of the 19 WGS cases or 142 WES cases, contrasting with findings from Molenaar et al. Rearrangements of *ARID1B* (focal exon deletions) and single cases of *ARID1A*, *VANGL1*, and *PBRM1* mutations were also identified. The absence of recurrent fusions limits fusion-targeted therapeutic strategies in this tumor type.

## Open Questions

- Do *ATRX*-altered neuroblastomas rely on alternative lengthening of telomeres (ALT), and can ALT pathway inhibition serve as a therapeutic target in this subgroup?
- Do *MYCN* point mutations (p.Pro44Leu) confer MYC-family dependency equivalent to high-level amplification, and would these tumors respond to BET bromodomain or CDK7 inhibitors?
- What non-mutational mechanisms (epigenetic, copy number, structural) drive the majority of high-risk neuroblastomas that lack identified somatic driver mutations?
- Can germline variants in *CHEK2*, *PINK1*, and *BARD1* be validated as neuroblastoma predisposition alleles in larger case-control cohorts?
- Do pathway-level RAS/MAPK alterations (across *ALK*, *PTPN11*, *NRAS*) collectively define a subgroup that could benefit from MEK or SHP2 inhibition?
- How does the interplay of *MYCN* amplification, chromosomal copy number alterations (1p, 11q), and the sparse somatic mutation landscape determine tumor evolution and treatment resistance?

## Limitations

- WGS was performed on only 19 of 240 cases, limiting detection of structural variants, non-coding mutations, and chromothripsis in the full cohort.
- Phi29-based whole genome amplification (WGA) was used for 80 cases, introducing oxoG artifact that required bioinformatic correction and may have affected sensitivity.
- The germline analysis was compared against a European American ESP cohort, limiting power to detect predisposition variants in non-Caucasian patients (~20% of cohort).
- RNA-seq was available for only 10 Illumina WGS cases, restricting transcriptomic validation of mutations and fusion detection to a small subset.
- The study focused exclusively on stage 4 high-risk neuroblastoma, so findings may not generalize to lower-stage or MYCN-non-amplified tumors.
- Pathway enrichment analyses showed discordance with prior neuroblastoma studies (e.g., Rac/Rho pathway), highlighting limitations of gene set methods at low mutation frequencies.
