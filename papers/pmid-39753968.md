## Summary

The largest clinicogenomic study of PDAC to date, profiling 2,336 patients with MSK-IMPACT across all disease stages. The study establishes three genomic subtypes (KRAS-MUT, other-MAPK-MUT, MAPK-WT) and demonstrates that **KRAS mutant allele dosage gains**—present in 1 in 5 diploid KRAS-mutant tumors—are a hallmark of disease progression and an independent prognostic marker of poor OS across all stages. KRAS-WT tumors have significantly better OS even absent targeted therapy. Germline pathogenic variants occur in 10% of patients. The findings have immediate implications for KRAS-targeted therapy trials and for clinical sequencing reporting standards.

## Key Findings

### F1: Three genomic subtypes of PDAC with distinct biology and OS

**Finding:** 95% of PDACs are KRAS-MUT; 3.3% harbor other oncogenic MAPK alterations (BRAF, NRAS, NF1, NTRK1/3, FGFR2, ERBB2, MAP2K1, ROS1, MET, RAF1); 2.2% are MAPK-WT. MAPK-WT and other-MAPK-MUT tumors have significantly better OS (HR adj ≈ 0.69 vs. KRAS-MUT, p ≈ 0.014–0.041) even excluding patients who received targeted therapies.

**Detail:** Other-MAPK-MUT tumors are enriched for oncogenic fusions (3.2% vs 0.04% in KRAS-MUT). BRAF in-frame deletions (exons N486–P490) occur in 7/26 PDAC BRAF alterations—a PDAC-specific alteration pattern distinct from melanoma or thyroid. MAPK-WT patients are enriched for germline ATM pathogenic variants (18% vs 1.4% in KRAS-MUT), African ancestry, and mutations in GNAS, SMARCB1, and PIK3CA. MAPK-WT patients present at younger age (median 58 vs 67 years in KRAS-MUT). OS advantage in MAPK-WT persists after excluding targeted therapy recipients, indicating intrinsic biological differences beyond targetable alterations.

### F2: KRAS mutant allele dosage gains are prevalent and correlated with advanced disease

**Finding:** Among 1,157 evaluable KRAS-MUT tumors, 42% have allelic imbalance at the KRAS locus. One in five (20%) non-WGD diploid KRAS-MUT tumors harbor mutant allele dosage gains (≥2 mutant copies). Gains are markedly more common in metastatic disease (29%) than in resectable (8%) or locally advanced (14%) tumors (chi-squared trend p = 5.7×10^−11).

**Detail:** Allelic imbalance categories: focal/arm amplifications 4%, shallow gains 16%, copy-neutral LOH 5%, LOH 11%, losses post-WGD 5%. Whole genome doubling (WGD) occurs in 19% of KRAS-MUT tumors; WGD tumors have 75% allelic imbalance rate (vs. 30% non-WGD), and mutant allele gains are 2× more common in WGD tumors. Selection for the KRAS mutant allele is strong: 93% of all imbalance events favor mutant allele retention or gain. KRAS G12D, G12V, and G12R have similar WGD and dosage gain rates, suggesting dosage biology is allele-agnostic.

### F3: KRAS mutant dosage gains are an independent prognostic factor across all disease stages

**Finding:** In non-WGD diploid KRAS-MUT tumors, any KRAS mutant allele dosage gain confers significantly worse OS vs. single-copy mutant (HR adj = 1.7, 95% CI 1.4–2.0, p = 3.5×10^−7). This effect holds across all stages: resectable (median OS 23 vs. 32 months, HR adj = 2.16, p = 0.03) and metastatic (8.5 vs. 13 months, HR adj = 1.63, p = 4.9×10^−5).

**Detail:** Analysis restricted to non-WGD tumors (n=934) to avoid confounding by WGD, itself a strong negative prognostic factor. KRAS mutant dosage gain is the dominant molecular prognostic marker: of 13 genes evaluated in metastatic KRAS-MUT patients (n=304), only RNF43 loss (HR adj = 2.79, p adj = 0.047) and AKT2 amplification (HR adj = 2.03, p adj = 0.048) remained significant after multiple testing correction—neither eclipses the dosage signal. Among tumors with dosage gains, concomitant loss of the WT allele (CNLOH) is associated with even worse OS (HR adj = 1.64, p = 0.016 vs. dosage gain with WT retained). Loss of WT allele alone (single mutant copy) does not worsen OS.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `RNF43`, `AKT2` · PDAC

### F4: KRAS variant-specific differences in OS and co-mutation landscape

**Finding:** G12D (41%), G12V (32%), and G12R (16%) are the dominant KRAS hotspot mutations. KRAS G12R-mutant tumors have significantly better OS than G12D (HR adj = 0.78, 95% CI 0.67–0.92, p = 0.003) after accounting for dosage gains, stage, and other covariates.

**Detail:** G12R patients are less likely to have a smoking history (40% vs. 55% in G12D, p = 2×10^−4). SMAD4 alterations are significantly more common in G12R (30% vs. 21% in G12D, p = 0.001), while ARID1A alterations are more frequent in G12D (10% vs. 5% in G12R, p = 0.002). TP53 co-mutation rates and WGD rates are similar across variants. No significant difference in progression-free survival between FOLFIRINOX vs. gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel first-line treatment within any KRAS variant subgroup. All three major variants are now covered by investigational RAS-targeted therapies.

### F5: Germline pathogenic variants in 10% of patients; BRCA2 biallelic loss necessary but insufficient for PARPi response

**Finding:** 10% of patients harbor germline pathogenic variants in cancer predisposition genes: BRCA2 (3.7%), BRCA1 (1.8%), ATM (1.8%), PALB2 (0.5%), and Lynch syndrome genes (0.7%). Germline BRCA variants are more common in metastatic disease (12.3% vs. 9.6%, p = 0.01) and in Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry. Among 29 metastatic patients treated with PARPi, only 38% (11/29) received benefit (>6 months); all 10 BRCA2-mutant responders had biallelic inactivation, yet 6 non-responders also had biallelic BRCA2 loss—indicating biallelic loss is necessary but insufficient.

**Detail:** Strong LOH selection is observed for germline BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, and ATM carrier tumors. Germline ATM and somatic TP53 mutations are mutually exclusive (p = 4×10^−11); biallelic ATM loss is strongly depleted for TP53 co-mutation regardless of germline vs. somatic origin. Lynch syndrome detected in 0.7% of patients; 35% of these showed MSI from sequencing. MAPK-WT tumors are enriched for germline pathogenic variants (25% vs. 10% in KRAS-MUT), driven by ATM germline mutations co-occurring with somatic GNAS alterations (IPMN-derived PDAC).

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRCA2`, `BRCA1`, `ATM`, `PALB2`, `MLH1`, `MSH2`, `MSH6`, `PMS2`, `GNAS` · PDAC

## Open Questions

- Does KRAS mutant dosage gain predict resistance or reduced depth of response to KRAS G12D/V/R inhibitors in clinical trials?
- Can ctDNA-based allele-specific copy number assessment reliably quantify KRAS mutant dosage non-invasively for serial monitoring?
- What is the mechanism underlying the improved OS in MAPK-WT tumors beyond targetable alterations—intrinsic biology, immune microenvironment, or different primary driver pathways (non-MAPK)?
- Does germline ATM biallelic inactivation predict sensitivity to ATR inhibitors or DNA damage therapies in PDAC as it does in other cancers?
- Is there a dose-response relationship between KRAS mutant copy number (2 vs. ≥3 copies) on OS that larger cohorts could resolve?

## Limitations

- Single-institution (MSK) cohort; selection bias toward patients fit for standard-of-care clinical sequencing
- Allele-specific copy number inference requires sufficient tumor purity; analysis restricted to higher-quality samples
- WGD tumors excluded from dosage analysis to avoid confounding—limits generalizability to 80% of KRAS-MUT tumors
- Shallow gains (single-copy gains) currently not reported by any clinical sequencing platform, limiting immediate clinical translation
- PARPi response analysis limited to n=29 patients; underpowered to identify co-predictors of response beyond BRCA2 biallelic status
- RNA-seq not systematically available; gene expression subtypes (classical vs. basal-like) not integrated
