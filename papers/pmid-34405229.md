## Summary

This study compared clinical, histopathologic, somatic, and germline genomic features of 759 early-onset (EO-CRC, age <50 years) and 687 average-onset (AO-CRC, age ≥50 years) colorectal cancer patients at MSK. After excluding mismatch repair–deficient and hereditary syndrome cases, the authors found that EO-CRCs are more commonly left-sided and present more frequently with rectal bleeding and abdominal pain, but are otherwise clinically and genomically indistinguishable from AO-CRCs. Pathogenic germline variants were significantly more common in the youngest EO-CRC patients (≤35 years), suggesting genetic predisposition as a contributing factor.

## Key Findings

### F1: EO-CRC is predominantly left-sided

**Finding:** Early-onset CRC is significantly more likely to be left-sided compared to average-onset CRC.

**Detail:** Left-sided tumors were present in 80.8% of patients ≤35 years and 83.7% of 36–49-year-olds versus 63.9% in AO-CRC (P<.001 for both comparisons). Rectal bleeding was also more common in EO-CRC (41% vs 25.9%, P<.001) as was abdominal pain (37.1% vs 26.8%, P=.01).

### F2: No somatic genomic differences by age after adjusting for tumor sidedness

**Finding:** Apparent differences in somatic mutation frequency between EO-CRC and AO-CRC (TP53 and RTK-RAS) were explained by tumor sidedness, not age.

**Detail:** On univariate analysis, differences in *TP53* and RTK-RAS signaling pathway alterations were initially observed by age. However, in multivariate analysis including tumor sidedness, no statistically significant differences at the gene or pathway level were demonstrated (Benjamini-Hochberg q-value cutoff 0.1). This argues against a distinct molecular biology for EO-CRC in microsatellite-stable tumors.

### F3: Higher pathogenic germline variant rate in youngest EO-CRC patients

**Finding:** Pathogenic germline variants were significantly more common in patients diagnosed at ≤35 years versus average-onset CRC.

**Detail:** 23.3% of patients aged ≤35 years harbored a pathogenic germline variant versus 14.1% in AO-CRC (P=.01, one-sided Fisher exact test). The germline analysis included a broader cohort including patients with Lynch syndrome and hereditary predispositions. A 76–88-gene MSK-IMPACT germline panel was used.

**Genes / cancer types:** Germline cancer predisposition genes (including *BRCA1/2*, Lynch syndrome genes) · Colorectal cancer

### F4: Similar survival and chemotherapy response across age groups

**Finding:** Among advanced microsatellite-stable CRC patients, chemotherapy response rates and overall survival were equivalent regardless of age at diagnosis.

**Detail:** Kaplan-Meier analysis and Cox proportional hazards modeling showed no statistically significant differences in progression-free or overall survival between EO-CRC and AO-CRC cohorts in the metastatic setting. This argues against more aggressive treatment regimens based solely on young age.

### F5: No histopathologic differences between EO-CRC and AO-CRC

**Finding:** Tumor grade, stage at presentation, and histopathologic features were not significantly different between EO-CRC and AO-CRC after excluding predisposition syndromes.

**Detail:** Sex, race, tumor grade, and stage at presentation were not statistically different between the three age cohorts (≤35, 36–49, ≥50 years). Advanced (stage IV) disease was the majority in all groups, reflecting the MSK sequencing population. This suggests that high stage in EO-CRC reflects a selection bias from lack of screening rather than inherently more aggressive biology.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can cBioPortal be used to test whether left-sidedness fully explains genomic differences across age cohorts in larger, multi-institutional CRC cohorts (e.g., TCGA, MSK-MetTropism)?
- Are specific germline pathways (e.g., DNA repair, mismatch repair) differentially enriched in EO-CRC in the crc_eo_2020 study?
- Does EO-CRC MSI frequency differ from AO-CRC when controlling for germline Lynch syndrome carriers?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Single-center study (MSK) with enrichment for advanced/stage IV disease due to sequencing eligibility bias.
- Germline analysis included patients with hereditary predispositions, unlike the somatic analysis, making direct comparisons complex.
- Relatively small sample in the youngest (≤35 years, n=151) subgroup limits power for some analyses.
- Exclusion of MMR-deficient and IBD-related CRCs may limit generalizability.
