## Summary

TCGA multi-platform analysis of 496 papillary thyroid carcinomas (PTCs) — the largest cohort studied at the time — using WES (n=402 pairs), SNP CNA arrays (n=495), RNA-seq, miRNA, DNA methylation, and RPPA. Key findings: PTC has the lowest somatic mutation density of any TCGA cancer type (0.41 non-synonymous/Mb); the set of known drivers is extended to include EIF1AX, PPM1D, CHEK2, and diverse gene fusions (RET, BRAF, ALK, NTRK); 'dark matter' (unknown drivers) is reduced from 25% to 3.5%. BRAF V600E and RAS mutations are the two dominant and mutually exclusive drivers defining BVL-PTCs (less differentiated, classical/tall cell histology) and RL-PTCs (more differentiated, follicular variant). TERT promoter mutations associate with high recurrence risk and dedifferentiation. Molecular subtypes better predict risk than histological grade. Full data available in `thca_tcga_pub` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: BRAF V600E and RAS define two mutually exclusive driver axes with distinct differentiation and signaling outputs

**Finding:** 248 of 402 patients (61.7%) carry BRAF mutations (overwhelmingly V600E); 52 patients (12.9%) carry RAS mutations (NRAS, HRAS, KRAS codons 12/61). These two groups are virtually mutually exclusive (P=1.1×10⁻⁵), define BVL-PTCs (BRAF-like, less differentiated, classical/tall cell histology) vs. RL-PTCs (RAS-like, more differentiated, follicular variant histology), and have profoundly different signaling outputs measurable by the BRAF-RAS Score (BRS) and Thyroid Differentiation Score (TDS).

**Detail:** BRAF V600E signals as a RAF monomer insensitive to ERK feedback, causing constitutive high MAPK output, DUSP4/5/6 overexpression, and downregulation of thyroid iodine metabolism genes (TG, TPO, SLC5A5/SLC26A4). RAS tumors signal via RAF dimers that respond to ERK feedback, preserving thyroid differentiation gene expression and activating PI3K/AKT signaling (higher pAKT by RPPA) and BAD phosphorylation via p90RSK. BRAF V600E mutations are clonal in essentially all informative cases (cancer cell fraction ~1.0), contradicting prior reports of subclonal BRAF in PTC. TDS correlates with BRS (Spearman=0.78, P=3.1×10⁻⁸⁰) and predicts RAI avidity and therapeutic response.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRAF, NRAS, HRAS, KRAS · Papillary thyroid carcinoma

### F2: Gene fusions in 15% of PTCs extend driver catalog; RET, BRAF, ALK, NTRK3, ETV6 fusions identified

**Finding:** 74 of 484 (15.3%) PTCs carry gene fusions (RET 6.8%, BRAF 2.7%, ETV6/NTRK3 1.2%, PAX8/PPARG 0.8%, ALK 0.8%, THADA 1.2%). All fusions are mutually exclusive with BRAF/RAS/EIF1AX point mutations (P=4.9×10⁻⁴³), confirming they act as independent oncogenic drivers. Four novel RET fusion partners and 13 BRAF fusions with diverse partners are identified. Fusion-positive tumors are associated with younger age at diagnosis.

**Detail:** Most RET fusions preserve the kinase domain; novel partners extend the known catalogue. BRAF fusions include SND1/BRAF (also seen in gastric cancer cell lines) and MKRN1/BRAF (supporting kinase domain activation). Unlike BRAF V600E fusions, most non-V600E BRAF fusions (and the K601E mutation) show RAS-like (RL) signaling behavior. EML4/ALK fusion—the canonical lung adenocarcinoma driver—is found in PTC, suggesting eligibility for crizotinib. PAX8/PPARG fusions are mostly RL, consistent with their prevalence in follicular-patterned tumors. Fusion tumors were not enriched for viral pathogens (HBV identified in 2 cases, HPV45 in 1 case, unlikely to be pathogenic).

### F3: TERT promoter mutations associate with recurrence risk and dedifferentiation — independent of BRAF status

**Finding:** TERT promoter mutations (C228T: 7.0%, C250T: 2.1%, C228A: 0.3%) are present in 36 of 384 (9.4%) informative PTCs. They associate strongly with older age, high MACIS score (P=1.3×10⁻¹¹), high recurrence risk (P=7×10⁻⁸), and lower TDS (dedifferentiated tumors, P=4.2×10⁻⁵). These associations hold within BRAF V600E-mutant tumors, suggesting TERT mutations are an independent prognostic marker rather than simply co-occurring with BRAF.

**Detail:** TERT mutations are present across all histological PTC subtypes and show modest association with mutation drivers (P=0.029) and arm-level SCNAs (P=0.023) but not with BRAF mutation or gene fusions specifically. They are enriched in less-differentiated PTCs (low TDS) and high MACIS scores. These findings suggest that TERT promoter mutation testing may have clinical utility in PTC risk stratification, identifying patients at higher risk of recurrence who may benefit from more aggressive treatment.

### F4: Novel SMGs EIF1AX, PPM1D, CHEK2 and dark-matter resolution from 25% to 3.5%

**Finding:** Beyond MAPK pathway drivers, MutSig identifies EIF1AX (1.5%), PPM1D (1.2%), and CHEK2 as significantly mutated genes. EIF1AX mutations are nearly mutually exclusive with BRAF/RAS (P=0.013), suggesting an independent oncogenic mechanism in PTC. Including arm-level SCNAs as potential drivers, the fraction of PTCs without any identified oncogenic driver falls from 25% to 3.5% (14/402 cases).

**Detail:** EIF1AX encodes the eukaryotic translation initiation factor 1A; six mutations cluster in the gene in BRAF/RAS-negative tumors. PPM1D and CHEK2 encode interacting DNA repair proteins; their mutations co-occur with MAPK pathway drivers (not mutually exclusive) and may represent progression-related events. PPM1D germline mutations are associated with breast/ovarian predisposition and may impair p53 function. Tumors with DNA repair gene mutations have higher median mutation density (Mann-Whitney P=0.022) and are enriched for high-risk patients (P=0.018). Network-based stratification (NBS) defines 3 PTC subtypes (NBS1: RAS/PTEN/PAX8-PPARG; NBS2: RET/NTRK3; NBS3: BRAF V600E) with distinct clinical associations.

### F5: SCNA landscape is mostly quiet; 22q loss and 1q amplification define distinct PTC risk groups

**Finding:** Only 135 of 495 PTCs (27.2%) carry somatic copy number alterations, confirming PTC as a relatively genome-stable cancer. Four CNA classes are defined: SCNA-quiet (72.9%), SCNA-22q-del (9.9%, enriched in follicular variant, associated with NF2/CHEK2 loss), SCNA-low-1q-amp (14.8%, enriched in tall cell variant and BRAF V600E, highest MACIS scores and recurrence risk), and SCNA-high (2.4%). SCNAs are significantly enriched in cases without point mutation or fusion drivers (P=4.4×10⁻⁴).

**Detail:** 22q loss overlaps with CHEK2 mutations in 4 of 75 cases (P=0.0035), suggesting cooperative inactivation of this DNA repair locus. 1q amplification is strongly associated with TCV histology, high MACIS scores, and high risk of recurrence—consistent with reports of 1q gain as an aggressive PTC marker. Arm-level SCNAs are significantly more frequent in follicular-variant (FV) than classical-type (CT) PTCs (FDR<0.1), linking FV biology to follicular adenoma/carcinoma CNA patterns. GISTIC analysis identifies few focal recurrent events beyond PTEN deletion (5 cases) and BRAF focal deletions (5 BRAF fusion cases with detectable SNP-array lesions at 7q34).

## Open Questions

- Does TERT promoter mutation status predict RAI therapy failure and therefore identify PTC patients who should be offered BRAF/MEK inhibitors or other systemic therapies?
- Can the BRAF-RAS score (BRS) or thyroid differentiation score (TDS) be implemented as a clinical assay from RNA profiling of fine-needle aspirates before surgery?
- Do BRAF fusions (which behave as RL-PTCs despite being BRAF-derived) predict differential response to BRAF V600E inhibitors (vemurafenib, dabrafenib), and should patients be excluded from BRAF inhibitor trials?
- Is EIF1AX an actionable target, and what is the oncogenic mechanism — does it alter translation efficiency for specific oncogenic mRNAs?
- Why does the SCNA-1q-amplified class strongly associate with tall cell variant and worse prognosis — what is the functional driver gene on 1q?

## Limitations

- Aggressive PTC subtypes (poorly differentiated, undifferentiated/anaplastic carcinomas) were excluded by design to focus on PTC-initiating alterations; findings may not apply to dedifferentiated thyroid cancers
- TERT promoter analysis was limited to 384 informative tumors, and the promoter region is not captured by standard exome baits — underdetection is likely
- Low background mutation rate provides statistical power to detect drivers in ≥3% of cases, but ultra-rare drivers in <1% remain below detection
- RPPA and methylation analyses performed on subsets of the cohort; some cross-platform integrations are based on reduced sample sizes
- Gene fusion detection relies on RNA-seq; fusions not expressed or expressed below sequencing depth may be missed
- Clinical follow-up duration varies widely; some recurrence risk associations are based on pathological surrogates (MACIS, ATA risk scores) rather than observed outcomes
