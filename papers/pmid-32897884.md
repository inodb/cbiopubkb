## Summary

Study of 15 patients with primary mediastinal nonseminomatous germ cell tumors (PM NSGCTs) and co-incident hematologic malignancies, using MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing and WES of paired GCT, blood cancer, and matched normal DNA from fingernails. Phylogenetic reconstruction of all cases demonstrates GCTs and hematologic malignancies evolved from a common shared precursor — not by direct transformation of GCT elements. The shared ancestral clone almost universally harbors TP53 (91%) and/or RAS pathway mutations (63% KRAS, 37% NRAS). Hematologic malignancies in this setting are genetically more similar to PM NSGCTs than to de novo AML/MDS, lacking hallmark myeloid drivers (FLT3, DNMT3A, TET2, NPM1). Isochromosome 12p [i(12p)] is shared between GCTs and blood cancers but absent in de novo AML. Median survival is 6.3 months. Data available in `gct_msk_2020` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: GCTs and associated hematologic malignancies evolve from a common shared precursor, not by GCT transformation

**Finding:** In every patient with WES data (n=5), phylogenetic reconstruction reveals divergent clonal evolution from a shared ancestral clone, with private driver mutations acquired independently in the GCT and hematologic malignancy. This definitively rules out direct malignant transformation of teratoma or yolk sac elements into the hematologic cancer, which had been the prevailing hypothesis.

**Detail:** In one representative case, a 24-year-old had both PM NSGCT and CMML with shared i(12p) and KRAS G12S mutations plus 8 additional shared truncal mutations of unknown significance, followed by independent branching with distinct oncogenic mutations. In another case, a TP53 R248L mutation with CNLOH defined the shared precursor, with each malignancy independently evolving distinct alterations. A 19-year-old developed 4 distinct cancers (GCT, histiocytic sarcoma, CMML, AML) all traceable to a TP53 Q144Rfs*26 + PIK3CD + shared CNA precursor with two independent daughter precursors. For the allogeneic SCT case, the histiocytic sarcoma arising post-transplant was host-derived and shared the precursor clone (not donor-derived), confirming origin from the original precursor rather than the hematopoietic system.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, KRAS, NRAS, PIK3CD, BCOR, RRAS2 · Primary mediastinal nonseminomatous GCT; AML; MDS; CMML; histiocytic sarcoma

### F2: TP53 mutations present in 91% of GCT + HM patients vs. 61% of GCT-only vs. 9% of de novo AML

**Finding:** TP53 mutations are near-obligatory in PM NSGCTs that develop associated hematologic malignancies (91%), significantly more frequent than in PM NSGCTs without hematologic malignancy (61%) and far more common than in de novo AML (9%). The near-universal TP53 mutation provides a biological basis for both cisplatin resistance (in GCTs) and adverse outcomes (in myeloid neoplasms), explaining the dismal 6.3-month median survival.

**Detail:** TP53 mutations in the shared precursor frequently undergo copy-neutral loss of heterozygosity (CNLOH), creating homozygous p53 loss. In multiple cases, GCT and the paired hematologic malignancy each independently acquired distinct TP53 hotspot mutations with independent CNLOH events — evidence of parallel convergent evolution at the TP53 locus. This pattern is mechanistically explained by cisplatin resistance mediated by TP53 loss and the known poor prognostic impact of TP53 alterations in myeloid neoplasms.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, KRAS, NRAS · Primary mediastinal GCT; AML; MDS

### F3: Hematologic malignancies in GCT patients are genetically more similar to PM NSGCTs than to de novo myeloid neoplasms

**Finding:** Blood cancers arising in PM NSGCT patients share i(12p), TP53 mutations, and RAS pathway mutations with the paired GCT, while lacking hallmark myeloid driver mutations (FLT3, DNMT3A, TET2, NPM1). This makes them genetically distinct from de novo AML/MDS and may explain their refractoriness to standard myeloid-directed therapies.

**Detail:** i(12p), a chromosomal marker near-universal in GCTs, is present in hematologic malignancies from GCT patients but never in de novo AML (n=200). AML subtypes in GCT patients skew toward acute megakaryoblastic leukemia (M7 AML, 47% of AML cases in this cohort), which is itself unusual. Multiple distinct hematologic malignancy subtypes can develop in the same patient — including histiocytic sarcoma, CMML, and AML — each with distinct private mutations but all traceable to the shared GCT-lineage precursor. Two patients had clinical diagnoses of Klinefelter syndrome, consistent with known increased PM NSGCT incidence in Klinefelter patients.

**Genes / cancer types:** i(12p), TP53, KRAS, NRAS, FLT3 (absent), DNMT3A (absent), TET2 (absent), NPM1 (absent) · PM NSGCT; AML (M7); CMML; MDS; histiocytic sarcoma

### F4: Autologous SCT products from PM NSGCT patients do not contain clonal hematopoiesis

**Finding:** Genetic analysis of G-CSF–mobilized hematopoietic stem cell products from 5 PM NSGCT patients (including 1 who subsequently developed hematologic malignancy) found no evidence of clonal hematopoiesis (CH) in any of the 5 products. The hematologic malignancy in the index patient developed post-SCT but contained mutations shared with the GCT precursor clone and absent from the SCT product, confirming origin from the GCT-lineage precursor rather than from the hematopoietic system.

**Detail:** The SCT product from the index patient (who developed MDS 1 year after GCT diagnosis, then histiocytic sarcoma 1 year after allogeneic SCT) contained no detectable mutations. The post-allogeneic SCT histiocytic sarcoma was confirmed as host-derived (not donor-derived) by shared VAF patterns. All three tumors (GCT, MDS, histiocytic sarcoma) shared a BCOR G943D mutation and RRAS2 activating mutation with CNLOH, with TP53 mutations independently acquired with CNLOH in each. This argues that hematologic malignancies in GCT patients do not arise from the hematopoietic system but from the GCT cell of origin (hypothesized to be embryonal carcinoma cells with totipotent differentiation capacity).

**Genes / cancer types:** BCOR, RRAS2, TP53, NRAS · PM NSGCT; MDS; histiocytic sarcoma

### F5: The syndrome of PM NSGCT + hematologic malignancy is a distinct clinical entity with dismal prognosis (median OS 6.3 months)

**Finding:** All 15 patients in the cohort were male (ages 18–33, median 25) with PM NSGCTs; no testicular or seminomatous GCTs developed clonally related hematologic malignancies. Only one patient was alive at time of publication. Median survival was 6.3 months (95% CI 4.6–25.2 months). 1 in every 17 PM NSGCT patients develops a hematologic malignancy in this setting.

**Detail:** The most common hematologic malignancy was AML (53%), followed by MDS, histiocytic sarcoma, and CMML. Four patients developed concurrent GCT and hematologic malignancy before any anticancer therapy, excluding therapy-induced secondary malignancy as the explanation. Median time between GCT and blood cancer diagnoses was only 4.8 months. The poor prognosis likely reflects both TP53-mediated cisplatin resistance in the GCT and the refractoriness of GCT-lineage hematologic malignancies to standard myeloid-directed therapy. Future studies of germline predisposition (including Klinefelter syndrome mosaicism) are warranted.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, KRAS, NRAS, i(12p) · PM NSGCT; AML; MDS; CMML; histiocytic sarcoma

## Open Questions

- What is the cell of origin of this syndrome — are embryonal carcinoma cells truly capable of differentiating into hematopoietic precursors, and can this be demonstrated experimentally?
- Can prospective genomic surveillance of PM NSGCT patients (e.g., at diagnosis and at stem cell collection) identify the precursor clone before hematologic malignancy develops?
- Do germline variants (including Klinefelter syndrome mosaicism, other chromosomal abnormalities) predispose to the development of this dual malignancy syndrome?
- Could treatment strategies targeting the shared TP53/RAS precursor (rather than standard AML or GCT protocols) improve outcomes for patients who develop hematologic malignancy?
- Why are only PM NSGCTs associated with this syndrome, and not testicular GCTs or seminomas?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=15 patients with dual malignancy) collected over 21 years, reflecting rarity of the syndrome
- WES performed in only 5 patients; IMPACT-only data in remaining patients limits resolution of phylogenetic trees
- Normal DNA from fingernails, which may contain somatic mutations from normal aging, introducing potential false negatives in somatic mutation calling
- Post-allogeneic SCT histiocytic sarcoma analysis required exclusion from CNA analysis due to donor contamination; VAF-based exclusion is an imperfect correction
- Comparison with de novo AML uses TCGA data (matched by age range) rather than a contemporaneous matched control cohort
- No functional validation of the embryonal carcinoma cell-of-origin hypothesis in model systems
