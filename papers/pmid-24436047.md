## Summary

This NCI/Children's Oncology Group/Broad Institute collaboration presents the most comprehensive genomic characterization of rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS) at its time — 147 tumor-normal pairs analyzed by whole-genome sequencing (WGS, n=44 discovery), whole-exome sequencing (WES, n=103 validation), high-resolution SNP arrays (all 147), and whole-transcriptome sequencing (n=80). The study establishes that PAX gene fusion status (PAX3/7-FOXO1 present vs. absent) — rather than histological subtype (alveolar vs. embryonal) — defines two molecularly distinct RMS genotypes. Fusion-negative (PFN) tumors carry a higher somatic mutation burden with frequent RAS/PI3K/RTK pathway mutations, while fusion-positive (PFP) tumors have lower mutation burden. Crucially, the RTK/RAS/PIK3CA axis is disrupted in 93% of all RMS cases, providing a rational framework for genomics-directed therapy. Novel recurrent mutations in FBXW7 and BCOR are identified. A shared genetic target space between PFP and PFN tumors (genes regulated by PAX3-FOXO1) is revealed, explaining the molecular convergence of the two genotypes.

## Key Findings

### F1: PAX gene fusion status — not histological subtype — defines two molecularly distinct RMS genotypes

**Finding:** The presence or absence of a *PAX3/7-FOXO1* gene fusion defines two biologically distinct RMS genotypes (PFP vs. PFN) more accurately than the traditional alveolar/embryonal histological classification. PFN tumors have ~2.8× higher somatic mutation burden (17.8 vs. 6.4 non-synonymous mutations per tumor) and significantly more aneuploidy. Transcriptomic clustering separates PFP from PFN tumors more cleanly than histology. Seven histologically ARMS tumors with "fusion-negative" status cluster with ERMS by expression.

**Detail:** This reclassification has major clinical ramifications: fusion-negative ARMS patients have outcomes more like ERMS patients, and the presence/absence of PAX fusion is a stronger prognostic indicator than ARMS/ERMS histology. One PFP tumor (from a 3-month-old) had zero somatic protein-coding alterations other than PAX3-FOXO1 fusion and 11p LOH, illustrating the minimal mutational requirements for fusion-driven oncogenesis. Novel PAX3-INO80D fusion identified in one case with massive chromosome 2 rearrangement.

### F2: RTK/RAS/PIK3CA axis disrupted in 93% of fusion-negative RMS — dominant actionable framework

**Finding:** Mutations affecting the receptor tyrosine kinase/RAS/PIK3CA pathway are present in 93% of PAX-fusion-negative (PFN) RMS cases and are the most prevalent alterations overall. FGFR signaling is the most significantly enriched pathway by Reactome analysis (P=4.6×10⁻⁵) in the WGS discovery set.

**Detail:** In PFN tumors: NRAS mutation (11.7%, codons 12/13/61), FGFR4 mutation (9.6%), PIK3CA mutation (7.4%, H1047R or Q546), NF1 mutation (5.3%), KRAS (6.4%), HRAS (4.3%), BRAF V600E (1 case), PDGFRA (1.4%), ERBB2 (1.4%), PTEN homozygous deletion (1 case). PI3K pathway: PIK3CD mutation (1 case). No RAS mutations were found in any PFP tumor. FGFR4 mutations occur exclusively in PFN. FGFR4 is a known downstream target of PAX3-FOXO1, providing the molecular link between PFP and PFN genotypes through a common pathway. Two cases had concurrent PIK3CA + RAS mutations.

### F3: Novel recurrent mutations in FBXW7 and BCOR identified as new potential therapeutic targets

**Finding:** Two genes not previously implicated in RMS are recurrently mutated: *FBXW7* (E3 ubiquitin ligase, 7.4% of PFN tumors, all at conserved arginine residues R387P, R441G, R367P in WD40 substrate-recognition domain) and *BCOR* (Xp11.4 transcriptional repressor, 7% overall: 9 in PFN by mutation/focal deletion, 1 in PFP).

**Detail:** FBXW7 mutations cluster at known loss-of-function hotspots (same arginine residues mutated in T-ALL, colorectal, and other cancers), suggesting this is a genuine tumor suppressor in RMS. FBXW7 loss stabilizes oncoproteins including c-Myc, cyclin E, and Notch-ICN. BCOR mutations/deletions are predominantly in PFN tumors; BCOR alterations have since been found to define a distinct sarcoma subtype (BCOR-CCNB3 sarcoma). These findings open new avenues for therapeutic intervention and highlight RMS as part of a broader FBXW7/BCOR-driven tumor biology.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FBXW7`, `BCOR` · Rhabdomyosarcoma (predominantly fusion-negative)

### F4: PFP-specific copy-number amplifications — MYCN (2p24), CDK4/12q13, MDM2/12q15, PAX7-FOXO1 self-amplification

**Finding:** PAX-fusion-positive tumors show distinct and recurrent copy-number amplification patterns: *MYCN* amplification at 2p24 (predominantly PFP, 8/9 cases), CDK4/12q13-q14 amplification (predominantly PFP, 12/13 cases; worse overall survival independent of fusion status), MDM2/12q15 amplification (predominantly PFN, 9/10 cases), and PAX7-FOXO1 fusion gene self-amplification (12/15 PAX7-FOXO1 tumors).

**Detail:** LOH at 11p15.5 including the IGF2 locus occurs in 50% of all RMS tumors (16 PFP, 59 PFN), and focal IGF1R amplification in 2.7% of cases, together highlighting insulin/IGF signaling as broadly relevant. 12q13-q14 amplification (CDK4-containing) is independently associated with worse overall survival. MDM2 amplification in PFN parallels the TP53/MDM2 axis. CDKN2A homozygous deletion (3%) and 9p21.3 LOH (9%) also observed. Chromosome 8 gain in 46% of PFN (recurrent aneuploidy consistent with prior reports). MIR17HG amplification at 13q31-32 exclusively in PFP (4.5%).

### F5: PFP and PFN tumors share a common genetic axis via PAX3-FOXO1 target gene disruption

**Finding:** Genes mutated in PFN RMS tumors are significantly enriched in genes regulated by PAX3-FOXO1 in experimental cell line and mouse models, demonstrating that fusion-negative and fusion-positive RMS alter a shared set of downstream targets (including FGFR4, CCND2, IGF2, MYOD1, MET, CNR1) through distinct upstream mechanisms — either through the fusion protein directly (PFP) or through somatic mutations of its targets (PFN).

**Detail:** A human fibroblast PAX3-FOXO1 stable expression model (7250_PF) identified 444 genes with >4-fold expression change, enriched for PFN mutation targets by GSEA (P=3×10⁻³). This enrichment was confirmed in two independent PAX3-FOXO1 transgenic mouse models (forelimb P=0.001, somite P=0.01). Top shared targets: FGFR4 (leading edge in all 3 GSEA analyses), CCND2, IGF2. This convergence model explains why histologically distinct RMS subtypes converge on similar biology and provides a rationale for targeting the shared downstream pathway (FGFR4, IGF signaling) regardless of fusion status.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR4`, `CCND2`, `IGF2`, `MYOD1`, `MET`, `CNR1` · Rhabdomyosarcoma (both fusion-positive and fusion-negative)

## Open Questions

- Can FGFR4-targeted therapies (FGFR inhibitors) benefit the 9.6% of PFN RMS cases with FGFR4 mutations, and are there also relevant FGFR4 expression-high cases without mutation?
- Does FBXW7 loss in RMS create sensitivity to CDK inhibitors or Notch pathway inhibitors (similar to other FBXW7-mutant cancers)?
- What is the clinical significance of 7 "fusion-negative ARMS" cases that molecularly resemble ERMS — should they be treated on ERMS protocols?
- Does PAX7-FOXO1 self-amplification confer a different prognosis or therapeutic sensitivity than non-amplified PAX7-FOXO1?
- Can IGF1R or IGF2 pathway inhibition be effective in the 50% of RMS cases with 11p15.5 LOH, and what is the biomarker strategy?
- Are the novel PAX3-NCOA1 and PAX3-INO80D fusions as oncogenic as PAX3-FOXO1, and do they respond similarly to treatments targeting PAX3-FOXO1-driven tumors?

## Limitations

- The WGS discovery set is relatively small (n=44); rare mutations may be missed or overestimated in frequency
- Pediatric tumor-normal sequencing: germline burden of variants not fully characterized; one TP53 germline case identified serendipitously (RMS212)
- No integrative methylation or proteomics data; epigenetic regulation of RMS biology (particularly PAX3-FOXO1 chromatin remodeling) not captured
- Long-term clinical outcome data not systematically analyzed against genomic features
- FGFR signaling enrichment in PFN WGS cohort (25 cases) may not be stable; pathway analysis needs larger validation
- Study does not characterize spindle/sclerosing RMS (now recognized as molecularly distinct MYOD1-mutant subtype)
- 2014 study predates recognition of VGLL2/NCOA2 fusions and MYOD1 L122R as distinct infant RMS subtypes
