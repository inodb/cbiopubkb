## Summary

Whole-exome (n=55) and whole-genome (n=5) sequencing of 60 adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC) tumor/normal pairs from salivary glands establishes the first large-scale mutational landscape for this aggressive and chemoresistant malignancy. The study reveals a very low somatic mutation rate (0.31 non-silent events/Mb), comparable to hematologic malignancies, with wide inter-tumor heterogeneity. Five major biological pathways emerge as recurrently disrupted: MYB/MYC pathway (MYB-NFIB translocation in 57%; additional MYB pathway mutations in 8%), chromatin remodeling (SMARCA2, CREBBP, KDM6A; 35% of tumors), DNA damage/checkpoint (TP53, UHRF1; 8ŌĆō13%), PKA signaling (RYR3/RYR2; 27%), and FGF/IGF/PI3K signaling (PIK3CA, PTEN, FGFR4; 30%). Functional validation confirms KDM6A mutations abrogate H3K27me3 demethylase activity, PIK3CA/PTEN mutations activate downstream AKT signaling, and FAT4 knockdown increases growth in salivary cells, underscoring the biological relevance of these events. The data provide the first molecular framework for understanding ACC oncogenesis and identify potentially targetable pathways in this otherwise untreatable disease.

## Key Findings

### F1: MYB-NFIB fusion and additional MYB pathway mutations are the cardinal genetic event in ACC

**Finding:** The MYB-NFIB t(6;9) translocation is confirmed by FISH in 57% (34/60) of ACC cases. An additional 8% harbor coding or splice-site mutations in MYB exon 10 (disrupting the leucine-rich negative regulatory domain, likely conferring constitutive activation) or in MYB pathway-associated genes (*MYCBP2*, *MGA*, *MCM4*). Truncating mutations and homozygous deletions in *NFIB* are also observed, suggesting an independent oncogenic role for NFIB beyond the fusion. Together, MYB pathway alterations affect at least 65% of ACC cases, making this the dominant oncogenic axis.

**Detail:** MYB exon-10 splice-site mutations are predicted to remove the negative regulatory domain and trigger constitutive MYB transcriptional activation. Five NFIB homozygous deletions (all MYB-NFIB positive) and two NFIB truncating mutations (Y249*, P390fs in CTF/NFI modulation domains) were identified. MYB-NFIB was the only recurrent structural variant from WGS analysis across 5 samples; 17 total SVs were found. The study also found a tandem duplication within FGFR2 in one sample, paralleling activating rearrangements described in hematologic malignancies.

### F2: Chromatin remodeling gene mutations are highly enriched in ACC (35% of cases)

**Finding:** ACC is characterized by a striking enrichment of somatic mutations in chromatin state regulators, present in 35% of tumors (q = 4.5 ├Ś 10Ōü╗┬│). The most recurrently mutated chromatin genes are *CREBBP* (7%), *KDM6A/UTX* (7%), and *SMARCA2* (5%), with additional single mutations in *SMARCE1*, *ARID1A*, *ATRX*, *EP300*, *MLL3*, and histones (*HIST1H2AL*, *HIST1H1E*). Functional experiments confirm that KDM6A missense mutations abrogate H3K27me3 demethylase activity (dominant-negative in some cases) and promote growth in overexpression assays, providing direct evidence of oncogenic activity.

**Detail:** SMARCA2 mutations cluster within the Helicase C domain (T1126I, G1132V, G1164W). CREBBP mutations cluster in the KAT11 histone acetyltransferase domain (R1446C, I1453N, W1472S). KDM6A mutations span multiple residues; cells overexpressing mutant but not wild-type KDM6A show loss of H3K27me3 demethylation by immunofluorescence, and KDM6A mutants augment rather than suppress growth. This convergence on chromatin dysregulation parallels findings in bladder cancer (ARID1A), B-cell lymphoma (CREBBP), and leukemia (KDM6A) but represents a novel finding in ACC.

### F3: FGF/IGF/PI3K pathway mutations in 30% of ACC associate with solid histology and activate AKT signaling

**Finding:** Mutations in genes of the FGF/IGF/PI3K signaling axis are identified in 30% of ACC cases (q = 0.024), all occurring in tumors of the aggressive solid histological subtype (Fisher's exact p < 1.6 ├Ś 10Ōü╗┬│). These include hotspot mutations in *PIK3CA* (3 cases, all at COSMIC v61 hotspots) and dual catalytic domain mutations in *PTEN* (R130fs, K144Q), as well as alterations in *FOXO3* (7%), *FGF16*, *FGFR4*, *IGFBP2*, and *IL17RD*. IHC with p-AKT and p-PRAS40 shows functional PI3K pathway activation exclusively in PIK3CA/PTEN-mutant tumors, validated by GSEA enrichment of PI3K gene sets.

**Detail:** The PI3K-mutant ACC subgroup represents a potentially druggable subset. PIK3CA hotspot mutations (E542K, E545K, H1047R) are the same activating alleles found in breast, endometrial, and colorectal cancers. PTEN dual-mutation (R130fs + K144Q) in a single tumor suggests biallelic inactivation. IL17RD encodes an FGFR inhibitor, so its mutation may relieve FGFR-mediated PI3K inhibition. Downstream PI3K target activation confirmed by pAKT/pPRAS40 IHC provides orthogonal functional validation not commonly available in landscape studies.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PIK3CA`, `PTEN`, `FOXO3`, `FGFR4`, `FGF16` ┬Ę Adenoid cystic carcinoma (solid subtype)

### F4: DNA damage checkpoint mutations (TP53, UHRF1) and PKA pathway alterations define a second tier of ACC drivers

**Finding:** Genes involved in the DNA damage response are significantly enriched for mutations in ACC (q = 5.6 ├Ś 10Ōü╗┬│). *TP53* is mutated in 5% (missense P151S; nonsense R213*, R342* in the DNA-binding and tetramerization domains). *UHRF1* (ubiquitin ligase, p53-dependent checkpoint) is altered in 8% (2 missense mutations + 3 homozygous deletions). The PKA pathway is mutated in 27% (q = 4.2 ├Ś 10Ōü╗┬│), predominantly through recurrent *RYR3* mutations (7%) and *RYR2* (2%), alongside tyrosine phosphatase loss-of-function (*PTPRG*, *PTPRH*, *PTPRK*). TP53 pathway alterations correlate with downregulation of TP53 transcriptional targets by PCR array.

**Detail:** TP53 mutations in ACC are at canonical hotspot positions but at relatively low frequency (5%), unlike in HNSCC where TP53 is mutated in ~70% of HPV-negative cases. UHRF1 homozygous deletions suggest potential epigenetic co-regulation since UHRF1 maintains DNA methylation patterns. RYR3/RYR2 encode ryanodine receptor calcium channels; calcium influx via RYRs facilitates CREBBP recruitment through PKA and CREB phosphorylation, providing a mechanistic link between PKA pathway disruption and impaired CREBBP function (separately mutated in 7% of ACCs). Tyrosine phosphatase mutations (PTPRG nonsense E736*; PTPRH nonsense W602*; PTPRK frameshift L457fs + 4 homozygous deletions) suggest cooperative pathway disruption.

### F5: Recurrent chromosomal losses at 6q24, 12q13, and 14q define genomic subgroups with clinical associations

**Finding:** GISTIC2.0 analysis of copy-number data from 60 ACC cases identifies three recurrent high-level deletions: 6q24, 12q13, and 14q. Loss of 14q correlates significantly with solid histology (Fisher's exact p = 2.0 ├Ś 10Ōü╗Ōü┤), while loss of 6q24 is enriched in advanced-stage tumors (p = 0.04). Copy-number clustering reveals four sub-clusters: a 14q-loss cluster, a 12q13-loss cluster, a complex cluster (high overall CNA burden), and a large cluster with minimal CNAs. The ACC genome is globally "quiet" with few recurrent focal amplifications or deletions outside these three regions.

**Detail:** The 6q24 region contains tumor suppressor candidates relevant to other cancers (TXNIP, a known cancer-suppressed protein with a frameshift mutation also identified in 1 ACC case). The 12q13 and 14q deletion regions harbor multiple candidate genes. The overall paucity of focal CNAs distinguishes ACC from most adult solid tumors and suggests that point mutations and structural rearrangements (primarily MYB-NFIB) are the dominant somatic mechanisms. Expression arrays on 23 ACC tumors showed no distinct expression subgroups, suggesting CNA-driven or mutation-driven heterogeneity does not map cleanly to expression subtypes. One sample harboring a MYB-NFIB translocation also showed an IDH1 R132H hotspot mutation.

**Genes / cancer types:** `IDH1`, `TXNIP`, `FAT4`, `FAT3` (regional genes at deletion loci) ┬Ę Adenoid cystic carcinoma

## Open Questions

- Can MYB-NFIB fusion status or MYB exon-10 mutation serve as a stratifying biomarker for clinical trials in ACC?
- Do PI3K pathway-mutant ACC tumors (30% solid histology) respond to PI3K/AKT/mTOR inhibitors, and are there validated cell lines or PDX models to test this?
- What is the functional consequence of UHRF1 homozygous deletion in ACC ŌĆö does it affect DNA methylation reprogramming or p53 pathway signaling?
- Could HDAC/BET inhibitors targeting chromatin dysregulation (CREBBP, KDM6A, SMARCA2 mutations) show activity in ACC given HDAC inhibitor activity in CTCL?
- Does IDH1 R132H in ACC (1 case) produce 2-hydroxyglutarate and alter the epigenetic landscape cooperatively with chromatin remodeling gene mutations?
- What drives the 35% of ACC cases with no CHASM-designated driver mutations ŌĆö noncoding regulatory mutations, non-MYB translocations, or epigenetic alterations?

## Limitations

- Small WGS cohort (n=5) limits structural variant discovery; additional translocations beyond MYB-NFIB cannot be excluded
- No RNA-seq; fusion transcript detection relies on FISH and RT-PCR, which may miss alternative transcript configurations
- Exome sequencing misses noncoding driver mutations (e.g., TERT promoter, enhancer hijacking at MYB locus)
- Available ACC cell lines were largely misidentified/cross-contaminated at time of publication, limiting functional validation in authentic models
- Solid histology enrichment of PI3K mutations may reflect both biology and referral bias (more aggressive cases biopsied more often)
- No clinical outcome data (recurrence, metastasis, survival) to correlate genomic features with prognosis
- CHASM driver classifier trained on other cancer types may underperform for ACC-specific biology
