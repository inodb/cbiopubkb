## Summary

This retrospective cohort study of 273 appendiceal adenocarcinoma (AC) patients profiled by MSK-IMPACT defines three molecular subtypes of mucinous appendiceal adenocarcinoma (MAAP) based on co-occurring somatic mutations in *KRAS/NRAS* (RAS), *GNAS*, and *TP53*. The RAS-mut predominant subtype (RAS-mut/GNAS-wt/TP53-wt) is genomically simple and clinically indolent — no cancer-related deaths were observed in metastatic patients. The GNAS-mut predominant subtype is chemotherapy-resistant with high peritoneal tumor burden. The TP53-mut predominant subtype is highly aneuploid and aggressive. Molecular subtype outperforms histopathology in predicting 3-year overall survival (AUC 0.75 vs. 0.67). Data are publicly available in cBioPortal as `appendiceal_msk_2022`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Three molecular subtypes of MAAP defined by RAS, GNAS, and TP53 co-mutation patterns

**Finding:** GNAS and RAS mutations significantly co-occur (BH-corrected P=.006) and are nearly mutually exclusive with TP53 mutations (P<.001), defining four distinct MAAP subtypes: RAS-mut predominant (15%), GNAS-mut predominant (46%), TP53-mut predominant (28%), and triple-negative (11%).

**Detail:** FACETS clonality analysis shows RAS mutations typically occur first, followed by either GNAS or TP53 acquisition in a near-mutually exclusive pattern. This branching oncogenesis implies distinct tumor evolution trajectories. KRAS is mutated in 79% of MAAP and GNAS in 52%; both are far more frequent than in colorectal cancer. CTAAP tumors have higher TP53 rates (54%) and higher TMB (4.4 mut/Mb). All tumors were MMR-proficient.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `NRAS`, `GNAS`, `TP53` · Mucinous appendiceal adenocarcinoma (MAAP)

### F2: RAS-mut predominant MAAP confers markedly improved overall survival — no cancer deaths observed

**Finding:** No cancer-related mortality was observed in metastatic patients with RAS-mut predominant MAAP after a median follow-up of 54 months (median OS not reached). This subtype was independently associated with reduced mortality vs. TP53-mut predominant (P=.004) and triple-negative (P=.01) subtypes in multivariable Cox models.

**Detail:** The molecular subtype model (AUC 0.75 for 3-year OS) outperforms histopathology-only models (AUC 0.67) in metastatic MAAP. RAS-mut predominant tumors have significantly fewer nonsynonymous mutations and lower aneuploidy than other subtypes. These tumors behave clinically like low-grade appendiceal mucinous neoplasms (LAMNs) — indolent, with rare metastatic progression. Patients with this subtype may be candidates for surgery alone without systemic chemotherapy.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `NRAS` · Mucinous appendiceal adenocarcinoma (MAAP), metastatic

### F3: TP53-mut predominant MAAP is highly aneuploid and independently predicts poor prognosis

**Finding:** TP53-mut predominant MAAP is highly aneuploid; high tumor aneuploidy independently associates with death (HR 3.7, 95% CI 1.9–6.9) irrespective of tumor grade. TP53-mut tumors account for 63% of tumors with the highest (≥75th percentile) aneuploidy scores despite comprising only 31% of the cohort.

**Detail:** Aneuploidy score (sum of chromosome segment length × copy-number deviation from diploid) correlates strongly with fraction of genome altered (Pearson r=0.91). The TP53-mut predominant subtype exhibits destructive stromal invasion in 91% of metastatic specimens (vs. 25% in RAS-mut), explaining its aggressive peritoneal infiltration behavior. Tumor aneuploidy was prognostically significant across all AC histologic subtypes.

### F4: GNAS-mut predominant MAAP is chemotherapy-resistant with high peritoneal burden

**Finding:** Only 5–6% of GNAS-mut predominant patients responded radiographically to first-line chemotherapy, compared with 50% of RAS-mut predominant patients (adjusted OR 0.03, P=.03). Peritoneal cancer index (PCI) was significantly higher in GNAS-mut (median 21) vs. RAS-mut tumors (median 13, P=.04).

**Detail:** GNAS mediates cAMP/PKA signaling and activates mucin production, potentially creating a microenvironment that supports metastasis and drug resistance (analogous to pancreatic cancer). Biochemical marker response (CEA/CA19-9 ≥10% decrease) during chemotherapy was also significantly lower in GNAS-mut predominant patients (1/7, 14%) vs. others (6/7, 86%; OR 0.04, P=.04).

**Genes / cancer types:** `GNAS` · Mucinous appendiceal adenocarcinoma, metastatic

### F5: Actionable alterations present in ~25% of AC; KRAS G12C in 6% of MAAP

**Finding:** OncoKB level-1 or level-3B actionable alterations were identified in ~25% of AC patients. KRAS G12C was present in 6% of MAAP (n=10/164) and represents a potential therapeutic target; one MAAP tumor harbored an NTRK1 fusion (level-1 biomarker).

**Detail:** BRAF V600E was rare (1%, n=3/273). This frequency of actionable alterations supports routine MSK-IMPACT profiling for all AC patients, as therapy options (KRAS G12C inhibitors, TRK inhibitors) exist for a meaningful minority. The absence of MMR deficiency and low overall TMB across subtypes limit immunotherapy applicability for most AC patients.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `NTRK1`, `BRAF` · All appendiceal adenocarcinoma subtypes

## Open Questions

- Can the molecular subtype classification be applied to ctDNA or pre-operative biopsies to guide cytoreductive surgery decisions?
- What explains the near-complete mutual exclusivity between GNAS and TP53 mutations — are there synthetic lethal interactions?
- Do KRAS G12C inhibitors have activity in MAAP, and does molecular subtype modulate response?
- Are triple-negative tumors driven by copy-number alterations rather than point mutations — what are their defining genomic features?
- Can GNAS mutation status guide selection of patients who should avoid aggressive cytoreductive surgery given high PCI and chemoresistance?

## Limitations

- Rare cancer type limits statistical power; no independent external validation cohort
- Low-frequency alterations may fall below detection threshold in low-purity tumors
- Triple-negative subgroup (18/164 MAAP) is poorly characterized — likely driven by CNAs not fully captured
- All patients from a single institution (MSK); referral bias toward complex/metastatic disease
- Chemotherapy response data available only for a subset (n=49 MAAP); response assessment based on first-line therapy only
