## Summary

This paper reports the results of a Phase II proof-of-concept clinical trial (NCT02649972) of cobimetinib, an oral MEK1/2 inhibitor, in 18 adult patients with histiocytic neoplasms of any mutational status. The study addresses the unmet need for patients without *BRAF* V600 mutations (~50% of histiocytosis), for whom no standard therapy exists. The overall response rate was 89% by FDG-PET (90% CI: 73–100%), with 72% complete responses and 17% partial responses — all at sites including the CNS. No patient who responded has progressed (median follow-up 11.9 months), and 100% of responses were ongoing at 1 year. Responses were observed across all MAPK genotypes tested: *BRAF* (V600 and non-V600), *MEK1*, *MEK2*, *ARAF*, *RAF1*, *KRAS*, and *NRAS*. Novel mutations identified during the trial (*MEK2* Y134H, *BRAF* N486_T491delinsK, *RAF1* K106N) were functionally confirmed to be activating and ERK-dependent by Ba/F3 in vitro assays, directly correlating with clinical responses. All clinical and sequencing data are publicly available in cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Cobimetinib achieves 89% ORR by PET across all histiocytosis subtypes and MAPK genotypes

**Finding:** MEK1/2 inhibition with cobimetinib produced an overall response rate of 89% (by PET criteria) in adults with histiocytic neoplasms, including complete responses in 72% of patients — a rate far exceeding historical responses to cytotoxic chemotherapy, immunosuppression, or interferon-α.

**Detail:** 13 complete responses (72%), 3 partial responses (17%), 1 stable disease (6%), 1 not evaluable (died before first assessment, counted as non-responder per protocol). By RECIST v1.1 in 14 evaluable patients: ORR 57% (90% CI: 37–100%), with 2 CR and 6 PR. Median time to best PET response: 3.2 months (range 1.6–15.9). Responses occurred across all histiocytosis subtypes and at all disease sites including CNS — a critical site associated with high morbidity/mortality in ECD. 50% of patients remained on protocol therapy at data cutoff; 3 additional patients (all in CR) elected to continue cobimetinib off-label.

### F2: Durable responses with no acquired resistance observed — unlike MEK inhibition in solid tumors

**Finding:** Responses to cobimetinib were not only near-universal but also remarkably durable: neither the median duration of response nor median PFS was reached, 100% of responders maintained response at 1 year, and no patient has acquired resistance as of the data cutoff.

**Detail:** Median follow-up: 11.9 months (range 4.6–26.4 months). Among patients who discontinued for any reason: median time on treatment was 11.9 months (range 1.6–23.7 months). 94% of all patients remained progression-free at 1 year. This durability is remarkable given that single-agent MEK1/2 inhibitors in RAS/RAF-altered solid tumors (NSCLC, pancreatic, colorectal) generally produce minimal clinical benefit with rapid acquired resistance. The unique MAPK dependence of histiocytic neoplasms — and possible lack of adaptive feedback mechanisms — may underlie this exceptional response kinetics. Comparable durability was previously seen with vemurafenib (BRAF inhibitor) in the BRAFV600 subset of histiocytoses.

### F3: Response observed across all MAPK pathway genotypes including novel and rare mutations

**Finding:** Cobimetinib produced responses in patients with *BRAF* V600, non-V600 *BRAF*, *MEK1*, *MEK2*, *ARAF*, *RAF1*, *KRAS*, and *NRAS* mutations, as well as in 2 of 3 patients with unknown mutational status — establishing universal MAPK pathway dependence in histiocytoses.

**Detail:** MAPK pathway mutation distribution: *BRAF* V600E n=4 (22%), *BRAF* V600E + *NRAS* G12D + *KRAS* G13C n=1; *BRAF* N486_T491delinsK n=1; *MEK1* mutations n=4 (Q56P, P105_I107del, P124L, P124Q); *ARAF* mutations n=2 (S225V, P216A); *RAF1* K106N n=1; *MEK2* Y134H n=1; *KRAS* mutations n=2 (R149G, G12R); *NRAS* G12D n=1 (co-mutation); unknown n=3. Among the BRAF V600 patients, 3 had previously failed or were intolerant to BRAF inhibitors. Responses in MEK1-mutated patients were unexpected given preclinical data suggesting some MEK1 mutations (e.g. P124L) confer resistance to MEK inhibition in other contexts.

### F4: Novel MAPK mutations (MEK2 Y134H, BRAF N486_T491delinsK, RAF1 K106N) characterized as activating and cobimetinib-sensitive in vitro

**Finding:** Three novel MAPK alterations identified in trial patients — *MEK2* Y134H, *BRAF* N486_T491delinsK (an in-frame deletion-insertion), and *RAF1* K106N — were functionally validated as activating mutations that confer ERK activation and cytokine-independent growth in Ba/F3 cells, and sensitize those cells to cobimetinib.

**Detail:** All three mutations drove cytokine-independent proliferation of murine IL3-dependent Ba/F3 pro-B cells (statistical significance ****p < 0.0001 for MEK2 Y134H and RAF1 K106N; similar for BRAF N486_T491delinsK vs wild-type). Western blot confirmed phospho-ERK1/2 activation by each mutant vs. wild-type control. IC50 values for cobimetinib were significantly lower in cells expressing each mutant vs parental Ba/F3 (p < 0.01, one-way ANOVA). Clinical responses in the corresponding patients: MEK2 Y134H patient (ECD) showed dramatic SUV reduction in femoral lesions; BRAF N486_T491delinsK patient (LCH) showed resolution of skull lesions; RAF1 K106N patient (mixed histiocytosis) showed response in sacral lesions. *RAF1* mutations had not been previously described in histiocytoses; the specific *RAF1*, *MEK2*, and *BRAF* indel variants were functionally uncharacterized prior to this study.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MAP2K2` (MEK2 Y134H), `BRAF` (N486_T491delinsK), `RAF1` (K106N) · ECD, LCH, mixed histiocytosis

### F5: MAPK pathway dependence in histiocytic neoplasms is unique compared to solid tumors with similar mutations

**Finding:** Single-agent MEK inhibition is remarkably effective in histiocytic neoplasms — in contrast to generally disappointing results in solid tumors with RAS/RAF/MEK alterations — suggesting a fundamental biological difference in MAPK pathway dependence or adaptive feedback capacity in these histiocytic diseases.

**Detail:** Prior work in solid tumors showed that RAS-mutated cancers vary in MEK1/2 dependence, and some MEK1 mutations are predicted to confer resistance to MEK1/2 inhibitors in preclinical models. Despite these predictions, MEK1-mutated histiocytosis patients responded to cobimetinib in this trial. Furthermore, in other MAPK-altered cancers (e.g. KRAS-mutated NSCLC), MEK inhibitor monotherapy produces minimal benefit. The authors hypothesize that histiocytic neoplasms may be uniquely unable to adapt to tonic MEK1/2 inhibition, potentially due to limited pathway redundancy in hematopoietically derived histiocytes vs. epithelial solid tumors. This parallels the exceptional efficacy of vemurafenib (BRAF inhibitor) in BRAFV600-mutant histiocytoses compared to its benefit in colorectal cancer harboring the same mutation.

## Open Questions

1. What is the mechanism underlying the lack of acquired resistance in histiocytic neoplasms, and why do these MAPK-driven neoplasms behave so differently from solid tumors with similar alterations?
2. Is combination MEK + BRAF inhibition superior to MEK inhibition alone, particularly for patients with BRAFV600 who progressed on or were intolerant to BRAF inhibitors?
3. Can cobimetinib or other MEK inhibitors be successfully combined with BRAF inhibitors (e.g. vemurafenib) to prevent or delay the rare cases of resistance that may emerge with longer follow-up?
4. Do the novel MAPK alterations (MEK2 Y134H, RAF1 K106N, BRAF N486_T491delinsK) have specific structural/biologic features that predict response to MEK inhibition vs. BRAF inhibition?
5. Can this trial inform responses in other rare MAPK-driven hematologic malignancies (e.g. hairy cell leukemia, mast cell disease)?

## Limitations

- Small trial (n=18) powered for proof-of-concept, not definitive efficacy estimates; CIs are wide.
- ECD constituted 67% of patients — results may not fully generalize to LCH, RDD, or other histiocytoses.
- 17% of patients had unknown MAPK mutation status (sequencing failure or insufficient tissue); these non-responders are counted per protocol.
- Median follow-up 11.9 months is insufficient to establish long-term durability or identify late resistance.
- PET response criteria (primary endpoint) differ from RECIST — RECIST ORR was lower (57%), reflecting difficulty measuring infiltrative histiocytic disease by CT.
- Trial required adult patients (≥16 years); pediatric histiocytosis outcomes not addressed.
