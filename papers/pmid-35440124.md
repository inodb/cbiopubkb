## Summary

A resource of 46 extensively annotated SCLC PDX/CDX models derived from 33 patients, profiled by MSK-IMPACT targeted NGS, RNA-seq, and IHC. Paired analyses of 18 matched PDX–clinical specimen pairs demonstrated that primary genomic and proteomic features are preserved in PDX models. All four SCLC molecular subtypes (ASCL1/SCLC-A, NEUROD1/SCLC-N, POU2F3/SCLC-P, and YAP1/inflamed/SCLC-I) are represented, including the first extensive characterization of the rare POU2F3 subtype in PDX. YAP1 protein expression was consistently absent or very low despite RNA detection, questioning its utility as a subtype marker. This PDX library, available via `lung_pdx_msk_2021`, provides an important pre-clinical resource for SCLC research where tissue access is severely limited.

## Key Findings

### F1: PDX models faithfully preserve primary tumor genomic and proteomic landscapes

**Finding:** Direct comparison of 18 matched PDX–clinical specimen pairs demonstrated that TP53 and RB1 alterations — the hallmark co-drivers of SCLC — were retained in 89% (16/18) of matched pairs. Beyond TP53 and RB1, additional driver mutations identified in clinical samples were largely preserved in corresponding PDX models. PDX genomes showed higher density of copy number alterations than clinical samples due to absence of human stromal contamination (higher tumor purity).

**Detail:** TP53 mutations (missense and truncating) were found in 92% of clinical samples; RB1 alterations (truncating, splice, structural variant, and deep deletion) in 81%. The two pairs losing TP53 concordance (MSK599, MSK871) may reflect clonal heterogeneity in the biopsy used for PDX generation. One patient (MSK304B) lost PIK3CA and FOXA1 amplification but gained MYCL amplification in the PDX, illustrating possible bottleneck selection. Two never-smoker patients showed EGFR mutations consistent with histologic transformation from lung adenocarcinoma to SCLC. Two KRAS-amplified, TP53/RB1-wild-type samples harbored MDM2 amplification, which may functionally inactivate TP53 and phenocopy missense mutations.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, RB1, MYCL, MYCN, MYC, PIK3CA, KMT2D, SPEN, EGFR, MDM2, KRAS · SCLC

### F2: All four SCLC molecular subtypes confirmed; POU2F3 (SCLC-P) extensively characterized for first time in PDX

**Finding:** SCLC subtypes defined by ASCL1 (SCLC-A), NEUROD1 (SCLC-N), POU2F3 (SCLC-P), and the inflamed/YAP1 signature (SCLC-I) were confirmed in this cohort. Ten PDXs from a single POU2F3+ patient (MSK773, derived from one primary and nine metastatic sites post-mortem) provided the first extensive characterization of SCLC-P in a PDX system. All POU2F3+ PDXs strongly co-expressed MYC and lacked ASCL1, NEUROD1, and functional YAP1 protein. Most PDXs expressed NCAM1 (neuroendocrine marker), except in the POU2F3 subset.

**Detail:** IHC on 37 PDX/CDX samples showed that many co-express ASCL1 and NEUROD1, defining an intermediate SCLC-A/N subtype. Subtype classification by IHC and RNA-seq largely overlapped. Seven of 43 models showed high HLA and antigen-presentation gene expression consistent with the inflamed/SCLC-I designation (MSK831b, MSK511c, MSK325d, MSK773B/H, MSK761, MSK891). YAP1 RNA was detectable in some PDXs but protein was absent in all — suggesting post-transcriptional regulation or YAP1 protein instability in SCLC. Strong YAP1 downstream gene expression (NOTCH2, NOTCH3) was found in POU2F3+ PDXs despite absence of YAP1 protein itself.

### F3: YAP1 protein consistently absent despite RNA expression; SCLC-I redefined by inflamed gene signature

**Finding:** YAP1 protein expression was absent or very low across all 37 PDX/CDX samples assessed by IHC, regardless of YAP1 RNA levels. This finding, consistent with recent clinical data, challenges YAP1 as a reliable IHC-based subtype marker. The SCLC-Y subtype is better redefined as SCLC-I (inflamed), based on expression of HLA genes, antigen presentation machinery, and immune checkpoint genes rather than YAP1 protein alone.

**Detail:** Some PDXs with high YAP1 RNA (MSK599b, MSK831b, MSK511c, MSK773G/E) showed no protein staining. POU2F3+ PDXs highly expressed YAP1 downstream Hippo pathway targets (NOTCH2, NOTCH3) regardless of YAP1 expression itself. ASCL1- and NEUROD1-driven PDXs showed lower expression of Hippo/NOTCH targets, consistent with Hippo pathway inactivation in NE subtypes. Two SCLC-A samples (MSK761, MSK900b) showed intermediate YAP1 RNA expression similar to POU2F3 models without protein. These data support a model where Hippo pathway activity, not YAP1 protein per se, distinguishes non-NE from NE SCLC.

### F4: Clonal heterogeneity and treatment history preserved across PDX generations from one SCLC-P patient

**Finding:** Ten PDX models (MSK773 A–L) derived from one SCLC-P patient — biopsied at primary tumor and nine metastatic sites post-mortem — preserved clonal heterogeneity and subtype identity across all models. Consistent splice site TP53 mutations and CDKN1B/PTEN mutations were maintained. Co-expression of POU2F3, MYC, and high NOTCH pathway activity was uniform across all ten models, providing an unprecedented in vivo platform to study the biology and treatment vulnerabilities of the POU2F3 subtype.

**Detail:** MSK773 models exhibited CDKN1B (p27) mutations and PTEN missense mutations in addition to TP53 splice mutations — a mutational combination more commonly observed in non-small cell lung cancer. MYC co-expression with POU2F3 is consistent with prior studies reporting MYC amplification as frequent in SCLC-P. The availability of ten spatially and temporally distinct models from one patient enables direct comparison of primary vs. metastatic biology and intra-patient clonal diversity. Treatment history is annotated (platinum-etoposide), enabling correlation with genomic features and acquired resistance.

### F5: SCLC subtypes differ in neuroendocrine marker expression, immune profile, and Hippo/NOTCH pathway activity

**Finding:** NE markers (synaptophysin, NCAM1) were high in ASCL1 and NEUROD1 subtypes but lower or absent in POU2F3+ models. The seven inflamed (SCLC-I) models expressed high levels of HLA genes and antigen-presentation machinery independent of subtype identity, consistent with an immune-permissive state. TF subtype classification by IHC and RNA-seq concorded in most cases, validating RNA-seq as a reliable subtype assignment tool when IHC is unavailable.

**Detail:** Models were diverse in inflamed signature assignment — not clearly attributable to a single TF subtype or treatment status (naïve vs. treated). ASCL1/NEUROD1 PDXs showed low Hippo pathway and NOTCH target activity; POU2F3 PDXs showed high NOTCH2/NOTCH3 regardless of YAP1 status. DLL3 (a NOTCH ligand and SCLC therapeutic target) expression patterns across subtypes are relevant to rovalpituzumab tesirine clinical development. The immune hot models express PDCD1 (PD-1), CD274 (PD-L1), CTLA4 ligand genes — potential ICB response biomarkers.

## Open Questions

- Do SCLC-P (POU2F3) tumors have distinct therapeutic vulnerabilities (e.g., PARP inhibitors, BCL2 inhibitors, or Notch pathway inhibitors) compared to NE subtypes?
- Can the seven inflamed/SCLC-I PDX models predict which patients benefit from immune checkpoint blockade in the clinic?
- Does the subtype of PDX generation (tissue vs. CDX) introduce systematic biases in subtype representation or mutation spectrum?
- How does the plasticity between SCLC subtypes (ASCL1↔NEUROD1↔POU2F3) manifest over the course of treatment, and can the MSK773 multi-site models capture this evolution?
- Are KRAS-amplified/TP53-RB1-wild-type SCLC cases a biologically distinct entity, potentially representing histologic transformation rather than de novo SCLC?

## Limitations

- PDX models require immunosuppressed murine hosts, precluding evaluation of immune-directed therapies in the native tumor microenvironment
- CDX engraftment rates may select for specific cell populations not representative of the bulk tumor
- MSK-IMPACT is a targeted panel (341-505 genes), not WGS; structural rearrangements, complex SVs, and non-panel driver events may be missed
- Proteomics data limited to IHC for a small panel of markers; mass spectrometry–based proteomics not performed
- PDX engraftment rate was only ~24% for pure SCLC tissue, introducing selection bias toward more aggressive or easily transplantable clones
