## Summary

This multi-cohort observational study (discovery n=4,141; prospective validation n=1,426; international generalizability n=463 NSCLC) demonstrates that ctDNA detection from plasma liquid biopsies is independently associated with cancer-associated venous thromboembolism (VTE), with a dose-dependent relationship (higher VAF → higher VTE risk). A random survival forest (RSF) machine learning model trained on liquid biopsy variables (ctDNA VAF, cfDNA concentration, genomic content) achieves c-indices of 0.73–0.74 versus 0.57 for the Khorana Score (KS). In real-world data, anticoagulation is associated with lower VTE rates in ctDNA-positive (HR 0.50) but not ctDNA-negative patients (HR 0.89), suggesting ctDNA-guided anticoagulation deserves randomised prospective evaluation. Data available in cBioPortal as `msk_ctdna_vte_2024`.

## Key Findings

### F1: ctDNA detection is independently associated with VTE (HR 2.49) across multiple cancer types

**Finding:** ctDNA detection is associated with significantly higher rates of VTE in the discovery cohort (HR = 2.49; 95% CI 1.99–3.11; P < 0.001), confirmed in the prospective validation and generalizability cohorts, and independent of clinical/radiographic features including Khorana score, disease stage, number of metastatic sites, and cfDNA concentration.

**Detail:** The association is replicated across sensitivity analyses: time from diagnosis (left-truncated), 6-month landmark analysis (patients at risk >6 months post-draw), and when death is treated as censoring rather than competing risk. ctDNA levels are also elevated in patients with prior VTE (higher VAF than those without, P = 3.6×10⁻⁹). Association holds in NSCLC, melanoma, and pancreatic cancers but not in bladder, hepatobiliary, or colorectal cancers — suggesting tumour-type-specific biology of ctDNA-mediated thrombogenicity. In matched-tumour analysis (n=2,873 with MSK-IMPACT), ctDNA detection remains associated with VTE while specific gene-level alterations (including KRAS, STK11, KEAP1) are not, indicating ctDNA's association with VTE is largely independent of specific tumour genomics.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `STK11`, `KEAP1` (analysed in gene-level subanalysis) · Pan-cancer; NSCLC, pancreatic, melanoma highlighted

### F2: ctDNA VAF shows dose-dependent relationship with VTE risk; single-variable AUROC of 0.66 exceeds KS (0.58)

**Finding:** VTE risk increases monotonically across ctDNA variant allele fraction (VAF) quartiles, demonstrating a dose-response relationship. As a single variable, ctDNA VAF achieves an AUROC of 0.66 for 6-month VTE prediction, exceeding Khorana Score (0.58), cfDNA concentration (0.52), and the recent published RAM model (0.61).

**Detail:** At optimal Youden's index threshold, ctDNA VAF achieves: sensitivity 0.83, specificity 0.44, PPV 0.60, NPV 0.72. The dose-response (VAF quartile → VTE risk) supports a biological mechanism where greater ctDNA shedding reflects more aggressive tumour physiology and/or micrometastatic disease. Serial ctDNA draws show that later measurements have stronger VTE association than earlier ones, suggesting dynamic prognostic utility.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-level ctDNA VAF (pan-cancer) · All cancer types

### F3: Machine learning liquid biopsy model (LB+ RSF) outperforms Khorana Score with c-index 0.74 vs. 0.57

**Finding:** A random survival forest (RSF) trained on liquid biopsy variables (ctDNA VAF, cfDNA concentration, genomic content) combined with cancer type and chemotherapy receipt (LB+ model) achieves a 5-fold cross-validated c-index of 0.74 (95% CI 0.71–0.77), versus 0.57 for KS and 0.62 for RAM; validation c-index 0.73, generalizability c-index 0.67.

**Detail:** cfDNA concentration (not just ctDNA fraction) is the most important feature in the 'All' model by permutation importance. Models trained on KS/RAM variables alone, even with nonlinear machine learning, achieve inferior performance to LB-based models. The 6-month dynamic AUROC of the LB+ model exceeds KS. Highest-risk patients (top risk quantile) have cumulative VTE incidence >25% — 3× higher than high-risk KS patients (~10%) for whom guidelines already recommend prophylaxis. Lower c-index in generalizability cohort (0.67) is largely attributable to cancer-type homogeneity (NSCLC only); NSCLC-only cross-validation in discovery yields c-index of 0.70.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer liquid biopsy features · NSCLC, breast, pancreatic, melanoma, all cancer types

**Data availability:** `available`
> Input variables (ctDNA, cfDNA, cancer type, chemotherapy, KS components) available in `msk_ctdna_vte_2024`. Model outputs not in cBioPortal; require external ML implementation.

### F4: Anticoagulation is associated with lower VTE in ctDNA-positive patients (HR 0.50) but not ctDNA-negative

**Finding:** In real-world observational data, anticoagulation is associated with lower VTE rates in ctDNA-positive patients (adjusted HR = 0.50; 95% CI 0.30–0.81) but shows no benefit in ctDNA-negative patients (adjusted HR = 0.89; 95% CI 0.40–2.0), suggesting ctDNA status could guide anticoagulation decisions.

**Detail:** This is an exploratory analysis using real-world anticoagulant prescription data (non-randomised), adjusted for age, cancer type, and time since diagnosis. Similarly, statin use is associated with lower VTE rates in ctDNA-positive but not ctDNA-negative patients. Aspirin shows no VTE-reducing association in either group. The authors emphasise that interventional randomised prospective studies are needed to confirm clinical utility. The non-randomised design means confounding by indication cannot be excluded.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer ctDNA status · All cancer types

### F5: KRAS, STK11, KEAP1 alterations in ctDNA associate with VTE in univariate but not multivariate analysis

**Finding:** In gene-level subanalyses, alterations in known thrombogenic genes (KRAS, STK11, KEAP1) show univariate association with VTE, but in multivariate analysis including ctDNA detection and tissue-confirmed mutations (n=2,873), gene-level alterations lose significance while ctDNA detection remains independent — indicating ctDNA's VTE association is not driven by specific tumour genomics.

**Detail:** KRAS, STK11, and KEAP1 are known to be associated with NSCLC-related VTE risk from prior studies. The finding that ctDNA detection — a proxy for tumour shedding and aggressiveness — supersedes these specific alterations in multivariate models suggests the VTE association is mediated by broad tumour burden/physiology rather than specific oncogenic pathways. This is corroborated by the dose-response relationship between ctDNA VAF and VTE risk.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `STK11`, `KEAP1` · NSCLC (primary analysis), pan-cancer (extended)

## Open Questions

- Can a ctDNA-guided randomised prospective trial confirm that prophylactic anticoagulation benefits ctDNA-positive cancer patients and avoids unnecessary treatment in ctDNA-negative patients?
- Why is ctDNA not associated with VTE in bladder, hepatobiliary, and colorectal cancers — is this due to different cfDNA shedding biology, distinct coagulation pathways, or sample size limitations?
- What is the mechanistic link between ctDNA shedding and thrombogenesis — does ctDNA/cfDNA directly activate NETs and coagulation cascades, or reflect aggressive tumour biology that independently promotes clotting?
- Does ctDNA VAF trajectory (rising vs. falling on treatment) dynamically predict VTE risk, enabling real-time anticoagulation adjustments?
- Can the LB+ RSF model be validated prospectively in a multi-centre, community-oncology setting with diverse sequencing assays?

## Limitations

- Observational study; anticoagulation analyses are non-randomised and subject to confounding by indication
- ctDNA-VTE association not observed in bladder, hepatobiliary, and colorectal cancers — unclear if biological or statistical
- Generalizability cohort limited to NSCLC (100%), reducing cross-cancer generalisability
- VTE definition and ascertainment may differ between institutional records and registry data; some events may be missed
- cfDNA extraction methods differ between MSK-ACCESS and ctDx Lung assays; linear correction used but residual variability possible
- Real-world medication data (anticoagulant prescription) is a proxy for anticoagulation use; dosing, duration, and adherence not captured
- Model requires liquid biopsy infrastructure not available at all centres; implementation requires standardisation of cfDNA extraction and sequencing
