## Summary

Retrospective review of the first 101 consecutive patients enrolled in the Precision in Pediatric Sequencing (PIPseq) Program at Columbia University Medical Center (Jan 2014–Apr 2016). High-risk pediatric oncology and hematology patients (defined as <50% 5-year OS, rare/relapsed disease, or suspected cancer predisposition) underwent tumor-normal whole exome sequencing (WES), RNA-seq, and/or a 467-gene targeted panel in a CLIA-certified laboratory with molecular tumor board review. Key results: 38% of patients had potentially actionable alterations (only 16% received matched therapy); an additional 38% received clinically relevant non-targetable information (diagnostic, prognostic, pharmacogenomic); and RNA-seq was independently impactful in 57% of the 65 patients tested. Overall, 66% of patients had clinically impactful findings; this rose to 75% in patients tested with both WES and RNA-seq. Germline cancer predisposition alterations were found in 14% of patients. Full data in `mixed_pipseq_2017` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: 38% of pediatric patients have potentially actionable alterations, but only 16% receive matched therapy

**Finding:** 38/101 patients (38%) had at least one potentially targetable genomic alteration — 32% of solid tumor patients and 47% of hematologic patients. However, matched therapy was only received by 6/38 (16%) of patients with targetable alterations.

**Detail:** Targetable alterations were tiered by strength of evidence: Tier 1 (same tumor type, same gene — e.g., BCR-ABL1, IDH1, c-KIT); Tier 2 (different tumor type, same gene — e.g., SMARCA4 EZH2 inhibitor, TSC1 mTOR inhibitor); Tiers 3–5 (preclinical and board-determined evidence). RAS pathway alterations (NRAS, KRAS, PTPN11) were the most common targetable finding in hematologic malignancies; kinase fusions (BRAF, ABL1, NTRK3, PDGFRB) and receptor mutations (FGFR4) predominated in solid tumors. The gap between actionability identification and matched therapy receipt reflects off-label barriers, trial eligibility, and clinical preference — establishing a baseline for precision oncology programs.

**Genes / cancer types:** BCR-ABL1, IDH1, KIT, NRAS, KRAS, PTPN11, SMARCA4, BRAF-fusions, FGFR4, TSC1, PIK3CA, NUP214-ABL1, FOXP1-ABL1, SMARCB1 · Pediatric solid tumors and hematologic malignancies

### F2: RNA-seq provides independent clinical impact beyond DNA sequencing in 57% of tested patients

**Finding:** Of 65 patients with RNA-seq data, 37 (57%) had clinically impactful findings from RNA alone — providing diagnostic/prognostic information in 17 (26%) and identifying therapeutic targets in 15 (23%).

**Detail:** RNA-seq identified BCR-ABL1–like expression patterns (leading to confirmation of NUP214-ABL1 fusion and successful addition of dasatinib to an otherwise refractory ALL regimen), novel/rare fusions (EML4-NTRK3 in congenital fibrosarcoma, SMARCC2-PDGFRB in ALL, TMEM106B-BRAF in pleomorphic xanthoastrocytoma), overexpression of diagnostic markers (STAT5B mutations distinguishing gamma-delta T-cell lymphoma from T-ALL), and pharmacogenomic modifiers. The combination of WES + RNA-seq yielded clinically impactful findings in 75% of patients, versus 66% overall. Transcriptome reference was built from 124 normal tissues (blood, liver, kidney, lung, brain) enabling relative overexpression calls.

**Genes / cancer types:** NUP214-ABL1, FOXP1-ABL1, EML4-NTRK3, TMEM106B-BRAF, SMARCC2-PDGFRB, STAT5B, MYC · Pediatric oncology (pan-cancer)

### F3: Germline cancer predisposition alterations found in 14% of high-risk pediatric patients

**Finding:** Known or likely pathogenic germline alterations were identified in 18/90 patients (20%) with germline data; 14% harbored alterations in cancer predisposition genes. ACMG secondary findings were identified in 6 patients.

**Detail:** Constitutional WES (proband ± parents) was performed for 22 patients, primarily for suspected constitutionally encoded disease (e.g., familial HLH). Germline analysis of the broader cohort revealed alterations in cancer predisposition genes including NF1, TP53, and others. ACMG secondary findings (unrelated to the primary indication) were returned to 6 patients. The informed consent framework allowed opt-in/opt-out for secondary findings and EMR posting; 94% opted to learn secondary findings, and 69% consented to EMR posting. No patients were charged for sequencing.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, NF1, and cancer predisposition genes (ACMG list) · Pediatric oncology (pan-cancer)

### F4: Non-targetable genomic findings provide diagnostic and prognostic impact in 38% of patients

**Finding:** In an additional 38% of patients beyond those with targetable alterations, sequencing provided clinically relevant non-actionable information: diagnostic reclassification (n=23 patients), prognostic stratification, pharmacogenomic guidance, or health maintenance recommendations (n=32 patients).

**Detail:** Key examples: STAT5B mutation reclassified a 5-year-old's T-ALL to gamma-delta T-cell lymphoma, altering management completely. PTPN11 mutation in a 4-year-old changed diagnosis from de novo AML to JMML. Serial sequencing in individual patients revealed clonal evolution (e.g., emergence of new alterations at relapse), critical for targeted therapy selection. Pharmacogenomic findings included variants in drug-metabolizing enzymes relevant to chemotherapy dosing. Pathogenic germline findings triggered cascade genetic testing for family members and specialized surveillance.

**Genes / cancer types:** STAT5B, PTPN11, TP53, NRAS, KRAS, H3F3A · Pediatric solid tumors and hematologic malignancies

### F5: Most common driver genes: TP53 in solid tumors; NRAS/KRAS in hematologic malignancies

**Finding:** TP53 was the most frequently mutated gene in solid tumors (9/101, 9%); RAS pathway genes dominated hematologic malignancies (NRAS n=5, KRAS n=3). Mean mutational load was 216.9 variants overall, higher in solid tumors (mean 84.3; median 85) versus hematologic conditions (mean 56.8; median 47).

**Detail:** 92/101 patients (91%) had reportable genomic aberrations. A total of 180 somatic mutations and 20 fusions were reported post-filtering. Solid tumor samples had a mean of 2.91 reportable aberrations per sample (median 2); hematologic samples had a mean of 5.2 (median 4), reflecting the larger gene panel and more complex clonal architecture of leukemias. Serial sampling in individual patients demonstrated temporal clonal evolution — an important consideration for adjusting targeted therapy choices at relapse.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, NRAS, KRAS, BCR-ABL1, NUP214-ABL1, FGFR4, H3F3A · Pediatric solid tumors and hematologic malignancies

## Open Questions

- What is the optimal timing and frequency of repeat sequencing to capture clonal evolution and guide therapy changes at relapse?
- How does broader adoption of WES + RNA-seq in pediatric oncology affect clinical outcomes at the population level, beyond feasibility demonstration?
- Can pharmacogenomic findings from germline WES be routinely integrated into chemotherapy dosing decisions in pediatric patients?
- What are the ethical frameworks and practical protocols for returning ACMG secondary findings to children and adolescents?
- Does the benefit of WES + RNA-seq differ by pediatric tumor histology (solid vs. hematologic, rare vs. common diagnoses)?

## Limitations

- Single-institution retrospective review (CUMC, 2014–2016); selection bias toward high-risk patients with >50% mortality risk
- Only 16% of patients with actionable alterations actually received matched therapy, limiting clinical outcome evaluation
- Reimbursement highly variable (0% from government plans, partial from commercial); cost-effectiveness not evaluable
- WES reference transcriptome built from 124 normal tissues across mixed platforms — may not optimally represent all pediatric tumor types
- Turnaround time (~60 days for final report) may limit real-time clinical utility in rapidly progressing disease
- Small cohort limits statistical power to identify histology-specific patterns of actionability
