## Summary

Targeted sequencing (OncoPanelv2, 504 cancer-associated genes) of 29 lymph node metastases from patients with cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (cSCC) — a rare, poorly characterized disease affecting ~5% of all cSCC patients. All samples were HPV-negative and UV-driven (CT transitions = 67% of spectrum). Core tumor suppressors: *TP53* (79%), *CDKN2A* (48%), *NOTCH1/2* (24–69%). Oncogenic activating mutations identified in 38% of cases (EGFR, HRAS, KRAS, BRAF, FGFR3, KIT, MTOR, PIK3CA). Chromatin remodeling genes altered in 48% of cases. RAS/RTK/PI3K pathway and chromatin remodeling mutations both significantly correlated with worse progression-free survival. Findings support basket trial design pooling cSCC with lung SCC and head and neck SCC. Data available in `cscc_dfarber_2015` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: UV-driven mutation landscape dominated by *TP53*, *CDKN2A*, and *NOTCH1/2* loss — confirming squamous lineage hallmarks

**Finding:** CT transitions comprise 67% of the mutation spectrum (87% occurring after a pyrimidine), confirming UV as the dominant mutagenic force. *TP53* was mutated in 79% (23/29) of samples; *CDKN2A* was altered by both mutation and homozygous loss in 48% (14/29). *NOTCH1/2* showed truncating inactivating mutations in 24% and any nonsynonymous mutation in up to 69% of samples. *RIPK4* (squamous differentiation regulator) was mutated in 24% and *SMAD4* in 7%.

**Detail:** These findings in metastatic cSCC are consistent with primary cSCC exome studies, validating that the metastatic lymph node specimens faithfully reflect the genomic architecture of the primary tumor. The UV mutational signature explains the extremely high mutation rates (average 33 mut/Mb in paired samples; up to 117 mut/Mb in unpaired). No HPV TpCG signature was detected, and all samples were independently confirmed HPV-negative, distinguishing metastatic cSCC from HPV-associated head and neck SCC. The diversity of mutation rates across samples (4–117 mut/Mb) suggests a biologically heterogeneous disease.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, CDKN2A, NOTCH1, NOTCH2, RIPK4, SMAD4 · Metastatic cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma

### F2: Activating oncogenic mutations in 38% of cases span EGFR, HRAS, KRAS, BRAF, FGFR3, and KIT — mostly mutually exclusive

**Finding:** Activating or functionally validated oncogenic mutations were found in 11/29 (38%) of tumors. These included *BRAF* G464R/G469R (2 cases), *KRAS* G12C (1), *FGFR3* G380R constitutively active (1), *HRAS* G13D (1), *EGFR* S720F (1), *ERBB4* E563K (1), *KIT* E562D (1), *EZH2* Y641S (1), *MTOR* S2215F (1), and *PIK3CA* P471L (1). Nearly all activating mutations were mutually exclusive, with each tumor typically bearing only one.

**Detail:** The mutual exclusivity pattern across EGFR, HRAS, KRAS, BRAF, FGFR3, KIT, and PIK3CA is consistent with convergent activation of a single RAS/RTK/PI3K signaling axis. This is analogous to the mutually exclusive driver mutation landscape in lung adenocarcinoma, where oncogene addiction is well-characterized. *EGFR* was also significantly recurrently amplified across the cohort, though high-level gain was seen in only one sample. The presence of *FGFR3* G380R — which causes achondroplasia as a germline mutation — highlights the constitutively activating nature of this transmembrane domain mutation. Collectively, these mutations suggest targetable single-driver oncogene addiction in ~38% of metastatic cSCC.

### F3: RAS/RTK/PI3K pathway mutations correlate significantly with worse progression-free survival

**Finding:** In exploratory survival analysis, mutations in the RAS/RTK/PI3K pathway (activating mutations in EGFR/HRAS/KRAS/BRAF/FGFR3/KIT/MTOR/PIK3CA, excluding *EGFR* and *ERBB4*) correlated significantly with worse PFS (average 12 months vs. 50 months in RAS/RTK/PI3K-WT; log-rank p significant). Paradoxically, patients with *EGFR* or *ERBB4* mutations had the longest PFS (average 79 months), suggesting these may define a distinct, more indolent subgroup.

**Detail:** The *EGFR*/*ERBB4* PFS paradox is intriguing: these receptor-activating mutations may represent less aggressive oncogenic activation than downstream pathway mutations (HRAS, KRAS, BRAF) or may mark tumors more likely to respond to EGFR-directed therapies (consistent with a phase II trial of gefitinib showing an 18% complete response rate in advanced cSCC). The RAS-pathway poor-prognosis correlation suggests that downstream signaling activation — bypassing the EGFR receptor — is associated with more aggressive metastatic behavior. A combination of RAS/RTK/PI3K pathway mutations and chromatin remodeling gene mutations further increased the prognostic significance.

### F4: Chromatin remodeling gene mutations in 48% of metastatic cSCC and independently associated with worse PFS

**Finding:** Forty-eight percent of metastatic cSCC samples had truncating or COSMIC mutations in chromatin remodeling genes: *CREBBP* (6 cases), *MLL2* (5 cases), *ARID1A/ARID2/ARID5B* (5 cases), *EP300* (3 cases), *SMARCA4* (1 case), and *EZH2* Y641S activating mutation (1 case). *ARID2* was the most recurrently inactivated chromatin modifier (truncating mutations in 10%). Chromatin remodeling gene mutations correlated independently with worse PFS; the combination with RAS/RTK/PI3K mutations further increased significance.

**Detail:** *NOTCH1/2/4* truncating mutations and *EP300* truncating mutations were mutually exclusive across the cohort, suggesting convergent disruption of the NOTCH co-activator axis. *CREBBP* and *EP300* are histone acetyltransferases whose loss broadly impairs transcriptional activation programs. The *EZH2* Y641S activating mutation (common in follicular lymphoma) promotes polycomb-mediated silencing of tumor suppressor genes. The independent prognostic impact of chromatin remodeling alterations on PFS suggests epigenetic dysregulation is a driver of aggressive behavior in metastatic cSCC, not merely a passenger, and may represent a therapeutic target (e.g., EZH2 inhibitors, HDAC inhibitors).

**Genes / cancer types:** CREBBP, EP300, MLL2 (KMT2D), ARID1A, ARID2, ARID5B, SMARCA4, EZH2 · Metastatic cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma

### F5: Metastatic cSCC shares a targetable landscape with lung SCC and HNSCC, supporting histology-agnostic basket trials

**Finding:** Recurrently altered genes in metastatic cSCC — including *EGFR*, *FGFR3*, *HRAS*, *BRAF*, *PIK3CA*, *TP63* amplification, *NOTCH1/2* loss, *CDKN2A/B* deletion, *SMAD4* loss — overlap substantially with the genomic landscape of lung squamous cell carcinoma and head and neck SCC. The squamous differentiation pathway (*TP63*, *NOTCH1/2*, *RIPK4*, NF-kB via *CARD11*) was recurrently disrupted. Copy number gains of *MYC*, *EGFR*, and amplification of *TP63* (24%) were notable recurrent CNA events.

**Detail:** GISTIC analysis identified 25 significantly amplified and 11 significantly deleted gene regions. The most significant recurrent loss was at 9p21 (*CDKN2A/B*, 21%). *TP63* amplification at 24% mirrors the ~20% rate in lung SCC. *LAMA5* was the most recurrently highly amplified gene (4/29 samples), with potential roles in metastatic migration. *CARD11* activating mutations in CARD domains were associated with better PFS in exploratory analysis, consistent with NF-kB-mediated differentiation. The pan-SCC genomic convergence supports multi-site basket trial enrollment to test EGFR, FGFR3, PI3K, and MEK inhibitors across squamous histologies regardless of tissue of origin.

## Open Questions

- Does EGFR expression (without activating mutation) predict response to EGFR inhibitors (cetuximab, gefitinib) in metastatic cSCC, as seen in HNSCC?
- Do *FGFR3* G380R or other constitutively active FGFR3 mutations confer sensitivity to FGFR inhibitors in cSCC basket trial settings?
- Why do *EGFR*/*ERBB4* mutations paradoxically associate with better PFS in this cohort — intrinsic sensitivity to EGFR-directed therapy, less aggressive biology, or confounding?
- Can EZH2 inhibitors target the minority of *EZH2*-activating-mutant or ARID-loss-driven epigenetically dysregulated cSCC tumors?
- Is the high chromatin remodeling mutation burden a consequence of UV mutagenesis, an active oncogenic driver, or both?

## Limitations

- Very small cohort (N=29); exploratory survival correlations lack power and a validation cohort; all findings hypothesis-generating
- All samples are lymph node metastases; primary tumor comparisons not available; clonal evolution from primary cannot be assessed
- Targeted panel sequencing (504 genes) at relatively low depth (avg 82×); may underestimate mutation frequencies compared to WES
- No RNA sequencing; fusion transcripts, expression subtyping, and immune infiltrate characterization not available
- Clinical outcome data limited by small N and heterogeneous treatment; no treatment-stratified survival analysis possible
