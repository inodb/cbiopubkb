## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing of 59 metastatic biopsies from 58 HR+/HER2− breast cancer patients with CDK4/6 inhibitor (CDK4/6i) exposure reveals that resistance is mechanistically heterogeneous. Eight categories of genomic alterations — biallelic *RB1* disruption, activating alterations in *AKT1*, *RAS* family genes, *AURKA* amplification, *CCNE2* amplification, *FGFR2* alterations, *ERBB2* alterations, and ER expression loss — collectively account for 66% of resistant tumors while being rare in sensitive specimens. Three of these (AKT1, RAS, AURKA) had not previously been reported as CDK4/6i resistance mechanisms. Cancer cells cultured to CDK4/6i resistance recapitulate these alterations and show enhanced sensitivity to AURKA, ERK, or CHEK1 inhibitors. A clinical vignette confirms that an AURKA inhibitor (LY3295668) produced prolonged disease control in an AURKA-amplified CDK4/6i-refractory patient, providing translational proof-of-concept that these mechanisms define actionable therapeutic vulnerabilities.

## Key Findings

### F1: Eight genomic categories of CDK4/6i resistance present in 66% of resistant tumors — mechanistically diverse, mostly absent in sensitive specimens

**Finding:** Bioinformatic enrichment analysis followed by functional validation identified eight categories of genomic alterations as drivers of CDK4/6i resistance. Collectively present in 27 of 41 resistant biopsies (65.9%) but only 3 of 18 sensitive biopsies (16.7%). The categories are: biallelic *RB1* disruption, activating *AKT1* mutations/amplification, *RAS* family activating mutations (*KRAS*, *HRAS*, *NRAS*), *AURKA* amplification, *CCNE2* amplification, *FGFR2* alterations, *ERBB2* mutations/amplification, and loss of ER protein expression. Multiple mechanisms co-occur in 12 of 41 resistant samples (29.3%).

**Detail:** Frequencies in resistant biopsies (n=41): RB1 biallelic disruption 9.8%, AKT1 4.9% mutation + 7.3% amplification = 12.2%, RAS 9.8%, AURKA 26.8%, CCNE2 14.6%, FGFR2 7.3%, ERBB2 12.2%, ER loss 7.3%. All mechanisms validated in isogenic cell models (CRISPR knockout or lentiviral overexpression) conferring palbociclib resistance. In cells cultured to CDK4/6i resistance, concordant molecular alterations (RB1 loss, AURKA/CCNE2 overexpression, RAS/ERK activation) emerged, confirming these are bona fide resistance drivers rather than passenger events.

### F2: AURKA amplification is the most frequent novel resistance mechanism (26.8%) — zero in sensitive specimens; clinical proof-of-concept with AURKA inhibitor

**Finding:** Aurora Kinase A (*AURKA*) amplification was identified in 11 of 41 resistant biopsies (26.8%), occurring in both intrinsic (n=7) and acquired (n=4) resistance contexts. Zero sensitive biopsies harbored AURKA amplification. Even low-magnitude AURKA amplifications (as seen in this cohort) are associated with significantly elevated AURKA mRNA expression in primary HR+ breast cancers (TCGA data), suggesting functional impact. A patient with AURKA-amplified HR+/HER2− MBC who progressed on CDK4/6i was enrolled in a Phase 1/2 clinical trial of the AURKA inhibitor LY3295668 and achieved prolonged disease control.

**Detail:** AURKA is a serine/threonine kinase critical for mitotic entry and centrosome maturation. Its amplification allows cells to bypass the G1/S checkpoint arrest imposed by CDK4/6i by engaging CDK2-independent mitotic progression. Cells cultured to palbociclib resistance showed elevated AURKA protein expression, and AURKA inhibition in these cells induced cell death disproportionate to parental cells. In the clinical vignette (NCT03092934), the patient was palbociclib + letrozole refractory; LY3295668 treatment resulted in measurable disease control, providing the first translational validation that AURKA amplification in CDK4/6i-resistant HR+ MBC is an actionable target.

### F3: RB1 biallelic disruption is exclusively seen in CDK4/6i-resistant tumors — diverse inactivation mechanisms, mechanistically necessary for resistance

**Finding:** Biallelic *RB1* inactivation was found exclusively in resistant biopsies (4/41 = 9.8%) and in zero sensitive specimens. All four resistant cases showed a distinct combination of mechanisms: each had a heterozygous copy-number loss of one allele combined with a point mutation, splice site alteration, or frameshift indel in the second allele — illustrating diverse routes to biallelic inactivation. RB1 protein loss abolishes the primary substrate of CDK4/6, rendering CDK4/6 inhibition irrelevant.

**Detail:** CDK4/6 inhibitors function by maintaining RB1 in a hypophosphorylated, active (growth-suppressive) state. Loss of RB1 protein entirely bypasses this mechanism of action — cells are CDK4/6i-insensitive regardless of pathway. RB1-null cells cultured to resistance in palbociclib confirmed this mechanism. RB1 biallelic disruption is uncommon in treatment-naïve HR+ breast cancer (<2% primary tumors) but is enriched in endocrine-refractory disease, establishing it as an acquired (or pre-existing rare subclonal) event. RB1-null tumors may be more sensitive to CHEK1 (prexasertib) inhibition (shown in culture-to-resistance experiments), nominating checkpoint kinase inhibition as a post-CDK4/6i strategy.

### F4: PIK3CA mutations are equally frequent in sensitive and resistant tumors — not a CDK4/6i resistance marker; ESR1 enriched in resistant but insufficient as sole CDK4/6i resistance driver

**Finding:** *PIK3CA* mutations occurred in 44.4% of sensitive and 43.9% of resistant biopsies — essentially identical frequencies — establishing that *PIK3CA* is unlikely to be a CDK4/6i resistance marker. By contrast, *ESR1* mutations were enriched in resistant tumors (34.1% vs. 16.7% sensitive), but ESR1-mutant tumors in the sensitive group all received fulvestrant (SERD), and ESR1 mutations alone are insufficient to drive CDK4/6i resistance; additional mechanisms must co-occur.

**Detail:** This finding is clinically important: PIK3CA-mutant tumors (treated with alpelisib + fulvestrant in the SOLAR-1 setting) do not appear intrinsically CDK4/6i resistant. ESR1 mutations accumulate under endocrine pressure and co-occur with CDK4/6i in many patients, but are not drivers of CDK4/6i insensitivity per se. TP53 alterations were strikingly enriched in resistant biopsies (58.5% vs. 0% in sensitive), but preclinical follow-up did not support TP53 as a sufficient CDK4/6i resistance driver, possibly reflecting overall genomic instability and therapy history rather than a direct mechanistic role.

### F5: Cells cultured to CDK4/6i resistance acquire concordant genomic alterations and become sensitive to orthogonal targeted therapies — translational resistance circumvention strategy

**Finding:** MCF7 and T47D cells cultured to resistance in palbociclib acquired alterations in RB1, AURKA, CCNE2, and KRAS — mirroring the spectrum observed in patient biopsies. Critically, culture-to-resistance cells showed enhanced sensitivity to AURKA inhibition, ERK inhibition, and CHEK1 inhibition (prexasertib), relative to palbociclib-sensitive parental cells, demonstrating that each resistance mechanism creates a new targetable dependency.

**Detail:** Three distinct therapeutic strategies emerged from resistance modeling: (1) AURKA-amplified resistant cells respond to AURKA inhibitors (LY3295668 or alisertib); (2) KRAS-mutated or RAS-activated resistant cells respond to ERK inhibitors; (3) RB1-null resistant cells respond to CHEK1 inhibitors (prexasertib). Apoptosis assays in RB1-null resistant cells confirmed prexasertib-induced apoptosis disproportionate to parental MCF7. These findings provide a roadmap for post-CDK4/6i precision therapy selection based on the mechanism of resistance identified at tumor re-biopsy.

## Open Questions

- What fraction of CDK4/6i-resistant patients have resistance mechanisms not captured by the eight categories defined here, and what are those mechanisms?
- Do the eight resistance mechanisms respond differently to sequential therapies (e.g., AURKA-amplified → AURKA inhibitor vs. CCNE2-amplified → CDK2 inhibitor)?
- Can liquid biopsy (ctDNA) prospectively identify emerging resistance mechanisms before radiographic progression, enabling pre-emptive therapy switching?
- Does the co-occurrence of two or more resistance mechanisms (29.3% of resistant cases) predict worse outcomes, and does it require combination targeting?
- Is resistance category (intrinsic vs. acquired) clinically relevant for therapy selection — e.g., should AURKA amplification found at baseline (intrinsic) be treated differently than when found at acquired resistance?
- Does CHEK1 inhibition (prexasertib) benefit RB1-null tumors in clinical settings, and can this be tested in biomarker-stratified trials?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=59 biopsies, 58 patients) limits statistical power for rare mechanisms and for intrinsic vs. acquired resistance subgroup comparisons
- Whole-exome sequencing; structural variants (gene fusions) and regulatory mutations (e.g., TERT promoter) not fully captured
- Single biopsy per patient — intratumoral/spatial heterogeneity of resistance mechanisms not assessed
- No matched pre-CDK4/6i samples in most patients — cannot determine which mechanisms pre-existed vs. were acquired
- Majority received palbociclib (84.5%); generalizability to ribociclib or abemaciclib cohorts requires separate validation
- ER expression loss assessed by IHC (semi-quantitative); mechanistic basis (genomic or epigenetic) not determined
- Clinical vignette (AURKA inhibitor response) is a single patient — requires prospective biomarker-selected trial for validation
- Two ERBB2 variants included as putative resistance drivers were variants of uncertain significance (L377M, P1074L)
