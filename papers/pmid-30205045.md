## Summary

A large prospective MSK-IMPACT cohort of 1,918 breast cancer patients (1,501 HR+, 692 post-hormonal therapy) reveals the genomic landscape of endocrine-resistant advanced disease. Beyond the known *ESR1* ligand-binding-domain mutations (18% of post-endocrine therapy tumors), the study identifies two new mutually exclusive resistance pathways: (1) MAPK pathway activation — principally *ERBB2* activating mutations and *NF1* loss-of-function mutations, each enriched more than 2-fold post-therapy — and (2) estrogen receptor transcriptional regulators (*MYC*, *CTCF*, *FOXA1*, *TBX3*). Together these alterations are present in 22% of post-hormonal-therapy HR+ tumors, are mutually exclusive with *ESR1* mutations, and associate with dramatically shorter PFS on aromatase inhibitors (3.5 vs. 15.2 months). Matched pre-/post-treatment sequencing (74 pairs) and an autopsy case (10 sites) demonstrate that receptor tyrosine kinase–level MAPK events (*ERBB2*, *EGFR* amp) often pre-exist therapy, while downstream effectors (*NF1*, *KRAS*, *MAP2K1*) are preferentially acquired. Coexisting spatially distinct resistance mechanisms within a single patient are documented.

## Key Findings

### F1: ESR1 ligand-binding-domain mutations in 18% of post-endocrine therapy HR+ tumors — the canonical resistance mechanism

**Finding:** *ESR1* hotspot mutations (ligand-binding domain) are present in 18% (128/692) of post-hormonal-therapy HR+/HER2− specimens — confirming them as a major mechanism of acquired endocrine resistance. ESR1-mutant tumors show intermediate PFS on fulvestrant (SERD) similar to pan-wild-type tumors, but shorter PFS on aromatase inhibitors.

**Detail:** ESR1 mutations arise predominantly after exposure to aromatase inhibitors. On SERD therapy, *ESR1*-mutant patients had PFS comparable to pan-wild-type (ESR1 mutations do not confer SERD resistance), while on AI they had median PFS of 4.1 months versus 15.2 months in pan-wild-type (p=0.0003). ESR1 mutations were mutually exclusive with ERBB2/NF1/MAPK alterations (mutual exclusivity p<0.0001), defining parallel non-overlapping resistance mechanisms that converge on ER-independent growth signaling.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ESR1` · HR+/HER2− Metastatic Breast Cancer

### F2: ERBB2 activating mutations and NF1 loss-of-function mutations each enriched >2× in post-endocrine therapy HR+ tumors — new MAPK-mediated resistance mechanisms

**Finding:** Functional hotspot mutations in *ERBB2* and loss-of-function mutations in *NF1* are each more than twice as common in post-hormonal-therapy tumors compared to treatment-naïve HR+ primaries (p=0.00003 and p=0.0004, respectively). These MAPK-activating events are mutually exclusive with *ESR1* mutations, establishing them as alternative, non-overlapping resistance mechanisms.

**Detail:** Beyond ERBB2 and NF1, pathway analysis identified additional MAPK activating mutations (ERBB3, KRAS G12R, HRAS, BRAF, MAP2K1/MEK1) and 12 focal EGFR amplifications in post-therapy specimens — all arising predominantly in ESR1-wild-type tumors. In total, 22% of all post-hormonal therapy tumors (n=692) harbored a MAPK or transcription factor alteration. MAPK-altered tumors had median PFS of 3.5 months on AI versus 15.2 months in pan-wild-type patients (p=1.4×10⁻⁹). Functional validation: EGFR overexpression in MCF7 cells conferred fulvestrant resistance reversible by EGFR kinase inhibitors (erlotinib/gefitinib) or ERK inhibitors.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ERBB2`, `NF1`, `EGFR`, `KRAS`, `HRAS`, `BRAF`, `MAP2K1`, `ERBB3`, `FGFR4` · HR+/HER2− Breast Cancer

### F3: Estrogen receptor transcriptional regulator alterations (MYC, CTCF, FOXA1, TBX3) define a third endocrine resistance arm — mutually exclusive with ESR1 and MAPK lesions

**Finding:** Amplifications and hotspot mutations in transcriptional regulators of ER — *MYC*, *CTCF*, *FOXA1*, and *TBX3* — are enriched in post-hormonal-therapy specimens and arise predominantly in tumors lacking *ESR1* or MAPK alterations, defining a third non-overlapping resistance pathway. Transcription factor–altered tumors had median PFS of 6.4 months on AI versus 15.2 months pan-wild-type (p=1.3×10⁻⁶).

**Detail:** FOXA1 hotspot mutations are histology-specific: lobular cancers harbor wing-2 forkhead domain mutations while ductal cancers carry wing-1 mutations — suggesting different protein interaction disruptions. CTCF mutations (H284, Y226) and TBX3 mutations (N297) were exclusively ductal and lobular, respectively. SPOP E78K mutations (not previously reported in breast cancer) identified in three post-AI HR+ patients likely prevent SPOP-mediated ER degradation, analogous to endometrial SPOP LOF. MYC amplifications were approximately half pre-existing and half acquired; CTCF/FOXA1 hotspots showed mixed pre-existing and acquired patterns.

### F4: Matched pre/post-treatment sequencing distinguishes pre-existing from acquired resistance mechanisms — NF1 and downstream RAS/RAF lesions predominantly acquired, RTK-level events often pre-existing

**Finding:** In 74 matched tumor pairs (treatment-naïve primary vs. post-endocrine-therapy metastasis), MAPK alterations at the RTK level (*EGFR* amp, *ERBB2* mutations) were frequently pre-existing (60% and 82% of cases, respectively), while downstream RAS/MAPK effector mutations (*NF1*, *KRAS*, *MAP2K1*, *BRAF*) were predominantly acquired under the selective pressure of endocrine therapy (74% of affected patients). This distinction has implications for biomarker-guided early intervention vs. salvage therapy design.

**Detail:** WES (30 initial pairs, 44 additional) plus high-depth MSK-IMPACT for 74 total matched pairs. Detection sensitivity ~1.3% cancer cell fraction by targeted sequencing. Three representative cases: one showed KRAS G12R emerging clonally after therapy (pre-treatment RHOA L22R subclone not selected); two others showed de novo biallelic NF1 inactivation in post-therapy specimens. SWI/SNF complex mutations (ARID1A, ARID2) were also more often acquired, consistent with their metastatic enrichment in the prospective cohort.

### F5: Coexisting spatially distinct resistance mechanisms within a single patient — ESR1 and ERBB2 mutations in separate metastatic deposits, confirmed by ddPCR

**Finding:** A research autopsy with WES of 10 metastatic sites from one HR+ patient revealed that distinct metastatic deposits carried completely different mechanisms of endocrine resistance: ESR1 Y537 mutation exclusively in pleural disease, and five different metastatic sites carrying three distinct ERBB2 driver mutations (S310, L755, D769) — none of which were detected in the pre-mortem recurrence biopsy. Droplet digital PCR confirmed mutual exclusivity at <0.03% sensitivity. This demonstrates that single biopsies fundamentally underestimate the diversity of resistance mechanisms in a patient.

**Detail:** The shared clonal mutations across all sites (TP53, CDH1, FGFR1 amplification, 40+ others) establish common ancestry. The divergent ESR1/ERBB2 mutations are spatially restricted, consistent with independent evolutionary selection in different metastatic niches. This result reconciles the observation that ESR1 and ERBB2 mutations appear mutually exclusive in population-level analysis (different clones) yet coexist in an individual patient. Implications: (1) cfDNA may be needed to capture the full spectrum of resistance; (2) combination therapies targeting multiple resistance mechanisms simultaneously may be required.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ESR1`, `ERBB2`, `TP53`, `CDH1`, `FGFR1` · HR+/HER2− Metastatic Breast Cancer (autopsy)

**Data availability:** `partial`

## Open Questions

- Does sequential biopsies at progression (or cfDNA monitoring) prospectively predict the emerging resistance mechanism in individual patients, and can that information guide selection of next-line therapy?
- Can combined anti-MAPK + endocrine therapy (e.g., ERK inhibitor + fulvestrant) prevent acquired MAPK-mediated resistance or improve outcomes in MAPK-altered tumors?
- Which patients are likely to have coexisting resistance mechanisms in different metastatic sites, and does baseline cfDNA heterogeneity predict this?
- Do the novel PIK3CA hotspots (12 identified here, validated in MCF10A) predict response to PI3K inhibitors equivalently to canonical hotspots?
- What drives the organotropism of specific resistance mutations (e.g., CDH1 mutations with ovarian metastasis, GATA3 loss with lung metastasis)?
- Is the frequency of spatial resistance heterogeneity (as seen in the autopsy case) captured in the broader cohort through cfDNA analysis?

## Limitations

- Cannot separate metastasis-specific from therapy-induced mutations (87.5% of metastatic biopsies had prior therapy); dedicated de novo metastatic cohort would be needed
- Only 125 treatment-naïve metastatic specimens — insufficient power to isolate metastasis-specific alterations from therapy effects
- MSK-IMPACT (468 genes) is not whole-exome: driver mutations outside the panel are not captured
- Single-site biopsies underrepresent spatial heterogeneity (demonstrated by the autopsy case)
- Limited matched pair data (n=74) with variable sensitivity depending on tumor purity
- Functional validation performed for EGFR/NF1 only; other MAPK alterations inferred from pathway logic
- Clinical outcome data (PFS) relies on medical record annotation and may be subject to ascertainment bias
- Short follow-up for survival endpoint in some subsets; metastatic setting PFS is primary rather than OS
