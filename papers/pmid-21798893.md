## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing of 74 HNSCC tumor/normal pairs (filtered from 92 sequenced) identifies the mutational landscape of this sixth-most-common cancer, revealing both confirmed drivers and a new class of vulnerabilities. The study confirms known HNSCC genes (*TP53*, *CDKN2A*, *PTEN*, *PIK3CA*, *HRAS*) using the unbiased MutSig algorithm and, critically, identifies *NOTCH1* as a recurrently mutated tumor suppressor (11% of cases) — the first demonstration of somatic NOTCH1 loss-of-function mutations in a squamous malignancy. The most penetrating conceptual finding is that mutations in genes governing squamous epithelial differentiation are present in at least 30% of cases, including *NOTCH1*, *IRF6*, *TP63*, and 11 additional genes with known roles in squamous development — suggesting that disruption of the squamous differentiation program is a fundamental and unifying pathogenic event in HNSCC. The study also validates massively parallel sequencing as an HPV detection tool, correlates mutation rate with tobacco exposure and anatomic site, and discovers elevated mutation rates in some non-smokers linked to DNA repair gene mutations.

## Key Findings

### F1: NOTCH1 is a recurrent loss-of-function tumor suppressor in HNSCC — first demonstration in any squamous malignancy

**Finding:** *NOTCH1* somatic point mutations are identified in 11% (8/74) of HNSCC tumors, with additional focal deletions in 2 cases — making it the most significantly mutated novel gene in this study. All mutations appear to be loss-of-function: nonsense mutations generate truncated proteins lacking the C-terminal ankyrin (transactivation) domain; five missense/in-frame deletion mutations cluster in conserved residues of the extracellular ligand-binding domain; two splice-site mutations affect proteolytic cleavage or ligand-binding residues. This is the first identification of somatic NOTCH1 loss-of-function mutations in any squamous cancer. Additionally, *NOTCH2* or *NOTCH3* mutations are found in 11% of samples, with a NOTCH3 focal deletion in one additional case.

**Detail:** In T-ALL and CLL, NOTCH1 mutations are activating (gain-of-function); in HNSCC, the mutations resemble those in myeloid leukemia where NOTCH1 acts as a tumor suppressor. The findings have immediate therapeutic implications: gamma-secretase inhibitors (GSIs), which inhibit NOTCH activation, were in clinical trials at the time — but this study predicts that GSIs could actually worsen outcomes by further suppressing NOTCH1 tumor suppressor function and potentially inducing squamous tumors (consistent with mouse knockout models). Clinical experience subsequently confirmed increased squamous neoplasms in GSI trial patients.

### F2: Disruption of the squamous differentiation program is a unifying pathogenic event in >30% of HNSCC

**Finding:** Gene-set enrichment analysis of significantly mutated genes (q < 0.25) reveals the highest-scoring gene set is "epidermal development." Beyond *NOTCH1*, *IRF6*, and *TP63* (all significantly mutated, q < 0.25), 11 additional recurrently mutated genes have documented roles in squamous differentiation based on mouse knockouts (*Notch1*, *Notch2*, *Irf6*, *Tp63*, *Ripk4*, *Cdh1*, *Ezh2*, *Dicer1*), human germline syndromes causing orofacial clefting (*IRF6*, *TP63*, *CDH1*, *MLL2*), or keratinocyte differentiation block phenotypes (*TP63*, *NOTCH1*, *IRF6*, *MED1*). Together, squamous differentiation pathway mutations affect >30% of HNSCC cases.

**Detail:** The biological logic is as follows: ΔNp63α (the dominant TP63 isoform in squamous epithelium) promotes basal keratinocyte renewal by suppressing NOTCH1 and CDKN2A. IRF6 drives proteasomal degradation of ΔNp63α, forcing differentiation. Genotoxic stress (via TP53) induces NOTCH1-mediated terminal differentiation, antagonized by ΔNp63. Mutations across multiple nodes of this circuit (TP53 ubiquitously, NOTCH1 in 11%, IRF6 and TP63 in significant fractions, and nuclear polarity genes SYNE1/SYNE2 in 20%/8%) collectively disable squamous maturation. Also mutated: *CASP8* (8%), *DDX3X* (4%), *PRDM9* (11%), *EZH2* (6%), *RIMS2* (11%), *PCLO* (12%).

### F3: Tobacco exposure drives a G→T transversion signature and high mutation rates; HPV status inversely correlates with TP53 mutation

**Finding:** The average G→T transversion rate at non-CpG sites is 12% ± 6%, characteristic of tobacco-induced DNA damage. Patients with smoking history show a positive correlation between G→T transversion frequency and overall mutation rate (p=0.02). HPV-positive tumors have significantly lower mutation rates (2.28/Mb vs. 4.83/Mb for HPV-negative; p=0.004) and inversely correlate with TP53 mutation (p=0.006), consistent with HPV E6-mediated TP53 degradation replacing the need for somatic TP53 mutation. Laryngeal HPV-negative cancers show higher mutation rates and G→T transversion frequencies versus oral cavity/oropharyngeal HPV-negative cancers (p=0.008 and p<0.0001). Several HPV-negative non-smoker tumors show outlier-high mutation rates, some linked to DNA repair gene mutations, raising the possibility of endogenous mutational processes.

**Detail:** The PathSeq algorithm applied to sequencing data detects HPV-16 reads in 19% of tumors, with 11/14 also positive by PCR. Three PCR-negative tumors had very low HPV sequence read counts (contamination vs. low dosage). This demonstrates WES as a clinically useful HPV detection tool. The mutation rate gradient across anatomic sites (larynx > oropharynx/oral cavity) among HPV-negative tumors is consistent with differential tobacco exposure patterns across subsites.

### F4: Known HNSCC genes (TP53, CDKN2A, PIK3CA, HRAS, PTEN) confirmed as significantly mutated by unbiased MutSig analysis

**Finding:** MutSig analysis validates five genes with prior evidence in HNSCC at FDR q < 0.1: *TP53* (most commonly mutated, also disrupted by a 100 kb deletion detected by WGS), *CDKN2A*, *PTEN*, *PIK3CA*, and *HRAS*. Copy-number analysis replicates prior findings of frequent *CCND1* amplification, *CDKN2A* deletion, and rarer amplifications of *MYC*, *EGFR*, *ERBB2*, and *CCNE1*. The validation of these known genes alongside novel discoveries demonstrates the analytical rigor of the MutSig framework and confirms the collection is genetically representative of HNSCC.

**Detail:** TP53 is the most frequently mutated gene in HNSCC across all studies; this dataset confirms that finding with the additional discovery of a large structural deletion (100 kb deletion encompassing TP53) detectable only by WGS. PIK3CA mutations in HNSCC are typically activating hotspot mutations at E542K, E545K, H1047R (as in other cancer types) and represent a potentially actionable target. HRAS mutations (vs. KRAS/NRAS in other cancers) are a distinctive HNSCC feature. PTEN mutations cooperate with PIK3CA to activate PI3K signaling. The published study notes that most mutated genes in the dataset did not reach MutSig significance, suggesting many passenger events in a high-background mutation rate tumor type.

## Open Questions

- Given NOTCH1 loss-of-function in HNSCC, can synthetic lethal dependencies downstream of NOTCH inactivation (e.g., specific developmental pathway gene dependencies) be identified and exploited therapeutically?
- Does the squamous differentiation pathway disruption in HNSCC represent a "lineage dependency" — analogous to MITF in melanoma — that could be therapeutically targeted with differentiation-inducing agents?
- Do specific NOTCH1 mutation types (truncating vs. missense in the ligand-binding domain) predict different biological behaviors or drug sensitivities?
- What drives elevated mutation rates in HPV-negative non-smokers — APOBEC deaminase activity (a signature not analyzed in this 2011 paper), mismatch repair deficiency, or novel mechanisms?
- Can HPV detection by tumor sequencing replace ISH/PCR in routine clinical diagnostics, and does detection of very low HPV read counts (PCR-negative) have prognostic significance?
- Do SYNE1/SYNE2 mutations that disrupt nuclear polarity cooperate functionally with NOTCH1 mutations in promoting an immature basal-like phenotype?

## Limitations

- WES filtered to 74 of 92 samples due to stromal admixture; excluded samples may have distinct biology (e.g., desmoplastic tumors)
- MutSig analysis relies on background mutation rate model calibrated to smoking-related mutational signatures — may underperform for HPV-positive tumors with different mutational processes
- Mutation validation by mass spectrometric genotyping has reduced sensitivity at low allelic fraction; some somatic mutations may be missed
- Only 2 tumors underwent WGS — structural variants (fusions, large deletions, inversions) not systematically captured for the cohort
- APOBEC-driven mutagenesis (now recognized as important in HNSCC, especially HPV-positive oropharyngeal) was not analyzed — it was not characterized as a signature until after this study
- No germline sequencing — inherited variants in HNSCC risk genes not assessed
- No RNA-seq — gene expression correlates of mutations and HPV integration sites not characterized
- 2011 study predates TCGA HNSCC analysis (2015) which substantially expanded the cohort and data dimensions
