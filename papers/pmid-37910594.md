## Summary

This retrospective study of 128 IDH-mutant WHO Grade 2 glioma patients at MSKCC integrates 3D volumetric MRI measurements with comprehensive molecular profiling (MSK-IMPACT) to establish tumor volume growth rate (TVGR) as a quantitative imaging biomarker during the active surveillance ("watch and wait") period after surgical resection. Using log-linear mixed modeling, the cohort-wide median TVGR was 10.46% per 6 months (doubling time ~3.5 years). CDKN2A/B homozygous deletion — a WHO 2021 high molecular grade marker — was associated with a 2-fold higher TVGR than low molecular grade tumors. Critically, each 1-unit increase in log tumor volume was associated with a >3-fold increase in risk of death (HR=3.83). The study proposes TVGR as a surrogate endpoint for clinical trials of novel IDH-targeted therapies in this population. Genomic data from the MSKCC IDH-mutant glioma cohort are accessible via difg_msk_2023 in cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: IDH-mutant low-grade gliomas grow at 10.46% per 6 months (doubling time ~3.5 years) during active surveillance

**Finding:** Using log-linear mixed modeling of serial 3D volumetric MRI measurements across 528 time points, the entire cohort of IDH-mutant Grade 2 gliomas showed a continuous percent TVGR of 10.46% per 6 months (95% CI: 9.11%–11.83%), corresponding to a tumor volume doubling time of 3.5 years (95% CI: 3.10–3.98 years). This quantitative growth rate serves as a population-level baseline for the "watch and wait" natural history of this disease.

**Detail:** The log-linear model was selected because it best fit the observed growth kinetics over the surveillance period. 3D volumetric measurements were performed on T2/FLAIR sequences and were found to be more reproducible than historical 2D (bidimensional) measurements. Growth was continuous rather than episodic in most patients, consistent with a slow exponential model. This quantitative benchmark is critical for designing clinical trials targeting the active surveillance period, as it establishes what magnitude of TVGR reduction would represent a meaningful clinical benefit.

### F2: CDKN2A/B homozygous deletion (high molecular grade) doubles TVGR compared to low molecular grade IDH-mutant glioma

**Finding:** IDH-mutant gliomas with homozygous deletion of CDKN2A/B — classified as high molecular grade per WHO 2021 — had a TVGR of 19.17% per 6 months (95% CI: 15.57%–22.89%), significantly higher than low molecular grade tumors (9.54% per 6 months; 95% CI: 7.32%–11.80%; P<0.0001). This two-fold difference in growth rate validates CDKN2A/B deletion as a clinically meaningful molecular prognostic feature detectable on imaging.

**Detail:** CDKN2A/B homozygous deletion is a WHO 2021 criterion that upgrades IDH-mutant astrocytomas to Grade 4. This study provides radiographic validation that this molecular distinction corresponds to meaningfully different tumor growth kinetics during the surveillance period, preceding the histologic transition to higher grade. The growth rate difference supports more aggressive monitoring and earlier intervention in CDKN2A/B-deleted patients. The finding also suggests that TVGR could serve as an early imaging surrogate for molecular grade.

### F3: Each log-unit increase in tumor volume is associated with >3-fold increased risk of death (HR=3.83)

**Finding:** Joint modeling of longitudinal TVGR and overall survival demonstrated that each one natural logarithm increase in tumor volume (approximately a 2.7-fold volume increase) was associated with an HR of 3.83 for death (95% CI: 2.32–6.30; P<0.0001), establishing tumor volume as a strong independent predictor of OS in IDH-mutant low-grade glioma under active surveillance.

**Detail:** Joint modeling is a statistical approach that simultaneously models a longitudinal biomarker trajectory (here, log-tumor volume over time) and its association with a time-to-event outcome (OS), accounting for the fact that the biomarker and outcome are co-measured over time. This approach provides a more nuanced estimate of the relationship between tumor dynamics and survival than a single cross-sectional tumor size measurement. The strong HR supports using TVGR as a surrogate endpoint: treatments that reduce the rate of tumor volume increase could translate to improved survival. This finding is particularly relevant for IDH inhibitor trials (e.g., vorasidenib) evaluating this population.

### F4: 1p19q codeletion associated with older age at diagnosis and different growth kinetics than 1p19q-intact (astrocytoma) subtype

**Finding:** 1p19q codeleted oligodendrogliomas had a significantly older median age at diagnosis (41.3 years; IQR: 35.2–50.4) compared to 1p19q-intact astrocytomas (32.8 years; IQR: 26.2–39.3; P<0.001), consistent with prior literature. Growth rate differences between 1p19q-codeleted and intact subtypes were observed, though the major TVGR driver was CDKN2A/B status rather than 1p19q codeletion alone.

**Detail:** The cohort was nearly equally split: 69 patients (53.9%) with 1p19q codeleted oligodendroglioma and 59 patients (46.1%) with 1p19q intact astrocytoma. The younger age at diagnosis of IDH-mutant astrocytomas vs. oligodendrogliomas is consistent with established epidemiology. The study demonstrates that 3D volumetric surveillance parameters can be meaningfully stratified by WHO 2021 molecular classification criteria, validating the radiographic correlate of molecular subtype assignment. TERT promoter mutation status (enriched in oligodendrogliomas) was also included in molecular characterization.

### F5: TVGR proposed as surrogate endpoint for IDH-targeted therapy clinical trials, potentially replacing years-long survival endpoints

**Finding:** The study makes a formal proposal — supported by the strong TVGR–OS association (HR=3.83 per log-volume unit) and the alignment of TVGR with WHO 2021 molecular classification — that TVGR on serial MRI should be incorporated as a surrogate endpoint in prospective clinical trials of IDH-inhibitors and other novel agents for IDH-mutant low-grade glioma, potentially shortening the time required to demonstrate clinical benefit from years to months.

**Detail:** Prior IDH-mutant LGG trials (e.g., RTOG 9802) required >10 years to demonstrate OS benefit from chemoradiation. With IDH inhibitors (vorasidenib FDA-approved 2023 for Grade 2 IDH-mutant glioma), demonstrating OS benefit in a slow-growing disease remains a years-long challenge. TVGR derived from serial 3D MRI could serve as an intermediate endpoint: a pre-specified TVGR reduction (e.g., 50% reduction relative to natural history rate of 10.46%/6mo) would provide early evidence of clinical activity, guiding go/no-go decisions for larger trials. The proposed framework aligns with the FDA's evolving interest in imaging-based surrogate endpoints for oncology drug development.

**Genes / cancer types:** `IDH1`, `IDH2`, `CDKN2A` · IDH-mutant low-grade glioma

## Open Questions

- Can TVGR be standardized across institutions with different MRI protocols, segmentation approaches, and inter-reader variability?
- Does vorasidenib (IDH1/2 inhibitor) reduce TVGR in the active surveillance setting, and can TVGR predict which patients achieve durable disease control?
- Is CDKN2A/B deletion status detectable non-invasively via liquid biopsy (cfDNA or ctDNA) in IDH-mutant LGG, and does it correlate with imaging-based TVGR?
- What is the TVGR threshold that predicts imminent malignant transformation to Grade 3/4, and can early intervention at that threshold prevent transformation?
- Does the TVGR–OS relationship hold equally across 1p19q codeleted (oligodendroglioma) and 1p19q intact (astrocytoma) subtypes?
- How does TVGR change after initiation of treatment (radiation, chemotherapy, IDH inhibitors), and does treatment-induced TVGR reduction correlate with depth of molecular response?

## Limitations

- Retrospective single-institution cohort (MSKCC) limits generalizability; prospective multi-institution validation needed
- MRI protocols and sequences may vary over the 1997–2019 study period, introducing temporal technical variability in volumetric measurements
- 3D volumetric segmentation is time-consuming and not yet standardized for routine clinical use; semi-automated segmentation used here requires manual correction
- No centralized independent radiological review of growth rate measurements
- Active surveillance selection introduces survivor bias (patients healthy enough to defer treatment)
- Median follow-up duration not explicitly reported; censored patients may limit OS model estimates
- Clinical trial outcome data with IDH inhibitors are not yet mature enough to validate TVGR as a formal surrogate (Prentice criteria)
- CDKN2A/B deletion detected by FISH/MLPA in some early cases; MSK-IMPACT coverage of CDKN2A may differ across panel versions
