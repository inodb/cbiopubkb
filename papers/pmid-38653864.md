## Summary

Single-arm phase 2 trial (NCT03241745) of nivolumab (PD-1 blockade) in 35 patients with dMMR/MSI-H or hypermutated advanced or recurrent endometrial or ovarian cancer. Co-primary endpoints (ORR and PFS at 24 weeks) were both met early, triggering early closure. ORR was 58.8% (97.5% CI: 40.7–100%); PFS24 rate was 64.7%; median OS not reached at 42.1 months of follow-up. Exploratory biomarker analyses identified: terminally dysfunctional CD8⁺PD-1⁺TOX⁺ T cells and their proximity to PD-L1⁺ cells as independent TME predictors of benefit; MEGF8 and SETD1B somatic mutations as genomic correlates of benefit; TMB and mechanism of dMMR (genetic vs. epigenetic) were not associated with response. Data linked to `ucec_msk_2024` in cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Nivolumab achieves ORR of 59% and durable responses in dMMR gynecologic cancers

**Finding:** Among 34 evaluable patients, nivolumab achieved ORR of 58.8% (7 complete responses, 13 partial responses) and DCR of 73.5%. Responses were observed across all histologic subtypes, dMMR mechanisms (MLH1 hypermethylation, somatic or germline MMR mutations), and in both endometrial and ovarian primaries. Median DOR and OS were not reached at nearly 3.5 years of median follow-up.

**Detail:** PFS24 rate was 64.7%, meeting the pre-specified 50% threshold. 1-year OS rate was 79% (95% CI: 60.9–89.4%); median PFS was 21.6 months (95% CI: 4.9–NE). 83% of patients had endometrioid endometrial cancer; most had one prior line of cytotoxic therapy (86%). One patient enrolled based on partial MLH1 loss by IHC was found to be MMR-proficient by NGS and had early progression, underscoring the need for NGS confirmation of dMMR/MSI status.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 · Endometrial carcinoma, ovarian carcinoma

### F2: Dysfunctional T cell states — not PD-L1 expression or total CD8 count — predict immunotherapy benefit

**Finding:** Among 25 patients with archival tumor available for multiplexed immunofluorescence, terminally dysfunctional T cells (CD8⁺PD-1⁺TOX⁺) and dysfunctional T cells (CD8⁺PD-1⁺) were both strongly associated with PFS24 (P=0.001 and P=0.006, respectively). PD-L1 expression (tumor cell or combined) was not associated with benefit.

**Detail:** A multivariate model combining % CD8⁺PD-1⁺TOX⁺ T cells (parameter 1) and % PD-L1⁺ cells within 50 μm of CD8⁺PD-1⁺ T cells (parameter 2) — a spatial proximity metric — achieved AUC 0.897 (P=0.0007) for predicting clinical benefit. Median distance between CD8⁺PD-1⁺ cells and nearest PD-L1⁺ cell was 52 μm in patients with benefit vs. 212 μm without. FoxP3⁺ Treg infiltration and CD8/FoxP3 ratio were not predictive alone, though Treg proximity to CD8⁺PD-1⁺ cells was associated with benefit.

**Genes / cancer types:** PDCD1 (PD-1), CD274 (PD-L1), TOX, CD8, FOXP3 · Endometrial carcinoma, ovarian carcinoma

### F3: MEGF8 and SETD1B mutations are genomic correlates of nivolumab benefit

**Finding:** Among 33 patients with WES, MEGF8 mutations (Hedgehog pathway) were present in 32% of patients with PFS24 benefit vs. 0% without (P=0.027); SETD1B mutations (histone lysine methyltransferase) were present in 58% vs. 14% (P=0.015). SETD1B mutations were also associated with higher CD8⁺PD-1⁺TOX⁺ T cell infiltration, linking this genetic alteration to immune recognition.

**Detail:** Frequent somatic mutations across the cohort included ARID1A (82%), PTEN (76%), PIK3CA (48%), MLH1/MSH2/MSH6/PMS2 (30%), JAK1 (24%), MEGF8 (18%), and PTCH1 (18%). 79% of tumors were MSI-H by WES; seven were MSS (all with secondary dMMR signatures). TMB (18.1 vs. 14.4 mut/Mb, P=0.24), MSI status, and dMMR mechanism were not associated with clinical benefit. JAK1, PIK3CA, PTEN, and CTNNB1 mutations — known resistance markers in melanoma — showed no significant association with response in this cohort.

**Genes / cancer types:** MEGF8, SETD1B, ARID1A, PTEN, PIK3CA, JAK1, CTNNB1, POLE · Endometrial carcinoma, ovarian carcinoma

### F4: TMB and MLH1 hypermethylation vs. germline MMR mutation do not predict nivolumab response

**Finding:** Neither TMB level nor mechanism of dMMR (epigenetic MLH1 hypermethylation vs. somatic/germline MMR gene mutation) was associated with clinical benefit from nivolumab. This challenges the clinical assumption that higher TMB or genetic dMMR predicts better PD-1 blockade response.

**Detail:** Median TMB was 18.1 mut/Mb in patients with benefit vs. 14.4 mut/Mb without (P=0.24). MLH1 promoter hypermethylation was the dMMR mechanism in 66% of patients; germline MMR mutation in 14%; somatic MMR mutation or unknown in 20%. The one MSS patient (with POLE exonuclease mutation and highest TMB) had early progression, adding nuance to the hypermutated enrollment criterion. Responses were seen across all mechanisms of dMMR.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, POLE · Endometrial carcinoma, ovarian carcinoma

### F5: Safety profile is consistent with known PD-1 toxicity; rare but serious immune-mediated events observed

**Finding:** 91% of patients had any-grade TRAE; most were grade 1–2 (arthralgia, fatigue, pain, pruritis in 29% each). Grade 3–4 TRAEs occurred in 29%, including myocarditis (n=1), optic neuritis (n=1), hemolysis (n=1), and new-onset type 1 diabetes (n=1). No grade 5 events occurred. All events resolved with standard immunosuppressive management.

**Detail:** One patient with grade 3 myocarditis co-developed grade 2 myasthenia gravis after the first dose. Immune-mediated events mirror those of other PD-1 inhibitor studies; no new safety signals. The patient who developed insulin-dependent diabetes remained on study after blood sugar stabilization. Safety data establish nivolumab as a feasible treatment with manageable toxicity in this gynecologic cancer population.

## Open Questions

- Can the CD8⁺PD-1⁺TOX⁺ T cell spatial proximity model (AUC 0.897) be validated prospectively as a clinical biomarker for patient selection in dMMR gynecologic cancers?
- What is the mechanistic link between SETD1B mutations and increased dysfunctional T cell infiltration — does SETD1B loss alter antigen presentation or neoantigen generation?
- Should hypermutated but MSS tumors (e.g., POLE-mutant with secondary dMMR signature) be included in dMMR-defined trials, given the single progression seen here?
- Does nivolumab monotherapy remain the optimal PD-1 inhibitor strategy or should combinations (e.g., with VEGF inhibitors or CTLA-4 blockade) be prioritized for dMMR gynecologic cancers?
- Would prospective SETD1B and MEGF8 mutation status improve patient selection in randomized dMMR endometrial cancer trials?

## Limitations

- Single-arm phase 2 study with n=35; underpowered for definitive predictive biomarker conclusions and requires external validation
- Multiplexed immunofluorescence available for only 25 of 35 patients; spatial TME analyses are hypothesis-generating only
- WES-based biomarker analyses limited to 33 patients; no pre-specified primary hypothesis on individual gene mutations
- No randomized control arm; response rates cannot be compared to chemotherapy alone
- Gender information not collected on the study (all patients biologically female)
- MLH1 IHC alone was insufficient for one patient who was ultimately found to be MMR-proficient, suggesting that NGS confirmation of dMMR is needed
