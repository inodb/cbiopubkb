## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing of 121 melanoma tumor/normal pairs combined with a novel permutation framework—using intronic mutation rates to control for UV-induced passenger mutation load—identified 11 significantly mutated genes including six novel drivers: *PPP6C*, *RAC1*, *STK19*, *SNX31*, *TACC1*, and *ARID2*. RAC1 P29S was validated as the third most frequent hotspot mutation in melanoma (3.9%), and 46% of all driver mutations bore direct UV-mutagenic signatures, providing unequivocal genomic evidence for UV light's causal role in melanoma.

## Key Findings

### F1: Novel permutation framework resolves UV passenger mutation confounding

**Finding:** A permutation test using local intronic/UTR mutation rates as a per-gene background model identifies 11 significantly mutated genes, avoiding the ~544 false positives yielded by uniform-rate approaches.

**Detail:** Median somatic mutation rate: 14.4 coding mutations/Mb (highest of any cancer type studied at the time). 82.2% of mutations are YC>YT (UV signature). Standard approaches identified 544 "significant" genes; the intronic permutation model reduced this to 11 (q≤0.2). No synonymous mutation burden was found in any gene (internal control).

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF` · `NRAS` · `PTEN` · `TP53` · `CDKN2A` · `MAP2K1` · `PPP6C` · `RAC1` · `SNX31` · `TACC1` · `STK19` · Cutaneous melanoma

### F2: RAC1 P29S is the third most frequent melanoma hotspot

**Finding:** RAC1 P29S is a gain-of-function activating mutation that locks RAC1 in the GTP-bound state and occurs in 3.9% of melanomas—the most frequent hotspot after BRAF and NRAS.

**Detail:** 6 mutations in 5 tumors (5%) in discovery set; all at the same c.85C>T nucleotide. Validated at 3.9% frequency across 355 patients from three independent cohorts. Biochemical pull-downs confirmed elevated GTP-loaded RAC1(P29S) vs WT. Homologous P29 mutations also found in RAC2, RHOT1, and CDC42 activating positions.

**Genes / cancer types:** `RAC1` · `CDC42` · Cutaneous melanoma

**Data availability:** available
> skcm_broad includes RAC1 mutation data.

**Tool support:** native
> RAC1 P29S visible in OncoPrint and Mutations tab of skcm_broad.

### F3: BRAF and NRAS mutations define largely mutually exclusive subgroups with different co-mutation patterns

**Finding:** BRAF and NRAS hotspot mutations are mutually exclusive (p=3×10⁻¹⁴) and found together in 83% of melanomas, with BRAF-mutant tumors showing much higher PTEN co-alteration.

**Detail:** BRAF mutated in 73/121 samples; NRAS in 27/121. PTEN mutation or focal deletion in 44% of BRAF-mutant melanomas vs only 4% of NRAS-mutant melanomas (p=4.9×10⁻⁵). In 21 BRAF/NRAS wild-type tumors, NF1 LoF in 25% (5/21) vs 2% of others (p=5.8×10⁻³); additional HRAS, CRAF, MAP2K1, KIT, and GNA11 alterations account for 81% of the WT subtype.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF` · `NRAS` · `PTEN` · `NF1` · `MAP2K1` · `KIT` · `GNA11` · Cutaneous melanoma

**Data availability:** available

### F4: Six novel driver genes discovered — PPP6C, RAC1, STK19, SNX31, TACC1, ARID2

**Finding:** Six genes not previously recognized as melanoma drivers are statistically significantly mutated, with distinct functional consequences (gain-of-function hotspots vs loss-of-function tumor suppressors).

**Detail:** PPP6C mutated in 9% (hotspot R264C clustering in 60% of PPP6C-mutant cases); STK19 D89N in 4–5%; TACC1 in 7%; SNX31 in FERM-like domain; ARID2 LoF in 7% (SWI/SNF component). Overall SWI/SNF complex LoF (ARID2 + ARID1B + ARID1A + SMARCA4) in 13% (16/121) of discovery samples.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PPP6C` · `STK19` · `TACC1` · `SNX31` · `ARID2` · `ARID1A` · `ARID1B` · `SMARCA4` · Cutaneous melanoma

### F5: 46% of melanoma driver mutations are directly UV-attributable

**Finding:** Nearly half of all defined driver mutations bear C>T or G>T UV signatures, providing direct genomic evidence of UV mutagenesis as a driver—not just a passenger—in melanoma.

**Detail:** Of 262 driver mutations across 21 genes: 46% were C>T (37%) or G>T (9%) transitions. Excluding BRAF/NRAS, 67% (103/150) of driver mutations are UV-attributable. TP53 had the greatest number of UV-induced driver mutations. Newly discovered genes ARID2, PPP6C, SNX31, and TACC1 also showed high UV C>T fractions.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` · `PTEN` · `CDKN2A` · `ARID2` · `PPP6C` · Cutaneous melanoma

**Data availability:** available

### F6: Cell cycle checkpoint (RB/p53) disrupted in ≥24% of melanomas via somatic mutation

**Finding:** CDKN2A (p16INK4a) mutations in >20% of samples combined with RB1 splice/nonsense mutations and CDK4 R24 activation account for direct RB-pathway disruption in ≥24% of tumors.

**Detail:** p16INK4a mutated in 25 samples (>20%), 14/25 being LoF. RB1: 3 LoF mutations. CDK4 R24 activating mutations: 3 cases. TP53 mutated in 19%; mostly in cases without p14ARF or p16INK4a mutation. SWI/SNF + EZH2 + IDH1 mutations in 17% of melanomas.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDKN2A` · `RB1` · `CDK4` · `TP53` · `EZH2` · `IDH1` · Cutaneous melanoma

**Data availability:** available

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- What is the frequency and functional consequence of RAC1 P29S across other tumor types in cBioPortal?
- Do PPP6C mutations co-occur with BRAF or show mutual exclusivity (AurA-Aurora pathway overlap)?
- Can the UV-attributable driver mutation fraction stratify melanoma subtypes or predict immunotherapy response?
- Do SWI/SNF-mutant melanomas respond differently to checkpoint immunotherapy?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Exome-only; does not capture non-coding driver mutations (e.g., TERT promoter)
- Discovery set skewed toward short-term cultures from metastatic tumors (76/121), limiting primary tumor biology
- 121 patients may be underpowered to detect rare drivers at <5% frequency
- Low-frequency UV passenger mutations from loci with high regional mutation rates may still confound even the intronic permutation approach
