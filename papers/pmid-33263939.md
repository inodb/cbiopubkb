## Summary

A small but highly informative WES study characterizing the genomic landscape of juvenile papillomatosis (JP) of the breast — a rare benign mass-forming lesion in young women — and its clonal relationship to synchronously identified breast carcinoma. Three cases underwent WES of JP and, in one case, coexisting DCIS and IDC-NST. Key findings: (1) JP harbors clonal *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations in 2/3 cases (H1047R, E542K) and shows a paucity of CNAs; (2) the JP, DCIS, and IDC-NST of the one mixed case (JuP3) are clonally related — sharing the *PIK3CA* E542K mutation as a shared trunk event; (3) DCIS and IDC-NST acquired additional alterations absent in JP, including 1q gains/16q losses, *ARID1A* truncating mutation, *KMT2C* and *PIK3CB* mutations; (4) a DCIS subclone expanded to become dominant in IDC-NST (clonal selection pattern); (5) mutational signatures shift from aging-dominated in JP to APOBEC-dominated in DCIS/IDC-NST. The study provides the first molecular evidence that JP can be a substrate from which breast carcinoma develops.

## Key Findings

### F1: JP harbors clonal PIK3CA hotspot mutations in 2/3 cases — establishing PIK3CA as a recurrent early driver in this rare benign lesion

**Finding:** WES of three JP lesions identifies clonal *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations in 2/3 cases: JuP2 harbors H1047R (kinase domain hotspot) and JuP3 harbors E542K (helical domain hotspot). JuP1 has a germline *PIK3CA* I391M variant of uncertain significance (not somatic). No other genes are recurrently mutated across cases. JP shows very few total mutations (median 9 per case) and a near-complete absence of CNAs — a strikingly quiet genomic profile consistent with a benign lesion.

**Detail:** These *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations are the canonical activating mutations found in invasive breast cancer (H1047R is the most common breast cancer *PIK3CA* mutation; E542K is a frequent helical domain hotspot). The finding extends the spectrum of benign breast lesions harboring *PIK3CA* mutations — previously identified in papillary neoplasms, usual ductal hyperplasia, and columnar cell change — to include JP. The somatic acquisition of *PIK3CA* mutation as an early/clonal event in JP provides a molecular basis for the observed clinical association between JP and subsequent breast carcinoma. *AKT1* mutations were not identified in these three cases (contrasting with the prior Guillet et al. report of AKT1 or PIK3CA mutations in JP).

### F2: JP and synchronous DCIS/IDC-NST are clonally related — PIK3CA E542K is the shared trunk event, providing direct evidence of JP as a breast cancer substrate

**Finding:** In JuP3, the JP component, synchronous DCIS, and IDC-NST are all clonally related (pairwise clonality index). The three components share a clonal *PIK3CA* E542K hotspot mutation (confirmed clonal in JP, DCIS, and IDC-NST) and two additional shared mutations (*CES1* S12A, *SMG1* R420Q), establishing JP as the probable founder lesion. This is the first direct genomic evidence that JP can be the precursor substrate from which DCIS and invasive breast carcinoma develop.

**Detail:** DCIS and IDC-NST of JuP3 share 29 additional synonymous and nonsynonymous mutations not seen in JP — including a truncating *ARID1A* mutation (chromatin remodeling), missense mutations in *KMT2C* and *PIK3CB*, and a *NUP93* E14K hotspot mutation (nucleoporin implicated in cell migration). These DCIS/IDC-NST-specific mutations represent events acquired during or after the divergence from JP, consistent with progression. The JP component shows only 1q/16q copy-number stability (normal), while synchronous DCIS and IDC-NST already display 1q gains and 16q losses — the hallmark CNA of ER-positive breast cancer — indicating that these chromosomal events arose after the JP–carcinoma split.

### F3: Clonal selection during progression — DCIS minor subclone becomes dominant in IDC-NST; STAG2 and FGF12 acquired as private events

**Finding:** Phylogenetic analysis (PyClone and mutation-based tree) of JuP3 reveals that a *PIK3C2A* missense mutation, subclonal in DCIS, becomes dominant in the IDC-NST — consistent with a clonal selection model of progression from DCIS to invasive cancer. Additional branch events are acquired sequentially: *STAG2* truncating mutation (cohesin complex tumor suppressor) is private to DCIS; *FGF12* missense mutation is private to IDC-NST. This stepwise genetic diversification in a rare benign precursor provides direct evidence of multi-step clonal evolution in the JP → DCIS → IDC-NST axis.

**Detail:** The phylogenetic topology of JuP3 — shared trunk (PIK3CA E542K + 2 shared mutations) → DCIS-specific branches (ARID1A, KMT2C, PIK3CB, NUP93, STAG2 among others) → IDC-NST-specific branch (FGF12 + subclonal DCIS clone expansion) — is fully consistent with a linear progression model. *STAG2* encodes a cohesin subunit; truncating mutations are associated with chromosomal instability and are recurrent in bladder cancer and hematologic malignancies. Its appearance as a DCIS-specific event may contribute to the 1q/16q CNA pattern. The JuP3 phylogenetic tree precisely mirrors the models of DCIS-to-invasive progression described in the larger PMID32220886 (Pareja et al.) cohort.

### F4: Mutational signature shift from aging-dominant in JP to APOBEC-dominant in DCIS/IDC-NST — implicating APOBEC in breast cancer progression

**Finding:** SigMA mutational signature analysis reveals that JP is dominated by aging-related signatures 1 and 5 (signatures 1+5 dominant in JuP2 and JuP3; signature 5 second in JuP1 after signature 18). By contrast, the synchronous DCIS and IDC-NST of JuP3 display substantially greater APOBEC signature 2 exposure — DCIS: 55%, IDC-NST: 64% — compared to JP (signature 2 exposure 20%). The DCIS and IDC-NST also accumulate more total mutations than JP, consistent with accelerated mutagenesis during progression.

**Detail:** The transition from aging-dominated to APOBEC-dominated mutational processes during the JP → carcinoma progression is mechanistically consistent with the known biology of APOBEC (APOBEC3A/B cytidine deaminase activity generating C>T/G mutations at TpC contexts) being a driver of breast cancer mutagenesis and genome instability. The increase in absolute mutation burden from JP (median 9 mutations) to DCIS/IDC-NST (higher, consistent with progression) parallels the shift in signature exposure. Whether APOBEC mutagenesis is a cause or consequence of genomic instability during progression — or both — remains to be determined.

### F5: JP shows hallmark ER-positive breast cancer CNAs (1q gain, 16q loss) absent in JP — CNAs arise at or after JP–carcinoma divergence

**Finding:** Genome-wide CNA profiles reveal that JP components in all three cases have a nearly flat copy-number profile — a paucity of CNAs consistent with a benign lesion. In JuP3, however, the synchronously identified DCIS and IDC-NST display concurrent 1q gains and 16q losses — the hallmark CNA pattern of ER-positive breast cancer. The appearance of these chromosomal changes specifically in the malignant compartment (not in JP) indicates that 1q gain/16q loss arises at or after the divergence from JP, likely representing a critical genomic event in the JP-to-carcinoma transition.

**Detail:** The 1q gain/16q loss pattern is the most recurrent CNA pattern in luminal (ER-positive) breast cancer, reflecting a chromosomal instability event resulting from unequal exchange or translocation between the long arms of chromosomes 1 and 16. Its absence in JP (with its genomic stability) and presence in DCIS/IDC-NST (which share PIK3CA E542K with JP) precisely pinpoints when in the evolutionary trajectory this CNA first arises — after PIK3CA mutation but before or concurrent with DCIS development. This timing is consistent with CNA-driven instability as an accelerant of DCIS-to-invasive progression.

## Open Questions

- In the ~10% of JP patients who develop subsequent breast carcinoma, is *PIK3CA* mutation status in JP a predictive biomarker for carcinoma risk?
- Does the APOBEC signature transition observed at the JP-to-carcinoma boundary reflect APOBEC enzyme activation by a specific trigger (e.g., viral infection, replication stress, immune response)?
- What is the temporal relationship between *PIK3CA* mutation (early, clonal in JP) and 1q gain/16q loss (arising at or after the JP–carcinoma split) — can one cause the other, or are they independent events?
- Would broader screening of JP cases with WES or targeted sequencing identify additional recurrent driver genes beyond *PIK3CA* and *AKT1*?
- Can molecular subtyping of JP (PIK3CA-mutant vs. wildtype) guide clinical management decisions for surveillance frequency?
- Is the germline *PIK3CA* I391M variant in JuP1 pathogenic, and could it explain the patient's JP without a somatic hotspot mutation?

## Limitations

- Extremely small cohort (n=3 JP cases; n=1 with coexisting carcinoma) — findings are hypothesis-generating, not statistically definitive
- All three cases are from a single institution; selection bias toward cases with available tissue and family history
- FFPE tissue used for WES — reduced sensitivity and potential for artifactual mutations; variant calling thresholds may exclude low-VAF clones
- Only one case with coexisting carcinoma (JuP3) available for clonal relationship analysis — the JP-as-precursor conclusion rests on a single case
- No immunohistochemistry or proteomics to confirm functional consequences of identified mutations (e.g., PIK3CA H1047R effect on AKT/mTOR signaling in JP)
- No matched normal tissue from adjacent non-neoplastic breast in all cases — germline variant filtering relies on matched blood or alternative normal sources
- Low mutation counts in JP (median 6 nonsynonymous) limit mutational signature analysis accuracy (SigMA is designed for low-mutation contexts but uncertainty is high)
- Long-term follow-up data for JuP1 and JuP2 (who did not have coexisting carcinoma at diagnosis) not reported
