## Summary

Phase Ib hypothesis-generating trial of nivolumab (anti-PD-1) in 91 patients with clear-cell metastatic renal cell carcinoma (mRCC) at four dose levels (0.3, 2, 10 mg/kg previously treated; 10 mg/kg treatment-naïve). The study collected paired baseline and on-treatment biopsies alongside serum chemokine measurements and whole-blood gene expression to characterize pharmacodynamic immunomodulatory effects. Key findings: nivolumab increases tumor-infiltrating CD3+/CD4+/CD8+ lymphocytes and upregulates interferon-γ–stimulated genes (notably CXCL9, CXCL10, IDO1, PD-L1) within tumors; serum CXCL9 and CXCL10 rise in parallel; baseline PD-L1 expression (≥5%, 32% of patients) does not consistently predict response. ORR across all groups was 15%. Median OS ranged from 16.4 months (0.3 mg/kg) to not reached (2 mg/kg, treatment-naïve). Data are available in `ccrcc_iatlas_choueiri_2016` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Nivolumab increases tumor-infiltrating T cells across all dose groups

**Finding:** On-treatment tumor biopsies (C2D8 vs. baseline) showed significant enrichment of CD3+, CD4+, and CD8+ tumor-associated lymphocytes across all nivolumab dose groups. Median percent increases were 69% (CD3+), 180% (CD4+), and 117% (CD8+) in the 36 patients with evaluable paired samples.

**Detail:** Multiplex IHC on paired biopsies confirmed T-cell infiltration increases in both previously treated and treatment-naïve cohorts. RNA expression of CD3D and CD8A transcripts in tumor biopsies corroborated IHC findings (CD3D: 1.7-fold, P=0.006; CD8A: 1.7-fold, P=0.002). CD4 transcripts did not significantly change (1.2-fold, P=0.175), consistent with the very low baseline levels of intratumoral CD4+ cells. Changes were not dose-dependent.

**Genes / cancer types:** CD3D, CD8A, CD4, FOXP3 · Clear-cell metastatic RCC

### F2: Interferon-γ–stimulated gene signature upregulated in tumor on treatment

**Finding:** Statistical modeling of tumor biopsy gene expression identified 108 transcripts significantly changing over time (≥1.3-fold, P<0.01). Seventy-one were immune lineage–associated genes, all upregulated at C2D8. Sixteen were interferon-regulated, including CXCL9 and CD274 (PD-L1). Forty-seven overlapped a gene set associated with ipilimumab response in melanoma.

**Detail:** The upregulated immune genes span lymphoid-lineage (GZMA/G/H, KLRB1/D1/G1, CTLA-4, ICOS, CD8A/B, CD3D/E/G) and myeloid-lineage (CXCL11, PD-L1, IDO1) subsets. Interferon-γ was the only interferon represented among the regulated genes, suggesting a preferential IFN-γ pharmacodynamic axis. Thirty-seven additional probesets showed dose- or treatment-group–specific patterns. Whole-blood gene expression also shifted within 24 hours (C1D2), with interferon-regulated genes increasing and TCR α/β/γ subunit transcripts decreasing, reflecting receptor internalization.

**Genes / cancer types:** CXCL9, CXCL10, CXCL11, IDO1, CD274 (PD-L1), GZMA, GZMH, CTLA4, ICOS, CD8A, CD3D, KLRB1 · Clear-cell metastatic RCC

### F3: Serum CXCL9 and CXCL10 rise after nivolumab and correlate with tumor gene expression

**Finding:** Median serum CXCL9 increased 101% (range 45–1,730%) and CXCL10 increased 37% (range −30% to 936%) from baseline to C2D8 across 83 evaluable patients. Tumor transcript levels of CXCL9 (2.4-fold, P<0.001) and CXCL10 (2-fold, P=0.011) at C2D8 correlated with same-patient serum levels (CXCL9: r=0.37, P=0.006; CXCL10: r=0.30, P=0.029).

**Detail:** Both chemokines attract T cells to tissues via CXCR3; their elevation suggests nivolumab-induced IFN-γ release drives systemic T-cell recruitment. Changes in CXCL9 were consistently larger than CXCL10 within the same patient; the two were highly correlated. No correlation was seen between baseline serum levels and baseline biopsy transcripts, confirming that the correlation is a pharmacodynamic rather than constitutive phenomenon. These serum biomarkers could serve as minimally invasive pharmacodynamic readouts in future trials.

**Genes / cancer types:** CXCL9, CXCL10, CXCR3 · Clear-cell metastatic RCC

### F4: PD-L1 expression by IHC does not consistently predict nivolumab response in ccRCC

**Finding:** Among 56 evaluable baseline biopsies, 32% had ≥5% PD-L1 tumor membrane staining. There was no consistent change in PD-L1 expression from baseline to on-treatment biopsies. Across dose groups, PD-L1 positivity did not dichotomously distinguish responders from non-responders.

**Detail:** PD-L1 was assessed with the validated Dako 28-8 antibody IHC assay (≥5% threshold). Dynamic PD-L1 changes on treatment were variable and lacked a consistent directional trend across patients. This is consistent with emerging evidence in other tumor types that PD-L1 IHC is an imperfect biomarker and that the immunological context (T-cell infiltration, IFN-γ signaling) may be more informative. The upregulation of CD274 (PD-L1) transcripts in tumor as an IFN-γ–regulated gene on treatment may partly explain the variable dynamic IHC results.

**Genes / cancer types:** CD274 (PD-L1), PDCD1 (PD-1) · Clear-cell metastatic RCC

### F5: Clinical activity: ORR 15%, OS up to not reached at highest dose and in treatment-naïve patients

**Finding:** ORR across all 91 treated patients was 15% (95% CI 8.7–24.5). Median OS was 16.4 months for the 0.3 mg/kg group, not reached for the 2 mg/kg group, and 25.2 months (95% CI 12.0–NR) for the 10 mg/kg previously treated group; treatment-naïve patients on 10 mg/kg had median OS not reached. OS rates at 24 months ranged from 44% to 76%.

**Detail:** PFS rate at 24 weeks was 43% overall and at 48 weeks was 25%. 46% of patients achieved stable disease as best response. No new safety signals were identified; grade 3–4 AEs occurred in ~50% of patients, mainly fatigue. The phase III CheckMate 025 data (nivolumab vs. everolimus, HR 0.73, OS 25 vs. 19.6 months) contextualize these phase Ib estimates and confirm anti-tumor activity across dose groups.

## Open Questions

- Can serum CXCL9 or CXCL10 dynamics serve as validated pharmacodynamic biomarkers of response to PD-1 inhibition across tumor types?
- What explains the lack of dose–response relationship in immunomodulatory effects, and why does median OS not consistently increase with dose?
- Is baseline tumor immune gene expression (e.g., the 47-gene ipilimumab response signature) more predictive of nivolumab response than PD-L1 IHC in RCC?
- How do immunomodulatory effects of nivolumab differ between clear-cell and non-clear-cell RCC subtypes?
- Do combination strategies (nivolumab + VEGFR-TKI or CTLA-4 blockade) exploit the immunomodulatory changes identified here to improve durable response rates?

## Limitations

- Hypothesis-generating, non-randomized phase Ib design; not powered to detect clinical efficacy differences between dose groups
- Paired biopsy requirement limited the evaluable population for each biomarker endpoint (36 for IHC, 42 for gene expression, 83 for serum chemokines)
- Single biopsy site may not capture tumor heterogeneity; some patients had biopsies from different lesions at the two time points
- Affymetrix U219 array data deposited in ArrayExpress but not integrated into cBioPortal, limiting reproducibility within the portal
- PD-L1 IHC cut-off (≥5%) was investigator-defined; different thresholds or antibodies may yield different correlations
- Short median follow-up for treatment-naïve group limits OS estimates
