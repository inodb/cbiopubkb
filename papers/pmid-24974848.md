## Summary

A recurrent, single-nucleotide missense mutation in *GTF2I* (chr7 c.74146970T>A; p.Leu404His) is found at very high frequency in indolent type A (82%) and AB (74%) thymomas but rarely in aggressive thymic carcinomas (8%), defining a molecularly distinct, prognostically favorable subgroup. Thymic carcinomas instead carry a higher mutational burden and recurrent alterations in *TP53*, *CYLD*, and *CDKN2A*. The *GTF2I* mutation stabilizes TFII-I protein and promotes cell proliferation in vitro, establishing it as a functional, tissue-specific oncogenic driver.

## Key Findings

### F1: GTF2I hotspot mutation dominates indolent thymomas

**Finding:** A single recurrent missense mutation in *GTF2I* (c.74146970T>A) is present in 82% of type A and 74% of type AB thymomas, and 43.4% of all 270 TETs evaluated.

**Detail:** The mutation is the exact same nucleotide change (T>A) at the same chromosomal position in every affected case—an unusual feature indicating strong positive selection. Frequency decreases progressively in more aggressive histotypes: 8% in thymic carcinomas. Stage also correlates: 57% in early-stage (I–II) vs. 19% in advanced (III–IV) disease (χ² P < 0.001).

**Genes / cancer types:** `GTF2I` · Thymoma (type A, AB)

### F2: GTF2I mutation marks favorable prognosis

**Finding:** Patients with *GTF2I*-mutant tumors have significantly better survival than those with wild-type tumors (96% vs. 70% 10-year survival; log-rank P < 0.001, n=204 evaluable).

**Detail:** In multivariate analysis including stage, WHO histotype, and GTF2I status, only stage is an independent prognostic factor, reflecting the tight correlation between GTF2I mutation and indolent histotypes. Only 2/83 (2.4%) patients with GTF2I-mutant tumors died of tumor progression vs. 26/121 (21%) with wild-type tumors.

### F3: Thymic carcinomas carry high mutation burden and known cancer gene alterations

**Finding:** Thymic carcinomas have significantly higher average mutation counts than thymomas (43.5 vs. 18.4 mutations per tumor; Mann-Whitney P = 0.001) and carry recurrent mutations in established cancer genes.

**Detail:** Recurrent mutations in thymic carcinomas include *TP53*, *CYLD* (~19%), *CDKN2A*, *BAP1*, and *PBRM1*. Arm-level copy number aberrations are also enriched in aggressive subtypes (B3, carcinoma) vs. indolent A/AB thymomas. *KIT* mutations reported in ~10% of thymic carcinomas by prior studies.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` · `CYLD` · `CDKN2A` · `BAP1` · `PBRM1` · Thymic carcinoma

### F4: GTF2I mutation stabilizes TFII-I protein and promotes proliferation

**Finding:** The p.Leu404His mutation disrupts a putative noncanonical destruction box (RXXL motif), reducing proteasomal degradation and elevating TFII-I protein levels without increasing mRNA, and both β and δ mutant isoforms stimulate NIH-3T3 cell proliferation more than wild-type isoforms.

**Detail:** Cycloheximide chase assays show slightly slower degradation of mutant TFII-I. TFII-I protein expression was higher by western blot in GTF2I-mutant tumors vs. wild-type (mRNA levels did not differ). The β and δ isoforms are predominantly expressed in TETs; both carry the L>H substitution in their expressed transcripts. No impact on soft-agar colony formation, suggesting a proliferation rather than transformation phenotype.

**Genes / cancer types:** `GTF2I` · Thymoma

### F5: Copy number landscape stratifies TET aggressiveness

**Finding:** Arm-level copy number aberrations are frequent in aggressive TETs, with two major CGH clusters—one enriched for type A/AB thymomas and one for aggressive histotypes (B3, carcinoma).

**Detail:** Most frequent arm-level losses: chromosomes 6p (26%), 6q (29%), 3p (22%), 13q (18%). Most frequent gains: 1q (55%), 7p (20%), 7q (15%), 20p (17%). Focal BCL2 locus amplification correlated with increased BCL2 RNA expression by RNA-seq.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BCL2` · Thymic carcinoma · Thymoma (B3)

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Do GTF2I-mutant type B1/B2 thymomas (rare) behave more like type A/AB in terms of response to therapies?
- Can the GTF2I hotspot mutation frequency (82% type A) be used as a near-diagnostic biomarker in liquid biopsy?
- Does TFII-I protein level (not mRNA) serve as a prognostic IHC marker in clinical practice?
- Does GTF2I mutation status provide independent prognostic information within a single WHO histotype?
- Is there a therapeutic vulnerability in TFII-I-overexpressing thymomas (e.g., via protein degradation pathways)?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Small sample size for thymic carcinomas with GTF2I mutation (n=3), limiting functional interpretation.
- NIH-3T3 model is an immortalized fibroblast line—limited translational relevance to thymic epithelium.
- Survival analysis involves relatively short median follow-up (39.4 months), underpowered for rare events.
- No treatment-stratified survival analysis; prognosis may be confounded by histotype-driven treatment differences.
- Clinical annotation depth varies across contributing institutions.
