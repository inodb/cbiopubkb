## Summary

The Beat AML program generated the largest functional genomic dataset of primary AML specimens to date, integrating whole-exome sequencing, RNA-sequencing, and *ex vivo* drug sensitivity profiling across 122 small-molecule inhibitors in 562 patients (672 specimens). The study reveals novel mutational events not previously detected in AML, demonstrates that drug sensitivity can be predicted by specific mutations and gene expression signatures, and identifies drug-mutation associations including sensitivity of FLT3-ITD/NPM1 co-mutant cases to ibrutinib. Multivariate modeling integrating mutation status and co-expression networks substantially improves drug response prediction. All data are publicly available at vizome.org and dbGaP/GDC; genomic data are accessible via cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Novel mutational events not detected in prior AML sequencing studies (TCGA)

**Finding:** Beat AML identified ~1,500 mutant genes not observed in TCGA AML, including 11 genes mutated in ≥1% of patients that were entirely novel to AML sequencing.

**Detail:** Comparison of top 33 most commonly mutated genes between Beat AML (n=531) and TCGA AML (n=200) showed generally similar frequencies, with higher *SRSF2* mutation rates in Beat AML (conserved when comparing only de novo cases). At <2% frequency, 939 genes were called only in TCGA, ~1,500 only in Beat AML, and 221 in common. Eleven genes mutated in ≥1% of Beat AML patients were not observed in prior AML studies. Significant co-occurrence and mutual exclusivity patterns among frequent variants revealed biological cooperativity (DISCOVER method, FDR-corrected).

**Genes / cancer types:** `SRSF2`, `FLT3`, `NPM1`, `DNMT3A`, `IDH1`, `IDH2`, `TP53`, `ASXL1`, `RUNX1`, `BCOR`, `U2AF1`, `ZRSR2` · AML

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `native`
> OncoPrint, Mutations tab, and Co-expression analysis in cBioPortal. Full interactive dataset and Volcano plots accessible at vizome.org.

### F2: Ex vivo drug sensitivity integrates with mutation status to identify novel drug-mutation associations

**Finding:** Profiling 409 primary AML specimens against 122 small-molecule inhibitors revealed mutation-specific drug sensitivities, including FLT3-ITD/NPM1 co-mutation conferring sensitivity to ibrutinib and entospletinib.

**Detail:** Volcano plot analysis (AUC difference mutant vs. wild-type; FDR-corrected q<0.1) confirmed FLT3-ITD sensitivity to FLT3 pathway inhibitors as proof-of-principle. New associations included: FLT3-ITD+NPM1 double mutants showing sensitivity to ibrutinib (BTK/TEC kinase inhibitor) and entospletinib (SYK inhibitor); RUNX1 mutants sensitive to PIK3C/mTOR inhibitors (e.g., BEZ235) and cediranib; BCOR+RUNX1 co-mutants specifically sensitive to all 4 JAK inhibitors (momelotinib, ruxolitinib, tofacitinib, JAK Inhibitor I). TP53 and ASXL1 mutations caused broad drug resistance; NRAS/KRAS mutations correlated with resistance except for predicted MAPK inhibitor sensitivity.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FLT3`, `NPM1`, `DNMT3A`, `RUNX1`, `BCOR`, `TP53`, `ASXL1`, `NRAS`, `KRAS`, `IDH1`, `IDH2`, `U2AF1`, `ZRSR2` · AML

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Mutation data available in `aml_ohsu_2018`; ex vivo drug sensitivity data (AUC values per drug per patient) available at vizome.org and dbGaP (phs001657), not directly in cBioPortal.

**Tool support:** `custom`

### F3: Transformed AML cases show broad drug resistance compared to de novo AML

**Finding:** Transformed AML specimens (arising from MDS, MPN, or therapy-related disease) were significantly less sensitive to the majority of drugs tested compared with de novo AML.

**Detail:** Of 122 drugs tested, 64 showed significantly (FDR<0.1) greater sensitivity in de novo vs. transformed cases; only 1 drug (panobinostat, an HDAC inhibitor) was significantly more sensitive in transformed cases. This broad resistance pattern in transformed AML has implications for clinical trial design and drug selection, suggesting that de novo and secondary AML should be analyzed and treated as biologically distinct entities.

**Genes / cancer types:** AML (de novo vs. transformed) · AML

**Data availability:** `available`
> Clinical metadata including disease origin (de novo vs. transformed) available in `aml_ohsu_2018`. Mutation landscapes can be compared between subgroups using Groups Comparison.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Groups Comparison tool enables survival and mutation frequency comparisons between de novo and transformed AML subsets. Drug sensitivity data requires external data download.

### F4: Gene expression signatures predict drug sensitivity independently of mutation status

**Finding:** Transcriptomic signatures derived from RNA-seq data are predictive of drug sensitivity for 78 of 119 tested drugs (FDR<0.05), often revealing immune and pathway-level biology not captured by single gene mutations alone.

**Detail:** For each drug, the 20% most and least sensitive specimens were compared by RNA-seq to identify differential expression signatures. A 17-gene expression signature distinguished ibrutinib-sensitive from ibrutinib-resistant cases. WGCNA co-expression network analysis identified 14 gene clusters; iterative lasso regression revealed that a 345-gene "turquoise" cluster (enriched in immune pathways) co-occurring with FLT3-ITD predicted ibrutinib sensitivity. A "magenta" 110-gene subnetwork predicted ibrutinib resistance and was linked to adverse ELN 2017 risk. Heatmaps for all 78 drugs available at vizome.org.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FLT3`, `NPM1` and co-expression networks · AML

**Data availability:** `partial`
> RNA-seq data available at dbGaP (phs001657) and GDC; gene expression signatures not directly queryable in cBioPortal `aml_ohsu_2018`. Co-expression heatmaps at vizome.org.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Download RNA-seq counts from dbGaP/GDC; run WGCNA or differential expression (DESeq2, edgeR) in R. Integrate with mutation data from cBioPortal via patient ID matching.

**Concepts:** #AML #leukemia #drug-response #molecular-subtypes #biomarker

### F5: IDH1 and IDH2 mutations have opposite drug sensitivity profiles

**Finding:** IDH2 mutation conferred sensitivity to a broad spectrum of drugs, while IDH1 mutation was associated with resistance to most drugs tested—an unexpected divergence between these related metabolic mutations.

**Detail:** Volcano plot analysis across all 122 drugs showed IDH2 mutants as broadly sensitive (positive AUC difference in majority of drugs); IDH1 mutants as broadly resistant. This divergence is clinically relevant given the existence of both IDH1 (ivosidenib) and IDH2 (enasidenib) inhibitors, and suggests the two mutations may confer different cellular vulnerabilities beyond their shared metabolic effect (2-HG production). Mechanistic exploration of this divergence was identified as a priority.

**Genes / cancer types:** `IDH1`, `IDH2` · AML

**Data availability:** `available`
> IDH1 and IDH2 mutation status available in `aml_ohsu_2018`. Compare mutation frequency and clinical outcomes between IDH1 and IDH2 mutant subgroups using Groups Comparison.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Groups Comparison, OncoPrint, and Mutations tab in cBioPortal. Drug sensitivity data for IDH1 vs. IDH2 comparison requires vizome.org.

**Concepts:** #IDH1 #IDH2 #AML #leukemia #drug-response #targeted-therapy

### F6: TP53 and ASXL1 mutations predict broad drug resistance and adverse outcomes

**Finding:** Somatic mutations in *TP53* and *ASXL1* were each independently associated with resistance to the majority of drugs on the panel, with *TP53* mutant cases showing potential sensitivity to elesclemol and *ASXL1* mutant cases to panobinostat.

**Detail:** TP53-mutant AML showed the broadest drug resistance pattern of any single gene; however, elesclemol (a copper-dependent oxidative stress inducer) trended toward sensitivity in TP53-mutant cases. ASXL1 mutants showed similarly broad resistance with a hint of panobinostat (HDAC inhibitor) sensitivity—potentially clinically exploitable given otherwise limited options. Both mutations are markers of adverse ELN 2017 risk.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `ASXL1` · AML

**Data availability:** `available`

**Tool support:** `native`

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can ex vivo drug sensitivity profiling be implemented clinically to guide individualized AML therapy in real-time (within the treatment window)?
- Do the ibrutinib/entospletinib sensitivity associations in FLT3-ITD+NPM1 AML translate to clinical efficacy in prospective trials?
- What accounts for the divergent drug sensitivity profiles of IDH1 vs. IDH2 mutations despite shared 2-HG production?
- Can the 17-gene ibrutinib expression signature be adapted to a clinical assay or validated in independent AML cohorts?
- What are the mechanistic bases for novel drug sensitivity associations in RUNX1 (PI3K/mTOR) and BCOR+RUNX1 (JAK pathway) subsets?

## Limitations

- Ex vivo drug sensitivity may not fully recapitulate in vivo drug response due to absence of bone marrow microenvironment, pharmacokinetics, and drug metabolism.
- Cohort is enriched for patients willing and able to donate specimens; may not fully represent all AML subtypes.
- Drug sensitivity data not available in cBioPortal; requires external data access (dbGaP/vizome.org), limiting direct integration with genomic queries.
- RNA-seq available for only 411/562 patients (73%); expression-based predictions may not apply to all genomic subgroups.
- Novel gene mutations (not in TCGA) mostly observed in single patients; biological significance requires further validation.
