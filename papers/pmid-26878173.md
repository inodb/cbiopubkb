## Summary

Comprehensive MSK-IMPACT (341-gene panel) sequencing plus mRNA expression profiling of 117 advanced thyroid tumors (84 PDTC, 33 ATC) defines the genetic evolution from papillary thyroid cancer (PTC) through poorly differentiated (PDTC) to anaplastic (ATC) thyroid cancer. Key findings: TERT promoter mutations are ubiquitous in ATC (73%) and clonal in both PDTC and ATC, in sharp contrast to their subclonal, low-frequency occurrence (9%) in TCGA PTCs — consistent with selection during dedifferentiation. BRAF^V600E^ and RAS mutations define distinct histological and clinical subtypes within PDTC. EIF1AX mutations cluster with RAS (14/15 co-occurrences) and represent a previously unrecognized co-driver in advanced thyroid cancer. ATCs display markedly higher rates of TP53 mutation (73%), SWI/SNF complex disruption (36%), histone methyltransferase (HMT) mutations (24%), and PI3K/AKT pathway alterations (39%) relative to PDTCs. Transcriptomic profiling shows that BRAF-RAS expression dichotomy is preserved in PDTC but lost in ATC, which are uniformly BRAF-like regardless of driver mutation; ATCs are also universally dedifferentiated (low thyroid differentiation score) and heavily infiltrated with M2 macrophages. Data available in `thyroid_mskcc_2016` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: TERT promoter mutations increase stepwise across thyroid cancer progression and predict poor survival

**Finding:** TERT promoter mutations (C228T or C250T) are present in 9% of PTCs (subclonal), 40% of PDTCs, and 73% of ATCs (both clonal), implying positive selection during dedifferentiation. They co-occur with BRAF/RAS mutations (P=0.004) and predict shorter survival in ATC patients (147 vs 732 days, P=0.03), especially with co-occurring BRAF or RAS mutations.

**Detail:** TERT promoter mutations create de novo ETS-family transcription factor binding sites (GABPA) that promote TERT overexpression in MAPK-activated cells — mechanistically linking BRAF/RAS signaling and telomere immortalization. TERT-mutated PDTCs developed more distant metastases (56% vs 20%, P=0.01). The clonality distinction between PTC (subclonal) and PDTC/ATC (clonal) indicates progressive selection for TERT mutations as an essential step in advanced thyroid tumorigenesis.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, BRAF, NRAS · Thyroid (PDTC, ATC, PTC)

### F2: BRAF^V600E^ and RAS mutations define biologically and clinically distinct PDTC subtypes

**Finding:** BRAF^V600E^ mutations occur in 33% PDTC / 45% ATC; RAS mutations in 28% PDTC / 24% ATC. In PDTCs, BRAF mutations associate with the MSKCC histological definition (high mitotic rate/necrosis), nodal metastases, and female predominance; RAS mutations associate with the Turin definition (insular/trabecular/solid pattern), distant metastases, and larger tumor size. BRAF-like transcriptional output (BRS) tracks driver mutation in PDTC but not in ATC (all ATCs are BRAF-like regardless of driver).

**Detail:** 92% of RAS mutations in PDTCs fulfilled the Turin criteria, while 81% of BRAF mutations met only MSKCC criteria. Gene fusions (RET/PTC, PAX8-PPARG, ALK) were found in 14% of PDTCs but were absent in all ATCs, occurring exclusively in younger patients (median 49 vs 58 years). NF1 mutations were restricted to BRAF/RAS-WT ATCs. The loss of BRS discrimination in ATCs reflects increased genomic complexity and possibly the contribution of chromatin-modifying mutations.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRAF, NRAS, HRAS, KRAS, NF1, RET, ALK · Thyroid (PDTC, ATC)

### F3: EIF1AX mutations are enriched in advanced thyroid cancer and strongly co-occur with RAS

**Finding:** EIF1AX mutations, found in only 1% of PTCs (TCGA), are present in 11% of PDTCs and 9% of ATCs. Crucially, 14/15 EIF1AX mutations in advanced thyroid cancer co-occur with RAS mutations (P<1×10⁻⁴), in sharp contrast to PTCs where EIF1AX and RAS are nearly mutually exclusive. EIF1AX mutations predict shorter survival in PDTCs (log-rank P=0.048) and are absent from PI3K/AKT-altered tumors.

**Detail:** EIF1AX encodes a component of the translation pre-initiation complex (PIC). Mutations cluster at N-terminal and C-terminal residues; the thyroid-specific p.A113splice mutation (exon 5/6 splice site) predicts a 12-amino acid in-frame deletion. Mutual exclusivity with PI3K/AKT pathway alterations suggests overlapping oncogenic functions. EIF1AX-RAS co-mutation may represent a distinct oncogenic cooperation not seen in less advanced disease, making it a potentially useful stratification marker for high-risk PDTCs.

### F4: TP53 mutation and SWI/SNF/HMT disruption define the ATC genomic landscape

**Finding:** TP53 mutations are found in 73% of ATCs but only 8% of PDTCs (P<1×10⁻⁴), making TP53 inactivation a key distinguishing event in ATC biology. SWI/SNF complex genes (ARID1A, ARID1B, ARID2, ARID5B, SMARCB1, PBRM1, ATRX) are mutated in 36% of ATCs vs 6% of PDTCs (P<1×10⁻⁴). Histone methyltransferases (KMT2A, KMT2C, KMT2D, SETD2) are mutated in 24% of ATCs vs 7% of PDTCs (P=0.02). PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway alterations affect 39% of ATCs vs 11% of PDTCs (P=0.001).

**Detail:** SWI/SNF mutations within individual tumors show mutual exclusivity (consistent with disruption of one subunit being sufficient), except for a highly heterogeneous ATC with concurrent subclonal ARID1A/ARID1B/SMARCB1 mutations. PIK3CA helical domain mutations (E542K/E545K) are exclusive to ATCs, whereas the kinase domain mutation (H1047R) was found in a PDTC. MMR gene alterations (MSH2, MSH6, MLH1) occur in 12% of ATCs vs 2% of PDTCs, with associated hypermutator phenotypes (median 16.5 vs 5 mutations in MMR-mutant vs WT ATCs). This finding — that SWI/SNF and HMT mutations are prominent in ATC — has not been previously reported in thyroid cancer.

### F5: Transcriptomic profiling reveals universal loss of thyroid differentiation and M2-macrophage infiltration in ATCs

**Finding:** PCA of mRNA expression cleanly separates PDTCs from ATCs. The 16-gene Thyroid Differentiation Score (TDS — NIS/SLC5A5, TG, TSHR, TPO, PAX8, etc.) is markedly suppressed in ATCs vs PDTCs, which resemble PTCs. A 68-gene M2-macrophage signature cleanly separates ATCs (heavily infiltrated) from PDTCs. BRAF-like transcriptional output (BRS) is maintained in PDTCs but lost in ATCs — even RAS-mutant ATCs are BRAF-like — reflecting profound dedifferentiation.

**Detail:** ATCs have severely suppressed mRNA levels for TG, TSHR, TPO, PAX8, SLC26A4, DIO1, and DUOX2 — genes essential for radioiodine incorporation — explaining their broad refractoriness to radioiodine therapy. TDS and BRS are strongly correlated in PTCs (r=0.74) and PDTCs (r=0.72) but not in ATCs (r=−0.43), where the link between driver mutation and differentiation state is lost. M2-macrophage infiltration in ATCs is consistent with their low tumor purity (median 42% vs 72% for PDTCs) and explains the need for deeper sequencing coverage.

**Genes / cancer types:** SLC5A5, TG, TSHR, TPO, PAX8, BRAF, NRAS · Thyroid (ATC, PDTC)

## Open Questions

- What is the biological mechanism of EIF1AX–RAS co-driver cooperativity in advanced thyroid cancer, and why is this combination absent from PTCs?
- Can SWI/SNF or HMT disruption be therapeutically exploited (e.g., via EZH2 inhibitors or BET bromodomain inhibitors) in ATC?
- Does M2-macrophage infiltration in ATC represent a therapeutic vulnerability for macrophage repolarization or CSF1R inhibition?
- Do TERT promoter mutations arise in the ATC component when PDTCs dedifferentiate, or do they represent a separate preexisting clone?
- Can the BRAF-RAS score or TDS computed from pre-treatment biopsies stratify PDTCs for intensity of follow-up or therapeutic escalation?

## Limitations

- Targeted panel sequencing (341 genes) cannot detect all driver alterations; genes not on the panel (e.g., RASAL1, MLH3) will be missed
- Whole-exome sequencing of ATCs is challenged by low tumor purity (median 42%); targeted deep sequencing (avg 739×) partially compensates but subclonal events may still be missed
- mRNA expression data available for only 37 of 117 tumors (fresh-frozen subset only), limiting transcriptomic findings
- No matched germline sequencing is described; some variants in tumor-only cases may represent germline polymorphisms
- Small ATC cohort (n=33) limits statistical power for less frequent genetic events
