## Summary

This TCGA study provides the most comprehensive multi-platform molecular characterization of invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC) to date, profiling 817 breast tumors (127 ILC, 490 IDC, 88 mixed) across whole-exome sequencing, RNA-seq, DNA methylation arrays, SNP arrays, and RPPA. Beyond confirming CDH1 loss as the defining ILC hallmark, the study identifies PTEN loss, AKT hyperactivation (highest among all breast cancer subtypes), and spatially clustered FOXA1 mutations as ILC-enriched features. RNA-based clustering defines three ILC transcriptional subtypes — reactive-like, immune-related, and proliferative — with distinct survival outcomes. Mixed IDC/ILC tumors resolve into molecularly pure ILC-like or IDC-like groups with no true hybrid features, with implications for treatment selection.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: CDH1 loss is the universal ILC hallmark confirmed across all molecular layers
- **Finding**: CDH1 mutations were found in 63% of ILC vs. 2% of IDC (q=3.94E-53), with most being truncating. CDH1 loss was confirmed at the DNA, mRNA, and protein levels in nearly all ILC cases (~90%).
- **Detail**: 12 of 27 CDH1 mutations in non-ILC cases occurred in mixed tumors that molecularly resembled ILC, reinforcing CDH1 as the canonical ILC driver. The study also showed that CDH1 promoter DNA methylation is NOT a mechanism of E-cadherin silencing in breast cancer, contradicting prior literature based on non-quantitative assays.
- **Genes**: CDH1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: somatic mutation, tumor suppressor, cell adhesion, ILC hallmark, epigenetic silencing (refuted)

### Finding 2: PTEN loss and PI3K/AKT hyperactivation are ILC-enriched and clinically actionable
- **Finding**: ILC tumors show significantly lower PTEN protein expression (p=4E-4) and the highest levels of phospho-AKT (at S473 and T308) among all breast cancer subtypes — comparable to Basal-like and HER2+ IDC.
- **Detail**: Upstream AKT activation signals (phospho-EGFR at Y1068/Y1173, phospho-STAT3 at Y705) are upregulated in LumA ILC vs. LumA IDC. PI3K/AKT pathway signature scores in LumA ILC were equivalent to those in the more aggressive Basal-like and HER2+ subtypes. E-cadherin loss itself may create a permissive cellular context for AKT activation. PI3K/AKT inhibitors entering clinical trials are highlighted as particularly relevant for ILC.
- **Genes**: PTEN, AKT1, PIK3CA, EGFR, STAT3, MTOR, RPS6KB1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway, PTEN loss, phosphoproteomics, RPPA, targeted therapy

### Finding 3: FOXA1 mutations are spatially clustered and ILC-enriched, activating ER transcriptional programs
- **Finding**: FOXA1 mutations occurred in 7% of ILC vs. 2% of IDC (q=0.08) and clustered in the W2 wing of the forkhead domain near acetylation sites. FOXA1 mutations correlated with increased FOXA1 mRNA expression and activity, and were mutually exclusive with GATA3 mutations.
- **Detail**: The clustering near EP300-dependent acetylation sites (K264, K267, K270) led to the hypothesis that these mutations impair FOXA1 acetylation without disrupting ER binding, thereby constitutively activating the FOXA1–ER transcriptional axis. This contrasts with IDC where GATA3 mutations and higher GATA3 expression are more prevalent, implying differential ER cofactor usage between ILC and IDC.
- **Genes**: FOXA1, GATA3, EP300
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: transcription factor mutation, ER activity, pioneer factor, mutational clustering, mutual exclusivity

### Finding 4: Three ILC transcriptional subtypes with distinct biology and survival outcomes
- **Finding**: Unsupervised clustering of 1,000 most differentially expressed genes in ILC identified three subtypes: reactive-like, immune-related, and proliferative. Reactive-like patients had significantly better disease-specific (p=0.038, HR=0.47) and overall survival (p=0.023, HR=0.50) compared to proliferative patients (validated in METABRIC, median follow-up 7.2 years).
- **Detail**: The immune-related subgroup showed increased phospho-mTOR and phospho-MEK1. The reactive-like subgroup showed increased SMO and ERK pathway activity. Higher PAM50 proliferation scores correlated with worse disease-specific survival (p=0.025, HR=2.0). A ClaNC-based classifier was built to assign new samples to these subtypes.
- **Genes**: SMO, MTOR, MAP2K1, ERK2
- **Data availability**: partial
- **Tool support**: custom
- **Concepts**: transcriptional subtypes, survival, tumor microenvironment, proliferation, immune infiltration

### Finding 5: Mixed IDC/ILC tumors resolve into ILC-like or IDC-like with no true hybrid molecular identity
- **Finding**: 88 histologically mixed IDC/ILC tumors were classified as either ILC-like or IDC-like based on molecular profiles, with no cases showing a true intermediate or hybrid molecular phenotype.
- **Detail**: ILC-like mixed tumors carried CDH1 mutations and showed PTEN loss, AKT activation, and FOXA1-enriched mutations similar to pure ILC. IDC-like mixed cases resembled IDC at the molecular level including higher GATA3 mutations and TP53 alterations. This classification has implications for clinical management, particularly for ILC's unique metastatic patterns (GI tract, peritoneal surfaces) and differential response to aromatase inhibitors vs. tamoxifen.
- **Genes**: CDH1, PTEN, GATA3, TP53, FOXA1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: histologic classification, molecular reclassification, mixed tumors, clinical decision support

### Finding 6: ILC is genetically distinct from IDC beyond CDH1 — multiple ILC-enriched and ILC-depleted alterations
- **Finding**: Among 178 total recurrent alterations across 817 tumors, ILC was enriched for mutations in TBX3 (9% vs. 2%, q=0.003), RUNX1 (10% vs. 3%, q=0.008), and PIK3CA (48% vs. 33%, q=0.02), and depleted for TP53 mutations (8% vs. 44%, q=1.9E-14) and MYC focal amplification (6% vs. 27%, q=7.42E-7).
- **Detail**: The low TP53 mutation rate and MYC amplification in ILC partly reflect the LumA predominance, but ILC also showed lower GATA3 mutations (5% vs. 13%, q=0.03) than expected even within LumA tumors, indicating ILC-specific biology beyond PAM50 subtype effects. CCNE1 amplification was essentially absent in ILC (0% vs. 7%).
- **Genes**: TBX3, RUNX1, PIK3CA, TP53, MYC, GATA3, CCNE1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: native
- **Concepts**: somatic mutation enrichment, copy number alteration, comparative genomics, ILC vs IDC

### Finding 7: CDH1 promoter methylation does NOT explain E-cadherin loss in ILC
- **Finding**: Comprehensive analysis of CDH1 promoter CpGs using HM27, HM450 arrays, and whole-genome bisulfite sequencing (5 samples) found no evidence of significant DNA hypermethylation at the CDH1 promoter in any breast tumor.
- **Detail**: Moderate methylation observed near exon 2 correlated with lower tumor purity and leukocyte infiltration, mirroring methylation in normal leukocytes. This refutes prior reports relying on methylation-specific PCR, a sensitive but non-quantitative assay. CDH1 loss in ILC is therefore driven by somatic mutation and/or other non-epigenetic mechanisms, not promoter silencing.
- **Genes**: CDH1
- **Data availability**: available
- **Tool support**: api
- **Concepts**: DNA methylation, epigenetic silencing, CpG island, bisulfite sequencing, WGBS

## Open Questions

1. Does the improved clinical response of ILC to aromatase inhibitors (letrozole) vs. tamoxifen relate to differential FOXA1/GATA3-mediated ER occupancy, and can FOXA1 mutation status guide endocrine therapy selection?
2. Can PI3K/AKT inhibitors produce better responses in ILC than in IDC given ILC's uniformly high Akt activation, and do PTEN loss and CDH1 status predict sensitivity?
3. Do the three ILC transcriptional subtypes (reactive-like, immune-related, proliferative) predict differential response to specific systemic therapies (e.g., immunotherapy for immune-related, anti-proliferative agents for proliferative subgroup)?
4. What non-epigenetic, non-mutational mechanisms drive CDH1 loss in ILC cases lacking CDH1 mutations (~37%)?

## Limitations

1. **Short follow-up in TCGA**: Median follow-up was less than 2 years, severely limiting survival analyses; survival findings required the METABRIC cohort (median 7.2 years) for validation.
2. **Incomplete RPPA coverage**: Proteomic (RPPA) data were available for only 633 of 817 samples, potentially introducing selection bias in protein-level findings.
3. **ILC transcriptional subtypes unvalidated prospectively**: The three ILC subtypes were derived and partially validated in METABRIC but require prospective validation and functional characterization; no ILC-specific clinical trial has stratified by these subtypes.
4. **Bisulfite sequencing validation limited**: CDH1 methylation refutation rests on WGBS of only 5 samples, though array data across all 817 tumors were consistent.
