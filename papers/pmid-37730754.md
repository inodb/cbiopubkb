## Summary

This pilot study investigates the genomic evolution of rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS) from primary to relapse in 35 patients (18 fusion-positive FP-RMS, 17 fusion-negative FN-RMS) from MSKCC and Institut Curie, using a multisample/multiplatform approach combining MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing, whole-exome sequencing (WES), and circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) analysis across multiple clinical timepoints. FP-RMS shows a remarkably stable genome at relapse, with major secondary alterations (*CDKN2A/B*, *MYCN*, *CDK4*) already present at diagnosis carrying significant prognostic impact. FN-RMS acquires substantially more new alterations at relapse (average +4.3 per patient), with *SMARCA2* missense mutations as the most common newly acquired event. A custom 36-gene RMS ctDNA panel detects pathognomonic variants in virtually all patients at diagnosis and 86–100% at relapse, with fusion read counts quantitatively correlating with disease burden. This study provides the foundation for integrating liquid biopsy into future RMS clinical trials for real-time disease monitoring.

## Key Findings

### F1: FP-RMS shows a genomically stable relapse landscape; secondary alterations CDKN2A/B, MYCN, and CDK4 present at diagnosis predict worse survival

**Finding:** Among 18 FP-RMS patients, the genomic landscape is largely stable from primary to relapse — half of patients acquire ≤1 additional alteration. Recurrent secondary alterations present at diagnosis include *CDKN2A/B* (28%, 5/18; 4 homozygous deletions), *MYCN* (22%, 4/18; 2 amplifications, 2 missense), and *CDK4* (17%, 3/18; all amplifications). *MYCN* alterations (P=0.0012), *CDKN2A* deletions (P=0.049), and their combination (P=0.0017) are significantly associated with worse overall survival.

**Detail:** FP-RMS is defined by *PAX3::FOXO1* (n=16) or *PAX7::FOXO1* (n=2) fusions; these fusions alone are often the only initial driver, with the secondary major alterations acting as co-drivers that accelerate disease. The 10 patients with major secondary alterations at diagnosis acquired zero or very few additional events at relapse, while 8 patients without major secondary events at diagnosis subsequently acquired new alterations at relapse. Two patients in the "no secondary alteration" group acquired CDK4 amplification or MYCN alterations at relapse. PAX7::FOXO1 cases were both associated with FOXO1 amplification. CDK4 amplifications co-occurred with GLI1 (2 cases) and MDM2 (1 case) at the 12q13-15 locus.

### F2: FN-RMS acquires significantly more alterations at relapse; SMARCA2 missense mutations are the most common newly acquired event

**Finding:** FN-RMS shows markedly higher genomic instability at relapse compared to FP-RMS: an average of 4.3 newly acquired alterations per patient (range 0–17) with a 111% overall increase in alteration count from diagnosis to relapse. *SMARCA2* missense mutations and frameshift deletions (4/17 patients, 24%) are exclusively present at relapse — the most common newly acquired event. *TP53* (41%), *BCOR* (30%), *PIK3CA* pathway genes (30%), and *MDM2* (24%) are also recurrently altered.

**Detail:** SMARCA2 (encoding BRM, the ATPase catalytic subunit of the SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex) mutations were found at VAF <50%, suggesting subclonal acquisition. Four different SMARCA2 point mutations were identified with no redundancy in protein domain. IHC in two cases with available material showed complete SMARCA2 protein loss (BRM) in one and partial loss in the second, confirming functional impact. No other SWI/SNF complex mutations or associated CNAs were found, indicating SMARCA2 as the primary target. *BAP1* deletions (2/17) also exclusively appeared at relapse. DNA repair gene alterations (*RAD51B*, *RAD21*, *ATR*, *ATM*, *FANCA*, *FANCC*) were exclusively present at relapse in 3 cases with high mutational counts. FN-RMS had significantly higher average mutations per sample than FP-RMS (5.6 vs. 3.2; P=0.002).

**Genes / cancer types:** `SMARCA2`, `TP53`, `BCOR`, `HRAS`, `NRAS`, `KRAS`, `PIK3CA`, `MDM2`, `BAP1` · Rhabdomyosarcoma (FN-RMS)

### F3: ctDNA detects pathognomonic RMS variants at diagnosis in 88% and at relapse in 90% of cases using a multi-analyte approach

**Finding:** In 10 patients with ctDNA analysis, a combined approach (36-gene targeted panel for SNV/fusions + shallow WGS for CNV) detects at least one variant in 88% of cases at diagnosis and 90% at relapse. PAX3::FOXO1 fusion is detected in 100% of FP-RMS at diagnosis (6/6) and 86% at relapse (6/7). FN-RMS ctDNA detection is 100% at diagnosis and relapse. Blood plasma is more sensitive than bone marrow plasma (86% vs. 61% at active disease timepoints).

**Detail:** The multi-analyte strategy is critical: fusion detection alone achieves 100% sensitivity for FP-RMS at diagnosis; CNV detection (CDK4 gain/amplification, MYCN amplification, CDKN2A/B deletion) adds sensitivity for secondary alterations. For FP-RMS over all active disease timepoints: PAX3::FOXO1 fusion 83% (33/40 timepoints), SNV 67% (18/27), CNV 66% (29/44). Unique molecular identifiers (UMI) enable quantitative fusion read counting, which correlates with disease burden and clinical trajectory — fusion reads increase at relapse and decrease during treatment response. CNV profiles from shallow WGS (4×) recapitulate primary tumor CNA profiles. No false positives were detected in 5 patients without active disease.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PAX3`, `PAX7`, `FOXO1`, `MYCN`, `CDK4`, `CDKN2A`, `RB1`, `PIK3CA`, `BCOR`, `HRAS`, `MDM2` · Rhabdomyosarcoma

### F4: Fusion read count by quantitative ctDNA correlates with disease burden and predicts clinical outcome in FP-RMS

**Finding:** Quantitative fusion read counts (UMI-based) in plasma correlate directly with clinical disease status across serial timepoints in all 7 FP-RMS patients with ctDNA data. Fusion reads increase at relapse and in patients with fatal outcomes; they decrease during treatment response and are lowest during follow-up remission periods. Higher fusion reads at any given timepoint correlate with higher disease burden, providing a real-time tumor burden surrogate.

**Detail:** Across 7 FP-RMS patients with ctDNA tracking (median 6 timepoints, range 2–9), PAX3::FOXO1 fusion read numbers were significantly higher in deceased patients compared to survivors (box-plot comparison). In one illustrative patient, fusion reads decrease during first-line chemotherapy, gradually increase as treatment resistance develops, and show major escalation at the palliative care stage. SNV VAF fluctuations (in 2 patients with sufficient data) mirror the same pattern — decreasing during remission, increasing at relapse. CNV profile from shallow WGS recapitulates primary tumor CNA pattern at matched blood timepoints. These quantitative dynamics support integration of liquid biopsy into RMS clinical trials as a treatment response monitoring tool.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PAX3`, `PAX7`, `FOXO1` · Rhabdomyosarcoma (FP-RMS)

**Data availability:** `not-available`

**Tool support:** `custom`

### F5: Defective DNA mismatch repair signature appears exclusively in relapsed FP-RMS; whole-chromosome gains are common in FN-RMS

**Finding:** De novo mutational signature analysis (COSMIC SBS comparison) identifies a defective DNA mismatch repair (MMR) SBS signature exclusively in recurrent (not primary) FP-RMS tumors, suggesting acquisition of MMR deficiency as a relapse mechanism. In contrast, the MMR SBS signature is present in both primary and recurrent FN-RMS. Whole-chromosome gains (chr2, chr5, chr8, chr12, chr14) are recurrently identified in FN-RMS and are stable between primary and relapse.

**Detail:** The presence of an MMR-defective signature exclusively at FP-RMS relapse (not at diagnosis) suggests that treatment-associated mutagenesis (from alkylating chemotherapy, e.g., ifosfamide/cyclophosphamide) may drive MMR deficiency acquisition in a subset of relapsed FP-RMS — analogous to the temozolomide-driven MMR hypermutator phenotype in GBM. This could confer susceptibility to checkpoint inhibitor therapy. FN-RMS whole-chromosome gains (chr2, chr5, chr8, chr12, chr14) are consistent between primary and relapsed tumors, suggesting these are early events fixed in the clone, not dynamically acquired. All chromosomal gains were identified from MSK-IMPACT CNV profiles in the 10 MSKCC FN-RMS.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MLH1`, `MSH2`, `MSH6`, `PMS2` (inferred from signature) · Rhabdomyosarcoma (FP-RMS relapse; FN-RMS)

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Copy number alteration data (including whole-chromosome gains) available in soft_tissue_msk_2023. Mutational signature data is not directly available in cBioPortal but SNV counts and mutation spectra can be exported via API for signature analysis.

## Open Questions

- Does SMARCA2 loss in relapsed FN-RMS confer synthetic lethality with SMARCA4 (which is not expressed in some FN-RMS), making EZH2 inhibitors or other SWI/SNF-deficiency therapeutics applicable?
- Can the quantitative PAX3::FOXO1 ctDNA fusion read count be validated prospectively as a pharmacodynamic biomarker in RMS clinical trials?
- Does the MMR-defective signature in relapsed FP-RMS predict response to PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibitors, and does treatment with alkylating agents (ifosfamide/cyclophosphamide) drive MMR mutation acquisition?
- Are the whole-chromosome gains in FN-RMS (chr2, 5, 8, 12, 14) driver events or passengers? Do they associate with specific outcome subgroups?
- Would WGS of FP-RMS reveal structural rearrangements (beyond PAX3/7::FOXO1) that explain the subset without secondary alterations but with poor outcomes?
- Can MYCN and CDK4 amplifications in FP-RMS be targeted with CDK4/6 inhibitors (e.g., palbociclib) or BET inhibitors in future clinical trials?

## Limitations

- Small pilot cohort (n=35 total; n=10 for ctDNA); severely underpowered for definitive statistical conclusions, especially for FN-RMS survival associations
- Heterogeneous sequencing platforms (MSK-IMPACT vs. WES vs. Dragon panel) across institutions limits direct cross-platform mutation rate comparisons
- ctDNA analysis performed retrospectively on archival plasma; prospective collection with standardized protocols not yet implemented
- Bone marrow aspirate plasma was less sensitive than blood plasma (61% vs. 86%), complicating interpretation of bone marrow-based ctDNA monitoring
- Mutational signature analysis performed only on MSKCC MSK-IMPACT cohort (limited SNV count for panel-based signatures); WES from Institut Curie not included in signature analysis
- No WGS available; structural variants beyond the targeted panel (e.g., complex rearrangements, inversions) not captured
- All patients received multimodal chemotherapy (VIA ± etoposide/irinotecan/cyclophosphamide) — treatment heterogeneity limits survival analysis
- SMARCA2 mutations are subclonal (VAF <50%) at relapse; functional significance and clonal dynamics not fully resolved without single-cell or longitudinal deep sequencing
