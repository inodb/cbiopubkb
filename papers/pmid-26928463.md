## Summary

A rapid autopsy study of 176 primary and metastatic tumors from 63 men with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC), profiled by whole exome sequencing (WES, n=141 tumors/56 men), array CGH (n=149/60), and transcript microarray (n=171/63). Despite extensive inter-patient molecular diversity, genomic alterations — including AR amplification/mutation, TMPRSS2-ERG fusion, TP53 mutation, CNA burden, AR activity, and cell cycle output — are highly concordant across spatially distinct metastases within the same individual. AR activity is paradoxically inversely correlated with cell cycle progression (CCP), while Fanconi anemia (FA) complex gene expression co-segregates with high CCP, E2F1 activation, and RB1 loss. Men with somatic aberrations in FA complex genes or ATM show significantly longer treatment response durations to carboplatin chemotherapy, providing a clinically actionable biomarker for platinum sensitivity in mCRPC.

## Key Findings

### F1: Substantial interindividual but limited intraindividual genomic diversity in mCRPC metastases

**Finding:** Across 38 patients with multiple sequenced tumors, the median percentage of CNA regions differing between tumors within an individual was 6.7% compared to 22.1% between individuals from different patients (median inter- vs. intra-individual difference 15.7%, 95% CI 15.5–15.9%, p<0.001). Unsupervised CNA clustering grouped tumors by patient of origin with near 100% accuracy. Mutation clustering also grouped tumors by patient. Where primary tumors were available, they clustered with the patient's metastases — not with primaries from other men.

**Detail:** Integrated analysis incorporating CNAs, mutations, and gene expression confirmed that all tumors from one individual cluster together and are distinct from tumors of other men. TMPRSS2-ERG status was 100% concordant across all metastases in 13 of 13 men evaluated by FISH; 32/34 men concordant by array CGH. Of 51 genes with metastasis-private NSVs in one patient (99-091), only 2 (CSMD1, NCOR2) were altered at frequencies >5% in TCGA or SU2C datasets — confirming that most private mutations are not oncogenic drivers. The limited intraindividual diversity supports the clinical utility of single-biopsy molecular profiling to guide therapy selection in mCRPC.

### F2: AR amplification/mutation in 63% of mCRPC men; AR activity maintained in 88% despite prior castration

**Finding:** AR genomic aberrations (amplification or mutation) occurred in 63% of mCRPC men — sharply contrasting with their rarity in untreated primary prostate cancer (Fisher's exact p<0.0001). Using a 21-gene AR transcriptional output signature, 88% of patients had tumors with robust AR activity, demonstrating near-universal AR pathway reactivation in castration-resistant disease. AR activity and AR expression were highly concordant within individuals but diverged substantially between patients.

**Detail:** AR amplification/mutation was concordant within individuals: all tumors had no AR aberration in 8 men; all tumors had AR copy gain or mutation in 24 men; 9 men showed discordance. One man showed convergent evolution — some metastases with AR mutation, others with AR amplification. In 15 tumors with high AR activity but low AR expression, alternative nuclear hormone receptors GR (NR3C1) and ESR1 were expressed, potentially activating canonical AR target genes. Two men with confirmed small cell neuroendocrine carcinoma had concordant high NE activity and low AR activity across all metastases. FOXA2 mutations identified as recurrent in NE-histology tumors.

### F3: AR activity is inversely correlated with cell cycle progression — a counterintuitive finding with therapeutic implications

**Finding:** AR activity (21-gene signature) is inversely correlated with cell cycle progression (CCP; 31-gene signature, r=−0.33, p<0.001), confirmed in an independent mCRPC dataset (Grasso et al., r=−0.42, p=0.01). AR activity also inversely correlates with E2F1 expression (r=−0.43, p<0.001). In vitro, LNCaP cells with overexpressed AR (LNCaP-AR) showed suppressed proliferation at high androgen concentrations but increased proliferation at castrate-level androgen concentrations — consistent with the paradoxical anti-proliferative activity of AR at physiological androgen levels.

**Detail:** CCP score (31 genes) was validated against Ki67 IHC (r=0.48, p<0.005, n=36 matched tumors). CCP scores were concordant within individuals. The inverse AR-CCP correlation was maintained after removing highly proliferative NE metastases. PC3 (AR-null) cells introduced with AR and exposed to 10−9 M R1881 showed significantly inhibited growth (p=0.02). This suggests that AR, at full androgen stimulation, can act as a growth suppressor in some contexts — and that castrate conditions may unmask an AR-driven proliferative phenotype (paradox of castration therapy). The finding has implications for understanding how AR activity score and CCP score together predict treatment responsiveness.

### F4: Fanconi anemia complex gene expression correlates with high cell cycle progression, E2F1 activation, and RB1 loss

**Finding:** Genome-wide correlation of gene expression with CCP score identified Fanconi anemia (FA) complex components — FANCA, FANCI, FANCD2, BRCA1, BRCA2 — as the most consistently co-elevated with high-CCP tumors. FA gene expression correlates with E2F1 (r=0.78, p<0.001 for 15-gene FA signature) and RB1 loss (p<0.01). siRNA knockdown of individual FA genes (FANCA, FANCI, FANCD2, BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, SLX4) significantly reduces proliferation in LNCaP, 22Rv1, VCaP, and PC3 cells and sensitizes them to carboplatin-induced γ-H2AX (DNA double strand breaks).

**Detail:** FA pathway genes are expressed at low levels in untreated primary PCs (low CCP); elevation in CRPC is associated with high proliferative states and RB1 loss. Subset of FA genes are E2F1 transcriptional targets, providing a mechanistic link: RB1 loss → E2F1 derepression → FA gene upregulation → replication stress accommodation → high proliferation. FA knockdown + carboplatin: all tested FA genes (except SLX4) showed significantly greater γ-H2AX vs. scrambled siRNA controls. This functional link between FA pathway activity and carboplatin sensitivity supports testing FA pathway status as a predictive biomarker for platinum chemotherapy.

### F5: Somatic FA complex or ATM aberrations predict significantly longer carboplatin treatment response in mCRPC

**Finding:** Among 21 men who received carboplatin chemotherapy, those with a somatic aberration in a DNA repair pathway gene (homozygous deleterious FA pathway event or heterozygous ATM inactivation) had significantly longer treatment response durations (time on drug; log-rank p=0.02). This mirrors the established role of BRCA/ATM aberrations in predicting PARP inhibitor sensitivity and extends it to platinum chemotherapy in mCRPC.

**Detail:** Definition of actionable DDR aberration: homozygous deleterious event in any FA pathway component (BRCA2, PALB2, etc.) OR heterozygous inactivating ATM event — events previously linked to olaparib response in mCRPC (SU2C/PCF dataset). 20 of 63 men received carboplatin; 21 available for biomarker analysis. KM: men with DDR aberrations had significantly longer time on carboplatin (p=0.02). The concordance of DDR aberration status across metastases within an individual (consistent with early clonal events) means single-biopsy assessment is sufficient to identify the biomarker. Note: this study precedes FDA approval of olaparib and rucaparib for BRCA-mutant mCRPC; the finding anticipated later clinical validation of DDR as a biomarker class.

## Open Questions

- Do the intraindividual concordance findings hold in patients treated with potent AR pathway inhibitors (abiraterone, enzalutamide) where AR splice variants (e.g., AR-V7) may arise heterogeneously across metastases?
- Can the FA complex gene expression signature from transcript profiling be translated into a clinically applicable assay for carboplatin/PARP inhibitor patient selection?
- Does the inverse AR-CCP correlation explain why patients with very high AR activity on AR pathway inhibitor therapy have lower proliferation (and potentially better prognosis), while castration-driven AR hyperactivation at low androgen promotes proliferation?
- Do neuroendocrine prostate cancers (NEPC), which uniformly co-cluster per patient, arise via convergent lineage plasticity or clonal divergence — and can single-site biopsy capture the AR-to-NE transition?
- Do the 5% of patients with MSH2/MSH6 mismatch repair deficiency and hypermutated genomes respond to PD-1 checkpoint inhibitors, as observed in other MMR-deficient cancers?
- Should RB1 loss be incorporated into a composite FA/DDR/cell-cycle biomarker score to better stratify carboplatin or PARP inhibitor candidate patients?

## Limitations

- Rapid autopsy cohort: all patients are end-stage mCRPC; findings may not generalize to earlier metastatic disease or patients who have not received extensive prior therapy
- Only 3 men received modern potent AR pathway antagonists (abiraterone or enzalutamide) — concordance findings may not extend to patients under evolutionary pressure from these agents
- Transcript profiling by microarray (not RNA-seq) limits splice variant detection (e.g., AR-V7) and absolute expression precision
- Carboplatin response analysis limited to 20–21 men — underpowered for definitive predictive biomarker validation; observational only
- CNA profiling by array CGH, not WGS — structural variants and complex genomic rearrangements not captured at full resolution
- Bone metastasis transcript profiles may be confounded by low tumor purity and technical processing differences (decalcification)
- DDR biomarker definition (homozygous FA loss or heterozygous ATM loss) requires refinement for prospective use — germline vs. somatic origin distinction affects clinical risk assessment
