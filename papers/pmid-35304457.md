## Summary

Translational genomics study characterizing molecular determinants of response and resistance to selpercatinib (selective RET inhibitor) in 72 patients with RET-driven cancers treated on the LIBRETTO-001 phase 1/2 trial at MSK. Pre-treatment genomics does not shape initial treatment response (except rare RAS-mediated primary resistance), while acquired resistance is driven by MAPK pathway reactivation via two distinct routes: (1) on-target secondary RET solvent front mutations (G810C, G810S) or off-target MET amplifications, and (2) selection of pre-existing RET-wild-type tumor cell populations driven by alternative mitogenic drivers (KRAS, NRAS, BRAF). Multiple mechanisms often co-exist polyclonally in the same patient. Results demonstrate the intrinsic MAPK dependence of RET-altered cancers and argue against simple sequential RET-directed therapy at progression.

## Key Findings

### F1: Pre-treatment genomic landscape does not predict selpercatinib response — except TP53 co-mutation (shorter PFS) and rare RAS primary resistance

**Finding:** Comprehensive pre-treatment sequencing revealed no consistent genomic predictor of differential response to selpercatinib, with one exception: concurrent TP53 mutations (n=8, all with RET fusions) were associated with significantly shorter median PFS (HR=3.5, 95% CI 1.3–9.7; p=0.016). Beyond TP53, no pre-treatment co-mutation pattern was associated with differential response. TMB and degree of subclonal heterogeneity were not associated with response depth or durability (p>0.4 for all comparisons).

**Detail:** In RET fusion-positive lung cancers, concurrent MAPK-activating alterations were not observed in tumor tissue, consistent with mutual exclusivity between RET fusions and other mitogenic drivers. In medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) RET-mutant cases, prior MKI therapy led to acquisition of secondary RET-activating mutations at baseline (V804M, G601E, K666E, D898Y) in 4 patients, but all remained sensitive to selpercatinib. PI3K pathway lesions (PTEN loss or PIK3CA mutations; n=11 patients) had a clinical benefit rate of 91% — not a resistance determinant. This result establishes selpercatinib efficacy as largely RET-fusion/mutation-class agnostic at baseline, simplifying patient selection.

### F2: Acquired selpercatinib resistance is driven by MAPK pathway reactivation — on-target RET solvent front mutations or bypass alterations

**Finding:** In 18 evaluable patients who progressed on selpercatinib, a genetically driven resistance mechanism was identified in 11 (61%). On-target resistance: 3 patients developed secondary RET solvent front mutations (G810C or G810S in KIF5B-RET fusion patients), consistent with bulky gatekeeper substitution impairing inhibitor binding. Off-target bypass resistance: 7 patients acquired MAPK-activating alterations including KRAS (G12A/R/V, G13D, A59del), NRAS (G13D, Q61R), BRAF activating mutations, or MET/FGFR1 amplifications.

**Detail:** The G810 solvent front residue is analogous to resistance hotspots in other kinase domains (e.g., ALK G1202R, EGFR T790M steric equivalents). For bypass resistance, the critical pattern is that emerging KRAS/NRAS/BRAF mutations arise in tumor cell populations that were distinct from the RET fusion-positive clone — i.e., pre-existing RET-wild-type cells driven by an alternative mitogenic driver are selected by selpercatinib therapy. The RET fusion allele frequency in plasma at progression was still 85% lower than pre-treatment baseline in one patient while the KRAS mutation grew out, confirming distinct cell populations. One patient with a pre-existing subclonal MET amplification responded to combination selpercatinib + crizotinib, demonstrating on-target re-sensitization.

### F3: Polyclonal resistance is common — multiple distinct resistance mechanisms co-exist in the same patient

**Finding:** Multiple distinct resistance mechanisms were observed in the same patient at progression, indicating polyclonal resistance. In two patients, RET solvent front mutations co-existed with KRAS G12A and G12R mutations that emerged at different time points in plasma, representing distinct resistant subclones. In one patient, a pre-existing subclonal MET amplification was selected by selpercatinib, and subsequent selpercatinib+crizotinib combination therapy revealed an emerging BRAF D594N mutation — a kinase-dead RAS/BRAF dimer-dependent mutant requiring MAPK reactivation.

**Detail:** This polyclonal resistance pattern — multiple simultaneously co-existing and genomically distinct resistance mechanisms — is a critical finding for clinical management. It implies that post-progression biopsies from a single site may miss the full spectrum of resistance alterations, and that sequential monotherapy strategies targeting individual mechanisms are likely inadequate. The BRAF D594N kinase-dead variant is functionally distinct from V600E, requiring RAS-BRAF dimerization for ERK activation, reinforcing the MAPK dependence of RET-driven cancers at every level of therapeutic escape.

### F4: cfDNA plasma profiling detects RET alterations, reveals occult resistant subclones, and tracks molecular response

**Finding:** Baseline plasma cfDNA (MSK-ACCESS) detected the qualifying RET alteration in 95% of RET-mutant (somatic/germline) and 74% of RET fusion-positive patients. Patients with no/low tumor shedding at baseline had lower baseline disease burden (p=0.02) and superior PFS on selpercatinib (HR=3.7, 95% CI 1.1–12.4; p=0.013). Among 23 patients followed longitudinally, the RET mutant allele frequency decreased in all but one patient (median 82.5% decrease), confirming target engagement. Crucially, cfDNA detected occult KRAS mutations not detected by tumor tissue sequencing in 2 primary-resistant patients, revealing inter-tumoral heterogeneity.

**Detail:** The two patients with RAS-mediated primary resistance had KRAS G12D and G12V detected only in plasma at low allele frequencies (0.1–0.8%), consistent with minor subclonal tumor populations shedding into circulation. In both cases, the RET fusion frequency decreased upon treatment (indicating RET-fusion clone sensitivity) while the KRAS allele increased at progression. This demonstrates the superior sensitivity of cfDNA for capturing spatially heterogeneous subclones that tissue biopsies miss — and validates liquid biopsy as an essential tool for resistance mechanism characterization in RET-driven cancers.

### F5: MAPK pathway intrinsic dependence of RET-driven cancers — all resistance converges on RAS/MAPK reactivation, arguing against sequential RET monotherapy

**Finding:** All identified mechanisms of primary and acquired resistance to selpercatinib converge on MAPK pathway reactivation: on-target RET solvent front mutations (maintaining kinase activity), MET/FGFR1 amplifications (activating parallel MAPK/ERK signaling), and KRAS/NRAS/BRAF mutations (directly reactivating ERK). No lineage/histology changes (e.g., small cell transformation) were observed in evaluable cases, contrasting with EGFR inhibitor resistance. No significant clonal evolution was observed in 4 matched pre/post tissue pairs without an identified genetic driver.

**Detail:** This convergence reveals the intrinsic MAPK dependence of RET-driven tumors: RET oncoproteins signal predominantly through RAS-MAPK, and resistance uniformly restores this axis through diverse genetic routes. The implication for clinical trial design is that sequential RET-directed therapy alone is insufficient — combination strategies targeting MAPK effectors (MEK, ERK, or specific bypass nodes such as MET, FGFR1) will be needed. This parallels emerging models for ALK, ROS1, and NTRK-driven cancers where kinase-targeted resistance uniformly involves MAPK pathway escape.

## Open Questions

- Can next-generation RET inhibitors (e.g., pralsetinib combined with MEK inhibitors) overcome concurrent on-target and bypass resistance in polyclonal patients?
- Do patients with low tumor shedding at baseline have superior PFS because of lower metastatic burden, lower tumor aggressiveness, or intrinsic tumor biology differences?
- Is the mutual exclusivity of RET fusions with other MAPK drivers (KRAS, EGFR) maintained in the post-selpercatinib resistance setting, or does treatment reveal co-existing drivers that were previously suppressed?
- What is the optimal timing and frequency of cfDNA monitoring to detect emergent resistance clones before radiographic progression?
- Can combination selpercatinib + MET inhibitor (following MET-amplification-driven resistance) achieve durable responses, or does rapid BRAF/RAS selection limit clinical benefit?
- Does the absence of lineage plasticity in RET-driven resistance (vs. EGFR-driven resistance) reflect a fundamental difference in RET vs. EGFR biology, or simply reflect limited post-progression biopsy availability?

## Limitations

- Single-institution cohort (MSK LIBRETTO-001 site, n=72); broader multi-center resistance characterization needed
- Post-progression biopsy available in only 18/27 progressors (67%); uncharacterized cases may harbor additional resistance mechanisms
- Plasma cfDNA sequencing (MSK-ACCESS: 129 genes) does not capture the full mutational landscape; whole-exome or genome-level cfDNA needed for comprehensive resistance gene discovery
- Non-genetic resistance mechanisms (epigenetic, transcriptional, stromal) not evaluable with this approach
- Small number of non-lung/thyroid RET fusion-positive cancers (5 unique cancer types) limits cancer-type-specific resistance analysis
- WES available for only 44/70 evaluable patients; mutational signature and TMB analyses restricted to this subset
- December 2019 data freeze predates longer follow-up; late-emerging resistance mechanisms in durable responders not captured
- Lineage plasticity evaluated in only 3 evaluable post-progression biopsies of lung cancers without identified genetic driver
