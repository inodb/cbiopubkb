## Summary

This TCGA multi-platform characterization of 66 chromophobe renal cell carcinomas (ChRCC) — using WES (n=66), WGS (n=50), mitochondrial DNA sequencing (n=61), RNA-seq, miRNA-seq, 450K DNA methylation arrays, and SNP copy-number arrays — establishes ChRCC as a molecularly distinct entity from clear-cell RCC (ccRCC) with unique pathogenic mechanisms. Key findings include: (1) Whole-chromosome losses (predominantly chromosomes 1, 2, 6, 10, 13, 17) as the primary CNA landscape, with very low point mutation rate (~0.4/Mb); (2) only *TP53* and *PTEN* reach mutational significance, with mTOR pathway (TP53 + PTEN + MTOR + TSC1/TSC2) altered in 23% of cases; (3) gene expression and DNA methylation profiles confirming ChRCC origin from the distal nephron vs. ccRCC origin from the proximal nephron; (4) mitochondrial DNA mutations — especially in the Complex I gene *MT-ND5* — in 18% of cases, preferentially in the eosinophilic ChRCC subtype, with an unexpected transcriptional signature suggesting maintained oxidative phosphorylation rather than Warburg-effect-like metabolic switch; and (5) genomic structural rearrangements creating breakpoints within the *TERT* promoter in 6/50 WGS cases, producing extremely high TERT expression — a novel mechanism of telomerase activation in cancer distinct from point mutations or gene amplification.

## Key Findings

### F1: Characteristic whole-chromosome losses define ChRCC copy-number landscape with no recurrent focal events

**Finding:** SNP array analysis reveals that loss of one copy of entire chromosomes 1, 2, 6, 10, 13, and 17 occurs in the majority of ChRCC cases (86% of cases show this pattern), with additional significant-frequency losses of chromosomes 3, 5, 8, 9, 11, 18, and 21 (12–58%). GISTIC2.0 identifies no focal copy-number events, distinguishing ChRCC from most other cancers, including ccRCC which has numerous focal CNA events (*VHL*, *PBRM1*, etc.). Classic histology universally shows the characteristic aneuploidy; eosinophilic cases show more heterogeneity (only ~50% with classic pattern, 4 eosinophilic cases with no CNAs at all).

**Detail:** This whole-chromosome aneuploidy pattern was first characterized by comparative genomic hybridization in 1994. The TCGA analysis confirms and extends these findings with high-resolution SNP arrays and demonstrates the absence of focal driver events at the copy-number level — suggesting ChRCC relies primarily on gene mutation (TP53, PTEN) and broad chromosomal imbalances rather than focal amplifications or deletions of individual oncogenes. The chromosomal loss pattern likely reflects an underlying chromosomal segregation defect rather than targeted focal selection. The eosinophilic cases with absent CNAs may represent a biologically distinct entity (possibly more related to renal oncocytoma).

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PTEN` (on chr 17/10 respectively — among chromosomes with frequent losses) · Chromophobe renal cell carcinoma

### F2: TP53 (32%) and PTEN (9%) are the only significantly mutated genes; mTOR pathway altered in 23% overall

**Finding:** WES identifies only 2 significantly mutated genes at MutSig q < 0.1: *TP53* (mutated in 32% — 21/66 cases; mutations correlate with decreased p53 transcriptional target expression) and *PTEN* (9% non-silent mutations; 6/66 cases), with 2 additional *PTEN* homozygous deletions. Beyond these, mutations in *MTOR* (2 cases), *NRAS* (1 activating mutation), and *TSC1* or *TSC2* (4 cases) expand the mTOR pathway to 15/66 (23%) of cases. No other genes exceed 5% mutation frequency. One outlier case with >10/Mb mutation rate shows a DNA mismatch repair deficiency signature.

**Detail:** The low overall mutation rate (~0.4/Mb, ~3-fold lower than ccRCC and ~10-fold lower than tobacco-driven carcinomas) means the study was statistically underpowered to identify genes mutated below ~10% frequency. The mTOR pathway aggregate (TP53 pathway + PTEN/mTOR/TSC1/TSC2) leaves ~47% of ChRCC cases without clear pathway-level mutation drivers, necessitating extension to mtDNA and structural variant analysis. MTOR mutations in ChRCC are actionable — mTOR inhibitors (everolimus, temsirolimus) are approved for other RCC subtypes, though ChRCC-specific clinical trials are lacking. TSC1/TSC2 mutations (Birt-Hogg-Dubé/Cowden syndrome genes) in sporadic disease extend the germline-to-somatic spectrum of ChRCC drivers.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PTEN`, `MTOR`, `TSC1`, `TSC2`, `NRAS` · Chromophobe renal cell carcinoma

### F3: ChRCC cell of origin is the distal nephron — established by gene expression and DNA methylation correlation with nephron atlas

**Finding:** Supervised analysis comparing gene expression profiles of 66 ChRCC and 417 ccRCC tumors to an external atlas of gene expression from microdissected normal nephron segments shows high correlation of ChRCC with distal nephron (connecting tubule, cortical/outer medullary collecting duct, distal convoluted tubule), while ccRCC correlates with proximal nephron segments (S1/S2 proximal tubule, glomerulus). This is confirmed by the same directional patterns in the subset of genes with inverse DNA methylation changes between ChRCC and ccRCC. ChRCC also shows widespread differences in methylation from ccRCC (>64,000 loci; p<0.001), with more hypomethylation and fewer hypermethylation events, consistent with the distinct chromatin environments of distal vs. proximal nephron cells.

**Detail:** The cell-of-origin inference from multi-platform molecular data resolves a longstanding controversy. Immunohistochemistry had suggested intercalated cells of the distal nephron for ChRCC, while the molecular data here provide genome-wide evidence. These cell-of-origin differences help explain the broad molecular divergence between ChRCC and ccRCC: different differentiation programs, different metabolic strategies (oxidative phosphorylation in ChRCC vs. glycolysis/Warburg in ccRCC), different epigenetic landscapes, and the specific chromosomal instability pattern (whole-chromosome losses vs. focal events) seen in ChRCC. Epigenetic silencing of *CDKN2A*/p16 was found in 4 ChRCC cases by methylation analysis.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDKN2A` (epigenetically silenced) · Chromophobe RCC vs. clear-cell RCC comparison

### F4: Mitochondrial DNA mutations in Complex I (MT-ND5) define the eosinophilic ChRCC subtype with maintained oxidative phosphorylation

**Finding:** mtDNA sequencing of 61 ChRCC cases identifies 142 somatic mutation events (not present in matched normal), with 35 events in 27 cases present at >50% heteroplasmy. Electron transport chain Complex I genes are altered in 18% (11/61) of cases. The most frequently mutated is *MT-ND5* (6 cases, all >70% heteroplasmy), with 5 of these being histologically eosinophilic ChRCC (p<0.01). Crucially, gene expression analysis of MT-ND5-mutated vs. non-mutated ChRCC reveals 719 differentially expressed genes (FDR<0.05), enriched for mitochondrial genes (*SDHB*, *NDUFS1*, *ATP5F1*, *COX10*, *COX11*), and — unexpectedly — nuclear-encoded complex I and oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) subunit expression is NOT downregulated in MT-ND5-mutated cases, suggesting maintained OXPHOS activity rather than a Warburg-like metabolic switch.

**Detail:** In renal oncocytomas (benign tumors closely related to eosinophilic ChRCC), homoplasmic complex I mutations lead to complex I deficiency and compensatory mitochondrial proliferation. The eosinophilic/oncocytoma-like appearance reflects dense mitochondrial accumulation staining with eosin. Here, mtDNA copy numbers are 4-fold higher in ChRCC than normal kidney, and PPARGC1A (mitochondrial biogenesis regulator) is overexpressed. PET/CT data from prior studies show radiolabeled acetate (not glucose) uptake in ChRCC — consistent with oxidative phosphorylation rather than glycolysis. The complex metabolic phenotype in MT-ND5-mutated ChRCC (maintained OXPHOS despite complex I mutation) suggests compensatory mechanisms or alternative roles for complex I alteration in cancer metabolism.

**Genes / cancer types:** `MT-ND5`, `SDHB`, `NDUFS1`, `PPARGC1A` · Chromophobe renal cell carcinoma (eosinophilic subtype)

### F5: Genomic structural rearrangements in the TERT promoter region drive extremely high TERT expression — a novel telomerase activation mechanism

**Finding:** WGS analysis with the Meerkat structural variant algorithm identifies genomic rearrangements with breakpoints within the *TERT* promoter region in 6/50 (12%) ChRCC cases. These 6 cases show dramatically elevated TERT expression (average >500 RNA-seq units; p<1E-20), far exceeding the 3 cases with TERT promoter point mutations (C228T; average ~1 unit) and the remainder of cases. All 6 rearrangements are confirmed by PCR spanning the breakpoint junction and PacBio sequencing. Five rearrangements are intrachromosomal (tandem duplications, inversions, deletions); one is a TERT-NEK5 inter-chromosomal translocation. Three cases with TERT promoter SVs also show the strongest manifestation of kataegis (p=0.001), establishing a spatial association between localized hypermutation and TERT promoter rearrangement.

**Detail:** TERT promoter point mutations (C228T, C250T) create *de novo* ETS/TCF binding sites and increase transcriptional activity 2–4-fold — widely observed in melanoma and other cancers. The structural rearrangements identified here produce TERT expression levels 100-fold higher than point mutation cases, suggesting a fundamentally different and more potent mechanism: cis-regulatory elements are repositioned to drive TERT, or the rearrangements release TERT from condensed chromatin. Kataegis (localized clusters of C>T or C>G mutations associated with genomic rearrangements, characteristic of APOBEC cytidine deaminase activity) is significantly enriched at TERT-promoter SV sites. APOBEC3B mRNA is elevated in ChRCC vs. normal kidney. APOBEC signature is detectable in WGS spectra of 6 ChRCC cases including all 3 with strong kataegis. The rearrangements are estimated to be early (clonal) events, suggesting they drive tumor initiation.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TERT`, `APOBEC3B` · Chromophobe renal cell carcinoma

## Open Questions

- What is the precise cis-regulatory mechanism by which TERT promoter structural rearrangements drive 100-fold greater TERT expression compared to point mutations — repositioning of enhancers, escape from repressive chromatin, or creation of neo-enhancers?
- Do TERT promoter SVs represent early truncation-induced crisis events in ChRCC precursor cells, and can they be detected in pre-malignant renal lesions?
- Why do complex I (MT-ND5) mutations in ChRCC not recapitulate the Warburg effect seen in other cancers? Does the distal nephron cell-of-origin (which normally relies on oxidative phosphorylation) constrain metabolic flexibility?
- Do eosinophilic ChRCC tumors with no chromosomal copy number alterations and MT-ND5 mutations represent a biologically distinct disease from classic ChRCC, and should they be treated differently?
- Can mTOR inhibitors (everolimus/temsirolimus) show meaningful activity in the 23% of ChRCC with mTOR pathway mutations, and is PTEN loss or TSC1/2 mutation a better predictor than in ccRCC?
- Is the APOBEC mutagenesis / kataegis pattern at TERT a general mechanism for TERT activation in other cancers, and does it represent a mutational process that can be therapeutically exploited (e.g., through APOBEC inhibition)?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=66) limits power to detect mutations below ~10% frequency; many candidate driver genes cannot be statistically confirmed
- Single-biopsy analysis — intra-tumor heterogeneity in ChRCC (documented in ccRCC) not assessed
- WGS performed on only 50/66 cases — 16 cases lack structural variant data including TERT promoter rearrangement analysis
- mtDNA analysis uses long-range PCR amplification which may introduce PCR bias and misses very low-level heteroplasmy events
- Cell-of-origin inference is based on correlation with a murine nephron atlas (Cheval et al.) — direct human lineage tracing studies not performed
- No clinical outcome data linked to genomic features (TERT SV, TP53 mutation, eosinophilic subtype) in a survival analysis
- The 4 eosinophilic cases with no chromosomal CNAs may represent misclassified renal oncocytomas rather than true ChRCC — histopathologic reclassification could affect conclusions
- Functional studies of TERT promoter rearrangements are limited to correlative analysis; no reporter assays confirm the causal mechanism of TERT upregulation
