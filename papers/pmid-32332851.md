## Summary

MSK study establishing the first UTUC-specific patient-derived xenograft (PDX) and cell line (PDC) models, reporting integrated genomic/transcriptomic profiling of 119 UTUC patients alongside 17 PDX models (50% take rate from 34 attempted). UTUC is predominantly luminal (87.5% luminal-papillary by consensus classifier) with recurrent actionable mutations in *FGFR3* (47%), *HRAS* (12%), *PIK3CA* (16%), *TSC1* (14%), and *ERBB2* (9%). Genomic concordance between patient tumors and PDX models was 72.7%. Chemotherapy sensitivity in PDX models closely mirrored clinical responses. A HER2 S310F-mutant PDX demonstrated that antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) therapy with DS-8201a was far superior to the kinase inhibitor neratinib, providing a mechanistic rationale for ADC approaches in ERBB2-mutant urothelial cancer. Genomic data available in `utuc_msk_2019` and `utuc_pdx_msk_2019` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Actionable genomic landscape of UTUC — FGFR3, HRAS, PIK3CA, TSC1, ERBB2

**Finding:** Prospective MSK-IMPACT sequencing of 119 UTUC patients confirmed high rates of potentially actionable mutations: *FGFR3* 47%, *PIK3CA* 16%, *HRAS* 12%, *TSC1* 14%, and *ERBB2* 9% (oncogenic/likely oncogenic mutations). *ERBB2* amplification was found in 4.4% of UTUC tumors. 39.3% of all somatic mutations were variants of unknown functional significance.

**Detail:** *ERBB2* mutations were more common in urothelial cancer (10.3% bladder, 5.9% UTUC) than in other solid tumors including breast (3.2%), endometrial (2.9%), colorectal (2.6%), and NSCLC (2.4%) across 44,183 prospectively profiled tumors. *FGFR3* mutations were restricted to the luminal subtype and have been associated with favorable prognosis in UTUC. *TERT* (53%) and *KDM6A* (24%) were also commonly mutated in PDX models. *CDKN2A*/*CDKN2B* deep deletions were observed in 29% of PDX models, consistent with selection pressure during engraftment.

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR3, ERBB2, HRAS, PIK3CA, TSC1, TERT, KDM6A, TP53, CDKN2A, CDKN2B · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma

### F2: UTUC is predominantly luminal-papillary; FGFR3 mutations restricted to luminal subtype

**Finding:** RNA-seq of 80 UTUC tumors showed 87.5% luminal phenotype (BASE47 classifier) and 12.5% basal. By consensus MIBC classifier: 82.5% luminal-papillary (LumP), 8.75% LumU, 6.25% basal/squamous (Ba/Sq). *FGFR3* mutations were exclusively found in the luminal subtype. Basal/squamous UTUC expressed high levels of CDH3, CD44, KRT5, EGFR, KRT6 — similar to basal bladder cancer.

**Detail:** All 14 low-grade UTUC tumors were classified as LumP. Median TMB was similar between luminal (4.21) and basal (4.56) phenotypes (P=0.89). This subtype classification mirrors bladder cancer biology, where the basal subtype is associated with a more aggressive course. Successful PDX engraftment correlated with the basal/squamous consensus subtype (P=0.04) and enrichment of cell cycle/DNA replication pathway genes (MCM5, MCM7, SMC1), suggesting more proliferative tumors engraft preferentially.

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR3, CDH3, CD44, KRT5, EGFR, KRT6, MCM5, MCM7 · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma

### F3: PDX models are genomically concordant with patient tumors and recapitulate chemotherapy response

**Finding:** PDX models showed 72.7% concordance of known/likely oncogenic mutations with corresponding patient tumors. Chemotherapy sensitivity in PDX directly mirrored clinical responses in three paired patient-PDX cases: gemcitabine/carboplatin responses in UCC03 and UCC19 were recapitulated in their PDX; MSI-H tumor UCC17 showed minimal response to gemcitabine/cisplatin in both patient and PDX.

**Detail:** Histological concordance was achieved in 16/17 tumor-PDX pairs (including retention of squamous differentiation). Phylogenetic WES analysis showed linear and branched evolution. MSI-H UTUC PDXs (4/17 engrafted) maintained MSI/MMR and aging mutational signatures across passages. Among 20 UTUC patients treated with neoadjuvant chemotherapy before RNU, pathologic response occurred in 10/17 (59%) MSS patients but 0/2 MSI-H patients — paralleling the established chemoresistance pattern seen in MSI-H colorectal cancer.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, FGFR3, RECQL4 · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma (MSS and MSI-H)

### F4: HER2 S310F mutation confers resistance to kinase inhibitor neratinib but sensitivity to ADC DS-8201a

**Finding:** The HER2 S310F-mutant UTUC PDC (UCC14) was relatively insensitive to the irreversible EGFR/HER2 kinase inhibitor neratinib (IC50 508 nM vs 0.1 nM in HER2-amplified BT-474 breast cancer cells). In contrast, the antibody-drug conjugate DS-8201a (trastuzumab deruxtecan) produced dramatic tumor regression in UCC14 PDX (P<0.0001 vs vehicle; P<0.0001 vs neratinib).

**Detail:** *ERBB2* mutations (oncogenic/likely oncogenic) are more prevalent in urothelial cancer than any other common solid tumor. HER2 kinase inhibitors have shown limited clinical activity in urothelial carcinoma. The mechanistic explanation demonstrated here: HER2 S310F is an extracellular domain mutation that does not directly affect kinase activity, meaning kinase inhibitors cannot efficiently suppress HER2 signaling. ADCs exploit antibody-mediated binding for internalization and intracellular payload delivery regardless of kinase activity. This work directly supported the development of ADC-based HER2 therapy in urothelial cancer.

**Genes / cancer types:** ERBB2 (S310F mutation) · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma, Urothelial carcinoma (bladder and upper tract)

### F5: Lynch syndrome UTUC is MSI-H, FGFR3 R248C-enriched, and chemoresistant to platinum agents

**Finding:** Of 4 MSI-H UTUC PDX models established (out of 17 total engrafted tumors), three arose from Lynch syndrome patients (germline mutations in MLH1 or MSH2). The FGFR3 R248C hotspot was present in 3/4 MSI-H tumor-PDX pairs — consistent with prior reports of FGFR3 R248C enrichment in Lynch-associated UTUC. All MSI-H PDX models were resistant to gemcitabine/cisplatin chemotherapy.

**Detail:** Lynch syndrome is associated with 8.7% of UTUC patients. The FGFR3 R248C hotspot is a specific activating mutation previously reported to be enriched in Lynch/MSI-H UTUC vs sporadic disease. This result supports the hypothesis that FGFR3 R248C may be a selected driver in the MSI context (frameshifts at mononucleotide repeats generate FGFR3 substrate hypermutation, with R248C arising as a specific selected clone). The chemoresistance of MSI-H UTUC to platinum agents parallels MSI-H CRC chemoresistance and has important implications: these patients may benefit from upfront immunotherapy rather than neoadjuvant platinum.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, MSH2, FGFR3 (R248C) · Upper tract urothelial carcinoma, Lynch syndrome

## Open Questions

- Can FGFR3-targeting drugs (erdafitinib) be tested in UTUC-specific PDX models with activating FGFR3 mutations to define predictive biomarkers of response?
- Do MSI-H UTUC patients derive superior benefit from immunotherapy over chemotherapy, analogous to MSI-H colorectal and endometrial cancers?
- What is the efficacy of HER2 ADCs (trastuzumab deruxtecan, disitamab vedotin) specifically in *ERBB2*-mutant (vs amplified) UTUC — and does mutation type (extracellular vs kinase domain) predict response?
- Can the genomic features predicting PDX engraftment (basal/squamous signature, cell cycle enrichment) serve as clinical biomarkers for aggressive UTUC biology?
- What role do TSC1/TSC2 mutations play in UTUC sensitivity to mTOR inhibitors?

## Limitations

- PDX take rate of 50% introduces selection bias toward more aggressive tumors (though invasive tumors numerically more likely to engraft, difference was not statistically significant)
- RNA-seq available for only 80/119 UTUC patients; chemotherapy response mirroring demonstrated in only 3 patient-PDX pairs
- PDX engraftment in immunocompromised NSG mice eliminates immune microenvironment — critical limitation for studying immunotherapy responses
- Mutational signature drift observed in some PDX models (APOBEC signature lost in UCC30 PDX)
- UTUC is a rare cancer; sample size limits statistical power for subgroup analyses by molecular subtype
- CDKN2A/B deletions enriched in PDX vs primary tumors suggest ongoing in vivo selection that may not reflect the human disease
