## Summary

Comprehensive genomic profiling (WES, low-pass WGS, RNA-seq) of 68 early-passage subcutaneous PDXs from 65 pediatric solid tumor patients across 16 cancer types, with matched patient tumors (PTs) in 27 pairs (40%). Overall mutational similarity between PTs and PDXs is 52% (median), with oncogenic mutations sharing 93%. About 30% of PT/PDX pairs show low mutational similarity (<20%), explained by clonal evolution: an aggressive PT subclone seeds the PDX, and evidence for immune suppression of this subclone in vivo (higher neoantigens, HLA expression, inflammasome activity in group 2 PTs) is presented. Copy number profiles are highly conserved (rho=0.98). A novel LRPAP1-PDGFRA fusion is identified and validated. Data available in `pancan_pdx_uthsa_2023` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: ~30% of PT/PDX pairs show low mutational similarity due to minor-clone seeding

**Finding:** Median mutational similarity between matched PTs and PDXs is 0.52 (78% of mutations shared overall). However, approximately 30% of pairs show low similarity (<0.2), including two pairs with no shared mutations. This is explained by clonal dynamics: a minor PT subclone outcompetes the major clone during engraftment in immunodeficient mice.

**Detail:** Three evolutionary patterns are described: (1) clone retention (70% of pairs) — PDX retains major PT clone; (2) clone sweeping (17%) — a PT subclone expands to become the PDX major clone; (3) branch seeding (13%) — PT clonal mutations are lost, and early-branch mutations persist. Group 2 (sweeping + branching) PDXs take significantly longer to engraft (median 22 vs. 13 weeks, p=0.03). Oncogenic/likely oncogenic mutations show high overlap (93%, 28/30) despite overall mutation disparity.

**Genes / cancer types:** CTNNB1 (hepatoblastoma), TP53 (pan-cancer) · Pediatric solid tumors (pan-pediatric)

### F2: Immune suppression of the PDX-seeding subclone in the patient tumor

**Finding:** In group 2 (clone sweeping/branch seeding) pairs, the PDX-dominating subclone is more proliferative than the PT major clone but also carries significantly more clonal neoantigens in PDXs vs. matched PTs (p=0.03, paired t-test). Group 2 PTs show higher HLA gene expression (p=0.02), inflammasome pathway enrichment (GSEA p=0.03), NK cell signatures, and lower cancer-associated fibroblast abundance — all consistent with active antitumor immunity suppressing the neoantigen-high subclone in vivo.

**Detail:** Mutational load does not differ between PTs and PDXs in either group. The increased neoantigen count in group 2 PDXs persists after controlling for clonal mutation count (p=0.03). These data argue that the immune microenvironment in pediatric solid tumors constrains expansion of more immunogenic subclones, and that loss of immune pressure in immunodeficient PDX hosts releases this constraint. This provides a mechanistic explanation for previously unexplained PT/PDX genomic discordance in pediatric cancers.

**Genes / cancer types:** HLA genes (HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C); inflammasome pathway · Pediatric solid tumors

### F3: Copy number profiles are highly conserved between PTs and PDXs, including in group 2 pairs

**Finding:** SCNA profiles are highly conserved between PTs and PDXs (pairwise rho ~0.98 for ploidy, strong correlations for arm-level and focal events). 86% of focal CNAs detected in PTs are present in matched PDXs. Ploidy conservation is observed even in group 2 tumors despite their mutational divergence, suggesting SCNAs are predominantly clonal events.

**Detail:** Cancer type-specific SCNA patterns match the literature: Wilms tumor shows chr1q gain (57%) and chr11 loss (30%); hepatoblastoma shows 1q, 2q, 20, and 8q gains; germ cell tumors show chr12p gain (85%), chr21 gain (62%), and chr4/5 loss. WGD is common in germ cell tumors (77%) and osteosarcoma (67%). Only two pairs (1959, 1979) showed dramatic ploidy change, coinciding with complete mutational discordance.

**Genes / cancer types:** MYC, CCND3, CCNE1, SETDB1, EZH2, TP53, PTEN, ATM, PD-L1 (deletion) · Pediatric solid tumors

### F4: Gene expression is highly conserved and dictated by tissue of origin

**Finding:** RNA-seq expression profiles of matched PT and PDX pairs are highly correlated (Spearman rho range 0.92–1.0). Unsupervised clustering groups samples by tissue of origin rather than PT vs. PDX status, confirming that early-passage PDXs faithfully recapitulate transcriptomic identity. High correlation is observed in all three evolutionary pattern groups (p=0.87 across groups).

**Detail:** Clear cell sarcoma is the exception, where samples cluster by collection site (kidney vs. bone) rather than PDX/PT status. Hepatoblastoma and Wilms tumor show significant intra-lineage correlations. Expression of proliferation markers and cell cycle signatures is higher in group 2 PDXs than in matched PTs, consistent with outgrowth of the more proliferative subclone.

**Genes / cancer types:** (Transcriptome-wide) · Pediatric solid tumors (pan-pediatric)

### F5: Novel LRPAP1-PDGFRA fusion identified and validated in glioblastoma and germ cell tumor

**Finding:** A novel gene fusion LRPAP1-PDGFRA was identified by RNA-seq in both a pediatric glioblastoma and a germ cell tumor. The fusion preserves the PDGFRA kinase domain and is associated with high PDGFRA expression. The fusion was validated by RT-PCR in both samples and is potentially targetable with PDGFRA inhibitors.

**Detail:** 161 high-confidence gene fusions were identified across all PDXs, including disease-defining fusions (EWSR1-ATF1 in clear cell sarcoma, BCOR-CCNB3 in Ewing-like sarcoma). 14 fusions involved kinase genes and 12 involved clinically actionable genes, including TAOK1-NTRK3. NTRK fusions are already clinically targetable. The LRPAP1-PDGFRA fusion is a novel finding not previously reported in pediatric solid tumors. 97% (29/30) of fusions detected in PTs are also detected in matched PDXs, confirming high fusion conservation.

**Genes / cancer types:** LRPAP1-PDGFRA, EWSR1-ATF1, BCOR-CCNB3, TAOK1-NTRK3 · Glioblastoma, germ cell tumor, clear cell sarcoma, Ewing-like sarcoma

## Open Questions

- Do the immune-suppressed subclones identified in group 2 pediatric tumors represent a generalizable mechanism of immune editing applicable to adult tumors as well?
- Can the LRPAP1-PDGFRA fusion be functionally characterized and does it respond to PDGFRA inhibitors (e.g., imatinib, avapritinib) in PDX models?
- Does the immune microenvironment status of the PT predict PDX engraftment success or evolutionary pattern, and can this be used to select optimal PDX establishment conditions?
- Why do two pairs (1959, 1979) show complete ploidy change and zero mutational overlap — is this contamination, mixed histology, or a genuine extreme clonal event?
- Can the pstPDX portal resource be used to systematically prioritize drug testing based on genomic profiles, and how well do results translate to pediatric clinical trials?

## Limitations

- Only 27 of 68 PDXs have matched patient tumor sequencing — limits statistical power for PT/PDX comparison analyses
- Subcutaneous (not orthotopic) PDX model; microenvironment findings may not translate to organ-specific or orthotopic settings
- Immune analyses based on bulk RNA-seq deconvolution, which is less precise than single-cell RNA-seq for characterizing immune cell populations
- HLA typing and neoantigen prediction involve computational estimation with known error rates, especially at low mutation counts
- Pediatric cohort is enriched for Hispanic ancestry (46%); may not be representative of all pediatric solid tumor populations
- Small sample sizes per cancer type limit cancer-type-specific statistical conclusions
