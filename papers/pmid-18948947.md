## Summary

The Tumour Sequencing Project (TSP) characterizes somatic mutations in 623 candidate cancer genes across 188 primary lung adenocarcinomas — the largest cancer gene sequencing effort for this disease at the time. More than 1,000 somatic mutations are identified across 163 tumors, with 26 significantly mutated genes detected. The study identifies new tumor suppressors (NF1, ATM, RB1, APC) and new proto-oncogenes (ERBB4, EPHA3, KDR, NTRK family) not previously implicated in lung adenocarcinoma, and demonstrates that five major pathways — MAPK, p53, Wnt, cell cycle, and mTOR — are each disrupted in a large fraction of cases. Integration of mutation data with SNP array copy-number and gene expression data confirms pathway-level convergence. Smoking status and tumor grade correlate with mutation burden and specific gene mutations. This landmark paper established the "pathway-centric" view of lung adenocarcinoma genomics and expanded the list of actionable targets.

## Key Findings

### F1: 26 significantly mutated genes identified — doubling the known lung adenocarcinoma driver gene list; MAPK pathway disrupted in 132/188 tumors

**Finding:** Three independent statistical methods identify 26 significantly mutated genes in 188 lung adenocarcinomas. These include five previously known high-frequency drivers (TP53, KRAS, STK11, EGFR, CDKN2A) plus 21 newly significantly mutated genes, more than doubling the characterized driver landscape. 132/188 (70%) of tumors carry at least one MAPK pathway mutation.

**Detail:** 289 mutations were found across 56 MAPK pathway genes (EGF, FGF, NTRK receptor families, KRAS, NF1). Newly significant genes include tumor suppressors (NF1, ATM, RB1, APC) and candidate proto-oncogenes (ERBB4, EPHA3, KDR, FGFR4, NTRKs). In total, 1,013 non-synonymous mutations were validated across 163 tumors: 915 point mutations, 29 insertions, 57 deletions, 12 dinucleotide mutations. Ten genes were significant by all three statistical methods (red tier), confirming high-confidence drivers. The breadth of MAPK pathway disruption established this pathway as the central organizing principle of lung adenocarcinoma oncogenesis.

### F2: NF1 and ATM newly established as lung adenocarcinoma tumor suppressor genes; co-mutations suggest functional pathway overlap with TP53

**Finding:** NF1 is mutated in 16 instances across 13 patients (7%) — including 4 nonsense, 5 splice-site, and 1 frameshift mutation — definitively establishing NF1 as a lung adenocarcinoma tumor suppressor. ATM is mutated in 14 instances across 13 tumors (7%), with strong mutual exclusivity from TP53 mutations (P=9.5×10^-5), suggesting ATM loss substitutes functionally for TP53 loss in cell-cycle checkpoint abrogation.

**Detail:** Three tumors harbor two NF1 mutations each, suggesting possible bi-allelic inactivation. NF1 is also found homozygously deleted in one tumor (SNP array). NF1 loss activates RAS signaling and may sensitize tumors to MEK inhibitors — a therapeutic implication noted by the authors. The strong mutual exclusivity between ATM and TP53 (P=9.5×10^-5) parallels the known biology: both regulate cell-cycle checkpoints via independent mechanisms (ATM via CHK2-p53 axis; TP53 directly), so loss of either may be sufficient and redundant. RB1 mutations (7 tumors) co-occur with TP53 mutations (5/7) or ATM mutations (2/7), suggesting a consistent pairing of checkpoint loss with RB pathway disruption.

### F3: EGFR and KRAS mutations are mutually exclusive and correlate with smoking status — confirming distinct molecular subtypes

**Finding:** EGFR and KRAS mutations are mutually exclusive (P<1×10^-7; no sample has both), and show opposite associations with smoking status: EGFR mutations correlate with never-smoker status (P=0.0046), while KRAS mutations correlate with smoker status (P=0.021). Smokers have on average threefold higher mutation rates than never-smokers.

**Detail:** The study confirms previously reported mutual exclusivity and smoking associations for EGFR/KRAS, and quantifies the mutation rate difference: smokers average three times more mutations per tumor than never-smokers (statistically significant, P=0.021, t-test). No never-smoker tumor had more than five mutations across the 623 screened genes, while smokers had up to 49. EGFR mutations also correlate with lower tumor mutation rate (P=0.05), consistent with EGFR being a strong early driver reducing selection pressure for additional mutations. KRAS mutations are stable across tumor grade (38%, 32%, 32% in grades 1, 2, 3 respectively), suggesting KRAS is an early/initiating event. STK11 mutations also co-segregate with smoker status (P=0.044) and are mutually exclusive with EGFR (P=7×10^-6).

### F4: mTOR pathway disrupted in >30% of tumors — NF1, PIK3CA, PTEN, TSC1/TSC2, AKT, STK11 all contribute

**Finding:** More than 30% of the 188 tumors carry mutations in at least one mTOR pathway component — spanning NF1 (RAS-dependent mTOR activation), PIK3CA, PTEN, AKT genes, TSC1/TSC2, and STK11 — suggesting dysregulated mTOR is a major and therapeutically targetable axis in lung adenocarcinoma independent of KRAS.

**Detail:** 13 tumors carry NF1 mutations (indirectly activating mTOR via RAS); PTEN, AKT, and PI3K gene mutations found across multiple samples; TSC1 and TSC2 each carry mutations in 3 of 188 tumors each (6 total); STK11 mutations (30+ mutations) disrupt AMPK-dependent mTOR suppression with 17 tumors showing LOH at the STK11 locus. PTEN and APC mutations are correlated with copy-number loss, and EGFR/KRAS mutations are correlated with copy-number gain — supporting dual role as oncogenes with amplified mutant alleles. Authors suggest rapamycin and its analogs should be tested more systematically in lung adenocarcinoma, particularly NF1-mutant tumors for MEK inhibitors and KDR/VEGFR-mutant tumors for sorafenib/sunitinib.

### F5: Wnt pathway mutations newly discovered in lung adenocarcinoma; TP53 and LRP1B mutations correlate with solid histological subtype and higher grade

**Finding:** 29/188 (15%) of lung adenocarcinomas carry mutations in Wnt pathway genes (APC, CTNNB1, SMAD2, SMAD4, GSK3B) — the first demonstration of Wnt pathway disruption in lung adenocarcinoma. TP53 mutations increase strikingly with tumor grade (13% grade 1, 24% grade 2, 52% grade 3) and positively correlate with the solid histological subtype, while EGFR mutations correlate with the papillary subtype.

**Detail:** APC mutations (13 mutations in 11 tumors) and CTNNB1 mutations (G34E and S37F in two tumors) provide the clearest evidence of Wnt pathway activation. All 11 APC-mutant tumors were pathologically confirmed as primary lung tumors (not metastatic colorectal). PRKDC mutations (DNA-PK, involved in double-strand break repair) are present in 6 of the most highly mutated tumors (average 24.3 mutations vs. 4.7 in PRKDC-WT; P=3.52×10^-59), identifying PRKDC as a mutational context gene rather than a driver. LRP1B mutations (17 in the dataset) positively correlate with solid subtype (P=2.29×10^-5), higher grade, and higher stage. INHBA mutations also correlate with solid subtype and higher grade.

## Open Questions

- Which NF1-mutant lung adenocarcinomas activate RAS vs. PI3K signaling preferentially, and which are most likely to respond to MEK inhibitors vs. PI3K/mTOR inhibitors?
- Do ERBB4 mutations in lung adenocarcinoma confer sensitivity to pan-ERBB inhibitors (e.g., afatinib), and are they mutually exclusive with EGFR mutations?
- Are EPHA3 and other ephrin receptor mutations driver events or passengers — and do they confer dependency on ephrin signaling that could be exploited therapeutically?
- Can the Wnt pathway APC/CTNNB1-mutant lung adenocarcinoma subset (15%) be distinguished clinically and therapeutically from APC/CTNNB1-wildtype tumors?
- Do NTRK-mutant lung adenocarcinomas (20 mutations identified) respond to entrectinib or larotrectinib, paralleling NTRK-fusion–driven cancers?
- What fraction of the 25 tumors with no discovered mutations in the 623 screened genes harbor driver alterations outside the panel — in introns, regulatory regions, or genes not included?

## Limitations

- Targeted sequencing of 623 genes, not WES/WGS; the majority of the genome is uncharacterized, and non-candidate gene drivers are missed
- Sample size (n=188) limits detection power for genes mutated in <5% of cases
- SNP array and expression data available only for subsets (383 and 75 samples respectively), not the full 188
- Mutation validation by orthogonal methods or re-PCR may have missed some low-VAF mutations
- No matched germline DNA for all samples; some rare germline variants may have been included as somatic
- No RNA-seq; fusion transcripts (e.g., ALK, RET fusions — not yet discovered in 2008) not detected
- 2008-era statistical methods and databases (COSMIC, OMIM); re-analysis with modern tools and databases would likely reclassify some mutations
- Clinical outcome data (OS, PFS) not analyzed in relation to individual gene mutations
- Predominantly resectable early-stage tumors; metastatic biology may differ
