## Summary

This study provides the first large-scale genomic landscape of esophageal adenocarcinoma (EAC), performing whole-exome sequencing (WES) on 149 tumor/normal pairs and whole-genome sequencing (WGS) on 16 pairs. EAC shows a very high mutation rate (median 9.9/Mb by WGS) exceeded only by lung cancer and melanoma, and harbors a unique mutational signature: A>C transversions at AA dinucleotides (34% of WGS mutations), likely attributable to gastroesophageal reflux-driven oxidative damage. MutSig analysis identifies 26 significantly mutated genes, including known tumor suppressors (TP53, CDKN2A) and novel candidates. Particularly notable are recurrent mutations in ELMO1 and DOCK2 — upstream RAC1 activators — found in 17% of EACs, with functional evidence that ELMO1 mutations increase cellular invasion. Chromatin-remodeling genes (ARID1A, SMARCA4, ARID2) are mutated in 20% of tumors. Actionable alterations are present in 48% of tumors when including gene amplifications.

## Key Findings

### F1: EAC-specific mutational signature — A>C transversions at AA dinucleotides — linked to gastroesophageal reflux

**Finding:** EAC harbors a unique, cancer-type-specific mutational signature: A>C transversions at AA dinucleotides (especially AAG context) constitute 34% of WGS mutations and are strongly associated with gene expression levels, suggesting transcription-coupled repair of reflux-induced oxidative damage.

**Detail:** Across 16 WGS samples, median 26,161 genome-wide mutations per tumor (range 18,881–66,225; median 9.9/Mb haploid genome). A>C mutations averaged 34% of all mutations; 84% of A>C mutations flanked by a 5′ adenine. AAG trinucleotide context was most pronounced (49.3/Mb vs. 6.7–16.8/Mb for other 3′ contexts). A>C transversions were significantly depleted in coding vs. intronic/intergenic regions (16% vs. ~34% of mutations, p<0.001, multiple contexts), and attenuated in highly expressed genes (3× stronger than for other mutation types). Strand bias confirmed: AA>C mutations occur more often when AA is on the non-transcribed strand (12.4 vs 11.2/Mb, p=0.0016), suggesting transcription-coupled repair. These AA transversions are absent in other cancer types. No recurrent gene fusions detected in WGS. Total rearrangements: median 172/tumor (range 77–402); 20% interchromosomal translocations.

**Genes / features:** Global mutational signature; AA dinucleotide context; `KRAS` c.351A>C (p.K117N) created by AA transversion in 1 sample

### F2: 26 significantly mutated genes identified; TP53 and CDKN2A most significant; novel drivers ELMO1, DOCK2 implicate RAC1 pathway

**Finding:** MutSig analysis of 145 MSI-negative EAC WES samples identifies 26 significantly mutated genes (q<0.1). Novel candidates include ELMO1 and DOCK2 — RAC1 pathway regulators mutated in 17% of EACs — alongside known EAC drivers TP53, CDKN2A, SMAD4, PIK3CA, and ARID1A.

**Detail:** 17,383 total mutations (16,516 non-silent + 1,954 indels) in 145 tumors; median 104 non-silent coding mutations/tumor; median non-silent frequency 3.51/Mb (range 0.97–10.8/Mb). TP53 and CDKN2A were most significant. Four previously EAC-associated genes: TP53, CDKN2A, SMAD4, PIK3CA. New genes with prior cancer association: ARID1A (chromatin remodeling), SMARCA4, ARID2. Truly novel candidates: ELMO1 and DOCK2 (RAC1 activation, 25/145 samples = 17%; p.K312 of ELMO1 mutated in 3 independent tumors); SPG20 (7%; 5 of 7 mutations from AA transversions); TLR4 (6%; innate immunity); AKAP6 (8%); HECW1 (8%); AJAP1 (6%); NUAK1 (3%); MYST3 (5%). Four MSI+ cases (excluded): hypermutation attributed to mutations in MSH6 and MSH3. No BRAF mutations observed; rare NF1 and KRAS mutations (2% and 3% respectively).

**Genes / features:** `TP53`, `CDKN2A`, `SMAD4`, `PIK3CA`, `ARID1A`, `SMARCA4`, `ARID2`, `ELMO1`, `DOCK2`, `SPG20`, `TLR4`, `AKAP6`, `HECW1`, `PBRM1`, `JARID2`, `ERBB2`, `KRAS`, `NF1`

### F3: ELMO1 mutations functionally increase cellular invasion; RAC1 pathway activation in ~17% of EACs

**Finding:** ELMO1 and DOCK2 — which cooperate to activate RAC1 GTPase — are mutated in 17% of EACs. Functional assays demonstrate that EAC-derived ELMO1 mutations significantly increase cellular invasiveness compared to wild-type ELMO1 in NIH/3T3 cells, supporting a gain-of-function oncogenic role.

**Detail:** 25 of 145 EACs harbor ELMO1 or DOCK2 mutations; 2 samples have mutations in both genes; 2 have two independent DOCK2 mutations. DOCK2 is a guanine nucleotide exchange factor (GEF) that activates RAC1 through GTP loading; ELMO1 interaction relieves mutual autoinhibition. Wild-type ELMO1 overexpression increased invasion 7-fold vs. GFP control (p=0.004). Five ELMO1 mutants (p.F59L, p.K312E, p.K312T, p.K349R, p.T421N) further increased invasion 2–7 fold vs. wild-type ELMO1 (all p<0.05). DOCK2-activating partners TRIO, TIAM1, VAV2, and ECT2 were also mutated in EAC. Focal amplification of PAK1 (11q13, a principal downstream RAC1 effector) previously noted in EAC further supports pathway activation. When the A>C signature is excluded, a known KRAS transforming mutation (p.K117N) can be created via AA transversion, observed in 1 EAC.

**Genes / features:** `ELMO1`, `DOCK2`, `RAC1`, `TRIO`, `TIAM1`, `VAV2`, `ECT2`, `PAK1`, `KRAS`

**Data availability:** `available`
> ELMO1 and DOCK2 mutation data including amino acid changes available in `esca_broad`. Amplification data for PAK1 may be available as copy number data.

### F4: SWI/SNF chromatin-remodeling genes mutated in 20% of EACs; 24% have any chromatin-modifying gene mutation

**Finding:** Chromatin-remodeling genes ARID1A, SMARCA4, and ARID2 are collectively mutated in 20% of EACs, with a candidate SMARCA4-DNM2 fusion also identified by WGS. Including PBRM1 and JARID2, 24% (35/145) of EACs harbor chromatin-modifying gene mutations.

**Detail:** ARID1A, SMARCA4 (enzymatic subunit of SWI/SNF, established tumor suppressor), and ARID2 are all significantly mutated (q<0.1 by MutSig). SMARCA4 was also targeted by a predicted in-frame fusion with DNM2 (exon 11 of SMARCA4 and exon 14 of DNM2), identified in 1 WGS sample, suggesting loss-of-function via rearrangement in addition to point mutation. PBRM1 and JARID2 (histone demethylase) were also mutated. The high frequency of SWI/SNF gene mutations in EAC parallels observations in gastric and ovarian carcinoma and underscores epigenetic dysregulation as a universal cancer mechanism. Unlike colorectal cancer (CRC), EAC showed rare KRAS hotspot mutations and no BRAF mutations, highlighting distinct oncogenesis despite shared gastrointestinal anatomy.

**Genes / features:** `ARID1A`, `SMARCA4`, `ARID2`, `PBRM1`, `JARID2`; `SMARCA4-DNM2` fusion

**Data availability:** `available`
> ARID1A, SMARCA4, ARID2, PBRM1, and JARID2 mutations available in `esca_broad`. Structural variant (SMARCA4-DNM2 fusion) from WGS may be in the fusions/SV data if deposited.

**Tool support:** `native`

### F5: Actionable alterations in 23% (mutations) to 48% (mutations + amplifications) of EAC tumors; PI3K pathway most commonly mutated

**Finding:** Querying for therapeutically targetable genes, 23% of EAC tumors harbor a mutation in a gene with approved or investigational inhibitors; expanding to include focal amplifications raises this to 48%. PI3K pathway is the most frequently altered oncogenic pathway by mutation (13%), while ERBB2 (HER2) and EGFR RTK amplifications dominate the amplification landscape.

**Detail:** PIK3CA was the most frequently mutated actionable gene; PIK3CA, PIK3R1, and PTEN together define the PI3K pathway mutation rate (13%). ERBB2 mutations in 5 tumors, including kinase domain mutations p.D769Y (×2) and p.G776V previously annotated in other cancers. KRAS: 3 of 5 mutations at p.G12; 1 at p.K117N (AA transversion). EGFR: 3 mutations, 2 predicted non-deleterious. Focal amplifications (RTKs, CCND1, CCNE1, CDK6, PAK1) affect far more samples than mutations in the same genes. Of note, ERBB2-targeted therapy (trastuzumab) is the only approved targeted agent for EAC/GEJ; ERBB2 is altered by co-occurring amplification and mutation in 3% of EACs, supporting mutation as a potential additional biomarker. BRAF mutations: zero detected. NF1 and KRAS mutations: 2% and 3%, respectively.

**Genes / features:** `PIK3CA`, `PIK3R1`, `PTEN`, `ERBB2`, `EGFR`, `KRAS`, `NF1`, `CCND1`, `CCNE1`, `CDK6`, `PAK1`

**Data availability:** `available`
> Mutation data for all actionable genes available in `esca_broad`. Copy number amplification data (focal gains, high-level amplification) available for ERBB2, CCND1, CCNE1, CDK6.

**Tool support:** `native`
> OncoPrint and Mutations tab show PI3K pathway mutation rates. OncoKB annotation overlay highlights actionable alterations by tier. Copy number view shows ERBB2 and CDK6 amplification. Co-occurrence of ERBB2 mutation and amplification queryable via OncoPrint.

## Open Questions

- What specific mutagen(s) in gastroesophageal refluxate (bile acids, nitrosamines, reactive oxygen species, 8-OH-dGTP) drive the unique AA>C transversion signature — can this be definitively mapped using in vitro mutagenesis assays?
- ELMO1 p.K312 is mutated in three independent EAC tumors — does this represent a recurrent gain-of-function hotspot amenable to targeted therapeutic intervention (e.g., RAC1 inhibitors, PAK inhibitors)?
- The 48% actionable alteration rate (mutations + amplifications) has not translated to approved targeted therapies beyond ERBB2/trastuzumab — do the RTK amplifications (EGFR, MET, FGFR) have functional driver roles that could be validated in PDX or organoid models from this cohort?
- SPG20 (Spartin) mutations cluster around AA transversions — is this simply a passenger effect of the AA signature, or does Spartin loss-of-function contribute to EAC through endosomal trafficking or BMP signaling disruption?
- MSI-positive EAC (4 cases, ~3%) may respond to immune checkpoint inhibitors — does the esca_broad dataset include outcome data that could correlate MSI status with survival?

## Limitations

- Cohort restricted to treatment-naive, fresh-frozen primary resections; does not capture EAC treated with neoadjuvant therapy or metastatic disease
- WGS performed on only 16 samples; genome-wide rearrangement analysis, mutational signature characterization, and AA transversion rates are powered primarily by this small subset
- mRNA expression arrays only on 14 WGS samples, not linked to WES; transcriptomic analyses are substantially underpowered relative to genomic analyses
- No proteomic, methylation, or immune microenvironment data; tumor heterogeneity and subclonal architecture not assessed (pre-dates ABSOLUTE-based CCF analysis in EAC)
- MSI exclusion (4 tumors) reduces sample size minimally but removes a potentially immunotherapy-relevant subset from driver gene analysis
- Functional validation limited to ELMO1 in NIH/3T3 cells; DOCK2, TLR4, SPG20, and other novel drivers lack functional evidence
