## Summary

Integrative genomic and transcriptomic analysis of 738 rectal adenocarcinomas profiled by MSK-IMPACT (DNA, 692 cases) and RNA-seq (114 cases), with outcome data for 346 locally advanced cases treated with neoadjuvant therapy (NAT). Key findings: APC mutations are significantly less frequent in lower rectal tumors (77% vs. 92% upper rectum), providing a genomic basis for the more aggressive biology of distal rectal cancer; no somatic alteration showed treatment-agnostic association with complete response; KRAS mutations were associated with shorter DFS specifically in patients treated with CRT-consolidative chemotherapy; IGF2 and L1CAM overexpression correlate with decreased NAT response; and a pMMR/MSS immune-hot subset (~8%) exhibited higher TIL infiltration, immune checkpoint gene expression, and favorable outcomes. All data publicly available in `rectal_msk_2022` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: APC mutations are significantly less frequent in lower rectal tumors — a genomic basis for anatomical differences

**Finding:** WNT pathway alterations were significantly less frequent with increasing proximity to the anal verge: 92% in upper rectum (UR), 90% in middle rectum (MR), and 77% in lower rectum (LR; p < 0.001). These differences were driven by APC alterations specifically — APC-altered tumors were located higher in the rectum (median 6.85 cm vs. 4 cm, p < 0.001). Biallelic inactivation of APC was also less frequent in LR tumors. Anal adenocarcinomas showed WNT pathway alterations in only 1/9 (11%) cases, extending this gradient to the most distal region.

**Detail:** The finding was replicated in an independent cohort of 157 MSK rectal patients. No significant differences in stage or dMMR/MSI frequency were observed by rectal location. APC C-terminal mutations were more common in rectal and left-sided colon tumors (consistent with prior reports), while N-terminal APC mutations predominated in right-sided (proximal) colon tumors. KRAS and AMER1 alterations were significantly co-occurring (q < 0.05). TP53 was mutually exclusive with PIK3CA and KRAS (q < 0.1). PIK3CA mutations were subclonal in ~one-third of cases where they co-occurred with APC or TP53, suggesting PIK3CA is a late event in rectal cancer evolution.

**Genes / cancer types:** APC, AMER1, TP53, KRAS, PIK3CA, FBXW7 · Rectal adenocarcinoma

### F2: Somatic mutation landscape of rectal cancer — most frequent alterations are APC, TP53, KRAS

**Finding:** Among 652 pMMR/MSS rectal tumors, the most frequently altered genes were APC (81%), TP53 (81%), KRAS (42%), FBXW7 (14%), and PIK3CA (12%). Signaling pathway alterations: WNT (85%), TP53 (82%), RAS (51%). TP53 biallelic inactivation correlated with chromosomal instability (fraction genome altered, FGA) — truncating mutations more so than missense. Among pMMR/MSS cases, 28% of tumors had at least one therapeutically actionable alteration (OncoKB-annotated).

**Detail:** The only Level 1 actionable alteration was BRAF V600E (1%). Level 2 was ERBB2 amplification (3%). Level 3A was KRAS G12C (2%). Level 3B included PIK3CA (12%), NRAS (4%), and ATM (2%) — though confirmed lack of sensitivity in CRC suggests limited clinical actionability for these. WGS of 97 tumors identified SBS1 (mitotic clock) as the dominant signature; SBS6/SBS15 (MMR-deficiency) enriched in dMMR/MSI. Compared to TCGA colon cancer, rectal tumors had lower TMB and more frequent TP53 pathway alterations, while proximal colon (PCA) had more RAS and PI3K pathway alterations. 20q amplification was more frequent in distal colon tumors.

**Genes / cancer types:** APC, TP53, KRAS, FBXW7, PIK3CA, NRAS, BRAF, ERBB2, ATM, AMER1 · Rectal adenocarcinoma

### F3: KRAS mutations associated with shorter DFS specifically in CRT-consolidative chemotherapy patients

**Finding:** No somatic alteration showed a significant treatment-agnostic association with complete response (CR) or disease-free survival (DFS) in multivariate analysis across all 290 pMMR/MSS LARC patients. However, when stratified by treatment type, KRAS mutations were significantly associated with shorter DFS in patients treated with CRT followed by consolidative chemotherapy (CRT-CNCT; p = 0.004), but not in patients treated with induction chemotherapy followed by CRT (INCT-CRT; p = 0.122). CR rate differences for KRAS by treatment type were not significant.

**Detail:** KRAS mutations were clonal in the majority of cases and occurred most frequently at G12/G13 codons. A borderline non-significant improved CR rate was observed for KRAS mutants in INCT-CRT patients (33% vs. 15%, p = 0.056), suggesting possible treatment-specific interactions. Confounders include different patient accrual periods, treatment guidelines, and surgical vs. non-operative management approaches across cohorts. The association between KRAS mutations and worse outcomes in CRT-CNCT is consistent with KRAS-driven RAS pathway activation impairing radiation response, potentially overridden by induction systemic chemotherapy in the INCT-CRT setting.

**Genes / cancer types:** KRAS · Rectal adenocarcinoma (locally advanced)

### F4: IGF2 overexpression and L1CAM overexpression identify poor responders to neoadjuvant therapy

**Finding:** RNA-seq analysis of pre-treatment biopsies (n=97 NAT-treated patients with outcome data) identified IGF2 and L1CAM as significantly overexpressed in incomplete-response (iCR) versus complete-response (CR) patients. IGF2 strong overexpression was seen in 12 patients — all had iCR — and was mutually exclusive with PI3K pathway alterations. High L1CAM expression correlated with lower CR rates, shorter DFS, and higher rates of distant metastasis; it also correlated with CMS4 subtype membership. Both findings were validated in an independent cohort of 15 LARC patients.

**Detail:** IGF2 overexpression (up to 100-fold) mirrors findings in colorectal cancer more broadly, where IGF2 amplification/overexpression is mutually exclusive with PIK3CA/PTEN alterations, suggesting it activates PI3K signaling via an alternative mechanism. L1CAM expression was detectable pre-treatment by IHC and increased during NAT in iCR patients (validated in matched pre/post-treatment samples), suggesting dynamic upregulation during therapy. CA9 (hypoxia marker) expression was increased in KRAS or PIK3CA mutant tumors, particularly double mutants (p < 0.001). CMS4 (mesenchymal) patients had the worst DFS overall; CMS2 patients exhibited slightly better DFS than CMS1 and CMS3.

**Genes / cancer types:** IGF2, L1CAM, CA9, KRAS, PIK3CA · Rectal adenocarcinoma (locally advanced)

### F5: pMMR/MSS immune-hot subset (~8%) with favorable NAT outcomes and checkpoint gene overexpression

**Finding:** Unsupervised hierarchical clustering of ssGSEA immune signatures in 96 pMMR/MSS RNA-seq samples identified three immune groups (IG1–IG3). IG3 (immune hot, ~7% of pMMR/MSS cases) showed extensive immune infiltration with high TILs, Th1 cell enrichment, and overexpression of checkpoint genes (PD-1/PDCD1, PD-L1/CD274, CTLA4, TIM3/HAVCR2, LAG3). IG3 and IG4 (dMMR/MSI) had better DFS and CR rates than IG1/IG2, though differences were not statistically significant in this small subset.

**Detail:** IG3 tumors had lower FGA than IG1/IG2, consistent with less chromosomal instability. IG3 tumors also had lower APC (p = 0.008) and TP53 (p = 0.005) mutation frequencies, and lower KRAS mutations than IG2 (p = 0.011). IG3 tumors showed higher regulatory T cell scores alongside Th1 enrichment. TGF-β, IFN-α/γ, IL6-JAK-STAT3, and IL2-STAT5 pathway scores were positively correlated with inflammatory response; glycolysis and peroxisome scores were negatively correlated. Results were replicated in an independent TCGA LARC cohort (n=42). The ~8% pMMR/MSS immune hot fraction could explain why overall ICI trials in pMMR/MSS CRC have failed despite occasional responders.

## Open Questions

- Can pre-treatment IGF2 overexpression be validated prospectively as a biomarker of NAT resistance, and would IGF1R/IGF2 inhibitors improve response in this subgroup?
- Does the pMMR/MSS immune-hot (IG3) subtype predict benefit from immune checkpoint blockade in LARC, and should it be used to stratify patients in ICB clinical trials?
- What is the mechanistic basis for the KRAS/treatment interaction — does induction chemotherapy (INCT-CRT) overcome KRAS-driven radiation resistance via a different mechanism than consolidative chemotherapy?
- Can L1CAM expression be used as a dynamic biomarker during NAT to guide organ preservation decisions, as its levels change between pre- and post-treatment biopsies?
- Do the anatomical differences in APC mutation frequency along the rectum reflect distinct embryonic origins, stem cell populations, or microbiome exposures?

## Limitations

- RNA-seq available for only 114 of 738 patients (15%), limiting power for transcriptomic associations; IGF2/L1CAM findings need larger RNA-seq validation
- Majority of samples sequenced with targeted panel (MSK-IMPACT, 341–505 genes) rather than WGS; broader mutational landscape including non-coding regions not captured
- Treatment heterogeneity across cohorts (CRT-CNCT vs. INCT-CRT) and time periods complicates interpretation of genomic predictors
- Outcome analysis restricted to pMMR/MSS patients (n=290); dMMR/MSI subset too small for separate genomic analysis
- Organ preservation (watch-and-wait) strategy only recently adopted; follow-up time may be insufficient to capture late recurrences in some cohorts
