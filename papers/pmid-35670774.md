## Summary

Analysis of 1,417 prostate cancer samples (825 primary, 592 metastatic) from the MSK-IMPACT cohort reveals that *PIK3R1* — encoding the p85α regulatory subunit of PI3Kα — is significantly more frequently lost or mutated in metastatic prostate cancer than in primary disease (genomic loss: 36% vs. 24%, p<0.001; driver alterations: 5% vs. 2.7%, p=0.030). PIK3R1 loss in primary tumors predicts significantly shorter biochemical recurrence-free survival, independent of Gleason grade. Functional studies demonstrate that PIK3R1 knockdown activates AKT, promotes cell proliferation, confers enzalutamide resistance, and sensitizes cells and organoids to AKT inhibitors (MK2206, ipatasertib). Strikingly, FDG-PET imaging reveals that *PIK3R1*-altered prostate tumors have significantly higher glucose uptake — the only major oncogenic driver in mCRPC uniquely associated with this imaging finding — linking PIK3R1 to the insulin-PI3K-glycolytic regulatory axis. Pan-cancer analysis shows PIK3R1 alterations are enriched in aggressive subtypes (TNBC 9.1%) and associate with worse outcomes than PIK3CA alterations.

## Key Findings

### F1: PIK3R1 genomic loss and driver alterations are significantly enriched in metastatic vs. primary prostate cancer — second only to PTEN among PI3K pathway genes in metastases

**Finding:** *PIK3R1* genomic copy-number loss occurs in 36% of metastatic prostate cancers vs. 24% of primary tumors (p<0.001). Driver alterations (oncogenic mutations and fusions) occur in 5% of metastatic vs. 2.7% of primary samples (p=0.030). Among all PI3K-AKT-mTOR pathway genes, only *PTEN* and *PIK3R1* are significantly enriched in metastatic disease; *PIK3CA* alterations do not differ (3% vs. 4.6%, p=0.152). PIK3R1 loss is thus the second most enriched PI3K pathway alteration in metastatic prostate cancer.

**Detail:** 51 of 1,417 patients (3.6%) harbor PIK3R1 driver mutations/fusions overall. GISTIC analysis confirms recurrent focal loss of 5q13.1/*PIK3R1* locus (q<0.001) in metastatic samples. Approximately 31% of PIK3R1 losses are shorter than 25% of chromosome 5q, indicating focal events rather than broad arm loss. PIK3R1 co-occurs significantly with PTEN alterations (p<0.05); co-occurrence of both may further amplify PI3K/AKT pathway activity. Driver mutation hotspots in prostate cancer cluster in the cSH2 domain (11/19 truncating mutations, p<0.001), distinct from endometrial cancer (nSH2/iSH2) — suggesting cancer-type-specific functional mechanisms.

### F2: PIK3R1 loss/reduced expression in primary prostate cancer predicts shorter biochemical recurrence-free survival, independent of Gleason grade

**Finding:** In the TCGA prostate cancer dataset, *PIK3R1* DNA copy-number loss is significantly associated with shorter disease/progression-free survival (HR 2.04, 95% CI 1.32–3.15, p=0.001). In the Taylor et al. dataset, this association is even stronger (HR 4.13, 95% CI 2.01–8.50, p<0.001). Decreased *PIK3R1* mRNA expression is associated with shorter biochemical recurrence-free survival after Gleason grade adjustment (TCGA: HR 1.83, p=0.048). PIK3R1 mRNA expression is the lowest in metastatic disease across four independent datasets, specifically attenuated in metastatic vs. primary vs. benign tissue — a pattern not seen for any other class I PI3K subunit (PIK3CA, PIK3CB, PIK3CD, PIK3CG).

**Detail:** PIK3R1 expression positively correlates with PTEN mRNA in TCGA (consistent with known PTEN-stabilizing function of p85α). In the Grasso et al. dataset (normal prostate, primary cancer, metastatic CRPC), PIK3R1 showed a 9-fold decrease in metastatic vs. normal tissue (p=2e−11) — the most striking expression loss of any class I PI3K subunit. In MSK-IMPACT metastatic cohort, PIK3R1 driver alteration was associated with non-significant trend toward worse OS in mCSPC (HR 1.67, p=0.30), but analysis was underpowered. Survival in mCRPC was not strongly associated with copy number loss (HR 1.13, p=0.63).

### F3: PIK3R1 knockdown activates AKT, promotes proliferation, reduces AR target gene expression, and confers enzalutamide resistance in prostate cancer cell lines

**Finding:** siRNA-mediated knockdown of *PIK3R1* in LAPC4 (castration-sensitive, wild-type PIK3R1/PTEN) and 22RV1 (castration-resistant, wild-type PTEN, mutant PIK3CA) cells activates AKT phosphorylation (pAKT-S473), increases cell proliferation in 2D culture, and promotes 3D prostatosphere formation. Crucially, PIK3R1 knockdown also reduced PTEN protein levels (consistent with p85α-mediated PTEN stabilization) and reduced AR target gene expression (PSA, PLZF, TMPRSS2, NKX3.1) without affecting AR protein itself. LAPC4 PIK3R1-knockdown cells exhibited relative resistance to enzalutamide, suggesting PI3K/AKT activation as a mechanism of antiandrogen resistance.

**Detail:** In insulin-stimulated 22RV1 cells (castration-resistant), PIK3R1 knockdown augmented insulin-induced AKT phosphorylation, consistent with canonical PIK3R1 function as an inhibitor of insulin-mediated PI3K pathway activation. The AR transcriptional suppression upon PIK3R1 knockdown is consistent with the known p85α transactivating function for AR — loss of this function may paradoxically reduce AR-driven PSA expression while promoting CDK2/cell cycle progression through AKT. These data support a model in which PIK3R1 loss simultaneously activates PI3K/AKT (pro-tumorigenic) and reduces AR output (endocrine resistance).

### F4: PIK3R1-mutant prostate cells and patient-derived organoids are sensitized to AKT inhibitors — clinical actionability for ipatasertib and MK2206

**Finding:** PIK3R1-knockdown LAPC4 and 22RV1 cells exhibit markedly greater sensitivity to the allosteric AKT inhibitor MK2206 (even at 100 nM, a very low concentration) compared to scrambled-siRNA controls. Patient-derived mCRPC organoid MSK-PCa3, which harbors a PIK3R1 iSH2 domain Asn564Asp mutation (likely pathogenic) and PTEN deletion, shows strong growth inhibition with both MK2206 and ipatasertib (as low as 10 nM), while PIK3R1 wild-type organoid MSK-PCa1 (also PTEN-deleted) is only modestly affected. This establishes PIK3R1 mutation — beyond PTEN loss alone — as a determinant of AKT inhibitor sensitivity.

**Detail:** The key translational implication is that PIK3R1-mutant tumors may represent a subset of PTEN-deleted/PI3K-activated prostate cancers with enhanced AKT dependency, conferring greater sensitivity to AKT inhibitors like ipatasertib (in Phase 2/3 trials for prostate cancer, e.g., IPATential150 trial). No other oncogenic PI3K-AKT pathway mutations were detected in the organoids, making PIK3R1 the primary sensitizing lesion. Consistent results in PIK3R1-knockdown MDA-MB-231 (TNBC) breast cancer cells with MK2206 and ipatasertib extend this actionability finding to breast cancer.

### F5: PIK3R1-altered prostate tumors show significantly elevated FDG-PET uptake — the only mCRPC driver uniquely linked to tumor glycolysis; novel imaging biomarker

**Finding:** Among 313 prostate cancer patients with paired MSK-IMPACT and FDG-PET/CT data, *PIK3R1*-altered tumors had significantly higher FDG uptake than wild-type tumors (p<0.001 across primary and metastatic). In mCRPC alone (n=146), PIK3R1 driver alterations were the only genomic event among all known oncogenic signaling pathways significantly associated with elevated FDG uptake (p=0.002, FDR=0.039). This is the first direct link between PIK3R1 mutation and increased tumor glycolysis detectable noninvasively by imaging.

**Detail:** FDG-PET signal reflects glucose uptake (Warburg effect). PIK3R1 loss/mutation is known to increase insulin-stimulated AKT activation and glucose uptake (validated in mouse models). Representative CT-guided biopsy images show a PIK3R1-mutant mCRPC bone metastasis (SUVpeak 13.23) versus PIK3R1-wild-type (SUVpeak 2.89) in comparable lesions. FDG uptake was higher in metastases than primary tumors and in mCRPC vs. mCSPC, consistent with disease progression increasing metabolic activity. Most established mCRPC drivers (AR alterations, PTEN loss, RB1, TP53) were not significantly associated with FDG uptake, underscoring the specific link between PIK3R1 and glycolytic regulation through the insulin-PI3K axis.

## Open Questions

- Does PIK3R1 mutation status prospectively predict response to ipatasertib in the IPATential150 or similar trials — should prospective biomarker-stratified trials be designed?
- Is PIK3R1 monoallelic (heterozygous) loss functionally significant as an oncogenic event, or is biallelic disruption required for AKT pathway activation?
- Can FDG-PET prospectively identify PIK3R1-mutant tumors as a screening tool for AKT inhibitor trials, given that most other prostate cancer drivers are FDG-occult?
- What is the mechanism by which PIK3R1 mutation specifically promotes enzalutamide resistance — is it primarily through AKT activation or through the loss of the AR transactivation function of p85α?
- Does co-occurring PTEN loss + PIK3R1 alteration create a synergistic PI3K pathway activation, and should this co-occurring genotype receive combination PI3K/AKT inhibitor therapy?
- Is the cancer-type-specific mutation hotspot (cSH2 in prostate vs. nSH2/iSH2 in endometrial cancer) functionally distinct, and does it predict differential sensitivity to specific PI3K pathway inhibitors?

## Limitations

- MSK-IMPACT does not capture mRNA expression; RNA-seq correlations required TCGA/Taylor external datasets, limiting direct clinical-genomic integration in the MSK cohort
- Stringent FACETS QC excluded ~48% of enrolled patients (1,417 of ~2,735), potentially introducing selection bias toward copy-number-rich (advanced) tumors
- Survival analyses in metastatic disease (OS in mCSPC, mCRPC) were underpowered for PIK3R1 driver alterations (small n with events); conclusions on metastatic prognosis remain inconclusive
- FDG-PET/CT available for only 313 of 1,417 patients — selection bias (FDG-avid or clinically indicated imaging) may inflate FDG signal in the PIK3R1-altered group
- Organoid sensitivity experiments based on 2 PIK3R1-mutant organoids and 1 wild-type organoid — requires larger organoid biobank validation
- No prospective clinical validation of AKT inhibitor sensitivity in PIK3R1-altered prostate cancer patients; existing ipatasertib trial data not stratified by PIK3R1 status
- Pan-cancer analysis relied on external expression databases (GEPIA, KM-plotter) with heterogeneous cohort compositions
