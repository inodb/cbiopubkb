## Summary

A synthesis paper from the TCGA PanCancer Atlas project marking the completion of genomic characterization of >11,000 tumors from 33 cancer types. Rather than presenting a single new dataset, this Cell perspective integrates findings from all 10 TCGA PanCancer Atlas analysis working groups (AWGs) — covering somatic drivers (299 genes, >3,200 oncogenic mutations), germline pathogenic variants (>800 in 99 predisposition genes, ~8% of cases), multi-omics interactions (methylome, transcriptome, proteome), and tumor microenvironment (6 immune subtypes) — to present a synthesized view of oncogenic processes. Key contributions include: characterization of germline-somatic interactions and the two-hit hypothesis at scale; a comprehensive map of somatic driver mutual exclusivity and co-occurrence networks (TP53 as prime hub); evidence that mutation type (missense vs. frameshift) differentially shapes the transcriptome; and demonstration that driver mutations sculpt immune infiltrate composition in a subtype-specific manner.

## Key Findings

### F1: 299 somatic driver genes and >800 germline predisposition variants catalogued across 33 cancer types — genome integrity dominates germline predisposition (63% of germline variants vs. 14% of somatic drivers)

**Finding:** The MC3 somatic mutation consensus dataset, analyzed with 26 bioinformatic tools, identifies 299 driver genes and >3,200 oncogenic mutations across 33 TCGA cancer types. Independently, the Germline AWG identifies >800 pathogenic or likely pathogenic germline variants in 99 predisposition genes affecting ~8% of TCGA patients. The two datasets reveal that germline predisposition is disproportionately concentrated in genome integrity genes (63% of germline variants vs. only 14% of somatic driver alterations), with BRCA1, BRCA2, CHEK2, ATM, BRIP1, PALB2, PMS2 showing the highest germline variant counts.

**Detail:** Germline mutations in DNA mismatch repair (MMR) genes: 18 of 60 germline pathogenic MMR variant carriers had high MSI status (MSIsensor score > 4); 16 of these 18 also had somatic mutations in MMR genes — a population with potentially higher neoantigen load and checkpoint blockade response. BRCA1/2 germline mutation carriers develop cancer at younger ages than somatic or wild-type: germline 54.4±13.0 years vs. somatic 62.3±13.4 years vs. WT (p = 2.07×10⁻¹⁰). Germline and somatic BRCA1/2 mutations associate with higher mutation load in OV, BLCA, and STAD. Fanconi anemia pathway genes are enriched in patients with multiple germline pathogenic variants.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRCA1`, `BRCA2`, `CHEK2`, `ATM`, `BRIP1`, `PALB2`, `PMS2`, `MLH1`, `MSH2`, `MSH6` · Pan-cancer (33 TCGA tumor types)

**Data availability:** `available`
> All 32 TCGA PanCanAtlas studies are in cBioPortal with full MC3 somatic mutation data. Germline pathogenic variant calls are available as supplementary data from the TCGA Germline AWG paper (Lu et al. 2015 + PanCancer Atlas germline paper). MC3 mutation data directly accessible in cBioPortal.

### F2: TP53 is the prime hub of somatic driver interaction networks — co-occurring with RB1, CDKN2A, IDH1, ATRX, PPP2R1A and mutually exclusive with PIK3CA, HRAS, CTNNB1, ARID1A, FGFR3

**Finding:** Systematic analysis of driver gene pair interactions (Cochran-Mantel test, FDR < 0.1) across the MC3 pan-cancer dataset reveals an extensive co-occurrence and mutual exclusivity network. *TP53* is the prime hub: it co-occurs with *IDH1*, *ATRX*, *PPP2R1A*, *RB1*, *CDKN2A* (consistent with its role in high-grade, genomically complex tumors) and is mutually exclusive with *PIK3CA*, *HRAS*, *CTNNB1*, *ARID1A*, *FGFR3* (consistent with alternative oncogenic pathway activation). Pathway-level mutual exclusivity (e.g., BRAF/HRAS/NRAS/KRAS in RAS-MAPK) is confirmed, but tissue-specific interactions show additional context-dependence.

**Detail:** BRAF and HRAS/NRAS/KRAS mutual exclusivity holds across virtually all 33 tumor types (both activate RAS signaling). IDH1/IDH2 mutual exclusivity and PIK3CA/PIK3R1 interaction are recovered. Tissue-specific patterns: BRAF, NRAS, HRAS are mutually exclusive in THCA; GNAQ/GNA11 in uveal melanoma; TP53 and KRAS are mutually exclusive in COAD/READ/LUAD but significantly co-occur in PAAD. UCEC shows two mutually exclusive driver networks: TP53/PPP2R1A (copy-number high phenotype) vs. CTNNB1/PTEN/CTCF (copy-number low or hypermutated). In COAD/READ: both APC/KRAS (mRNA cluster 15 + RPPA cluster C8) but separate into methylation clusters 10 and 11.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PIK3CA`, `KRAS`, `BRAF`, `PTEN`, `IDH1`, `ATRX`, `RB1`, `CDKN2A`, `CTNNB1`, `APC` · Pan-cancer

**Data availability:** `available`
> All driver genes are in 32 TCGA PanCanAtlas studies. Mutual exclusivity and co-occurrence analysis is natively available for any gene pair in cBioPortal across all PanCanAtlas studies simultaneously.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Co-expression/Mutual Exclusivity tab in cBioPortal directly implements Cochran-Mantel analysis across studies. OncoPrint visualizes co-occurring alterations. Tissue-specific mutual exclusivity requires querying individual TCGA cancer type studies.

### F3: Mutation type (frameshift/nonsense vs. missense) differentially regulates cis-expression of driver genes — frameshift/nonsense mutations typically suppress expression via NMD; missense mutations often upregulate

**Finding:** Integrating pan-cancer somatic mutations with gene expression reveals a consistent pattern: frameshift/nonsense mutations in driver genes are associated with reduced mRNA levels (consistent with nonsense-mediated decay, NMD), while missense mutations are associated with elevated driver gene expression (consistent with gain-of-function or dominant-negative activity). This trend applies to tumor suppressors and oncogenes respectively, and varies by tissue of origin and mutation context.

**Detail:** Frameshift/nonsense group → NMD-driven expression reduction in ATRX, BRCA1, NF1, RB1, CDH1, CASP8. Missense group → elevated expression for EGFR, KIT, GATA3 (in breast cancer, GATA3 frameshift paradoxically elevates expression due to gain-of-function mechanism; FDR = 4.54×10⁻¹⁸). GATA3 frameshift mutations in BRCA associate with downregulation of microtubule dynamics genes (not seen with missense). CDH1 frameshift in BRCA associates with upregulation of leukocyte migration genes. LGG TP53 mutations (any type) associate with downregulated leukocyte migration gene expression, but LIHC and BRCA TP53 mutations do not — demonstrating that both mutation type and tissue context determine transcriptional consequences. This finding has direct implications for interpreting mutation reports: the cis-expression context of a missense vs. frameshift mutation in the same gene may imply fundamentally different biology and drug sensitivity.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ATRX`, `BRCA1`, `NF1`, `RB1`, `EGFR`, `KIT`, `GATA3`, `CDH1`, `CASP8`, `TP53` · Pan-cancer

**Data availability:** `available`
> Mutation and mRNA expression data are both available in TCGA PanCanAtlas studies in cBioPortal. Expression-mutation correlation (cis-effects) can be explored via the Expression tab in gene-specific views.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Expression vs. alteration plots in cBioPortal's gene page allow inspection of mRNA levels by mutation type. Box-plot comparison of missense vs. frameshift/nonsense mRNA is available. Tissue-specific subgroup analysis requires selecting individual TCGA cancer type studies.

### F4: Six pan-cancer immune subtypes (C1-C6) defined; driver mutations sculpt immune infiltrate composition — BRAF-driven tumors have higher CD8+ T cells than NRAS-driven tumors in C3

**Finding:** The TCGA Immune Response Working Group (IRWG) defines 6 pan-cancer immune subtypes: C1 Wound Healing, C2 IFN-γ Dominant, C3 Inflammatory, C4 Lymphocyte Depleted, C5 Immunologically Quiet, C6 TGF-β Dominant. Specific driver mutations shape immune infiltrate composition within subtypes: in C3, BRAF-mutant tumors have higher CD8+ T-cell fractions than NRAS-mutant tumors (ANOVA p < 2×10⁻⁵); BRAF + PD-L1 combination therapy may be synergistic. In C5, ATRX or TP53 mutations associate with higher macrophage and lower CD8+ fractions. CD8+ T-cell fraction increases with neoantigen burden in C2 (FDR < 10⁻¹⁵).

**Detail:** Immune subtyping was based on gene expression immune signatures. Neural network-based analysis of H&E images validated lymphocyte infiltrate estimates across 13 cancer types. Neoantigen burden-immune cell associations differ by immune subtype: C2 has greatest overall immune activity (CD8+ T-cell fraction increases with neoantigen load); C3 shows CD4+ T-cell and neutrophil increases with neoantigen burden; C5 (dominated by gliomas) shows macrophage infiltration with neoantigen burden. IDH1-driven LGGs associate with lower STAT1 → reduced CXCL10 secretion → decreased immune infiltrate. KRAS/NRAS/HRAS and BRAF V600 mutations are among the most frequently predicted neoantigen-generating drivers. Transcriptional network models implicate Ras family members in transcriptional control of TME composition.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `NRAS`, `KRAS`, `ATRX`, `TP53`, `IDH1`, `GNA11`, `GNAQ` · Pan-cancer; particularly SKCM, THCA, UVM, LGG, GBM

**Data availability:** `available`
> Immune subtype annotations, ESTIMATE scores, and immune cell fraction estimates (CIBERSORT) are included in TCGA PanCanAtlas clinical data. Driver mutation data is in cBioPortal. Immune subtypes are accessible through clinical data download or the PanImmune paper supplementary data.

### F5: Multi-omics clustering reveals samples group primarily by tissue of origin, but driver mutations differentiate within-tissue methylation and molecular subtypes

**Finding:** Integrative clustering of DNA methylation (25 clusters), mRNA (41 clusters), and RPPA proteomics (10 clusters) across 9,759 TCGA samples reveals that while tissue of origin is the dominant determinant of cluster assignment, driver mutations differentiate samples within tissue-of-origin clusters via their methylomic profiles. IDH1-driven LGG vs. EGFR-driven GBM co-cluster in mRNA/RPPA space but separate into distinct methylation clusters. APC/KRAS-driven COAD/READ and PIK3CA-driven BRCA similarly co-cluster in expression/proteome space but separate in methylation clusters defined by their specific driver mutations.

**Detail:** COCA clustering of cluster assignments from 5 individual platforms (aneuploidy: 10 clusters, mRNA: 25 clusters ≥40 samples, miRNA: 15, methylation: 25, RPPA: 10) plus integrative iCluster analysis (28 iClusters across 9,759 samples). 90% of samples within a given cluster combination are from a single cancer type (tissue-of-origin dominance). Key examples of driver-specific within-tissue methylation differences: IDH1-LGG in methylation cluster 1 (330/351 IDH1-mutant), EGFR-GBM in methylation cluster 16. UCEC: ARID1A/PTEN/CTNNB1/PIK3CA cluster (methylation subtype) vs. TP53/PPP2R1A cluster — reflecting serous (copy-number high, TP53) vs. endometrioid (copy-number low, PIK3CA/PTEN) subtypes. 40 genes significantly associated with multiplatform clusters; TP53, KRAS, PIK3CA enriched in ≥10 clusters each.

**Genes / cancer types:** `IDH1`, `EGFR`, `TP53`, `PIK3CA`, `KRAS`, `APC`, `PTEN`, `ARID1A`, `CTNNB1` · Pan-cancer; particularly LGG, GBM, UCEC, COAD/READ, BRCA

**Data availability:** `available`
> All molecular data layers (mRNA, methylation, RPPA, CNA, mutation) are available across 32 TCGA PanCanAtlas studies in cBioPortal. Multi-omics cluster assignments (iCluster, COCA) are in supplementary data of the Cell of Origin AWG paper and accessible via UCSC Xena.

Native cBioPortal does not display iCluster assignments but individual data layers (mRNA, methylation, RPPA) are accessible. UCSC Xena provides integrated pan-cancer multi-omics visualization with cluster overlay.

## Open Questions

1. How should the germline-somatic interaction findings inform routine clinical sequencing panel reporting — specifically, when should somatic-only tests be supplemented with germline testing?
2. Can immune subtype assignment (C1-C6) from gene expression data be reliably inferred from clinical sequencing data (mutation + CNA) without RNA-seq?
3. Do the transcriptional consequences of missense vs. frameshift mutations in the same driver gene (e.g., GATA3, CDH1) predict differential drug sensitivity — and can this be tested in cBioPortal survival analyses?
4. For BRAF-mutant tumors with elevated CD8+ T cells in C3, does BRAF inhibition further increase or decrease immune infiltrate, and how does this affect PD-1/PD-L1 combination efficacy?
5. What proportion of the 33 cancer types' remaining driver landscape lies in non-coding regulatory elements, and how will PCAWG WGS data (PMID 32025007) extend the 299 driver gene compendium?

## Limitations

- Perspective/synthesis paper: no new primary data collection; conclusions reflect quality and completeness of contributing AWG analyses.
- RPPA proteomics available for only ~7,858 of 11,000 samples; protein-level driver analysis is underpowered for rare cancer types.
- Immune subtype analysis based on bulk RNA-seq deconvolution — single-cell resolution of tumor microenvironment requires scRNA-seq (not available in TCGA PanCancer Atlas).
- Germline data analysis requires controlled-tier access; not all findings are reproducible with open-access data.
- Neoantigen predictions are computational (HLA typing + binding affinity); not experimentally validated for immunogenicity.
- TCGA cohort is biased toward surgically resectable tumors and underrepresents metastatic disease, treatment-refractory cases, and minority ancestry groups.
