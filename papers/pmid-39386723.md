## Summary

Preprint multimodal spatial profiling study of 44 fallopian tube (FT) specimens from 43 individuals across the full spectrum of HGSOC development: normal FT, incidental p53 signatures (p53.I), incidental STICs (STIC.I), STIC with concurrent cancer (STIC.C), and invasive HGSOC. Using CyCIF (31-plex imaging), GeoMx whole-transcriptome spatial transcriptomics, and 3D CyCIF on adjacent tissue sections, the study maps immune and molecular transitions from precancer to cancer. Key findings: IFN-α/γ pathway activation begins at the p53 signature stage and persists through invasion; HLA-E and classical MHC-I are upregulated in early precursors enabling immune evasion via NK cell suppression; micronuclear rupture drives cGAS-STING signaling in CIN lesions; and the TME shifts from immune surveillance (cDC1, NK, TRM CD8+ T cells) in early STICs to progressive immune suppression in STIC.C. IRDS genes (MCL1, STAT1, MX-1) emerge in STIC.C, possibly coupling persistent IFN to therapy resistance. CyCIF images and GeoMx data are publicly available in `ovary_geomx_gray_foundation_2024` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: IFN-α/γ pathway activation initiates at the p53 signature stage and persists through HGSOC

**Finding:** Interferon (IFN-α and IFN-γ) signaling is robustly activated as early as p53 signatures and progressively intensifies through STIC to invasive cancer. Key IFN-stimulated genes including STAT1, ISG15, IFITM1, IRF7, IRF9, and HLA-A are upregulated in precursor epithelium relative to matched normal FT.

**Detail:** Bayesian ordinal regression modeling across disease stages confirmed monotonic upregulation of IFN pathway genes from p53.I through invasive cancer. GSEA of GeoMx spatial transcriptomics data showed IFN-α/γ hallmark enrichment at p53.I→STIC.I and STIC.I→STIC.C transitions. STIC.C lesions additionally show TGF-β, EMT, hypoxia, and TNF-α/NFκB pathway enrichment. Invasive cancer further acquires hedgehog signaling and angiogenesis programs. Constitutive IFN-ε (IFNE) expression, normally protective in FT epithelium, is downregulated in STIC.C and cancer but not in incidental precursors.

**Genes / cancer types:** STAT1, ISG15, IFITM1, IRF7, IRF9, HLA-A, IFNE, IFNA2, MX1, MCL1 · High-grade serous ovarian carcinoma

### F2: HLA-E and classical MHC-I upregulation in early precursors enables NK cell immune evasion

**Finding:** HLA-E (non-classical MHC-I) and classical HLA-A/B/C are upregulated in p53.I and STIC.I epithelial cells, coinciding with IFN pathway activation. HLA-E overexpression allows tumor cells to evade NK cell surveillance, and HLA-E+ regions show coordinated expression of IFN-stimulated genes and complement pathway components.

**Detail:** CyCIF quantification showed a stepwise increase in epithelial cells positive for IFN markers (p-TBK1, p-STATs, HLA-A/E): p53.I 22%, STIC.I 33%, STIC.C 43%, cancer 26%. Pairwise co-expression analysis demonstrated strong positive correlations between IFN marker pairs (OR 1.5–50) suggesting coordinated, spatially localized IFN activation. HLA-E+ GeoMx regions showed enriched antigen presentation genes (HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-DRA, TAP, TAPBP), complement genes (CFB, CFH, C1S, C4B), and MYC. The IFN-related DNA damage resistance signature (IRDS) was preferentially associated with HLA-E+ regions starting at p53.I.

**Genes / cancer types:** HLA-E, HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C, TAP1, TAPBP, MX1, IRF1, DDX60, OAS3, CFB, CFH, MYC · HGSOC / fallopian tube precursors

### F3: Micronuclear rupture drives cGAS-STING signaling in CIN precursor lesions

**Finding:** Chromosomal instability (CIN) in STIC lesions leads to micronuclei formation and rupture, activating cGAS-STING signaling which in turn drives persistent IFN pathway activation. This mechanistically links genomic instability to immune reprogramming during HGSOC precancer development.

**Detail:** 3D CyCIF imaging revealed micronuclei in STIC.C and invasive cancer epithelial cells, with co-expression of the IFN-inducible protein MX-1 and the antigen-presenting molecule HLA-A in PanCK+ cells (confirmed by 3D volumetric CyCIF). p-TBK1 puncta (cytosolic, consistent with cGAS-STING activation) co-occurred with HLA-E and p-STAT3 in STIC.I and STIC.C. Early events in STICs include CCNE1 amplification and higher ploidy consistent with CIN. The BRCA1/2 mutant tumors showed comparable IFN activation regardless of BRCA status.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, BRCA1, BRCA2, CCNE1, MX1, HLA-A, HLA-E · HGSOC

### F4: TME shifts from immune surveillance to immunosuppression along the HGSOC progression axis

**Finding:** Early STICs (STIC.I) are characterized by immune surveillance with conventional dendritic cells type 1 (cDC1), NK cells, and tissue-resident memory CD8+ T cells (TRM). Advanced STICs (STIC.C) show significant decline in these immune populations with molecular evidence of immune dysfunction and editing. Invasive cancer is further characterized by immune suppression.

**Detail:** CyCIF single-cell quantification across 44 specimens mapped progressive loss of cDC1, NK cells, and TRM CD8+ cells from STIC.I to STIC.C. IFN marker expression in the stromal compartment showed enrichment for IFN response and IL6-JAK-STAT3 signaling in early stages, consistent with immune modulation and paracrine signaling. STIC.C lesions are larger (higher epithelial cell count) and more numerous (median 5 vs 2 in STIC.I), indicating clonal expansion under immune editing. TGF-β and EMT pathways emerge in STIC.C stroma, further promoting immunosuppression.

**Genes / cancer types:** STAT1, JAK1, IL6, TGFB1, CD8A, FOXP3 (immune cell markers) · HGSOC

**Data availability:** `not-available`

### F5: IFN-related DNA damage resistance signature (IRDS) emerges in STIC.C, linking persistent IFN to therapy resistance

**Finding:** Chronic IFN activation leads to emergence of the IFN-related DNA damage resistance signature (IRDS) in STIC.C and invasive cancer, including upregulation of anti-apoptotic BCL-2 family member MCL1, STAT1, and MX-1. IRDS emergence may contribute to chemotherapy and radiotherapy resistance in established HGSOC.

**Detail:** Bayesian ordinal regression modeling confirmed upregulation of IRDS genes (MCL1, STAT1, MX-1) in STIC.C and cancer with a trend toward increased expression in STIC.I. IRDS was preferentially associated with HLA-E+ epithelial regions from the p53.I stage. IRDS has been linked to therapy resistance in multiple cancer types through sustained JAK-STAT pathway activity and protection from apoptotic cell death.

**Genes / cancer types:** MCL1, STAT1, MX1, IRF1, IFITM1 · HGSOC

**Data availability:** `available`

## Open Questions

- Does early HLA-E upregulation in p53 signatures represent a targetable vulnerability for NK cell-based immunotherapy or interception strategies?
- Can the spatial IFN activation signature or HLA-E positivity be used as an early biomarker for HGSOC risk in BRCA mutation carriers undergoing surveillance?
- What drives the transition from STIC.I (immune surveillance) to STIC.C (immune suppression) — is it clonal selection of IFN-high, HLA-E-high cells, or microenvironmental reprogramming?
- Does IRDS emergence in STIC.C predict platinum chemotherapy resistance in the subsequent invasive HGSOC?
- What is the role of the cGAS-STING–IFN axis in creating selective pressure for immune-evasive clones during precancer evolution?

## Limitations

- Preprint (bioRxiv); not yet peer-reviewed at time of data availability
- Retrospective tissue collection from multi-center collaboration with variable specimen quality and limited matched normal FT available for all cancer cases
- Spatial transcriptomics (GeoMx) measures bulk gene expression within ROIs, not true single-cell resolution; immune cell deconvolution approximates cell composition
- HLA typing and allele-specific expression not assessed; HLA-E functional impact inferred from expression data
- Small n for subgroup analyses (e.g., incidental STIC n=9); patient-to-patient variability is high
- BRCA mutation subgroup analyses limited by sample size; gBRCA2 and gBRCA1 not fully separated
- Causal ordering of IFN activation, CIN, and cGAS-STING signaling cannot be established from cross-sectional human tissue samples
