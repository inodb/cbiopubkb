## Summary

Among 2,069 MSK-IMPACT-sequenced prostate cancer patients (1,841 White, 165 Black, 63 Asian), tumor genomics differed by self-reported race even after adjusting for clinical features. Black men's tumors had more *AR* alterations, fewer *TP53* mutations, and significantly more chr8q gains (an adverse prognostic factor, +11 percentage points vs. White men). Asian men's tumors had more *FOXA1* class-1 mutations and *ZFHX3* alterations. Importantly, genetic ancestry largely mirrored race-associated genomic differences, but chr8q gains were also independently associated with community-level socioeconomic status — suggesting non-genetic, modifiable contributors to tumor genomic disparity. Clinical features (disease stage at sequencing) explained the *AR* differences for Asian men but not for Black men. Data are in cBioPortal as `prad_msk_stopsack_2021`.

## Key Findings

### F1: AR alterations are more prevalent in tumors from Black men, persisting after clinical adjustment

**Finding:** Oncogenic *AR* alterations were more common in tumors from Black men (18%) than White men (12%), with a 5 percentage-point difference persisting after adjustment for age, disease extent, PSA, stage, and sample type (95% CI: 1 to 8). For Asian men, the unadjusted excess AR alteration rate was entirely explained by more advanced disease at sequencing.

**Detail:** AR alterations are directly therapeutically relevant: AR is the primary target in castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC). The higher prevalence in Black men — independent of disease stage — suggests either earlier AR pathway engagement or a biologically distinct tumor evolution trajectory. BRAF alterations (not further detailed) were also more prevalent in tumors from Black men. Neither AR nor BRAF differences were attributable to germline mutations.

### F2: Tumors from Black men have more chr8q gains — an adverse prognostic factor also linked to socioeconomic status

**Finding:** 49% of tumors from Black men had chr8q gains vs. 37% from White men (+11 percentage points after adjustment; 95% CI: 4 to 18), an adverse prognostic CNA (HR 1.61 in White, HR 2.00 in Black men). Remarkably, chr8q gains were also independently associated with lower area-level household income (−2 percentage points per $50,000/year increase, adjusting for race and ancestry).

**Detail:** Despite fewer TP53 mutations (which typically drive aneuploidy), tumors from Black men were no less aneuploid overall and showed excess chr8q gains specifically. This apparent paradox suggests alternative pathways to genomic instability in tumors from Black men. Genetic ancestry (AFR proportion) mirrored the race-based chr8q association (+13 pp for 100% vs. 0% AFR ancestry; 95% CI: 4 to 23). Adjusting for income reduced the ancestry-chr8q association from 13 to 10 pp, quantifying the partial contribution of socioeconomic factors.

### F3: Fewer TP53 mutations in Black men's tumors — driven by fewer missense mutations, not truncating alterations

**Finding:** TP53 alterations were present in 19% of tumors from Black men vs. 30% from White men (adjusted −10 pp; 95% CI: −3 to −17), specifically due to fewer missense mutations (11% vs. 18%). Truncating mutations and frameshift rates did not significantly differ.

**Detail:** This finding is particularly notable because missense TP53 mutations (especially hotspot codons like R273) often confer dominant-negative or gain-of-function activity, suggesting qualitative differences in TP53 alteration biology by race. The lower missense rate is not explained by differences in tumor cellularity (no significant differences by race), stage, or timing of sequencing. Despite fewer TP53 mutations, tumors from Black men were not less aneuploid — they had more chr8q gains — indicating alternative genomic instability mechanisms.

### F4: Asian men's tumors have more FOXA1 class-1 mutations and ZFHX3 alterations

**Finding:** 27% of tumors from Asian men had *FOXA1* alterations vs. 13% from White men (+15 pp adjusted; 95% CI: 6 to 26), driven by class-1 mutations in the forkhead DNA-binding domain (21% vs. 8%). *ZFHX3* alterations (mutations + CNA) were also enriched in Asian men (17% vs. 7%; +8 pp adjusted; 95% CI: 1 to 17).

**Detail:** Class-1 FOXA1 mutations in the forkhead domain are early carcinogenesis events that remodel androgen receptor binding sites. These are functionally characterized but not yet therapeutically actionable. ZFHX3 is a zinc finger homeobox transcription factor that has been described in prostate cancer but is not yet directly targetable. The AR alteration excess in Asian men was fully attenuated by clinical adjustment (more advanced disease at sequencing), while FOXA1 and ZFHX3 differences persisted — indicating biological rather than access-to-care-driven differences.

### F5: Fewer PTEN alterations in tumors from Black and Asian men — relevant to PI3K inhibitor eligibility

**Finding:** *PTEN* alterations were less common in tumors from Black (14%) and Asian (14%) men vs. White men (20%; adjusted difference Black vs. White: −7 pp; 95% CI: −12 to −1), specifically due to fewer *PTEN* mutations.

**Detail:** Lower PTEN alteration prevalence is clinically relevant because PI3K pathway inhibitors (e.g., ipatasertib, copanlisib, capivasertib) are in phase 3 trials in prostate cancer and are enriched for efficacy in PTEN-altered tumors. Lower PTEN prevalence in non-White men may reduce their eligibility and expected benefit from such targeted therapies. Pathway-level analysis confirmed PI3K pathway alterations were consistently less common in Black and Asian men, consistent with individual gene differences in PTEN. No differences in DNA repair alteration prevalence, MSI, or TMB by race were found (contrary to one prior study).

## Open Questions

- What molecular mechanism(s) explain the paradox of fewer TP53 mutations but more chr8q gains in tumors from Black men — is there a TP53-independent aneuploidy pathway?
- Are chr8q gain rates in prostate cancer responsive to socioeconomic interventions (e.g., improved access to care), given the association with community income?
- Should prostate cancer clinical trials stratify by race/ancestry to assess differential genomic biomarker efficacy (e.g., for PTEN-loss-stratified PI3K inhibitor trials)?
- Do FOXA1 class-1 mutations in Asian men arise from the same early mutational processes as in European-ancestry populations, or reflect distinct exposures?
- How do prostate cancer genomic differences by race interact with treatment history (e.g., hormone therapy exposure), which may independently generate AR alterations?

## Limitations

- Black (n=165) and Asian (n=63) groups are relatively small; estimates have wide confidence intervals and may be underpowered for rare alterations
- Hospital-based (single MSK center) sample — likely better healthcare access than the general population, potentially underestimating race-related genomic differences in the broader population
- Area-level household income is an ecological measure (zip code), not individual-level socioeconomic status; actual mechanisms remain unidentified
- TMPRSS2-ERG fusions excluded from main analyses due to suboptimal sensitivity of DNA panel sequencing for fusions
- Germline alterations were included in all analyses — while examined separately, the relative contributions of germline vs. somatic to race-associated differences are not fully separable
- Hispanic/Latino patients not analyzed separately (grouped with White or not reported); other ancestry subgroups (e.g., within AFR, EAS, SAS) not distinguished
