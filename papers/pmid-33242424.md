## Summary

First large-scale proteogenomic study of pediatric brain tumors, profiling 218 tumors from 199 patients across 7 histologic types: low grade glioma (LGG, n=93), ependymoma (EP, n=32), high grade glioma (HGG, n=25), medulloblastoma (MB, n=22), ganglioglioma (n=18), craniopharyngioma (CP, n=16), and atypical teratoid rhabdoid tumor (ATRT, n=12). All samples from Children's Hospital of Philadelphia underwent whole genome sequencing (WGS), RNA-seq, and quantitative tandem-mass-tag (TMT) proteomic and phosphoproteomic profiling. Key discoveries: unsupervised proteomic clustering defines 8 clusters crossing histologic boundaries with distinct survival outcomes; a subset of pediatric CPs shows proteomic similarity to BRAF-V600E LGG (suggesting MEK inhibitor potential); WNT-active tumors have cold immune microenvironments; phosphoproteomic data reveal kinase activities invisible to RNA or protein alone; and CNV-RNA-protein cascade events identify context-specific functional amplifications. Genomic and clinical data available in `brain_cptac_2020` on cBioPortal; full proteogenomic data at http://pbt.cptac-data-view.org/.

## Key Findings

### F1: Proteomic clustering identifies 8 clusters crossing histologic boundaries with distinct survival outcomes

**Finding:** Consensus clustering of global proteomic data identified 8 clusters: Ependy, Medullo, Aggressive, Cranio/LGG-BRAF-V600E, HGG-rich, Ganglio-rich, LGG-BRAF-WT-rich, and LGG-BRAF-Fusion-rich. Clusters showed distinct overall survival, stemness scores, proliferation indices, and pathway activities — not always concordant with histologic diagnosis.

**Detail:** The Aggressive cluster, containing a mixture of EP and other diagnoses, had the worst survival outcomes. Aggressive EP tumors differed from Ependy-cluster EP more in proteomic than RNA profiles, and showed consistent upregulation of the PAF1 complex (PAF1, CDC73, CTR9, LEO1, RTF1) — a chromatin regulatory complex implicated in transcription and histone modification — not detectable at the RNA level. The Cranio/LGG-BRAF-V600E cluster co-clustered a subset of CPs with BRAF-V600E LGGs despite lacking BRAF mutations. Division of CP samples was invisible in RNA-based clustering, underscoring that proteomics captures functional biology beyond transcriptomics. Patient-wise RNA-protein correlation was lowest in these cross-histologic clusters.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRAF, PAF1, CDC73, CTR9, LEO1, RTF1, SMARCB1, CTNNB1 · Pediatric brain tumors (pan-histologic)

### F2: Subset of pediatric craniopharyngiomas proteomic pattern resembles BRAF-V600E LGG — MEK inhibitor hypothesis

**Finding:** Proteomic and phosphoproteomic data segregated CP into two subtypes (C4 and C8) without any genetic basis in BRAF mutation. C4 CPs showed upregulated MEK/ERK/AKT kinase activity and proteomic similarity to BRAF-V600E LGGs, while C8 CPs were similar to BRAF-WT LGGs. MRM mass spectrometry validated the C4/C8 distinction with high AUC.

**Detail:** BRAF-V600E mutation — an oncogenic event in some adult CPs — was not detected in any pediatric CP in this cohort. Yet the C4 CP proteomic signature mimicked BRAF-V600E LGG: MEK-regulated gene signatures were elevated, ERK1/2 downstream substrates were phosphorylated at higher levels, and AKT/mTOR pathway kinase activity was upregulated in C4 vs. C8. Importantly, these differences were not visible in RNA or protein abundance data — only in kinase activity scores derived from phosphoproteomics. This motivates a hypothesis that some pediatric CPs might benefit from MEK inhibitor therapy (used in BRAF-V600E LGG), potentially combined with mTOR inhibition to overcome resistance.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRAF, ERK1/2 (MAPK1/MAPK3), AKT1, CTNNB1, MTOR · Craniopharyngioma, Low grade glioma

### F3: Diverse immune microenvironments across and within pediatric brain tumor diagnoses

**Finding:** Cell type deconvolution from RNA-seq identified 5 immune clusters: Cold-medullo, Cold-mixed, Neuronal, Epithelial, and Hot. More aggressive proteomic clusters (Aggressive, Medullo, Ependy) had lower immune infiltration; LGG clusters had higher infiltration. BRAF-Fusion LGGs showed M1 macrophage enrichment; BRAF-V600E LGGs showed M2 macrophage enrichment. Epithelial (CP) cluster upregulated CTLA-4 and PD-1 molecules.

**Detail:** The Hot cluster (LGG + HGG + ganglioglioma) was characterized by macrophages, microglia, dendritic cells, and EMT pathway upregulation. Adenosine producers (ENTPD1, NT5E) were upregulated in the Hot cluster based on both RNA and protein, suggesting potential adenosine-targeted immunotherapy. The Neuronal cluster showed upregulation of glutamate receptor signaling (GRIA1, CAMK2A/B/G/D, PIK3CA, ADAP1) and decreased CD4/CD8 expression, suggesting glutamate-mediated immune evasion. Cold-medullo and Cold-mixed clusters showed WNT/β-catenin pathway upregulation only in protein/phospho data (not RNA), consistent with WNT-active tumors having cold immune environments. CP tumors in the Epithelial cluster expressed CTLA-4 and PD-1, raising the hypothesis of checkpoint therapy benefit.

**Genes / cancer types:** ENTPD1, NT5E, GRIA1, CAMK2A, PIK3CA, CD4, CD8A, CTLA4, PDCD1 (PD-1), BRAF · Pediatric brain tumors (all histologies)

### F4: Phosphoproteomics reveals functional consequences of mutations and CNVs invisible to RNA/protein alone

**Finding:** 1,541 genes showed simultaneous CNV-RNA-protein cis-cascade effects (CNV → RNA → protein change) in one or more diagnoses; 515 of these extended to phosphosite changes (CNV-RNA/Protein/Phospho). BRAF-V600E mutation in LGG produced significantly reduced BRAF protein abundance (not reflected at RNA level). CTNNB1 mutation in CP activated WNT-associated phosphosites on APC (Ser2812) and upregulated TCF25 (not TCF4) as the downstream transcriptional mediator.

**Detail:** Key CNV-driven functional events identified by this multi-omic cascade approach include: RABGAP1L (1q25) amplification — a CNV-RNA/Protein/Phospho cis-cascade gene in EP, independently predicting tumor progression; RAB3GAP2 (1q41) amplification — a cis-cascade gene in EP, MB, and HGG, important for neurodevelopment; FDPS (proximate to RABGAP1L) — a CNV-RNA/Protein cis-cascade gene specifically in HGG, linked to glioblastoma stemness maintenance. The same genomic region (1q25) shows different functional perturbations across diagnoses, demonstrating that histologic context modulates the protein-level consequence of identical genetic alterations.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRAF, CTNNB1, NF1, SMARCB1, APC, TCF4, TCF25, RABGAP1L, RAB3GAP2, FDPS · Low grade glioma, Craniopharyngioma, ATRT, Ependymoma, HGG, Medulloblastoma

### F5: Kinase-substrate co-expression network analysis identifies therapeutic targets across diagnoses

**Finding:** Phosphoproteomic kinase activity scoring and co-expression network analysis identified biologically important kinase axes: AKT/ERK signaling distinguishes CP subtypes; glutamate receptor signaling (CAMK2/PIK3CA axis) characterizes the Neuronal immune cluster; PAF1 complex phosphosites distinguish Aggressive EP. These kinase-level differences are not reflected in RNA or total protein abundance.

**Detail:** Kinase activity was inferred from phosphosite co-variation using a substrate-based scoring approach. For CP, differential AKT vs. ERK kinase activity between C4 and C8 subtypes was validated by immuno-MRM targeted mass spectrometry assays achieving high AUC for subtype classification — demonstrating clinical feasibility of phosphoprotein-based diagnostics. For the Neuronal immune cluster, CAMK2A/B/G/D and PIK3CA upregulation supports glutamine antagonism as a "metabolic checkpoint" strategy. For ATRT, SMARCB1 loss was consistently reflected in downregulated protein and RNA regardless of the specific DNA alteration mechanism (mutation, deletion, copy-neutral LOH).

**Genes / cancer types:** AKT1, MTOR, ERK1/2, CAMK2A, PIK3CA, ADAP1, SMARCB1 · Craniopharyngioma, HGG, LGG, Ganglioglioma, ATRT

## Open Questions

- Can MRM-based phosphoprotein assays (validated for CP subtyping) be translated into CLIA-certified clinical diagnostic tests?
- Do pediatric CPs proteomic-cluster C4 patients actually respond to MEK inhibitors (with or without mTOR co-inhibition), given the absence of BRAF mutations?
- What is the functional significance of ENTPD1/NT5E (adenosine pathway) upregulation in the Hot immune cluster — and do adenosine receptor antagonists have activity in these pediatric brain tumors?
- Why do the same CNV regions (e.g., 1q25 amplification) produce different protein-level functional effects in EP, MB, and HGG — is this driven by lineage-specific chromatin states?
- Can glutamate receptor signaling inhibition (glutamine antagonism) enhance immune checkpoint response in the Neuronal immune cluster of gliomas?

## Limitations

- All specimens from a single institution (Children's Hospital of Philadelphia), limiting generalizability and excluding rare histologies with insufficient samples
- Post-mortem samples (n=9) formed a distinct phosphoproteomic cluster, suggesting ischemia-induced PTM artifacts; excluded from phosphoproteomic downstream analyses
- Small cohort sizes within histologic subtypes (ATRT n=12, CP n=16, MB n=22) limit statistical power for diagnosis-specific analyses
- Proteomic and phosphoproteomic data not yet integrated into cBioPortal (available only at external CPTAC portal), creating friction for cross-study queries
- Low mutation burden in pediatric brain tumors limits variant-level analysis; most findings rely on CNV, expression, and protein data
- Deconvolution of immune infiltration based on bulk RNA-seq, not validated single-cell; microenvironment compositions are estimates
