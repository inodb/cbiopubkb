## Summary

This integrative genomic analysis of 97 high-grade urothelial carcinomas defines the landscape of potentially actionable alterations in bladder cancer. Using array CGH and targeted Sanger/mass-spectrometry sequencing, the study identifies that 61% of tumors harbor actionable alterations in the MAPK (35%) or PI3K/AKT/mTOR (35%) pathways, or in regulators of G1-S cell cycle progression (60%). Unsupervised clustering of copy number data reveals two distinct tumor subsets — high and low CNA burden — where *TP53* and *RB1* alterations co-segregate with the high-burden class. The predominantly non-overlapping distribution of actionable alterations within each pathway supports driver status and the rationale for molecularly stratified clinical trials. Functional validation demonstrates that PI3K/AKT pathway mutation context (upstream vs. downstream of the target) critically determines sensitivity to AKT inhibitors in bladder cancer cell lines.

## Key Findings

### F1: 61% of high-grade bladder tumors harbor potentially actionable alterations; MAPK and PI3K pathways affected in a largely mutually exclusive pattern

**Finding:** 61% of high-grade urothelial tumors harbored at least one actionable genomic alteration (defined as having clinical validation in another cancer type or having a selective inhibitor in clinical investigation). Within the MAPK pathway (35% of tumors), alterations in *FGFR1*, *FGFR3*, *ERBB2*, *MET*, *NF1*, *KRAS*, and *BRAF* occurred in predominantly mutually exclusive patterns in 2–13% of patients each, consistent with a single driver per pathway. PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway alterations included *PIK3CA* mutation (18%), *PTEN* deletion, *AKT1* mutation, and *TSC1* mutation.

**Detail:** *FGFR3* mutations (13%) and *PIK3CA* mutations (18%) were the most frequent individual actionable events. *ERBB2* focal high-level amplification occurred in 6.2% of samples, with concordant mRNA overexpression and 3+ HER2 IHC — a clinically validated drug target in breast and gastroesophageal cancer. *BRAF* mutations and *TSC1* mutations, though rare, are clinically validated targets (vemurafenib, everolimus). The mutual exclusivity within the MAPK pathway suggests these alterations have overlapping phenotypic effects, supporting the rationale for pathway-level drug targeting.

### F2: Unsupervised clustering identifies two CNA burden subsets; high CNA burden co-segregates with TP53 and RB1 alterations

**Finding:** Unsupervised hierarchical clustering of array CGH data revealed two distinct bladder tumor subsets — high and low CNA burden — that differ substantially in the degree of structural genomic aberration. High CNA burden tumors resembled serous ovarian cancers (among the highest CNA in all solid tumor types), while low CNA tumors resembled prostate and uterine endometrioid cancers. *TP53* (p<0.001) and *RB1* (p=0.003) mutations/deletions were significantly more common in high CNA burden tumors.

**Detail:** *TP53* overall mutation rate was 34%; *RB1* deletion/mutation co-segregated with the high-CNA cluster. The most frequent arm-level gains were 20q (41.2%) and the most frequent losses were 11p (36.1%). Focal amplifications included *CCND1* (11q13.2–13.3) and *ERBB2* (17q12). The most common focal deletion was *CDKN2A/CDKN2B* (9p21.3, 24%). Despite clear CNA subsets, the two groups did not differ significantly in recurrence-free survival, overall survival, or cancer-specific survival (p=0.98, 0.75, 0.49, respectively), indicating that CNA burden alone is not clinically prognostic in this cohort.

### F3: G1-S cell cycle regulators disrupted in 60% of tumors; E2F3 amplification is a lineage-specific bladder event

**Finding:** G1-S phase regulators were disrupted in 60% of tumors. *E2F3* amplification occurred in 21% of bladder samples, compared with only 4.9% of 1,932 non-urothelial epithelial tumors — making it a highly lineage-specific event in bladder cancer. *E2F3* or *RB1* alterations co-occurred with elevated E2F3 transcriptional activity scores. *CCND1* and *CCNE1* amplifications (14%, 5%) were mutually exclusive with *RB1* mutations/deletions.

**Detail:** *E2F3* amplification was associated with more advanced stage (28% stage III–IV vs. 6.7% stage I–II, p=0.028), implicating it in aggressive disease. Tumors with *RB1* mutations or homozygous deletions showed the highest E2F3 activity scores, while *CCND1*/*CCNE1* amplified tumors had lower scores — suggesting distinct mechanisms of G1-S dysregulation. Both *E2F3* amplification and *RB1* deletion/mutation were more prevalent in neuroendocrine-differentiated tumors (50% vs. 17% and 50% vs. 13%, respectively, p=0.03 and p=0.01), linking cell-cycle dysregulation to histological divergence.

### F4: PI3K/AKT pathway mutation context determines sensitivity to AKT inhibition — TSC1 loss confers resistance despite AKT inhibition

**Finding:** Functional cell line studies using MK2206 (allosteric pan-AKT inhibitor) showed that *PIK3CA*- and *AKT1*-mutant bladder cancer cell lines were highly sensitive, while *FGFR3*- and *HRAS*-mutant lines (upstream activators) were less sensitive, and *TSC1*-null lines were resistant despite potent AKT inhibition. The resistance in TSC1-null lines was explained by incomplete suppression of downstream S6 and 4EBP1 despite effective inhibition of proximal AKT effectors (PRAS40, FOX-O1/03).

**Detail:** In *PIK3CA*-mutant cells, MK2206 downregulated phospho-AKT and all downstream effectors including FOX-O1/03, PRAS40, S6, and 4EBP1. In TSC1-null cells, proximal AKT targets were inhibited but S6 and 4EBP1 were not — analogous to KRAS-mutant resistance to EGFR inhibitors. *PIK3CA* mutations (18%), *PTEN* deletions (present), and *TSC1* mutations were identified in both the tumor cohort and cell line panel. This highlights that patient stratification for AKT inhibitor trials in bladder cancer must consider the specific molecular basis of pathway activation, not just overall pathway status.

### F5: Genomic landscape of bladder cancer cell lines only modestly overlaps with primary tumor cohort, limiting preclinical translatability

**Finding:** Comparison of the tumor genomic profile with a panel of commonly used bladder cancer cell lines revealed only modest overlap. Notably, focal *ERBB2* amplification — present in 6.2% of primary tumors — was absent from all cell lines tested. Alterations in the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway were common in both, enabling functional AKT inhibitor testing. The limited genomic representativeness of standard cell line panels may explain why drug targets identified in bladder tumors fail to translate in preclinical studies.

**Detail:** The limited overlap highlights a critical gap between preclinical models and human disease. The authors argue that a substantially larger, better-characterized cell line panel is needed to model the full genomic complexity of high-grade bladder cancer. This finding has implications for drug development: relying on genetically narrow cell line panels may lead to false negatives or false positives for drug efficacy.

## Open Questions

- Does *ERBB2* amplification in bladder cancer predict response to HER2-directed therapies (trastuzumab, T-DXd) as in breast and gastroesophageal cancers?
- Can TSC1 mutation status prospectively identify patients unlikely to benefit from AKT inhibitors in bladder cancer trials?
- Do high- vs. low-CNA burden bladder tumors respond differently to chemotherapy or immunotherapy?
- Is *E2F3* amplification a viable therapeutic target, and do neuroendocrine-differentiated *E2F3*-amplified tumors require distinct treatment approaches?
- How do actionable alteration frequencies differ between primary and metastatic urothelial tumors (matched analysis not performed)?

## Limitations

- Targeted mutation profiling (Sanger + Sequenom hotspot panel) — not whole-exome or whole-genome; may miss non-hotspot driver mutations and structural variants beyond the 15 fully sequenced genes
- Array CGH-based CNA, not SNP array or WGS; lower resolution than modern approaches
- Neuroendocrine tumors included (10%) — may skew prevalence of TP53, RB1, and E2F3 alterations relative to pure urothelial cohorts
- No paired primary–metastatic samples; actionable alteration prevalence in metastatic disease not assessed
- Cell line panel genomically limited; ERBB2-amplified bladder cell lines unavailable for functional testing
- RNA expression from bulk tumor; intratumoral heterogeneity (squamous/sarcomatoid divergent differentiation in ~33% of tumors) not resolved at single-cell level
- Clinical outcome analysis underpowered; CNA burden subsets did not differ in survival despite biological differences
