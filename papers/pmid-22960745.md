## Summary

TCGA profiled 178 lung squamous cell carcinomas (lung SqCC) across WES, SNP copy-number arrays, RNA-seq, methylation, miRNA-seq, and WGS (n=19). With a mean of 360 exonic mutations, 323 copy number segments, and 165 rearrangements per tumor, lung SqCC is genomically complex. TP53 is mutated in nearly all cases (90%). Significantly altered pathways include NFE2L2/KEAP1 (oxidative stress, 34%), squamous differentiation genes (44%), PI3K/AKT (47%), and CDKN2A/RB1 (72%). Four gene expression subtypes (classical, basal, secretory, primitive) each associate with distinct genomic alterations. No recurrent targetable fusions were found, but potential therapeutic targets—FGFR kinases, PI3K, RTKs—were identified in the majority of tumors. The study also reported novel loss-of-function HLA-A mutations, suggesting immune-evasion relevance.

## Key Findings

### F1: Near-universal TP53 mutation and complex genomic instability define lung SqCC

**Finding:** TP53 is mutated in ~90% of lung SqCCs. Tumors display extreme genomic complexity (mean 360 exonic mutations, 323 CNA segments, 165 rearrangements) — higher than most other TCGA cancer types at the time. No KRAS or classic EGFR (exon 19 del / L858R) mutations were found.

**Detail:** CDKN2A inactivated in 72% by homozygous deletion (29%), methylation (21%), inactivating mutation (18%), or exon 1β skipping (4%). EGFR and KRAS mutations that drive lung adenocarcinoma are essentially absent in lung SqCC; this study validates why adenocarcinoma-targeted therapies are ineffective here. Two EGFR L861Q mutations were detected (known to confer sensitivity to EGFR TKIs). High C:G transversions at non-CpG sites consistent with tobacco mutagenesis.

### F2: NFE2L2/KEAP1/CUL3 oxidative stress pathway altered in 34% of tumors

**Finding:** NFE2L2 gain-of-function mutations (primarily in the DLG and ETGE KEAP1-interaction motifs) and loss-of-function KEAP1 or CUL3 mutations are mutually exclusive and collectively altered in 34% of lung SqCCs, constituting the most common oncogenic pathway activation after TP53 loss.

**Detail:** NFE2L2 and KEAP1 form a complex regulating cellular response to oxidative damage, chemotherapy, and radiation. NFE2L2-activating mutations are concentrated in the DLG/ETGE KEAP1-binding motifs, preventing KEAP1-mediated ubiquitination and degradation. This pathway alteration also occurs in HNSCC (classical subtype, larynx-enriched) and correlates with tobacco exposure and the classical expression subtype in lung SqCC. Functional PARADIGM analysis confirmed significant pathway impact.

### F3: Squamous differentiation pathway altered in 44% — SOX2/TP63 amplification and NOTCH1/2 truncating mutations

**Finding:** SOX2 and TP63 are amplified/overexpressed as oncogenes; NOTCH1, NOTCH2, and ASCL4 carry truncating (loss-of-function) mutations. These alterations are largely mutually exclusive, suggesting convergent disruption of squamous differentiation.

**Detail:** 3q26 amplification (containing SOX2, TP63, PIK3CA) is the most striking copy number difference between lung SqCC and lung adenocarcinoma (p<1×10⁻¹⁵). TP63 is predominantly expressed as the deltaN (p40) oncogenic isoform (89% of tumors). NOTCH1/2 loss-of-function in lung SqCC contrasts with NOTCH as an oncogene in hematologic cancers. FOXP1 focal deletion also contributes to the squamous differentiation signature. The 3q amplicon is also a hallmark of HNSCC, suggesting shared biology across squamous lineages.

### F4: Four gene expression subtypes with distinct genomic and clinical correlates

**Finding:** Classical (36%), basal (25%), secretory (24%), and primitive (15%) subtypes show distinct associations with copy number patterns, mutations, and methylation. Classical subtype has highest chromosomal instability and hypermethylation. Primitive subtype has highest RB1 and PTEN alterations.

**Detail:** Classical subtype: KEAP1/NFE2L2 mutations, PTEN loss, 3q26 amplification, highest deltaN-TP63, high methylation. Basal subtype: NF1 mutations enriched. Primitive subtype: RB1 and PTEN alterations enriched. FGFR1 amplification is anti-correlated with the classical subtype and with NFE2L2/KEAP1 mutations. CDKN2A alterations are common across subtypes without subtype specificity. iCluster integrative analysis confirmed subtype associations across platforms.

### F5: Actionable alterations in 64% of tumors — FGFR, PI3K, RTK families are key targets

**Finding:** 64% of tumors harbor a somatic alteration in a potentially targetable gene, with PI3K/AKT pathway altered in 47% (PIK3CA, PTEN, AKT3), RTK pathway in 26% (EGFR amplification, BRAF, FGFR amplification/mutation), and FGFR kinase family alterations frequent. Novel HLA-A loss-of-function mutations (8 cases) suggest immune-evasion and potential immunotherapy relevance.

**Detail:** FGFR1 amplification with co-amplification of WHSC1L1 is recurrent and was previously nominated as a therapeutic target (FGFR inhibitors). DDR2 mutations also present. The spectrum of EGFR mutations differs fundamentally from lung adenocarcinoma (no L858R/exon 19 del). PI3K/Akt alterations are mutually exclusive with EGFR alterations. HLA-A loss-of-function was not previously reported in lung cancer; combined with nascent data on PD-1/CTLA-4 immunotherapy, these mutations suggest that genotypic patient selection for immunotherapy is relevant. 96% of tumors have at least one mutation in tyrosine kinases, serine/threonine kinases, or PI3K subunits.

## Open Questions

- Do FGFR1-amplified or DDR2-mutated lung SqCCs respond to FGFR or DDR2 inhibitors in clinical practice? (Early trials were inconclusive.)
- What is the functional consequence of HLA-A loss in the context of PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy response?
- How do the four expression subtypes map to clinical outcomes and treatment response in prospective cohorts?
- Do NOTCH1 truncating mutations truly act as tumor suppressors in lung SqCC (analogous to HNSCC), and are there therapeutic opportunities downstream?
- What drives the absence of KRAS mutations in lung SqCC vs. adenocarcinoma despite shared tobacco exposure?

## Limitations

- Relatively small WGS cohort (n=19); rearrangement landscape (165/tumor) based on limited samples.
- Short median follow-up (15.8 months), limiting mature survival analyses.
- No recurrent targetable fusions found despite a hypothesis that fusions might be more common (later expanded cohorts confirmed this).
- Primarily North American cohort with 96% tobacco exposure; limited generalizability to non-smoker SqCC.
- Study predates immunotherapy era; HLA-A and PD-L1 findings were not prospectively studied.
