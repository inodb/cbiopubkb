## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing of 50 lethal castration-resistant prostate cancers (CRPCs) obtained at rapid autopsy — representing the most clinically advanced and heavily pretreated prostate cancer specimens studied at that time — plus 11 treatment-naïve high-grade localized prostate cancers reveals the mutational landscape of end-stage prostate cancer. The study confirms monoclonal origin of lethal CRPC, identifies nine significantly mutated genes (TP53, AR, RB1, PTEN, APC, MLL2, CDK12, ZFHX3, OR5L1), and makes three major conceptual advances: (1) CHD1 deletion/mutation defines a novel ETS-fusion-negative prostate cancer subtype (confirmed across >900 additional cases); (2) ETS2 is identified as a prostate tumor suppressor deregulated by both deletion (in TMPRSS2:ERG fusions) and somatic mutation; and (3) AR-interacting chromatin/histone modifiers (MLL complex members, UTX/KDM6A, ASXL1, FOXA1) are recurrently mutated in CRPC, with functional evidence that these interactions are required for AR-mediated signaling — implicating the AR-associated epigenetic machinery as a mechanism of CRPC persistence.

## Key Findings

### F1: CHD1 deletion/mutation defines a novel ETS-fusion-negative prostate cancer subtype validated across 954 cases

**Finding:** A recurrent peak of copy number loss on chromosome 5q21 centers on *CHD1*, encoding an ATP-dependent chromatin remodeling enzyme. Focal deletion or mutation of CHD1 is found in 10/119 (8%) prostate cancers in the exome/aCGH cohort, and this CHD1⁻ status is significantly associated with ETS gene fusion-negative (ETS⁻) status (Fisher's exact p=0.02). The association is validated across 13 additional DNA and RNA-based studies encompassing 835 more cancers: in total, 50/954 (5.2%) prostate cancers are CHD1⁻, of which 96% are ETS⁻ (p<0.0001). This establishes CHD1⁻/ETS⁻ as a novel, molecularly defined prostate cancer subtype.

**Detail:** The mutual exclusivity between CHD1 loss and ETS fusions (principally TMPRSS2:ERG) suggests these are alternative early oncogenic events that define distinct molecular subtypes of prostate cancer with potentially different biology and treatment sensitivities. CHD1 mutations were previously identified in 3/7 localized prostate cancers (Berger et al., 2011) but their clinical significance was unclear. The present study provides the first large-scale validation of CHD1 as a subtype-defining tumor suppressor. CHD1 encodes a chromatin remodeler that facilitates transcriptional activation through nucleosome remodeling; its loss may alter accessible chromatin at androgen-responsive loci.

### F2: AR-interacting chromatin/histone modifiers are recurrently mutated and required for AR signaling in CRPC

**Finding:** Multiple chromatin/histone modifying genes are found mutated or copy-number-altered in CRPC, including *MLL2* (significantly mutated at FDR ≤ 0.10; 8.6% of prostate cancers), *CHD1*, *UTX/KDM6A*, *ASXL1*, and multiple MLL complex members (MLL, MLL2, MLL3, MLL5). Immunoprecipitation of endogenous AR from CRPC (VCaP) cells demonstrates direct protein interaction with MLL complex members (MLL, MLL2, ASH2L), UTX, and ASXL1, while EZH2 (used as negative control) does not interact. Reverse IP confirms AR-MLL and AR-MLL2 interactions. siRNA knockdown of MLL or ASH2L inhibits androgen (R1881)-stimulated KLK3/PSA expression by >7.5-fold (each p<0.001), demonstrating that the MLL complex is functionally required for AR transcriptional activity.

**Detail:** This finding provides a mechanistic link between the otherwise unexplained recurrent mutations in chromatin modifiers and the central role of AR signaling in CRPC. The MLL complex deposits H3K4me3 marks at active promoters; its requirement for AR function suggests that androgen-stimulated genes need active chromatin marking through the MLL complex. Consequently, mutations in MLL complex members or their partners (UTX, ASXL1) could simultaneously impair AR signaling AND alter the broader chromatin landscape. This AR-epigenetic modifier axis also includes known AR co-regulators: FOXA1 (see F3), ERG (which directly interacts with AR via TMPRSS2:ERG fusion), and ETS genes.

### F3: FOXA1 mutations increase tumor growth and repress the AR transcriptional program — novel AR collaborator mutations in 3.4% of prostate cancers

**Finding:** A somatic FOXA1 frameshift (S453fs) is identified in localized prostate cancer sample T12 from the exome cohort. Extended screening of 147 prostate cancers identifies FOXA1 mutations in 4 localized cancers and 1 CRPC (total 5/147; 3.4%), with 4 of 5 mutations in the C-terminal transactivating domain. Transcriptome sequencing of 11 cell lines reveals additional FOXA1 indels in DU-145 (P358fs) and LAPC-4 (340fs). Stable expression of wild-type or mutant FOXA1 in LNCaP cells increases proliferation in androgen-present conditions, represses the AR transcriptional program (>50% of androgen-stimulated probes show reduced induction), increases soft agar colony formation, and significantly increases xenograft tumor volume (p<0.05). All FOXA1 mutants behave similarly — gain-of-proliferative-function with loss-of-AR-co-activation function.

**Detail:** FOXA1 (Forkhead Box A1) is a pioneer transcription factor that opens chromatin at androgen-responsive enhancers and is essential for AR binding to DNA. Paradoxically, mutations in this AR collaborator both increase growth and repress AR signaling — suggesting FOXA1 mutations redirect its chromatin-opening activity to activate growth-promoting genes independent of canonical AR signaling. This may represent a mechanism of AR signaling escape distinct from AR amplification/mutation. The C-terminal domain concentration of mutations (4/5 clinical samples + both cell line indels) suggests this domain is functionally critical for the interaction with AR that restrains proliferation.

### F4: ETS2 is deregulated as a tumor suppressor through deletion and somatic mutation in CRPC

**Finding:** In addition to the well-characterized TMPRSS2:ERG fusion (and ERG overexpression in ETS+ CRPC), the study identifies ETS2 as a novel prostate tumor suppressor. One CRPC patient (WA30, ETS⁻) harbors an ETS2 R437C mutation at a DNA-contacting residue in the ETS domain conserved among all Class I ETS transcription factors (including ERG, ETV1, ETV5 involved in prostate fusions). Additionally, focal deletions of ETS2 are found in ERG+ CRPCs with large TMPRSS2:ERG deletions extending telomeric to ERG, and WA31 shows focal ETS2 deletion with outlier ETS2 under-expression. In VCaP cells, wild-type ETS2 overexpression decreases migration, invasion, and proliferation, while R437C has opposite effects (increases all three), confirming tumor suppressor activity and dominant-negative behavior of the R437C allele.

**Detail:** ETS2 is located immediately telomeric to ERG in a head-to-head orientation in the commonly deleted region generated by TMPRSS2:ERG fusions through deletion. Prior speculation that the intervening region harbors tumor suppressors is validated by this ETS2 functional data. The R437C mutation is particularly informative: R437 contacts DNA in the ETS domain (confirmed by structural analysis of the homologous ETS1 R409 residue), and substitution with cysteine abrogates DNA binding and converts ETS2 from growth-suppressive to growth-promoting — a dominant-negative effect.

### F5: Lethal CRPC has a low overall mutation rate (2.00/Mb) but confirms monoclonal origin and multiple canonical driver pathways

**Finding:** Whole-exome sequencing confirms that even heavily pretreated lethal CRPC has a low median mutation rate (~2.00/Mb), significantly lower than typical carcinogen-exposed epithelial tumors. Three distinct CRPC foci from the same patient (WA43) share the same somatic mutations, confirming monoclonal origin of metastatic disease. Nine significantly mutated genes (FDR ≤ 0.10) include known prostate cancer drivers (TP53, AR, RB1, PTEN, APC) and novel genes: *MLL2* (histone methyltransferase), *CDK12* (cyclin-dependent kinase, recently identified in ovarian cancer), *ZFHX3*, and *OR5L1*. Multiple candidate driver mutations also occur in DNA damage response (*PRKDC*), spindle checkpoint, WNT/APC signaling, and classical oncogenes/suppressors.

**Detail:** The low mutation rate relative to tobacco-driven cancers suggests prostate cancer does not rely on extensive mutagenesis for progression to CRPC; rather, selection of specific AR pathway alterations, structural rearrangements (ETS fusions), and chromatin epigenetic changes drive lethality. CDK12 mutations (frameshift/nonsense in one localized cancer) are notable because CDK12 loss in prostate cancer was subsequently shown to create a tandem duplication genomic instability phenotype that sensitizes tumors to PARP inhibitors and immune checkpoint therapy. WNT/APC pathway mutations are identified as significantly disrupted, providing rationale for WNT inhibitor trials in CRPC.

## Open Questions

- Does CDK12 mutation in prostate cancer (identified here) create a "BRCAness" or tandem-duplication genomic instability phenotype that predicts PARP inhibitor or immune checkpoint sensitivity?
- Can MLL complex inhibitors (e.g., menin-MLL interaction inhibitors now in clinical trials for leukemia) impair AR signaling in MLL2-mutant or MLL-complex-dependent CRPC?
- What is the full repertoire of AR-interacting proteins mutated in CRPC — does the AR interactome represent a broader therapeutic vulnerability?
- Do FOXA1 mutations identify a subgroup of prostate cancers with distinct behavior toward ADT/enzalutamide — do they predict primary resistance or sensitization to FOXA1-targeted strategies?
- Does CHD1⁻/ETS⁻ prostate cancer have a distinct response to standard therapies (ADT, docetaxel, PARP inhibitors) versus ETS+ disease?
- Can the monoclonal evolution of CRPC (confirmed here) be exploited by identifying shared somatic mutations as tumor-specific neo-antigen targets for immune therapy?

## Limitations

- Rapid autopsy cases are heavily pretreated; somatic mutations acquired during treatment (e.g., AR point mutations under enzalutamide, which was not yet approved at time of study) may not be fully represented
- One hypermutated outlier (WA16) was excluded from some analyses — its hypermutation mechanism is described but not fully characterized
- WES may miss noncoding driver alterations (TERT promoter, AR enhancer amplifications) and structural rearrangements without WGS
- FOXA1 extended screening (n=147) used Sanger sequencing of selected regions — full exon coverage not guaranteed
- MLL2 nomenclature ambiguity (gene also called KMT2D in current nomenclature); the study uses older gene names
- No WGS on the full cohort — structural variants (inversions, large deletions beyond ETS fusions) not systematically captured
- Functional experiments use established prostate cancer cell lines (VCaP, LNCaP) which may not fully recapitulate the biology of rapid autopsy CRPC
- Cohort lacks racial diversity data; CRPC biology may differ by ancestry (e.g., African American prostate cancer)
