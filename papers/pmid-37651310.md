## Summary

Landmark MSK study of 1,882 prospectively sequenced endometrial carcinomas (ECs; 259 self-identified Black, 1,623 White) using clinical FDA-authorized tumor-normal MSK-IMPACT sequencing (up to 505 genes). Black patients had a dramatically different distribution of histologic types (68% high-risk: serous 29%, carcinosarcoma 20%) and molecular subtypes (69% CN-H/TP53abn vs 35% White; POLE only 1.2% vs 5.8% White). TMB was significantly lower (median 4 vs 7 Mut/Mb), and the proportion of patients with actionable alterations was substantially less (22.4% vs 39.7%). *CCNE1* amplification (15% vs 4%), *ERBB2* amplification (12% vs 3%), and *AKT2* amplification (9% vs 3%) were enriched in Black patients. Conversely, *PTEN*, *PIK3R1*, *KRAS*, *ARID1A*, and *CTNNB1* mutations were less common. Findings confirmed by genetic ancestry analysis. Data available in `ucec_ancestry_cds_msk_2023` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Black patients have dramatically higher rates of aggressive histologic subtypes — serous, carcinosarcoma

**Finding:** 68% of ECs in Black patients were high-risk histology (serous 29%, carcinosarcoma 20%, mixed/high-grade NOS 12%, G3 endometrioid 6.6%), whereas 47% of ECs in White patients were low-grade endometrioid. This distribution of histologic types persisted when primary and recurrent/metastatic ECs were analyzed separately.

**Detail:** Serous EC and carcinosarcoma together represent only 26–27% of ECs in White patients but over 49% in Black patients. These subtypes account for the majority of EC deaths despite comprising only 15–20% of total ECs. The 2-fold higher hysterectomy-corrected mortality rate in Black patients (compared to White) is thus substantially explained — though not entirely — by this overrepresentation of aggressive histologic subtypes. However, even within a given histologic type (e.g., carcinosarcoma), molecular differences persist between Black and White patients (see F5), suggesting biology beyond histologic subtype drives disparities.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, ERBB2, CCNE1 (enriched in high-risk histologies) · Endometrial carcinoma (all histotypes)

### F2: Molecular subtype distribution is markedly different — CN-H/TP53abn dominates in Black patients; POLE subtype nearly absent

**Finding:** 69% of ECs from Black patients were CN-H/TP53abn molecular subtype vs 35% in White patients (p<0.001). The POLE (ultramutated) subtype, associated with the best prognosis, occurred in only 1.2% (3/259) of Black patients vs 5.8% (94/1,623) of White patients. MSI-H subtype was also less common in Black patients (14% vs 25%).

**Detail:** CN-H/TP53abn ECs have the worst prognosis among the four TCGA EC molecular subtypes. The near-absence of the favorable POLE subtype in Black patients is particularly striking — it suggests that the molecular biology generating ultramutated ECs (exonuclease domain POLE mutations) is substantially less prevalent in this population. Within a given histologic type, molecular subtype distribution was similar between Black and White patients, except for the POLE subtype. This implies the molecular differences are primarily driven by the different distribution of histologic types, with some additional biological differences within subtypes (especially CN-H/TP53abn; see F5).

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, POLE, MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 · Endometrial carcinoma

### F3: TMB is lower and clinically actionable alterations are less frequent in Black patients

**Finding:** Median TMB was significantly lower in ECs from Black patients (median 4, mean 14 Mut/Mb) than from White patients (median 7, mean 24 Mut/Mb; p<0.001). Only 15% (39/259) of Black patients' ECs had TMB ≥10, vs 33% (532/1,623) of White patients. Frequency of OncoKB Level 1–3A actionable alterations (including TMB≥10 and MSI-H) was 22.4% in Black vs 39.7% in White patients (p<0.001).

**Detail:** Higher genomic instability in Black patients, measured by fraction of genome altered (FGA; median 30% vs 10%, p<0.001) and whole genome doubling (WGD; 32% vs 18%), reflects the enrichment of CN-H/TP53abn tumors. Within the CN-H/TP53abn subtype, TMB was still significantly lower in Black vs White patients (median 3.5 vs 4.4 Mut/Mb, p<0.001). The lower prevalence of clinically actionable alterations has immediate therapeutic implications: fewer Black EC patients are eligible for immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, dostarlimab) under current FDA-approved biomarker-driven indications (MSI-H or TMB≥10). This creates a health equity gap in access to effective therapy.

**Genes / cancer types:** (Genome-wide; TMB, MSI, FGA, WGD) · Endometrial carcinoma

### F4: CCNE1 and ERBB2 amplifications are enriched in Black patients — especially in carcinosarcomas

**Finding:** *CCNE1* amplification was significantly more prevalent in ECs from Black vs White patients (15% vs 4%, q<0.001 overall; 29% vs 10% in carcinosarcomas, q<0.1). *ERBB2* amplification was also enriched (12% vs 3%, q<0.001 overall), driven by amplification rather than point mutations (3% vs 3% for SNVs). *AKT2* amplification (9% vs 3%) and *KMT2B* mutations (16% vs 8% in carcinosarcomas) were additionally enriched in Black patients.

**Detail:** *CCNE1* (cyclin E1) amplification is associated with chemotherapy resistance, specifically to platinum-based regimens, and with aggressive clinical behavior. Its enrichment in carcinosarcomas from Black patients (29%) is clinically significant given that carcinosarcoma is already the most aggressive EC subtype. CDK2 inhibitors targeting CCNE1-overexpressing tumors are in clinical development. *ERBB2* amplification in EC is a Level 2 OncoKB actionable target (trastuzumab-based regimens), and its 4-fold enrichment in Black patients identifies a potentially therapeutic opportunity. *NF1* mutations were also enriched in carcinosarcomas from Black patients (12% vs 1%, q<0.1).

**Genes / cancer types:** CCNE1, ERBB2, AKT2, KMT2B, NF1, MED12 · Endometrial carcinoma (all histotypes; carcinosarcoma)

### F5: PTEN, PIK3R1, ARID1A, CTNNB1 mutations less common in Black patients — PI3K pathway altered differently

**Finding:** Genes commonly mutated in low-grade endometrioid ECs were significantly less prevalent in Black patients: *PTEN* (26% vs 55%), *ARID1A* (19% vs 47%), *PIK3R1* (13% vs 28%), *KRAS* (12% vs 21%), and *CTNNB1* (9% vs 17%; all q<0.1). Within CN-H/TP53abn ECs, *PPP2R1A* (15% vs 28%) and *PTEN* (6% vs 15%) mutations were less frequent in Black patients. *PIK3CA* alteration rates were similar overall (38% vs 46%), but the mechanism differed: amplification drove 12% of *PIK3CA* alterations in Black patients vs only 4% in White patients.

**Detail:** The lower prevalence of *PTEN* and *PPP2R1A* mutations in CN-H/TP53abn ECs from Black patients is noteworthy because co-occurrence of *PTEN* and *PPP2R1A* mutations has been associated with negative conditional selection (mutual exclusivity) and synthetic lethality, with mutual exclusivity linked to improved survival in high-grade ECs. The relative absence of these mutations in Black patients' CN-H/TP53abn ECs may therefore reflect a more aggressive molecular phenotype. *BRCA1* somatic mutations (3% vs 0.3%) were also enriched in CN-H/TP53abn ECs from Black patients. Serous ECs from Black patients harbored more frequent *MED12* p.G44 hotspot mutations (11% vs 0%, q<0.1) — a finding of unknown clinical significance.

**Genes / cancer types:** PTEN, PIK3R1, PIK3CA, AKT2, ARID1A, CTNNB1, KRAS, PPP2R1A, BRCA1, MED12 · Endometrial carcinoma (CN-H/TP53abn and endometrioid subtypes)

## Open Questions

- Do the higher rates of *CCNE1* amplification in Black patients predict differential response to platinum-based chemotherapy — and do CDK2 inhibitors offer a therapeutic opportunity in this population?
- Should *ERBB2* amplification rates in Black EC patients drive higher-priority testing for HER2-targeted therapies (trastuzumab/pertuzumab) in this specific population?
- Can biomarker-driven clinical trial designs specifically enrich for or stratify by race/ancestry to ensure equitable access to targeted therapies?
- What biological mechanisms underlie the near-absence of POLE-ultramutated ECs in Black patients — differential UV exposure, germline POLE variant spectrum, or other etiologic factors?
- Do the lower *PTEN*/*PPP2R1A* mutation rates in CN-H/TP53abn ECs from Black patients translate to worse survival independently of histologic type and molecular subtype — and can this be corrected for in treatment stratification?

## Limitations

- Single referral cancer center (MSK) with known patient demographics; Black patient representation (12%) likely underestimates actual epidemiologic distribution and reflects access inequities
- Self-reported race used as primary classification variable — conflates genetic ancestry, social determinants of health, and structural racism effects; genetic ancestry analysis performed secondarily
- No survival outcomes data available for this cohort in the paper; association of molecular differences with clinical outcomes not directly tested
- Restricted to Black and White self-identified patients; Hispanic, Asian, Native American, and other populations not analyzed (insufficient sample sizes)
- TCGA validation cohort had sample inclusion biases (predominantly surgical stage I, institutional selection) that limit generalizability of confirmatory analyses
- MSK-IMPACT targeted panel does not capture full somatic mutational landscape (no WGS); some copy number events at low-level may be missed
- Tumor microenvironment (TIL density, immune gene expression) not systematically assessed; differences in immune contexture between Black and White ECs remain to be characterized
