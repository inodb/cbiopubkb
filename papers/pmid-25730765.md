## Summary

Comprehensive whole-genome, exome, RNA, and targeted DNA sequencing of 65 infants (47 MLL-rearranged, 18 non-MLL-R) and 20 older children with MLL-rearranged leukemia (St. Jude PCGP) defines infant MLL-R ALL as having one of the lowest somatic mutation rates of any sequenced cancer — a mean of only 1.3 non-silent mutations in the dominant leukemic clone. Despite this genomic simplicity, activating kinase/PI3K/RAS pathway mutations are detected in 47% of infant MLL-R cases, yet are frequently subclonal and often lost at relapse — challenging the rationale for targeting these mutations therapeutically. In stark contrast, non-infant MLL-R leukemia in older children harbors significantly more somatic mutations (mean 6.5/case) with frequent epigenetic regulator mutations (45% vs. 14% in infants), implicating distinct target cell biology between infants and older patients.

## Key Findings

### F1: Infant MLL-R ALL has the lowest somatic coding mutation rate of virtually any sequenced cancer

**Finding:** Infant MLL-R ALL exhibits a median somatic coding mutation rate of 7.30×10⁻⁸ per base — 2 to 180-fold lower than any other adult or pediatric cancer reported at the time, with the sole exception of pediatric low-grade glioma (an indolent tumor). The dominant leukemic clone carries a mean of only 1.3 non-silent mutations, with only 0.6 expressed at the RNA level.

**Detail:** WGS detected an average of 111 somatic sequence mutations per case (29–229), with 8.2 affecting coding regions (2.2 non-silent SNVs + 6.0 CNA/SVs per case). However, 40% of identified SNVs had mutant allele frequencies <30% despite high tumor purity (average 92%), indicating subclonal heterogeneity. After removing MLL-related SVs and CNAs, the average non-MLL CNA/SV decreases to 3.0/case. This ultra-low mutation rate is consistent with the known short disease latency (some infants present at birth), in utero origin of the MLL translocation, and the oncogenic potency of MLL fusion proteins — which appear to require few cooperating mutations for transformation.

### F2: Kinase/PI3K/RAS pathway activated in 47% of infant MLL-R ALL — but mutations are predominantly subclonal and lost at relapse

**Finding:** Despite the ultra-low overall mutation rate, activating mutations in tyrosine kinase/PI3K/RAS signaling are detected in 22/47 (47%) of infant MLL-R cases. Recurrent mutations include *KRAS* (n=4), *NRAS* (n=2), *FLT3*, *NF1*, *PTPN11*, *PIK3R1* (internal tandem duplication), and *PIK3CA*. All activating alleles are expressed (100% RNA expression vs. 48% for other mutations). However, 65% of these activating mutations have mutant allele frequency <30%, indicating minor subclonal presence at diagnosis, and they are frequently lost at relapse.

**Detail:** Analysis of 7 matched diagnosis–relapse pairs (5 with a kinase/PI3K/RAS mutation): mutation maintained or increased in 2, lost in 2, decreased in 1. In MLL-AFF1 patients, those with an activating mutation are younger at diagnosis (mean 4.01 vs. 6.75 months, p=0.047), suggesting the mutation may accelerate but is not essential for disease. Novel findings include: (1) FLT3 N676K — previously identified only as a resistance mutation to PKC412 in AML; (2) PIK3R1 internal tandem duplication in the inter-SH2 domain, functionally validated (factor-independent BaF3 growth) as activating. Despite high expression, the prognostic impact was a non-significant trend toward worse EFS/OS, consistent with the subclonal nature of these mutations.

### F3: MLL rearrangements are predominantly unbalanced and complex; majority are accompanied by adjacent genomic losses/gains

**Finding:** More than half of MLL rearrangements are complex (involving ≥3 chromosomes and/or large insertions/deletions at breakpoints). Even cytogenetically "balanced" translocations harbor focal deletions and insertions at base-pair resolution. Only 10/22 cases encode an in-frame reciprocal partner-MLL fusion protein, and only 6/9 predicted in-frame reciprocal fusions are expressed. The predominant rearrangement mechanism is non-homologous end joining (NHEJ).

**Detail:** 133 total SVs/CNAs detected in the discovery cohort; ~67 are direct consequences of MLL rearrangement. Non-MLL SVs/CNAs most commonly affect *PAX5* (5 cases), *CDKN2A/CDKN2B* (3 cases), and non-coding RNA genes *DLEU1/2* (3 cases) — mostly monoallelic. A novel MLL-USP2 fusion (USP2 at 11q23.3, ~1 Mb from MLL) was identified by RNA-seq. Non-MLL-R infant ALL harbors novel fusions: *BRD9-NUTM1* (analogous to BRD3/4-NUTM1 in midline carcinomas), *ACIN1-NUTM1*, *BICD2-JAK2*, and truncated STAG2 — revealing biological links to NUT midline carcinoma biology.

### F4: Clonal evolution at relapse — relapsed disease emerges from a minor diagnostic subclone with additional mutations

**Finding:** Detailed analysis of two matched diagnosis-relapse pairs by WGS + deep sequencing reveals that relapse arises from a minor clone present at diagnosis (estimated 6.25% frequency), not from the dominant diagnostic clone. Relapsed tumors show markedly increased total mutation counts and CNAs/SVs compared to diagnosis. The founder relapse clone acquires additional mutations post-treatment while the mutational spectrum does not significantly change.

**Detail:** In INF002, five mutation clusters were identified at diagnosis (MAFs: 0.426, 0.236, 0.114, 0.058, 0.024) corresponding to 5 clones at 6.25–50% population frequency. The relapse clone descends from the 6.25% diagnostic subclone. INF001 follows the same pattern. This pattern implies that current ALL therapies eliminate the dominant diagnostic clone(s) but leave minor clones capable of re-expansion — and these minor clones may harbor distinct resistance mechanisms. The high degree of diagnostic clonal heterogeneity may directly contribute to the poor prognosis of infant MLL-R ALL (EFS 28–36%).

### F5: Non-infant MLL-R leukemia requires more cooperating mutations; epigenetic regulators mutated in 45% vs. 14% in infants

**Finding:** Non-infant MLL-R leukemia (ages 7–19; 9 ALL, 10 AML, 1 AUL) harbors significantly more somatic mutations than infant MLL-R ALL (mean 6.5/case vs. 1.3/case; p=7.15×10⁻⁵). Most mutations are present in the dominant clone (not subclonal as in infants). Crucially, somatic mutations in epigenetic regulatory genes are found in 9/20 (45%) of non-infant cases vs. only 3/22 (14%) of infant MLL-R cases (p=0.04), implicating a more permissive chromatin state in infant hematopoietic progenitors as potentially explaining the lower cooperating mutation requirement.

**Detail:** Epigenetic regulatory genes mutated in non-infant MLL-R leukemia: *CHD4*, *SETD2*, *CREBBP*, *L3MBTL3*, *ATR*, *KAT6A*, *KDM6A*, *NSD1*, *PARP8*, *SUPT3H*, *TET3* (across 9/20 cases). Kinase/PI3K/RAS activating mutations are equally prevalent in non-infant (50%) as infant (47%) cases, but are predominantly clonal in non-infant vs. predominantly subclonal in infants. *CDKN2A/B* deletions are more frequent in non-infant ALL (33%) vs. infant MLL-R (8.5%). Non-infant MLL-R cases also have significantly more CNAs (average 2.6 vs. 1.0/case, p=0.023). These differences suggest that age-specific chromatin states in the target hematopoietic progenitor cell — more permissive to MLL fusion-driven transformation in infants — underlie the divergent mutational landscapes.

## Open Questions

- Since kinase/PI3K/RAS mutations are subclonal and often lost at relapse, what drives relapse in the majority of infant MLL-R ALL cases — is it MLL fusion protein activity alone, or minor-clone-specific additional drivers not yet identified?
- Does the "permissive chromatin state" hypothesis (explaining why infants need fewer cooperating mutations) translate to differential sensitivity to BET bromodomain inhibitors, DOT1L inhibitors, or other epigenetic therapies targeting MLL-fusion activity?
- Can deep sequencing of minor diagnostic subclones prospectively identify which clone will give rise to relapse, enabling pre-emptive subclone-directed therapy?
- Do NUTM1 fusion genes (*BRD9-NUTM1*, *ACIN1-NUTM1*) in non-MLL-R infant ALL define a therapeutic subtype sensitive to BET inhibitors (by analogy to NUT midline carcinoma)?
- Does the FLT3 N676K mutation — a known resistance allele to PKC412 — respond to later-generation FLT3 inhibitors (gilteritinib, quizartinib) in the context of MLL-R ALL?
- What is the clinical significance of the MLL-USP2 novel fusion, and does USP2 protein function explain cooperativity with MLL fusion in transformation?

## Limitations

- Discovery cohort limited to 22 infants for WGS; validation used targeted 232-gene panel, not full exome or genome — low-frequency drivers outside the panel missed
- Matched diagnosis-relapse clonal evolution analysis performed on only 2 patients — insufficient to generalize clonal evolution patterns
- Non-infant MLL-R cohort uses WES (not WGS), potentially missing SVs and non-coding mutations
- Small patient numbers limit statistical power for clinical correlations (EFS/OS by mutation status is trend only, not significant)
- PCGP study (2015) predates comprehensive characterization of NUTM1 fusions in infant ALL — frequency of this subtype may be underestimated
- No single-cell sequencing — subclonal architecture inferred from bulk MAF clustering, which may miss complexity
- Functional validation performed only for FLT3 N676K and PIK3R1 ITD; most other mutations rely on literature-based classification
- Non-infant cohort includes both ALL and AML (mixed lineage) — biological heterogeneity may confound epigenetic regulator frequency estimates
