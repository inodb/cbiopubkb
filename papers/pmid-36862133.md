## Summary

The Make-an-IMPACT program enrolled 333 patients with rare cancers worldwide — primarily histiocytosis, female germ cell tumors (FGCTs), and pediatric malignancies — through direct-to-patient outreach via social media and disease advocacy groups. Free clinical MSK-IMPACT tumor genomic profiling was performed with return of results to patients and their local physicians. Of 250 patients successfully profiled, 18 histiocytosis patients received genomically guided therapy, with 17 of 18 (94%) achieving clinical benefit (mean 21.7 months). Whole-exome sequencing of ovarian GCTs revealed a striking haploid genotype phenotype rare in other cancers; actionable alterations were present in only 28% but included two cases with squamous transformation and high TMB — one achieving a complete response to pembrolizumab. This study establishes the feasibility of direct-to-patient crowdsourcing to define the genomic landscape of rare cancers and deliver clinically actionable genomic profiling globally, including to patients outside academic centers.

## Key Findings

### F1: MAPK pathway mutations dominate the histiocytosis genomic landscape; actionable in majority of cases

**Finding:** In 84 histiocytosis patients successfully profiled by MSK-IMPACT, *BRAF* mutations were detected in 33%, *MAP2K1* mutations in 13%, and *KRAS* mutations in 7%. Actionable fusions were identified in 4 patients including 3 *BRAF* fusions and 1 *TFG-ALK* fusion. An additional *PRDX1-NTRK1* fusion was detected by targeted RNA sequencing in a case with no DNA mutations.

**Detail:** The MAPK pathway alteration rate (>50% when combining BRAF, MAP2K1, KRAS, and rarer events) is consistent with prior histiocytosis literature and confirms the rationale for RAF/MEK-targeted therapy in this disease. *BRAF* V600E is actionable with vemurafenib (FDA-approved for Erdheim-Chester Disease). MAP2K1 mutations and RAS isoform mutations support MEK inhibitor use. The identification of rarer fusions (BRAF fusions, ALK, NTRK1) by DNA and RNA sequencing underscores the need for comprehensive profiling including fusion detection for this cancer type.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `MAP2K1`, `KRAS`, `CSF1R`, `ALK`, `NTRK1` · Histiocytosis (Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis, Erdheim-Chester Disease)

**Tool support:** `native`

### F2: Genomically guided therapy produces high clinical benefit rate in histiocytosis patients

**Finding:** 18 histiocytosis patients received genomically guided therapy identified through Make-an-IMPACT profiling. 17 of 18 (94%) achieved clinical benefit, with a mean treatment duration of 21.7 months (range 6–40+ months).

**Detail:** This remarkably high clinical benefit rate reflects the strong dependency of histiocytosis on MAPK pathway activation. The extended treatment durations (range up to 40+ months) suggest durable responses, consistent with published data for vemurafenib in BRAF V600E–mutated Erdheim-Chester Disease and MEK inhibitors in MAP2K1-mutant cases. Notably, these patients were identified through community outreach and many were treated by local physicians with genomic results transmitted remotely — demonstrating that decentralized profiling with return of results can translate into clinical benefit.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `MAP2K1`, `KRAS`, `ALK`, `NTRK1` · Histiocytosis

### F3: Ovarian GCTs exhibit a striking haploid genomic phenotype rare in other cancers

**Finding:** Whole-exome sequencing of ovarian GCTs identified a subset of tumors with haploid genotypes — near-complete haploid genomes with loss of one copy of nearly every chromosome. This phenotype is rarely observed in other adult solid tumor types and represents a distinctive genomic feature of ovarian GCT biology.

**Detail:** Haploid genotypes were identified by ABSOLUTE analysis of tumor purity and ploidy. The presence of haploidy in a subset of ovarian GCTs raises questions about the cell of origin, mechanisms of tumorigenesis, and potential therapeutic vulnerabilities (e.g., sensitivity to PARP inhibition or other synthetic lethal approaches). The rarity of this phenotype in other cancers makes it a potential diagnostic biomarker for ovarian GCT.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide ploidy · Ovarian germ cell tumor

**Data availability:** `available`

### F4: High TMB with squamous transformation in ovarian GCT enables immunotherapy response

**Finding:** Among ovarian GCT patients, actionable genomic alterations were rare overall (28%). However, two patients with ovarian GCTs with squamous transformation had high tumor mutational burden (TMB); one of these patients achieved a complete response to pembrolizumab.

**Detail:** Squamous transformation in GCTs is a rare and aggressive histological evolution associated with chemotherapy resistance. The observation that squamous transformation is associated with high TMB — and that pembrolizumab produced a complete response in one patient — is clinically significant for a disease with very limited treatment options in the platinum-resistant setting. This represents one of the rare success stories of TMB-based immunotherapy eligibility identification in a rare cancer context.

**Genes / cancer types:** TMB-high · Ovarian germ cell tumor (squamous transformation)

**Data availability:** `available`

### F5: Direct-to-patient outreach is feasible for rare cancer genomic profiling at international scale

**Finding:** 333 patients from 18 countries enrolled through social media, disease advocacy groups, and website outreach. 86.4% provided tumor tissue; 86.8% of those had sufficient DNA for MSK-IMPACT testing. 37% of patients were enrolled from 17 countries outside the US, including sites in Africa, Asia, South America, and Australia.

**Detail:** The enrollment funnel (359 expressed interest → 333 consented → 288 tissue received → 250 successfully profiled) demonstrates high conversion rates from community outreach to clinical-grade genomic profiling. The high technical success rate (86.8%) despite FFPE material shipped internationally is notable. Barriers to completion included stromal-rich histiocytosis tumors (23.7% technical failure rate in histiocytosis vs. lower rates in other subtypes). The program model — free testing, prepaid shipping, remote consent, international pathology review — provides a replicable template for rare disease genomic research.

**Genes / cancer types:** Platform-level finding · Rare cancers (histiocytosis, GCT, adenoid cystic carcinoma, pediatric)

**Data availability:** `available`

## Open Questions

- What fraction of histiocytosis patients with BRAF fusions or non-V600 BRAF mutations respond to RAF inhibitors vs. MEK inhibitors, and can sequencing predict this?
- Can longitudinal re-profiling of histiocytosis detect resistance mutations explaining cases without clinical benefit (1/18 in this study)?
- Is the haploid genotype in ovarian GCTs a marker of synthetic lethality (e.g., PARP inhibitor sensitivity) that could be prospectively tested?
- Do male GCTs share the haploid phenotype or high TMB in squamous transformation subtypes?
- Can the Make-an-IMPACT model be extended to other ultra-rare cancer types (e.g., adrenocortical carcinoma, thymoma) to define their genomic landscapes?

## Limitations

- Clinical benefit data for histiocytosis is observational and lacks a control arm; response attribution to genomically guided vs. prior/alternative therapies cannot be fully excluded
- High technical failure rate in histiocytosis (23.7%) due to stromal infiltration may bias the profiled cohort toward more tumor-rich, potentially less stromal, cases
- Germline analysis was not performed on Make-an-IMPACT patients, limiting detection of heritable cancer risk
- Enrollment via social media and advocacy groups may introduce selection bias toward more engaged, health-literate, or English-speaking patients
- WES was performed only on ovarian GCT (n=59); male GCTs and other rare subtypes analyzed only by MSK-IMPACT
- Treatment decisions were made by local physicians; uniformity of therapy selection and response criteria could not be controlled
- Study period predates routine use of KRASG12C inhibitors and newer BRAF/MEK combinations that might further improve outcomes
