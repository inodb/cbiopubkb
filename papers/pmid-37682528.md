## Summary

Largest single-institution prospective genomic characterization of FGFR2/3-altered urothelial carcinoma, integrating MSK-IMPACT tumor sequencing with clinical outcomes for patients treated with erdafitinib, plus longitudinal cfDNA (MSK-ACCESS) monitoring. From 1,507 sequenced urothelial tumors, 27.5% of patients harbored oncogenic FGFR3 alterations, with stark variation by disease state. In a real-world cohort of 32 erdafitinib-treated patients, ORR was 40% but mPFS only 2.8 months—substantially shorter than pivotal trial data—reflecting adverse real-world features including visceral metastases, multiple prior lines, and older age. cfDNA monitoring identified acquired resistance mechanisms: FGFR3 gatekeeper mutations (V553M, N540S), TP53 mutations, and AKT1 activation. A 26% rate of FGFR3 discordance between primary and metastatic tumors challenges the practice of testing archival tissue to guide FGFR inhibitor selection.

## Key Findings

### F1: FGFR3 alteration frequency varies markedly by disease state

**Finding:** Oncogenic FGFR3 alterations were identified in 27.5% of all sequenced urothelial carcinoma patients, but frequency ranged from 14% in muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC) to 43% in localized upper tract tumors.

**Detail:** FGFR3 S249C was the most frequent single alteration across all disease states. R248C was enriched in upper tract vs. bladder tumors (22% vs. 11%, p=.01). FGFR3 fusion carriers were younger (median 61.8 vs. 68.4 yr), less likely to smoke (44% vs. 69%, p=.005), and had lower TMB (median 5 vs. 9 mut/Mb, p=.0006) than FGFR3 mutation carriers. Only one actionable FGFR2 fusion was found, underscoring FGFR2 rarity. Female sex was enriched among FGFR3-altered patients (32.5% vs. 22.6% in FGFR3-WT, p=.0002).

**Genes / cancer types:** FGFR3, FGFR2 · Urothelial carcinoma (NMIBC, MIBC, upper tract, metastatic)

### F2: 26% discordance in FGFR3 status between primary and metastatic tumors

**Finding:** Among 27 patients with paired primary and metastatic tumor sequencing, 7 (26%) showed discordant FGFR3 alteration status—meaning archival primary tissue may not accurately reflect the metastatic genotype.

**Detail:** In four discordant cases, FGFR3 alterations were detected only in the metastatic biopsy (not the primary); in two, only in the primary tumor. One patient's primary and metastatic tumors harbored distinct FGFR3 alterations. Pre-treatment cfDNA detected the oncogenic FGFR3 alteration in only 70% (19/27) of patients whose tumor tissue was FGFR3-positive. This level of discordance far exceeds what is seen with EGFR in lung or BRAF in melanoma and underscores high genomic heterogeneity in bladder cancer.

### F3: Co-alterations with FGFR3: PI3K pathway, CDKN2A, and inverse TP53/RB1

**Finding:** In FGFR3-altered tumors, PI3K pathway alterations (PIK3CA 28%, TSC1 13%, AKT1 2.7%; 37% total) and CDKN2A (42%) are common co-alterations; TP53 and RB1 are significantly depleted (inverse association), suggesting mutual exclusivity as a biologically distinct FGFR3-driven pathway.

**Detail:** ERBB2 co-alteration occurred in 5% of FGFR3-altered tumors, with lower rates in NMIBC (2.5%) vs. MIBC (11%). The Log₂ odds ratio of FGFR3 vs. TP53 co-alteration was −1.8 (p<0.001) in both TCGA and the MSK cohort. Patients with baseline oncogenic TP53 alterations had a lower ORR to erdafitinib (22% vs. 40% overall) and 60% of primary progressors on erdafitinib carried baseline TP53 mutations.

### F4: Real-world erdafitinib outcomes markedly worse than clinical trial benchmarks

**Finding:** In 32 real-world erdafitinib-treated patients, ORR was 40% (matching trial rate) but mPFS was only 2.8 months (95% CI 2.6–5.4) and mOS 6.6 months (95% CI 4.6–17.3), vs. 5.5 months PFS and ~13 months OS in BLC2001.

**Detail:** 91% had visceral metastases, 47% had liver metastases, and all patients had received ≥1 prior therapy. Dose reductions occurred in 38% and interruptions in 50%. The most frequent grade ≥3 adverse events were mucositis (16%) and palmar-plantar erythrodysesthesia (9%). FGFR3 fusion carriers (n=4) fared worse—3/4 had progressive disease as best response. Only FGFR3 Y373C was associated with a high response rate (3/3 responded). These real-world outcomes contrast with BLC2001 trial data and highlight adverse selection bias in heavily pre-treated patients.

### F5: Acquired erdafitinib resistance via FGFR3 gatekeeper mutations, AKT1, and TP53

**Finding:** Serial cfDNA (MSK-ACCESS) monitoring in 27 erdafitinib-treated patients identified on-treatment acquisition of putative resistance mutations: TP53 (n=5), FGFR3 second-site/gatekeeper mutations (n=2 patients, 8 individual variants), and AKT1 E17K (n=1).

**Detail:** Novel FGFR3 V553M (gatekeeper) was acquired in two responders at progression; in silico modeling showed steric inhibition of erdafitinib binding at the FGFR1 paralogous V559 position. FGFR3 N540S (known to interfere with drug binding in vitro) was also acquired. Changes in cfDNA FGFR3 VAF tracked with radiographic response and progression. Patients who acquired new alterations had numerically longer time on erdafitinib (p=0.16). The relatively low rate of acquired kinase mutations may reflect treatment interruptions due to toxicity limiting the duration of selective pressure.

## Open Questions

1. Can FGFR3-isoform-specific inhibitors (e.g., TYRA-300, LOXO-435) reduce off-target toxicity and improve real-world tolerability and outcomes vs. pan-FGFR erdafitinib?
2. Does baseline TP53 co-mutation represent a validated predictive biomarker of erdafitinib resistance warranting prospective clinical study?
3. What is the clinical significance of FGFR3 primary/metastatic discordance—should all metastatic patients be re-biopsied or screened with cfDNA before FGFR inhibitor initiation?
4. Is there a survival benefit to erdafitinib in NMIBC, where TP53 co-alterations are less common and FGFR3 prevalence is highest?
5. How do the acquired FGFR3 gatekeeper mutations (V553M, N540S) influence next-generation FGFR inhibitor binding and could they be targeted by irreversible FGFR3 inhibitors?

## Limitations

- Retrospective analysis with inherent selection bias; real-world cohort had worse baseline characteristics (older, more visceral metastases, more prior therapies) than clinical trial participants.
- Only 32 erdafitinib-treated patients—underpowered for biomarker subgroup analyses (e.g., co-alteration effects on response).
- Not all patients had cfDNA collection at identical timepoints (COVID-19 pandemic disrupted schedules), limiting longitudinal analysis uniformity.
- MSK-IMPACT and MSK-ACCESS panels do not capture transcriptomic changes (e.g., FGFR3 expression levels, splicing isoforms) that could mediate resistance without DNA-level alterations.
- Discordance analysis based on only 27 paired primary/metastatic samples—larger paired cohorts needed to quantify the true impact on patient selection.
