## Summary

This prospective feasibility study at MSKCC establishes a clinical cancer whole-genome and transcriptome sequencing (cWGTS) workflow for 114 pediatric, adolescent, and young adult solid tumor patients, achieving a 9-day turnaround time. cWGTS identifies cancer-associated alterations in 99% of patients and provides oncogenic findings in 54% more patients than standard-of-care panel sequencing (MSK-IMPACT). The study defines 80× as the optimal sequencing depth for clinical WGS. Integration of germline, somatic DNA, and RNA data enables comprehensive variant prioritization — capturing all 13 germline predisposition variants and all 18 oncogenic fusions identified by panel assays, plus 8 novel driver fusions undetected by panels. Structural variants (SVs) and noncoding events — detectable only by WGS — account for most incremental findings. Data are available in cBioPortal as `mixed_kunga_msk_2022`.

## Key Findings

### F1: cWGTS achieves 9-day turnaround and identifies cancer-associated alterations in 99% of pediatric solid tumor patients

**Finding:** A fully optimized cWGTS workflow achieves a 9-day end-to-end turnaround from sample acquisition to tumor board-ready report — shorter than most clinical NGS panel TATs (2–4 weeks) — and detects cancer-associated alterations in 99% (113/114) of patients across all genomic variant classes.

**Detail:** During the developmental phase (n=59), median turnaround was 17 days (range 11–29), reaching 9 days in the optimized phase (n=16). Per-patient findings included: CNAs in 105 patients (92%), germline predisposition in 17 (15%), mutations in cancer-associated genes in 77 (68%), translocations/fusion transcripts in 27 (24%), disease-associated SVs in 75 (66%), and outlier TMB/MSI in 7 (6%). On average, 7,353 acquired mutations were identified per sample. The workflow required dedicated project management, lab operators, sequencing machines, bioinformatics pipelines, and high-performance computing with API integration into institutional and public databases.

### F2: cWGTS identifies oncogenic findings in 54% more patients than standard-of-care panel sequencing (MSK-IMPACT)

**Finding:** MSK-IMPACT identified therapy-informing biomarkers in only 24% (27/114) of patients, consistent with the low yield of targeted panels in rare pediatric cancers. cWGTS identified at least one additional cancer-associated oncogenic variant in 54% of patients (62/114), with 33 of these having direct clinical relevance (diagnostic, prognostic, therapy-informing, novel fusions, or germline).

**Detail:** Among 33 patients with directly clinically relevant cWGTS-only findings: 7 were diagnostic (21%), 15 prognostic (45%), 5 therapy-informing (15%), 5 previously undescribed oncofusions (15%), and 6 germline (18%). Only 27% of the incremental cWGTS findings could have been captured by WES + RNA-seq combined (10/62 by RNA-seq alone, 8/62 by WES alone, 17/62 combined), as the majority were driven by SVs in noncoding regions. WGS-MSK-IMPACT concordance for somatic mutations was ≥94%, with discordant calls attributable to intratumor heterogeneity (subclonal mutations) rather than assay failure.

### F3: Minimum 80× sequencing depth is optimal for clinical WGS — lower coverage misses subclonal oncogenic events

**Finding:** Benchmarking across coverage depths (30–40×, 60×, 80×, 100×) demonstrates that ≥80× is optimal for comprehensive somatic variant detection. At 30–40×, >91% of oncogenic findings are captured, but subclonal mutations (VAF range 4.3–31%) are systematically missed.

**Detail:** De novo variant calling was performed on 298 subsampled BAM files from 97 tumors with WGS coverage ≥60×. Detection sensitivity correlated with effective coverage (depth × purity): >91% of oncogenic calls recalled at 30–40×, >98% at 60–100×. The power to detect subclonal variants (median VAF=9.5%) was substantially lower at ≤60×. SVs showed slightly lower sensitivity than SNVs/indels at all depths. The study cohort achieved a median WGS depth of 95× (range 67–181) with median tumor purity of 64% effective coverage.

### F4: WGS detects SVs and noncoding events driving diagnosis and therapy in pediatric cancers — including 8 novel driver fusions

**Finding:** Eight novel in-frame driver fusion genes were identified by WGS + RNA-seq in patients with no prior findings by clinical testing, including diagnostically reclassifying fusions (PAX3-FOXO3 reclassifying ERMS as ARMS), a therapeutic target (NTRK3-SLMAP in neuroblastoma), and TP53 inactivation by noncoding SVs in 13/29 osteosarcoma patients.

**Detail:** Key novel findings: (1) PAX3-FOXO3 translocation reclassified an embryonal RMS to alveolar RMS; (2) UACA-LTK fusion in papillary thyroid carcinoma; (3) SH3PXD2A-HTRA1 fusion establishing schwannoma diagnosis; (4) NTRK3-SLMAP fusion in neuroblastoma — a potentially TRK inhibitor-actionable target; (5) EPC2-AFF3 and MAN1A2-ACBD6 in undifferentiated sarcoma; (6) CITED2-MGA in round-cell sarcoma NOS. TP53 noncoding SVs were identified in 13/29 osteosarcoma patients (only 3 reported by MSK-IMPACT), confirmed functional by correlated TP53 expression loss in RNA-seq — with direct implications for p53-modulating clinical trial eligibility.

### F5: Mutation signature integration with germline variant annotation resolves pathogenicity of DNA repair gene variants

**Finding:** Integration of WGS-derived genome-wide mutation signatures with germline DNA repair gene variant status resolves variant pathogenicity: MUTYH germline variant with 42% SBS36 attribution, and PMS2 germline variant with MSI-high hypermutation phenotype demonstrate functional validation of germline findings within a single cWGTS workflow.

**Detail:** Among three patients with germline DNA repair gene mutations: (1) MUTYH carrier (c.924+3A>C) showed pathogenic germline + somatic LOH with 42% SBS36 (MUTYH-associated) signature; (2) PMS2 carrier (c.538-1G>C) had MSI-high with hypermutation (TMB=11.23, indels=90,246, SNVs=17,840) and repeat-mediated deletions characteristic of PMS2 deficiency; (3) PMS2 VUS carrier showed low TMB and no PMS2 signature, supporting non-pathogenicity. An additional 12-year-old osteosarcoma patient outside the cohort was found to have PMS2 p.D699H (pathogenic) with somatic LOH and MSI-high after cWGTS triggered consent for germline testing. The MSK-IMPACT result had been indeterminate MSI (7.5), demonstrating cWGTS superiority for MSI classification in this case.

## Open Questions

- Can cWGTS replace multiple parallel clinical assays (MSK-IMPACT, MSK-Fusion, germline panel, methylation array) in a single workflow, and what is the cost-effectiveness threshold for pediatric cancer centers?
- Do the 5 novel oncofusions identified by cWGTS represent actionable therapeutic targets warranting prospective basket trial enrollment?
- Can WGS-derived TMB and SV burden reliably predict response to immune checkpoint inhibitors in pediatric solid tumors with high SV burden?
- What is the minimum tumor purity threshold that allows clinically reliable cWGTS reporting, and can spatial sampling strategies improve yield for necrotic/post-therapy specimens?
- Does routine germline integration with mutation signature analysis change clinical management decisions (cascade testing, trial eligibility) beyond what standard germline panel sequencing provides?

## Limitations

- 201 nominated cases reduced to 114 after pathology and purity exclusions (43% exclusion rate), biasing toward high-quality resection specimens
- Most excluded cases were post-therapy neuroblastoma and sarcoma with high necrotic content — potentially the cases where cWGTS findings would be most impactful
- RNA-seq was not available for all 114 patients (n=101 with RNA), limiting fusion and expression biomarker analysis for 13 patients
- No prospective clinical outcome data linked to cWGTS-specific findings — clinical actionability demonstrated in only two case examples (checkpoint blockade responders)
- Study was a single-center feasibility study; implementation costs, computing infrastructure, and specialized expertise may limit generalizability to community cancer centers
- Methylation-based tumor classification was not integrated into the primary workflow, limiting utility for CNS tumors requiring methylation diagnosis
