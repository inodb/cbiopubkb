## Summary

First large-scale WES study of 216 metastatic breast cancer (mBC) tumor-blood pairs from four prospective French trial cohorts (SAFIR01, SAFIR02, SHIVA, MOSCATO). Identified 12 significantly mutated genes in mBC; established ESR1 and RB1 as driver genes highly enriched in mBC versus early breast cancer (eBC). ESR1 was mutated in 14% of HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC (vs <1% eBC, OR=29, P=1.2e-12), with all mutations in the ligand-binding domain and all patients having received prior endocrine therapy. Eight genes were more frequently mutated in mBC vs eBC (FDR<0.01): ESR1, FSIP2, FRAS1, OSBPL3, EDC4, PALB2, IGFN1, AGRN. APOBEC-associated mutation signatures (Sig 2+13) were substantially elevated in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC (58.8%) vs primary TCGA (31.9%, P<2e-16). TSC1/TSC2 mutations were enriched in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC (6.3% vs 0.7% eBC). Genomic data are available in `brca_igr_2015` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: ESR1 is the dominant metastasis-specific driver gene (14% HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC, OR=29 vs eBC)

**Finding:** ESR1 was identified both as a driver gene and as a metastasis-enriched gene (FDR<0.01). 22 mBCs harbored ESR1 mutations (all missense mutations in the ligand-binding domain, plus 2 indels), and 9 additional mBCs had ESR1 focal amplification, for a total of 31 mBCs (27 HR+/HER2âˆ’, 19%) with ESR1 mutation or amplification. All 22 ESR1-mutated patients had received prior endocrine therapy.

**Detail:** ESR1 mutations included previously reported hotspots (Y537S, D538G) and 2 novel mutations. All mutations were in the hormone receptor/ligand-binding domain consistent with ligand-independent ER activation and endocrine therapy resistance. ESR1 mutations were predominantly subclonal (14/21 = 67%), suggesting acquisition under endocrine therapy selective pressure. ESR1 mutations occurred in 14% of HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC vs <1% of HR+/HER2âˆ’ eBC (Fisher test p=1.2e-12, OR=29). Focal ESR1 amplification added another 9 cases (7 HR+/HER2âˆ’). Combined ESR1 mutation+amplification affected 27/143 (19%) of HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC. The study confirmed ESR1 as the most frequent "metastasis-specific" driver in BC, representing an unmet medical need for novel endocrine therapies.

### F2: RB1 mutations enriched in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC (5%), predicting primary CDK4/6 inhibitor resistance

**Finding:** RB1 was identified as a driver gene specific to mBC: mutated in 7/143 (5%) HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC and 3/51 (6%) TNBC, versus <1% in HR+/HER2âˆ’ eBC (P=0.008, FDR=0.09). Most mutations were disruptive (5 nonsense, 3 splice site, 1 indel, 2 missense) consistent with loss-of-function. RB1 loss-of-function predicts primary resistance to CDK4/6 inhibitors (e.g., palbociclib) since pRb is required for palbociclib bioactivity.

**Detail:** RB1 mutations in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC were predominantly subclonal (50%) and included a homozygous deletion in 2 additional cases, for total 9/143 samples (6%) with RB1 loss-of-function alteration. The rarity in eBC (<1%) and frequency in mBC suggests RB1 loss is selected under therapy pressure. CDK4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib, abemaciclib) are now standard of care for HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC; this study was among the first to raise the concern that RB1 mutation status should be assessed on metastatic biopsies before initiating CDK4/6 inhibitor therapy.

### F3: APOBEC mutagenesis (Signatures 2+13) markedly elevated in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC vs primary tumors

**Finding:** APOBEC-associated mutational signatures (Signatures 2 and 13 combined) contributed 58.8% of mutations in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC versus 31.9% in HR+/HER2âˆ’ primary TCGA tumors (P<2e-16). This dramatic enrichment in APOBEC activity in the metastatic setting is specific to HR+/HER2âˆ’ subtype and may drive subclonal mutagenesis during therapy.

**Detail:** De novo mutational signature analysis identified 5 operative signatures in both metastatic and primary BC; none were metastasis-specific de novo. However, the contribution of existing signatures changed dramatically, especially Sig 2 and Sig 13 (APOBEC3A/3B-mediated C>T and C>G mutations at TCA/TCT context). The aging signature (Sig 1), HR deficiency signature (Sig 3), and MMR deficiency signature (Sig 6) were also present. APOBEC signatures were the most represented signatures in mBC samples overall. The metastatic enrichment of APOBEC activity is consistent with prior reports linking APOBEC mutagenesis to subclonal mutation acquisition under therapeutic selective pressure.

### F4: TSC1/TSC2 mutations enriched in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC (6.3% vs 0.7% eBC) â€” actionable mTOR pathway target

**Finding:** TSC1 and TSC2 (negative regulators of mTORC1) were mutated in 5 and 4 HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC samples respectively, for a combined frequency of 6.3% in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC versus 0.7% in HR+/HER2âˆ’ eBC (P=0.0004). TSC1/2 loss-of-function activates mTOR signaling and is potentially sensitive to everolimus or other mTOR inhibitors.

**Detail:** TSC1/TSC2 mutations were identified in the analysis of 278 genes more frequently mutated in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC vs eBC (FDR<0.1 enrichment analysis restricted to HR+/HER2âˆ’ subtype). Other actionable genes enriched in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC at FDR<0.1 included ERBB4 (n=8, missense including known COSMIC hotspots), NOTCH3 (n=7, missense+splice), ALK (n=7, missense+splice), EZH2 (n=3, including COSM220530), and BRAF (n=4, including V600E). Everolimus is already FDA-approved for HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC (BOLERO-2 trial); TSC1/2 mutations could serve as predictive biomarkers for mTOR inhibitor sensitivity in the subset of mBC lacking PIK3CA mutations.

### F5: Eight metastasis-enriched genes associate with 2-fold increased hazard of death (HR=1.97)

**Finding:** Eight genes were significantly more mutated in mBC vs eBC (FDR<0.01): ESR1, FSIP2, FRAS1, OSBPL3, EDC4, PALB2, IGFN1, and AGRN. mBC patients carrying at least one mutation in these 8 genes (n=76) had a 2-fold increased hazard of death versus the rest in multivariate analysis (HR=1.97, 95% CI 1.34â€“2.89, P=0.001), suggesting these mutations mark aggressive disease or therapy-resistant clones.

**Detail:** PALB2 (DNA repair, partner of BRCA2) was mutated in 8 (4%) mBC vs only 1 (0.1%) eBC (FDR=0.006); all 8 were somatic mutations with no paired deleterious germline PALB2 polymorphism, ruling out germline confounding. OSBPL3 and PALB2 were also identified as drivers by MuSiC and drGAP algorithms (FDR<0.1). Survival curves showed significantly worse OS for patients with any of these 8 mutations, independently of subtype. ESR1 and RB1 mutations were also predominantly subclonal, suggesting acquisition during therapy rather than truncal events.

## Open Questions

- Should RB1 mutation/deletion status be assessed routinely on metastatic biopsies before initiating CDK4/6 inhibitor therapy, and what fraction of CDK4/6 inhibitor failures in practice are explained by RB1 loss?
- Can liquid biopsy (cfDNA) monitoring of ESR1 hotspot mutations guide the timing of switching from aromatase inhibitors to fulvestrant or novel SERDs?
- What drives the dramatic increase in APOBEC signature activity in HR+/HER2âˆ’ mBC â€” does endocrine therapy exposure induce APOBEC3A/3B expression, and can APOBEC activity be suppressed?
- Are TSC1/TSC2 mutations in mBC predictive of everolimus benefit, and can this be tested in the context of existing mTOR inhibitor trials?
- Do the 8 metastasis-enriched genes (FSIP2, FRAS1, AGRN, etc.) have functional roles in metastasis or do they represent bystander mutations in a context of elevated APOBEC mutagenesis?

## Limitations

- Metastatic biopsies from multiple sites (liver, lung, LN, pleura, etc.); no bone metastases included (excluded by protocol due to tissue quality)
- Cohort from French multicenter trials with specific eligibility criteria (fresh-frozen biopsy, >30% tumor cellularity); may not represent real-world mBC population
- Biopsy timing varied (within a trial context, often at progression); not all patients had biopsy at the same treatment line
- Comparison to TCGA primary tumors (different sequencing pipeline) introduces potential bias; validated by showing 80% overlap in mutation calls on 33 shared samples
- Survival analysis limited by small subgroup sizes and potential confounding by treatment received after biopsy
- ESR1 and RB1 mutations were subclonal in >50% of cases; bulk WES may underestimate their frequency or miss low-VAF events
- Two capture kits used (SureSelect V5 and Clinical Research Exome); 50 unique mutations in 39 genes exclusive to one bait territory identified and confirmed as not affecting significantly mutated genes
