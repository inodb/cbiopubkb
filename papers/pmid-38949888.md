## Summary

Largest single-institution MSK-IMPACT sequencing analysis of MSI and TMB status in prostate cancer (n=2,257 patients; 3,244 tumors sequenced). MSI-H/dMMR occurred in 2.8% and TMB-H/MSS in 1.5% of patients. Both groups were enriched for Gleason grade group 5 and metastatic presentation. MSI-H/dMMR tumors had markedly higher TMB (median 41 vs. 15 mut/Mb), indel burden, and neoantigen burden compared to TMB-H/MSS. ICB (pembrolizumab) produced RECIST responses in 45% of MSI-H/dMMR patients vs. 0% of TMB-H/MSS patients; PSA50 responses were 65% vs. 50%. Radiographic PFS was longer in MSI-H/dMMR (median 38 months) than TMB-H/MSS (7 months). No genomic features distinguished responders from non-responders within MSI-H/dMMR. JAK1 mutations were enriched in MSI-H/dMMR (56%) vs. TMB-H/MSS (15%) and TMB-L/MSS (32%). Full dataset available in `prostate_msk_2024` on cBioPortal and AACR GENIE.

## Key Findings

### F1: MSI-H/dMMR (2.8%) and TMB-H/MSS (1.5%) are rare but biologically distinct prostate cancer subgroups

**Finding:** Of 2,257 prostate cancer patients, 63 (2.8%) had MSI-H/dMMR tumors and 33 (1.5%) had TMB-H/MSS tumors. Both were significantly enriched for Gleason grade group 5 (62% and 59% respectively vs. 40% in TMB-L/MSS, P<0.001). MSI-H/dMMR patients tended toward more metastatic presentation at diagnosis (37%) vs. TMB-H/MSS (16%), though this was not statistically significant (P=0.13 vs. TMB-L/MSS).

**Detail:** MSI-H/dMMR was defined by MSIsensor score ≥10 in 84% of cases; 16% required the combination of intermediate MSIsensor score (3–10) with deleterious MMR gene alteration. Three patients had oncogenic POLE mutations (P286R, V411M) with very high TMBs (183, 34, 169 mut/Mb) but microsatellite stable tumors — these were excluded from ICB analysis as POLE-driven TMB-H/MSS. Overall survival from metastasis did not differ significantly among the three groups (MSI-H/dMMR median 45 months, TMB-H/MSS 39, TMB-L/MSS 48 months; P=0.36), suggesting that MSI/TMB status alone does not confer a survival advantage without ICB treatment in the natural history.

**Genes / cancer types:** MSH2, MSH6, MLH1, PMS2, POLE · Prostate adenocarcinoma

### F2: ICB produces durable RECIST responses in 45% of MSI-H/dMMR but 0% of TMB-H/MSS prostate cancer

**Finding:** Among 27 MSI-H/dMMR patients treated with ICB (all POLE wildtype): RECIST overall response rate 45% (9/20 evaluable; 2 CRs, 7 PRs); PSA50 response 65% (15/23 evaluable). Among 8 TMB-H/MSS patients treated with ICB: 0 RECIST responses (5/5 evaluable had SD); PSA50 response 50% (3/6 evaluable). Radiographic PFS was substantially longer in MSI-H/dMMR (median 38 months) vs. TMB-H/MSS (7 months; HR=2.13, 95% CI 0.79–5.73, P=0.14).

**Detail:** The 45% RECIST ORR in MSI-H/dMMR prostate cancer is comparable to or exceeds rates reported for other non-colorectal MSI-H cancers (endometrial ~57%, gastric ~43%), supporting the tissue-agnostic pembrolizumab indication. The complete absence of RECIST responses in TMB-H/MSS despite a 50% PSA50 rate suggests PSA decline may not reliably indicate radiographic benefit in this subgroup. rPFS HR of 2.13 (non-significant due to small N=8 in TMB-H/MSS) suggests clinical-meaningful directional inferiority for TMB-H/MSS. An exceptional responder with MSI-H/dMMR achieved a complete response lasting >2 years with subsequent ICB discontinuation and ADT cessation; a subsequent prostate primary biopsy showed a clonally related MSS tumor (248 shared non-oncogenic mutations), indicating the ICB response was to the dMMR metastasis, not a new independent primary.

### F3: MSI-H/dMMR has higher TMB, indel burden, and neoantigen burden than TMB-H/MSS — likely explaining differential ICB benefit

**Finding:** WES of 48 tumors (20 MSI-H/dMMR, 28 TMB-H/MSS) showed: median TMB 27.39 vs. 25.02 mut/Mb (P=0.014); neoantigen burden 11.1 vs. 6.8 (P=0.011); indel burden 8.7 vs. 2.1 (P<0.001). Mutational signature analysis revealed that every MSI-H/dMMR tumor except one showed MMR single-base substitution signatures, while TMB-H/MSS tumors had heterogeneous signatures (MMR, aging, APOBEC, POLE, tobacco, and unknown etiologies).

**Detail:** The significantly higher indel burden in MSI-H/dMMR (4× vs. TMB-H/MSS) is mechanistically important: frameshift indels in microsatellite regions generate highly immunogenic neoantigens with peptides not present in the normal proteome. This may explain why MSI-H/dMMR produces more durable radiographic responses whereas TMB-H/MSS (where indels are rare despite elevated SNV burden) produces primarily PSA responses without RECIST benefit. The heterogeneous mutational signatures in TMB-H/MSS suggest that TMB elevation in these tumors arises from diverse etiologies that may not uniformly generate immunogenic neoantigens.

**Genes / cancer types:** MSH2, MSH6, MLH1, PMS2 (MMR pathway) · Prostate cancer

### F4: JAK1 mutations are enriched in MSI-H/dMMR prostate cancer; MMR gene alterations identified in 75% of MSI-H/dMMR cases

**Finding:** In the genomic comparison of MSI-H/dMMR (n=63) vs. TMB-H/MSS (n=33): JAK1 mutations were present in 56% of MSI-H/dMMR vs. 15% of TMB-H/MSS and 32% of TMB-L/MSS (P<0.001). Deleterious MMR gene alterations (MSH2, MSH6, MLH1, PMS2) were identified in 75% of MSI-H/dMMR patients, often with multiple MMR gene alterations, vs. 6–9% of individual MMR genes in TMB-H/MSS. ERG fusions were depleted in MSI-H/dMMR (3% vs. 12% in TMB-H/MSS and 0.2% in TMB-L/MSS; P=0.015). BRCA2/1 alterations were enriched in both MSI-H/dMMR (17.5%) and TMB-H/MSS (21%) vs. TMB-L/MSS (5.2%).

**Detail:** The high JAK1 mutation rate in MSI-H/dMMR prostate cancer likely reflects microsatellite instability-driven frameshift mutations at JAK1 coding microsatellites (a known mechanism in dMMR tumors). JAK1 mutations can theoretically impair interferon signaling, potentially modulating ICB response — though no significant difference in JAK1 rate was found between ICB responders and non-responders in this small cohort. ERG fusion depletion in MSI-H/dMMR may reflect distinct oncogenic pathway use, as TMPRSS2-ERG fusions define a major prostate cancer subtype driven by chromosome 21 rearrangements. BRCA2/1 co-enrichment in both high-mutational-burden subgroups may reflect homologous recombination deficiency as a shared contributor to genomic instability.

### F5: No genomic biomarker distinguishes ICB responders from non-responders within the MSI-H/dMMR subgroup

**Finding:** Exploratory analysis of MSI-H/dMMR ICB-treated patients stratified by RECIST response (responders: CR+PR, n=9; non-responders: SD+PD, n=8) found no statistically significant differences in frequency of any genomic alteration, MSIsensor score, or TMB between groups. Small differences could not be excluded given the sample size.

**Detail:** This negative finding suggests that within MSI-H/dMMR prostate cancer, neither the specific MMR gene mutated, the absolute TMB level, nor the overall genomic landscape predicts ICB benefit, in contrast to findings in other cancer types where TMB or specific genomic features correlate with response. The exceptional responder case illustrated that complete responses can occur even in patients with moderate TMB (18 mut/Mb, MSIsensor 26). The lack of predictors highlights a gap in MSI-H prostate cancer management: all patients with MSI-H/dMMR currently receive ICB (per FDA approval), but ~55% will not have a RECIST response. PD-L1 expression, tumor microenvironment composition, and HLA diversity were not assessed in this study and may be informative.

**Genes / cancer types:** MSH2, MSH6, MLH1, PMS2, POLE · Prostate cancer (MSI-H/dMMR ICB-treated)

## Open Questions

1. What are the immune microenvironment features (PD-L1 expression, TIL density, HLA diversity) that differentiate MSI-H/dMMR responders from non-responders in prostate cancer?
2. Should pembrolizumab be combined with androgen deprivation therapy, PARP inhibitors, or anti-CTLA-4 therapy to improve the ~55% non-response rate in MSI-H/dMMR prostate cancer?
3. Is the JAK1 mutation enrichment in MSI-H/dMMR prostate cancer a mechanism of primary ICB resistance, and can it be targeted with JAK inhibitors in combination with pembrolizumab?
4. What proportion of TMB-H/MSS prostate cancer patients benefit from ICB in larger prospective cohorts, and are there molecular features that enrich for PSA vs. radiographic response?
5. Can WES-derived neoantigen burden or mutational signature analysis better stratify ICB candidates than MSIsensor score or IMPACT-based TMB alone?

## Limitations

- Small ICB-treated cohorts (27 MSI-H/dMMR, 8 TMB-H/MSS) limit statistical power for comparisons of response and survival, particularly for rPFS HR confidence intervals.
- Observational retrospective design with heterogeneous ICB regimens (pembrolizumab mono, combination with IDO inhibitor, other checkpoint inhibitors) may introduce confounding.
- RECIST evaluability limited by absence of measurable disease at baseline in some patients (7/27 MSI-H/dMMR not RECIST evaluable).
- No PD-L1 IHC, HLA typing, RNA-seq (TIL quantification), or tumor microenvironment data limits mechanistic interpretation of response/resistance.
- POLE mutations excluded from ICB analysis, but their exclusion may have missed borderline TMB-H cases driven by POLE in MSS tumors.
- MSK-IMPACT is a targeted panel — structural variants outside of covered genes and non-coding mutations contributing to TMB are not captured.
