## Summary

Whole exome sequencing of 50 muscle-invasive urothelial carcinoma patients who received neoadjuvant cisplatin-based chemotherapy — 25 complete pathologic responders (pT0/pTis) vs. 25 non-responders (≥pT2) — identifies somatic *ERCC2* mutations as the only gene significantly enriched in cisplatin responders (q=0.007). All nine ERCC2 non-synonymous somatic mutations occurred exclusively in responders. Functional validation in an ERCC2-deficient cell line demonstrates that the identified mutations fail to rescue cisplatin or UV sensitivity, confirming loss-of-function. This study establishes the first genomic biomarker of cisplatin response in bladder cancer and defines a nucleotide excision repair (NER) deficiency model for cisplatin hypersensitivity, with implications for selecting patients most likely to benefit from neoadjuvant chemotherapy.

## Key Findings

### F1: Somatic ERCC2 mutations are the only significant genomic predictor of cisplatin response in muscle-invasive urothelial carcinoma

**Finding:** Among 3,277 genes with at least one possibly damaging somatic alteration, *ERCC2* is the only gene significantly enriched in the cisplatin responder cohort (q=0.007 by Benjamini-Hochberg FDR). All nine ERCC2 non-synonymous somatic mutations occurred exclusively in the 25 responders (P<0.001, Fisher's exact test), and enrichment remained significant after adjustment for the elevated overall mutation rate in responders (P=0.04, binomial test).

**Detail:** ERCC2 encodes the XPD helicase subunit of TFIIH, which is essential for nucleotide excision repair (NER) of bulky DNA adducts including cisplatin-induced intrastrand crosslinks. Responders had a significantly higher median mutation rate (9.7 vs. 4.4 mut/Mb), suggesting global NER impairment. The four cohort-wide significantly mutated genes (TP53, RB1, KDM6A, ARID1A) did not discriminate responders from non-responders. ERCC2 mutation frequency in the responder cohort (36%) was significantly enriched vs. unselected TCGA (7–12%) and Chinese bladder cancer cohorts.

### F2: Identified ERCC2 mutations cluster in conserved helicase domains and functionally abolish NER capacity

**Finding:** All somatic ERCC2 mutations in cisplatin responders occur at highly conserved amino acid positions within or immediately adjacent to the helicase domains of the XPD protein. In a functional assay, none of the five tested ERCC2 mutants rescued cisplatin sensitivity in an ERCC2-deficient XP cell line, confirming loss-of-function.

**Detail:** Wild-type ERCC2 expression in the ERCC2-deficient parent cell line (GM08207) significantly increased cisplatin IC50 (rescued sensitivity), while each of five mutant ERCC2 variants failed to do so (P<0.0001 vs. WT; ANOVA). The mutant cell lines were also not significantly different from the ERCC2-deficient parent in UV sensitivity, confirming broad NER deficiency. Mutation positions overlap with germline XP (xeroderma pigmentosum) mutations in helicase domains, but contrast with trichothiodystrophy mutations (distributed throughout the protein), which alter ERCC2's transcription role but not NER. This mechanistic specificity supports a NER-loss model of cisplatin hypersensitivity.

### F3: Elevated overall mutation rate in cisplatin responders suggests global NER deficiency as the underlying mechanism

**Finding:** Cisplatin responders have a significantly higher overall somatic mutation rate (median 9.7 mut/Mb) compared to non-responders (median 4.4 mut/Mb; P=0.0003, Mann-Whitney), and ERCC2-mutant tumors specifically have elevated background mutation rates (median 15.5 vs. 5.1 mut/Mb for ERCC2-wildtype; P=0.01). This pattern is consistent with a global NER deficiency that simultaneously causes cisplatin sensitivity and allows accumulation of additional somatic mutations.

**Detail:** The elevated mutation rate in responders raised the a priori concern that ERCC2 enrichment might be an artifact of higher mutational burden rather than a specific biological signal. The enrichment analysis was therefore adjusted for overall mutation rate (binomial test), and ERCC2 remained significantly enriched (P=0.04), ruling out a simple mutation-rate confound. The "hypermutation → NER defect → cisplatin sensitivity" mechanistic chain mirrors observations in BRCA1/2-deficient ovarian cancer (HRR defect → cisplatin/PARP inhibitor sensitivity), establishing NER deficiency as a druggability principle in bladder cancer.

### F4: ERCC2 mutation status is predictive but not prognostic — does not affect survival in unselected TCGA bladder cancer

**Finding:** In the TCGA urothelial carcinoma cohort (n=130), ERCC2 mutation status shows no significant association with overall survival (P=0.77, Log-Rank), demonstrating that ERCC2 is a predictive biomarker of cisplatin response specifically in the context of cisplatin treatment, rather than a general prognostic factor.

**Detail:** The TCGA cohort is a mixed population with various treatment histories, diluting any cisplatin-specific survival signal. This predictive-vs.-prognostic distinction is clinically critical: ERCC2 mutation should be used to select cisplatin-based therapy, not to stratify prognosis independent of treatment. In the 19 tumor types queried from TCGA (n=4,429 total), ERCC2 mutations are observed at low frequencies (<4%) in 11 other cancer types, suggesting bladder cancer is a privileged context for ERCC2 mutation-driven NER deficiency.

### F5: TP53, RB1, KDM6A, ARID1A are the significantly mutated genes in urothelial carcinoma — consistent with prior reports but not response-associated

**Finding:** Cohort-wide MutSigCV analysis identifies four significantly mutated genes in the aggregate 50-patient cohort: TP53, RB1, KDM6A, and ARID1A — all previously reported in bladder cancer — with neither enrichment in responders nor non-responders, confirming that standard bladder cancer drivers do not predict cisplatin response.

**Detail:** The mutation landscape of the 50-patient cohort is consistent with that reported in TCGA BLCA. TP53 mutations were found across both responders and non-responders. KDM6A (encoding a histone demethylase on chromosome X) and ARID1A (SWI/SNF chromatin remodeler) mutations reflect the chromatin/epigenetic remodeling bias characteristic of urothelial carcinoma. The failure of these drivers to predict cisplatin response highlights the need for functional pathway analysis (NER activity) rather than oncogene/tumor suppressor mutation profiling alone.

## Open Questions

- Can ERCC2 mutation status prospectively select muscle-invasive bladder cancer patients for cisplatin-based neoadjuvant chemotherapy in clinical trials?
- Do mutations in other NER genes (ERCC1, ERCC3, XPC, XPA) also predict cisplatin sensitivity, or is ERCC2's helicase role uniquely critical?
- Does ERCC2 mutation status predict response to immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors), given the elevated tumor mutation burden in ERCC2-mutant tumors?
- Is the NER-deficiency cisplatin sensitivity model transferable to other platinum-sensitive cancers (ovarian, NSCLC, head and neck)?
- Can ERCC2 mutation be detected in cell-free DNA or urine tumor DNA as a non-invasive predictive biomarker?
- Do ERCC2-mutant tumors that initially respond to cisplatin develop resistance, and what are the mechanisms (ERCC2 secondary mutations, compensatory NER pathway activation)?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=50) with only 25 responders; limited power for additional biomarker discovery beyond ERCC2
- Retrospective extreme-responder/non-responder design; not a prospective randomized trial
- Multiple cisplatin-based regimens used (GC, ddMVAC, GC-sunitinib); heterogeneity may confound attribution
- Functional validation performed on only five of nine ERCC2 mutations; the remaining four are inferred to be loss-of-function by structural analogy
- ERCC2 mutation does not predict response in all cases (36% of responders mutated; 64% lack ERCC2 mutations — other mechanisms explain their response)
- No RNA-seq or proteomic data; NER pathway gene expression not measured
- Short median follow-up (351 ± 363 days) limits survival analysis within this study cohort
- ERCC2 mutation status was not prospectively measured or used to guide therapy in this retrospective cohort
