## Summary

This foundational pan-cancer study from the TCGA PanCanAtlas initiative analysed 10 canonical oncogenic signalling pathways across 9,125 tumours spanning 33 cancer types and 64 molecular subtypes. Integrating somatic mutations, copy-number alterations, gene fusions, DNA methylation, and mRNA expression, the study charted pathway alteration frequencies, patterns of mutual exclusivity and co-occurrence, and therapeutic actionability across the entire TCGA cohort. Eighty-nine percent of tumours had at least one driver alteration in these pathways; 57% had at least one actionable alteration. Thirty percent of tumours carried multiple targetable alterations, underscoring the potential for combination therapy. All data are accessible through cBioPortal at `http://cbioportal.org/pancanatlas`.

## Key Findings

### F1: RTK-RAS is the most frequently altered pathway (46% of tumours), with KRAS as the top individual gene (9%)

**Finding:** The RTK-RAS pathway is altered in a median of 46% of samples across all 33 cancer types — the highest median of all 10 pathways. *KRAS* (9% across all samples), *BRAF* (7%), and *EGFR* (4%) are the most frequently altered individual genes. Tumour-type specific frequencies range from melanoma (95% RTK-RAS altered) to uveal melanoma (<5%).

**Detail:** *KRAS* alterations dominate in pancreatic cancer (72%), genomically stable colorectal cancer (69%), and lung adenocarcinoma (33%). *BRAF* hotspot mutations (predominantly V600E) predominate in melanoma (51%) and thyroid carcinoma (62%). *EGFR* alterations are most common in glioblastoma (50%), IDH-wild-type low-grade glioma (52%), and lung adenocarcinoma (13%). Rare recurrent *SOS1* hotspot mutations (A90V/T, N233Y/S) were identified in ~1% of lung adenocarcinomas and uterine carcinomas — a novel finding enabled by the large pan-cancer sample size. Within the RTK-RAS pathway, *EGFR* amplification was significantly mutually exclusive with *KRAS*, *BRAF*, *NF1*, and *RASA1* alterations, confirming functional redundancy in RAS pathway activation.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `BRAF`, `EGFR`, `ERBB2`, `NF1`, `NRAS`, `SOS1` · Pan-cancer (especially pancreatic, colorectal, lung, melanoma, thyroid, glioma)

### F2: 89% of tumours carry at least one pathway driver; 57% have an actionable alteration

**Finding:** Across all 9,125 tumours and 10 pathways, 89% harboured at least one driver alteration in a canonical signalling pathway. When OncoKB clinical actionability annotation was applied, 57% of tumours had at least one potentially actionable alteration — including genes outside the 10 pathways (notably BRCA1/2 and IDH1/2).

**Detail:** Standard-of-care (Level 1–2) actionable alterations were most prevalent in Her2-enriched breast cancer, melanoma (BRAF V600E → vemurafenib/dabrafenib), and chromosomally unstable esophagogastric cancer (ERBB2 → trastuzumab). The highest frequency of investigational (Level 3A) biomarkers was in luminal A breast cancer (PIK3CA, AKT1, ERBB2 mutations → PI3K pathway inhibitors). Uveal melanoma (2.5%) and testicular non-seminoma (8.5%) had the fewest actionable alterations. The 57% figure represents a lower bound, as the OncoKB database has continued to expand since 2018 and additional actionable alterations have since been approved.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer · All 33 TCGA cancer types

### F3: 30% of tumours carry multiple targetable alterations — opportunity for combination therapy

**Finding:** Thirty percent of TCGA tumours carried two or more potentially targetable alterations, indicating potential for combination therapeutic strategies. MSI-H and POLE-mutated subtypes had the highest frequencies of multiple actionable alterations. In non-hypermutated tumours, the most common drug-target combinations were CDK4 + MDM2 inhibitors (especially in dedifferentiated liposarcoma, 78%), HER2 + PI3K inhibitors (Her2-enriched breast cancer, 17%), and PI3K + MEK inhibitors (EBV+ stomach cancer, 10%).

**Detail:** CDK4 and MDM2 are co-amplified from chromosome 12q14 in dedifferentiated liposarcoma, making the CDK4+MDM2 inhibitor combination (e.g., palbociclib + milademetan) a rational therapeutic approach. In HER2+ breast cancer, PIK3CA co-mutations create a rationale for HER2 + PI3K dual blockade — later validated in the SUMMIT basket trial. Additional predicted combinations include PI3K + RAF inhibitors in melanoma (12%), IDH + PI3K inhibitors in IDH-mutant low-grade glioma (14%), and CDK4 + PI3K inhibitors in glioblastoma (7%). The prevalence of co-occurring actionable alterations provides a genomic basis for combination clinical trial design.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CDK4`, `MDM2`, `PIK3CA`, `ERBB2`, `BRAF`, `IDH1`, `IDH2` · Pan-cancer (especially liposarcoma, breast, melanoma, glioma, esophagogastric)

### F4: WNT pathway shows highest inter-tumour-type variability; NRF2 has the lowest frequency but squamous-type enrichment

**Finding:** WNT/β-catenin pathway alteration frequency ranges from near-universal in colorectal cancer to near-absent in renal cell carcinoma and breast cancer — the most variable of all 10 pathways. The NRF2/oxidative stress pathway has the lowest overall frequency (4%) but is selectively enriched in lung squamous cell carcinoma (25%) and esophagogastric squamous cell carcinoma (23%), defining a squamous-histology signature.

**Detail:** In colorectal cancer, APC mutation drives near-universal WNT pathway activation. In contrast, renal cell carcinoma (KIRC), breast cancer (BRCA), and testicular cancer (TGCT) have WNT pathway alteration frequencies below 5%. Cell cycle alterations are broadly common across most tumour types but sparsely present in uveal melanoma, thymoma, testicular cancer, and AML. The NRF2 pathway (NFE2L2/KEAP1/CUL3) was most enriched in squamous histologies, with significant co-occurrence of NRF2 and PI3K pathway alterations in lung squamous, esophagogastric squamous, head-and-neck squamous, and uterine carcinomas — suggesting metabolic and oxidative stress co-dependencies in these cancer types.

**Genes / cancer types:** `APC`, `CTNNB1`, `NFE2L2`, `KEAP1`, `CUL3`, `STK11`, `PIK3CA` · Pan-cancer (especially colorectal for WNT; lung/esophageal squamous for NRF2)

### F5: Mutual exclusivity and co-occurrence patterns reveal functional redundancy and pathway cross-talk across cancer types

**Finding:** Across 416 alteration pairs assessed using the SELECT method, 152 pairs showed significant mutual exclusivity and 116 showed significant co-occurrence. Mutual exclusivity within TP53, cell cycle, RAS, and PI3K pathways indicates functional redundancy (one alteration sufficient); co-occurrence within Hippo, RTK, and WNT pathways indicates synergistic multi-hit events. Between pathways, the most striking co-occurrence was between NRF2 and PI3K pathway alterations in squamous tumours.

**Detail:** TP53 mutations were mutually exclusive with CDKN2A deletion (which targets both p16/cell-cycle and ARF/p53 pathways simultaneously). MDM2 amplification was mutually exclusive with RB1 and CDKN2A loss. EGFR amplification was mutually exclusive with KRAS/BRAF/NF1/RASA1 mutations (RAS pathway redundancy), consistent with synthetic lethality observations. In glioblastoma, EGFR amplifications frequently co-occurred with EGFR mutations/fusions and with KIT/PDGFRA co-amplification — suggesting early co-amplification events. These patterns provide mechanistic insights for therapy: mutual exclusivity suggests a single alteration sufficiently activates a pathway; co-occurrence suggests potential synthetic vulnerabilities or resistance mechanisms.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `CDKN2A`, `MDM2`, `EGFR`, `KRAS`, `BRAF`, `NF1`, `PIK3CA`, `KEAP1`, `NFE2L2` · Pan-cancer

## Open Questions

- As TCGA comprised predominantly primary, treatment-naive tumours, do the same pathway alteration frequencies and co-occurrence patterns hold in metastatic, post-treatment cancers?
- How do the 10-pathway alteration portraits change as longitudinal sequencing captures tumour evolution under targeted therapy?
- Will rare pathway alterations identified here (e.g., SOS1 hotspot mutations at <1% in lung adenocarcinoma) prove clinically actionable in upcoming clinical trials?
- Can the 57% actionable alteration rate be prospectively applied to guide basket trial enrolment, and what is the actual clinical benefit rate versus molecular match rate?
- Do the predicted drug-combination pairings (e.g., CDK4 + MDM2 inhibitors in liposarcoma; PI3K + MEK in EBV+ stomach cancer) validate in prospective clinical trials?
- How do germline variants in pathway genes (e.g., BRCA1/2, APC) interact with somatic pathway alterations to shape tumour biology and treatment response?

## Limitations

- TCGA was designed for primary, untreated tumours — excludes metastatic disease (except metastatic melanoma subset) and post-treatment genomics
- Proteomic, metabolomic, and post-translational pathway activation data not included; mRNA expression used as proxy but does not capture protein-level pathway activity
- Some rare tumour types and haematologic malignancies (except AML) were not represented in TCGA at sufficient scale
- Pathway curation is manually defined and biologically selective — excludes DNA repair, spliceosome, epigenetic, and metabolic pathways treated separately by other PanCanAtlas working groups
- Actionability assessment reflects OncoKB as of 2018; significant expansion of approved indications since publication
- Individual cancer subtypes may have insufficient sample sizes to detect low-frequency driver alterations at statistical significance
