## Summary

This study performed whole exome sequencing (WES) of 12 adenoid cystic carcinomas (AdCCs) of the breast — the largest WES cohort of breast AdCCs at the time — with orthogonal validation of all candidate mutations. Breast AdCCs are a rare (<1%) triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) subtype with a paradoxically indolent clinical behavior, characterized by the *MYB-NFIB* gene fusion (present in 83% of cases). The study reveals that breast AdCCs have an exceptionally low exonic mutation rate (0.27 non-silent mutations/Mb), lack mutations in *TP53* and *PIK3CA* (which define common TNBC), and harbor a heterogeneous repertoire of mutations in chromatin remodeling, cell adhesion, RNA biology, and canonical signaling pathway genes (*BRAF*, *FBXW7*, *SF3B1*, *FGFR2*, *MYB*, *TLN2*). The mutational landscape of breast AdCCs resembles that of salivary gland AdCCs more than common TNBC. Intra-tumor genetic heterogeneity is present even in these genomically simple tumors. Data are available in cBioPortal as `acbc_mskcc_2015`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Breast AdCCs have an ultra-low mutation rate (0.27 non-silent mutations/Mb), unlike common TNBC

**Finding:** Breast AdCCs have a median of 12.5 non-synonymous somatic mutations per tumor (range 6–23), corresponding to 0.27 non-silent mutations/Mb — significantly lower than common basal-like breast cancer (1.41/Mb; p<0.001) and TNBC (1.38/Mb; p<0.001), and similar to salivary gland AdCCs (0.31/Mb; p>0.1).

**Detail:** The mutation spectrum is dominated by missense mutations (n=130 of 167 validated), with nonsense (n=18), frameshift (n=10), splice site (n=6), and in-frame indels (n=3). The mutation rate is comparable to pediatric malignancies. This low mutational burden — in a technically triple-negative tumor — has direct implications for chemotherapy response rates (generally low in AdCC), immunotherapy eligibility (TMB-based), and the inapplicability of common TNBC targeted agents.

### F2: MYB-NFIB fusion is the defining driver in 83% of breast AdCCs; MYB activation occurs even in fusion-negative cases

**Finding:** FISH and RT-PCR confirmed the t(6;9)(q22-23;p23-24) MYB rearrangement and/or MYB-NFIB fusion transcript in 10/12 (83%) breast AdCCs. The two fusion gene-negative cases lacked MYB rearrangement by FISH, but one (AdCC12T) had significantly elevated MYB 5' mRNA levels — suggesting MYB overexpression through an alternative, non-rearrangement mechanism.

**Detail:** MYB-NFIB chimeric transcript variants include MYB exon 14–NFIB exon 8c (most common), MYB exon 14–NFIB exon 9, and MYB exon 9–NFIB exon 8c. The fusion appears to drive MYB overexpression by disrupting a 3' regulatory region. The MYB-NFIB fusion-negative tumors showed no obvious histologic or clinicopathologic differences from fusion-positive cases, highlighting that MYB activation (by any mechanism) is the common unifying driver. One case (AdCC1T) harbored a somatic MYB mutation within the exon 13 splice site of the MYB-NFIB fusion allele itself.

### F3: Breast AdCCs lack TP53, PIK3CA, RB1, BRCA1/2 mutations that define common TNBC — a distinct mutational landscape

**Finding:** Zero somatic mutations in *TP53*, *PIK3CA*, *RB1*, *BRCA1*, or *BRCA2* were identified in any of the 12 breast AdCCs. Instead, potentially pathogenic mutations affect *BRAF* (8%; with 12% reported in an independent cohort), *FBXW7*, *SF3B1*, *FGFR2*, *RASA1*, *PTPN11*, and *MTOR*.

**Detail:** TP53 is mutated in 50–80% of common TNBC and PIK3CA in ~10%. Their complete absence in AdCCs — despite being triple-negative by receptor status — underscores that TNBC is a heterogeneous operational category. Agents targeting TP53 or PIK3CA pathways (e.g., PARP inhibitors acting through BRCA deficiency, platinum) are likely of limited value in breast AdCCs. Conversely, the presence of FGFR2, BRAF, and MTOR mutations suggests alternative actionable targets, though all are at low frequency requiring larger cohorts to confirm recurrence.

### F4: Breast AdCCs have simple genomes with recurrent 12q losses and 17q gains — resembling salivary gland AdCCs

**Finding:** Breast AdCCs display low levels of genetic instability (no amplifications or homozygous deletions). The most frequent CNAs are 12q12–q14.1 losses (5/12 cases; all MYB-NFIB fusion-positive) and 17q21–q25.1 gains (3/12 cases). No 5q losses or 8q gains (characteristic of common TNBC) were identified.

**Detail:** Recurrent 12q losses are also reported in salivary gland AdCCs and are associated with loss of genes in this region (potential tumor suppressors). The absence of common TNBC CNA patterns (8q gain, 5q loss) and the presence of 12q losses further confirm breast AdCC as genomically distinct from common TNBC. The CNA landscape is more similar to salivary gland AdCCs than to invasive ductal carcinoma, consistent with the histologic and molecular similarity between AdCCs from different anatomical sites.

### F5: Breast AdCCs harbor intra-tumor genetic heterogeneity at diagnosis despite simple genomes

**Finding:** ABSOLUTE analysis integrating CNA and validated mutations reveals that while most mutations are clonal (cancer cell fraction >80%), a subset are subclonal with cancer cell fractions of 9–79%. Subclonal mutations affect known cancer genes including *FBXW7*, *MTOR*, *MLL2 (KMT2D)*, *ARAF*, and *CDH1*.

**Detail:** All 12 breast AdCCs had diploid genomes (~2n). The identification of subclonal mutations in cancer-relevant genes demonstrates that even tumors with low mutational burden and simple genomes can harbor intra-tumor genetic heterogeneity, mirroring patterns seen in common TNBC. This observation suggests that standard single-biopsy profiling may not capture the full repertoire of potentially actionable alterations present at diagnosis in breast AdCCs, even in these simple-genome tumors.

## Open Questions

- What is the mechanistic basis for MYB activation in MYB-NFIB fusion-negative breast AdCCs — are there enhancer rearrangements or copy number gains at the MYB locus?
- Do BRAF mutations in breast AdCC define a targetable subgroup (BRAF V600E: vemurafenib/dabrafenib + trametinib eligibility)?
- Is the convergence of mutations in IGF1/FGF signaling and EMT pathways in both breast and salivary AdCCs exploitable therapeutically?
- Why do breast AdCCs behave so much more indolently than salivary gland AdCCs despite near-identical mutational landscapes?
- Are the subclonal mutations in known cancer genes (MTOR, FBXW7) drivers of tumor progression or incidental passengers?

## Limitations

- Very small cohort (N=12); represents the largest breast AdCC WES study at the time, reflecting the rarity of the tumor type (<0.1% of all invasive breast cancers)
- Normal breast tissue (not blood) used as germline source may introduce false-negative somatic calls if normal tissue harbors occult cancer-associated mutations
- Mutational signatures could not be analyzed due to the low number of somatic mutations per tumor
- No RNA-seq data available for transcriptome-level validation of pathway enrichment findings or MYB expression quantification
- Functional validation of non-MYB-NFIB mutations (BRAF, FGFR2, MTOR) not performed in this study
- Absence of cell line models of breast AdCC limits mechanistic follow-up experiments on MYB-NFIB fusion biology
