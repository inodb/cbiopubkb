## Summary

Multi-omic study characterizing the molecular transitions from normal keratinocytes through actinic keratosis (AK) to cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (cSCC). Single-cell expansion of 137 keratinocytes, 131 melanocytes, and 23 fibroblasts from 22 biopsies (15 donors, ages 35–95) revealed that wild-type keratinocytes have surprisingly low mutation burdens (median 1.14 mut/Mb) due to a low apoptotic threshold, whereas TP53- or NOTCH1-mutant keratinocytes exhibit much higher burdens, priming them for transformation. Deep targeted sequencing (380×) of matched SCC/AK pairs established a driver mutation order: TP53/NOTCH1 → CDKN2A/TERT (AK stage) → ARID2/MAPK (SCC stage). Spatial transcriptomics revealed immune checkpoint up-regulation specifically at the invasive tumor front. Data deposited in cBioPortal under three normal-skin studies and in dbGaP.

## Key Findings

### F1: Wild-type keratinocytes maintain low mutation burdens despite UV exposure; TP53/NOTCH1 mutations break this resistance

**Finding:** Keratinocytes have a lower median mutation burden (1.14 mut/Mb) than melanocytes (3.91 mut/Mb) or fibroblasts (1.92 mut/Mb), despite expected similar UV exposure. Every keratinocyte with a burden >3.5 mut/Mb harbored at least one pathogenic mutation. TP53 missense mutations (dominant-negative) were associated with the highest burdens.

**Detail:** The lower burden in keratinocytes likely reflects a lower apoptotic threshold: keratinocytes more readily undergo apoptosis after UV damage than melanocytes (Gilchrest et al.). Pathogenic TP53 or NOTCH1 mutations ablate this checkpoint, allowing cells to accumulate mutations rather than die. NOTCH1 mutations also induce a stem/progenitor state that may resist apoptosis. Critically, clones with pathogenic mutations were not larger than wild-type clones, suggesting the fitness advantage is primarily mutational (mutagenic phenotype) rather than proliferative. Mutational signatures: SBS7a (UV) was present in keratinocytes but at lower proportions than in melanocytes; clock-like signatures SBS1 and SBS5 predominated, consistent with cumulative mitoses.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, NOTCH1, FAT1 · Normal skin / precancerous keratinocytes / cSCC

### F2: Stepwise driver mutation order from normal skin through AK to SCC

**Finding:** Deep sequencing of matched SCC/AK pairs (n=5 true AK→SCC evolutionary pairs, from 16 total cases) plus reanalysis of a published cohort (3 additional cases) established a consensus mutational order: TP53/NOTCH1 mutations are trunk (earliest, selected even in normal epidermis); CDKN2A and TERT promoter mutations emerge in AK; ARID2 (SWI/SNF) and RTK-RAS-MAPK activating mutations define the SCC transition.

**Detail:** Phylogenetic trees were rooted at the germline state, and mutations were placed on trunk vs. branch based on variant allele frequency co-clustering. In the example case (Figure 3), AK had TP53, NOTCH1, NOTCH2, CDKN2A loss-of-function, and TERT promoter gain-of-function. The SCC additionally acquired ARID2 loss-of-function and CBL mutation (MAPK). Immunohistochemistry confirmed: p53 protein overexpression in both AK and SCC (consistent with dominant-negative TP53); elevated phospho-MAPK in SCC (consistent with CBL/MAPK mutation). Chromosome 9p loss of heterozygosity (affecting CDKN2A) was detected by allelic imbalance. Comparison of driver frequencies between normal skin, AK, and SCC cohorts confirmed this temporal order (Figure 4C).

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, NOTCH1, NOTCH2, CDKN2A, TERT, ARID2, CBL, FAT1 · cSCC / AK / normal skin

### F3: Adjacent AK and SCC are often clonally unrelated — "collision" of independent neoplasms

**Finding:** Of 16 SCC/AK pairs sequenced, 6 (38%) had no shared somatic alterations between adjacent lesions, indicating they arose as independent clones despite anatomical proximity. In 4 additional cases, the SCC and AK shared all mutations with no SCC-exclusive mutations, suggesting the AK is an extension of the SCC rather than a precursor. Only 5 cases showed true AK→SCC evolution.

**Detail:** This finding overturns the assumption that adjacent AK is the direct precursor of the neighboring SCC. The complex mosaic clonal architecture of keratinocytes in sun-damaged skin, where multiple independent clones intermix, makes "collision" of unrelated neoplasms common. Spatial transcriptomics confirmed satellite colonies of SCC cells physically distant from the main tumor mass, consistent with infiltration/mixing of clones. Clinically, histological proximity of keratinocyte lesions cannot be assumed to reflect clonal relatedness, and surgical margins defined by histological boundaries may not reliably demarcate tumor extent.

### F4: SWI/SNF (ARID2) and MAPK pathway mutations drive AK-to-SCC transition

**Finding:** Across the 8 cases with confirmed precursor-to-SCC evolution (5 from this study + 3 from Kim et al.), SWI/SNF complex mutations (predominantly ARID2) and RTK-RAS-MAPK pathway activating mutations were consistently found on phylogenetic branches leading to SCC, not to AK. In contrast, p53, NOTCH, cell-cycle (CDKN2A/Rb), and TERT pathway mutations occupied phylogenetic trunks.

**Detail:** This temporal pattern implies that SWI/SNF disruption and MAPK activation are specifically required for the invasive transition. An emerging mechanistic view is that SWI/SNF mutations (particularly ARID2) drive immune evasion by remodeling chromatin and altering immune checkpoint ligand expression, which may explain why they are enriched at the most advanced histological stage. Comparison of normal skin vs. SCC mutation frequencies confirmed that CDKN2A and ARID2 were rare in normal skin but common in SCC; TP53 and NOTCH1 were common in both.

**Genes / cancer types:** ARID2, CBL, RAS-MAPK pathway genes · cSCC

### F5: Spatial transcriptomics reveals immune checkpoint up-regulation at the invasive SCC front

**Finding:** 10X Visium spatial transcriptomics on 5 SCC/AK cases showed that immune checkpoint ligands (PVR, NECTIN2, CD274/PD-L1, CD80, CD86) were significantly elevated in tumor cells specifically at the invasive front. Concordantly, immune checkpoint receptors (CTLA4, TIGIT, PDCD1/PD-1) were up-regulated in lymphocytes at the same invasive border.

**Detail:** Spots clustered by cell type (stromal, immune, adnexal, tumor) and secondarily by cell state. Stem-like "tumor-specific keratinocyte" (TSK) gene expression programs were exclusive to the invasive front, corresponding to a partial epithelial-mesenchymal transition. Within both AK and SCC, hierarchies of differentiation (basal → suprabasal → spinous → corneocyte) were maintained, though SCC cells on average were less differentiated. Immune cell infiltrates extended along the borders of both AK and SCC, but expressed different programs depending on location. The inference is that immune equilibrium is maintained during the AK stage but broken at the SCC stage through tumor cell engagement of checkpoint receptors — and that SWI/SNF mutations may be mechanistically responsible for enabling this checkpoint engagement.

**Genes / cancer types:** PVR, NECTIN2, CD274, CD80, CD86, CTLA4, TIGIT, PDCD1 · cSCC / AK

## Open Questions

- Do SWI/SNF (ARID2) mutations mechanistically drive immune checkpoint up-regulation in cSCC, and can this be exploited therapeutically?
- What fraction of AK lesions at a given time will progress to SCC vs. remain stable or regress — and can mutational profiling predict this?
- Can the clonal collision finding inform surgical margin assessment for cSCC management?
- How does the immune equilibrium at the AK stage break down — is it driven by somatic mutation in tumor cells, or by stochastic immune exhaustion?

## Limitations

- Single-cell clonal expansion may introduce bias toward cells that grow efficiently ex vivo; average single-cell mutation burdens were ~3-fold lower than patient-matched bulk sequencing inferences.
- Only 5 (31%) of 16 matched SCC/AK pairs demonstrated true AK→SCC evolution, limiting statistical power for the temporal mutation order analysis.
- Spatial transcriptomics data provide snapshots without single-cell resolution; intra-tumor clonal structure within Visium spots cannot be fully resolved.
- As a preprint (two versions as of 2024 Dec 2), findings have not yet undergone full peer review.
- Normal skin panel was small (n=15 donors) and limited to a cancer gene panel that excluded TERT promoter for comparison across cohorts.
