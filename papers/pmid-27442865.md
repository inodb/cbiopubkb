## Summary

MSK-IMPACT sequencing of 151 patients with advanced (recurrent and/or metastatic) head and neck cancers from diverse histologies reveals a molecular landscape starkly different from primary tumors. Key findings include: TERT promoter mutations are dramatically enriched in recurrent/metastatic HPV-negative HNSCC (53% vs. 17% in primary; OR 5.5); 43% of recurrent/metastatic HPV-positive HNSCC tumors show genomic profiles resembling HPV-negative disease (with enriched TP53 mutations, whole-genome duplication, 3p deletion) — potentially explaining their therapeutic resistance; activating NOTCH1 mutations are specifically enriched in metastatic adenoid cystic carcinoma (22% vs. 3.3% in primary); and ETV6-NTRK3 fusion detection reclassified two tumors as mammary analog secretory carcinomas (MASC), enabling TRK inhibitor enrollment with dramatic responses. NGS guided therapy in 14% of all patients (25% of HNSCC), highlighting the clinical value of precision sequencing in this setting while revealing a major gap: only 21% had potentially actionable alterations.

## Key Findings

### F1: TERT promoter mutations dramatically enriched in recurrent/metastatic HPV-negative HNSCC (53% vs. 17% in primary) — especially tongue SCC (91%)

**Finding:** *TERT* promoter mutations were identified in 16 of 30 HPV-negative recurrent/metastatic HNSCCs (53%) — significantly more frequent than in primary HPV-negative HNSCCs (12/70, 17%; OR 5.5, p<0.001). In HPV-negative tongue SCCs, 10 of 11 (91%) harbored TERT promoter mutations. No TERT promoter mutations were found in any HPV-positive HNSCC, consistent with the ability of HPV E6 to activate telomerase independently, establishing TERT mutation and HPV infection as parallel mechanisms of telomerase activation.

**Detail:** TERT promoter mutations (C228T and C250T hotspots) upregulate telomerase expression, conferring replicative immortality. Their enrichment in recurrent/metastatic disease suggests selection pressure for this alteration during therapy or during transition from primary to advanced disease. The extremely high rate in tongue SCC (91%) is particularly notable and may have prognostic implications, as TERT promoter mutations have been linked with poorer survival in HNSCC. The near-total exclusivity from HPV-positive tumors (0/21 HPV-positive cases) reinforces biological divergence between HPV-positive and HPV-negative HNSCCs in the advanced setting — potentially relevant for treatment deintensification trials that assume HPV-positive tumors are uniformly favorable.

### F2: 43% of recurrent/metastatic HPV-positive HNSCCs have molecular profiles resembling HPV-negative tumors — enriched WGD, 3p deletion, TP53 mutation — with trend toward worse survival

**Finding:** A support vector machine classifier trained on TCGA primary HPV-positive vs. HPV-negative HNSCC correctly classified 91% of HPV-negative MSK-IMPACT cases but only 57% of HPV-positive cases (p=0.007). The 43% of advanced HPV-positive tumors misclassified as "HPV-negative-like" showed significant enrichment of whole-genome duplication (6.7-fold, p=0.024), 3p deletion (9.1-fold, p=0.002), and co-occurring 3p deletion + TP53 mutation (7.6-fold, p=0.03) relative to primary HPV-positive tumors. These "HPV-negative-like" HPV-positive tumors trended toward worse survival (HR 2.3, p=0.19; underpowered).

**Detail:** HPV-positive HNSCC is typically associated with favorable prognosis and high cure rates with chemoradiotherapy. The finding that a large fraction (43%) of recurrent/metastatic HPV-positive tumors acquire a genomic landscape resembling HPV-negative tumors — with TP53 mutation (15% vs. 3% in primary HPV-positive), whole-genome duplication (25%), and 3p deletion (55%) — provides a genomic explanation for their treatment resistance. TP53 mutations in HPV-positive recurrent tumors were predominantly subclonal (75% subclonal vs. 15% in HPV-negative), suggesting late emergence. Consistent findings observed in HPV-positive cohorts from Chung et al. and Chau et al. External confirmation also from HPV-positive cervical cancer: TP53 mutations in 25% of metastatic vs. 3% of primary (OR 8.3, p=0.008).

### F3: Activating NOTCH1 mutations specifically enriched in metastatic adenoid cystic carcinoma (22% vs. 3.3% in primary; OR 8.3) — nominating γ-secretase inhibitors

**Finding:** Activating *NOTCH1* mutations or amplifications were identified in 8 of 36 recurrent/metastatic ACCs (22.2%), a dramatic enrichment compared to 2 of 60 primary ACCs (3.3%; OR 8.3, p=0.005). Three samples carried recurrent truncating mutations at serine 2467 — an activating site also known in T-cell leukemia. Additional TERT promoter mutations were found in 5 ACCs (14%), a novel finding in salivary cancers. Activating NOTCH1 mutations confer sensitivity to γ-secretase inhibitors (which prevent NOTCH1 intracellular domain release), directly nominating these drugs as a rational therapeutic strategy for NOTCH1-mutated metastatic ACC.

**Detail:** ACC is a rare, indolent but ultimately fatal malignancy with no approved targeted therapies. Recurrent and metastatic ACC (n=36) had low mutational load (mean 2.3 mutations) and low copy-number burden (mean 12% genome altered), suggesting a "quiet" genomic landscape overall. The NOTCH1 enrichment is therefore a striking finding in this genomic context. MYB/MYBL1 fusions (the canonical ACC driver) were not specifically queried in this panel but NOTCH1 enrichment in advanced disease suggests selection for NOTCH-driven growth in the metastatic niche. Four ACC patients had NGS-guided treatment: FGFR3 mutation → FGFR inhibitor; PIK3CA mutation → PI3K inhibitor; PIK3R1 mutation → PI3K inhibitor; MDM2 amplification → MDM2 inhibitor.

### F4: ETV6-NTRK3 fusion detected by NGS reclassified two acinic cell carcinomas as MASC — enabling TRK inhibitor enrollment with near-complete responses

**Finding:** Two tumors initially diagnosed as acinic cell carcinomas were identified by NGS to harbor *ETV6-NTRK3* fusions, the defining molecular alteration of mammary analog secretory carcinoma (MASC). Both patients were subsequently enrolled on TRK inhibitor basket studies and achieved near-complete responses — among the most dramatic therapeutic successes reported. This exemplifies the diagnostic and therapeutic value of comprehensive tumor sequencing in rare cancers, where histology alone is insufficient to identify the correct diagnosis and matched therapy.

**Detail:** MASC was first described in 2010 as a salivary gland carcinoma resembling breast secretory carcinoma, carrying the ETV6-NTRK3 fusion (identical to that in congenital fibrosarcoma). The diagnosis is often missed without molecular testing because the histology resembles other salivary neoplasms. TRK (Tropomyosin receptor kinase) inhibitors (larotrectinib, entrectinib) are now FDA-approved for NTRK fusion-positive cancers regardless of histology. This study was conducted before FDA approval and used basket trial access. The near-complete responses in two patients represent compelling translational proof-of-concept for histology-agnostic targeting. One olfactory neuroblastoma in the cohort was also found to have an EWSR1-FLI1 fusion (diagnosed as Ewing sarcoma) — NGS changed the pathological classification and treatment regimen.

### F5: NGS guided therapy in 14% of all patients (25% of HNSCC); actionable alterations in 21%; basket studies provided half of clinical trial enrollments — demonstrating precision oncology clinical value in HNC

**Finding:** Of 151 patients, 21 (14%) had NGS-guided therapy decisions; 28 of 135 (21%) had potentially actionable alterations. Among HNSCC patients specifically, NGS guided therapy in 13 of 53 (25%). Notable successes included 4 HNSCC patients enrolled on PI3K inhibitor trials (PIK3CA/PTEN), 1 on ERK inhibitor (MAPK1 mutation), 1 on SMARCB1 deletion → EZH2 inhibitor, 1 on farnesyl transferase inhibitor (HRAS), plus 6 patients directed to immunotherapy due to *absence* of actionable alterations. Basket studies accounted for half of all clinical trial enrollments, highlighting their particular importance for rare and advanced HNC histologies.

**Detail:** Salivary duct carcinomas had the highest density of actionable alterations: PIK3CA, HRAS, ERBB2, AR amplification, and structural variants in NF1 and ROS1. One mucoepidermoid tumor with HRAS G13V was enrolled on farnesyl transferase inhibitor trial. In BCCs, high mutational load (1 hypermutated tumor, 122 mutations) led to immunotherapy enrollment. PTCH1 mutations were retrospectively confirmed in 3 BCCs responding to hedgehog pathway inhibitors. A HNSCC patient with double-hit FANCA (germline deletion + somatic stopgain) was identified — analogous to prostate cancer FANCA double-hit conferring cisplatin sensitivity. The study demonstrates NGS feasibility in clinical CLIA-certified settings for diverse HNC histologies.

## Open Questions

- Does the "HPV-negative-like" genomic profile of 43% of recurrent HPV-positive HNSCCs identify patients who should receive more aggressive therapy, or who would benefit from HPV-negative-directed regimens (e.g., immune checkpoint inhibition)?
- Can NOTCH1 activating mutation status be used prospectively to select metastatic ACC patients for γ-secretase inhibitor trials, and what is the response rate?
- What fraction of head and neck cancers currently sequenced with expanded gene panels (505 genes, MSK-IMPACT v5+) harbor actionable alterations beyond the 21% seen here, given the expanded coverage?
- Should de novo TERT promoter sequencing (not covered by most targeted panels) be added to precision oncology workups for HPV-negative HNSCC, given its association with poor prognosis?
- Can the APOBEC mutational signature (detected in both HPV-positive and HPV-negative tumors) predict response to immune checkpoint inhibitors independently of TMB or PD-L1 expression?
- What genomic features of the 26% unclassified (taxonomy "other") prostate cancers are analogous to the HPV-positive-to-HPV-negative transition seen here — i.e., are there general principles of convergent genomic evolution in advanced treatment-resistant cancer?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=151 total; n=53 HNSCC, n=36 ACC) limits power for subgroup analyses and rare event detection
- Comparison between recurrent/metastatic MSK-IMPACT and TCGA primary tumors introduces potential confounding: different platforms, patient populations, analytic pipelines, and sequencing depth
- Single-site biopsy at one time point; intratumor heterogeneity, temporal evolution not captured
- Follow-up time heterogeneous (minimum 6 months required); survival analyses underpowered for rare subtypes
- Standard-of-care targeted therapies were limited at the time of enrollment (2014–2015); many basket trials were early-phase with uncertain benefit
- Restricted to MSK patients; referral bias may enrich for complex or refractory disease compared to community settings
- Germline analysis performed but germline variants (FANCA, BRCA2) require confirmation in a familial context for clinical germline testing recommendations
- No prospective randomized validation of the clinical benefit of NGS-guided therapy vs. standard-of-care in this population
