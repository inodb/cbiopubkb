## Summary

MSK-IMPACT clinical tumor-normal sequencing study of 184 MSI-H endometrial carcinomas (ECs) stratified by mechanism of mismatch repair deficiency (MMR-D): germline MMR mutations (n=25), somatic MMR mutations (n=39), and *MLH1* promoter hypermethylation (MLH1ph; n=120). MLH1ph ECs were found in older, more obese patients and diagnosed at higher stages with more LVSI. They had distinct somatic mutation profiles (*JAK1* enriched; germline group enriched for *ERBB2*, *ERBB3*, *FBXW7*), lower TMB (32 vs 44–48 mt/Mb), and lower tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) scores across all compartments. Stage was the sole independent predictor of PFS in multivariable models; however, early-stage MLH1ph ECs had markedly worse 2-year PFS (70% vs 100%). Pembrolizumab retained activity in MLH1ph disease (75% clinical benefit rate). Full data available in `ucec_ccr_msk_2022` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: MLH1ph ECs have distinct clinicopathologic features — older, more obese, higher-stage at diagnosis

**Finding:** Patients with MLH1ph ECs were significantly older (median 64 vs 54 years), more obese (58% vs 26–42%), diagnosed at higher FIGO stage (p=0.025), and had higher rates of lymphovascular space invasion (LVSI; 61% vs 32–56%) compared to germline and somatic MMR-mutated ECs.

**Detail:** Among the 184-patient cohort, 65% had MLH1ph (the most common MMR-D mechanism), 21% had somatic MMR mutations, and 14% had germline mutations (Lynch syndrome). Histologic type (predominantly endometrioid) did not differ significantly across groups. The association of MLH1ph with obesity is biologically plausible, as estrogen-driven endometrial hyperplasia in obese patients is a known precursor to sporadic endometrial cancers, and MLH1 methylation is associated with aging and metabolic exposures.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, EPCAM · Endometrial carcinoma

### F2: Distinct somatic mutation landscapes across MMR-D mechanisms — JAK1 vs ERBB2/ERBB3/FBXW7

**Finding:** MLH1ph ECs were enriched for *JAK1* frameshift-indel mutations (45% vs 4% germline, p<0.05). Germline MMR-D ECs showed enrichment for *ERBB2* (29%), *ERBB3* (25%), and *FBXW7* (46%) hotspot mutations compared to MLH1ph (3%, 2%, 10% respectively). Frequencies of *ARID1A*, *PTEN*, and *PIK3CA* were similar across groups.

**Detail:** Because MSI-H tumors accumulate frameshift mutations at mononucleotide repeat sequences, *JAK1* frameshift-indels are a canonical consequence of the MMR defect. The enrichment in MLH1ph ECs may reflect a longer duration or different intensity of the MMR defect. The enrichment of *ERBB2* and *ERBB3* oncogenic mutations in germline MMR-D ECs is clinically actionable, as HER2-targeted therapies are available and ERBB2 amplification in endometrial cancer is FDA-recognized. Dominant mutational signature was also different: 77% of MLH1ph ECs had MMR-D signatures (6, 15, 20, 26) vs 38–46% in germline/somatic groups; germline/somatic groups more commonly had aging signature 1.

**Genes / cancer types:** JAK1, ERBB2, ERBB3, FBXW7, ARID1A, PTEN, PIK3CA, MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 · Endometrial carcinoma

### F3: MLH1ph ECs have lower TMB and lower TIL scores — potential implications for immunotherapy

**Finding:** Median TMB was significantly lower in MLH1ph ECs (32 mt/Mb) compared to germline (44 mt/Mb) and somatic MMR-mutated ECs (48 mt/Mb; p<0.001). TIL scores were significantly lower in MLH1ph across all compartments — intratumoral (p=0.002), stromal (p=0.002), and peritumoral (p=0.001).

**Detail:** Among germline mutation carriers, 83% of MSH6-associated ECs and 31% of MLH1/PMS2/MSH2-associated ECs were classified as MSS or MSI-indeterminate by MSIsensor, often due to low tumor content rather than true microsatellite stability. MLH1ph ECs uniformly classified as MSI-H. The lower TMB and TIL density in MLH1ph tumors raises the hypothesis that these tumors are less immunogenic, which could affect the magnitude or durability of response to pembrolizumab. Mutational signature analysis showed that aging signature 1 dominated in germline/somatic groups, while MLH1ph tumors predominantly carried MMR-D signatures.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, MSH6, PMS2, MSH2 · Endometrial carcinoma (MSI-H)

### F4: Stage — not MMR mechanism — is the primary driver of PFS; early-stage MLH1ph has worse prognosis

**Finding:** In multivariable models including MMR-D mechanism, age, stage, and LVSI, only stage was independently associated with PFS (p<0.001). However, in exploratory analysis of early-stage (I/II) disease, 2-year PFS was 70% for MLH1ph vs 100% for germline and somatic MMR-mutated patients.

**Detail:** Among 119 patients included in the outcomes analysis, 29 experienced progression and 11 deaths were observed at median follow-up of 25 months. On univariate analysis, MLH1ph was associated with inferior PFS (p=0.005), as was older age, higher stage, and LVSI. In multivariable models, these effects were confounded by stage. The exploratory finding that early-stage MLH1ph ECs have worse PFS than germline/somatic counterparts is hypothesis-generating and warrants prospective validation. Higher TMB showed borderline association with improved PFS (p=0.06).

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1 (epigenetic inactivation) · Endometrial carcinoma

### F5: Pembrolizumab retains activity in MLH1ph ECs — 75% clinical benefit rate

**Finding:** In a hypothesis-generating analysis of 18 patients with recurrent disease treated with on-label pembrolizumab, clinical benefit rate was 75% (12/16) and overall response rate 63% (10/16) in MLH1ph ECs. Disease progression was observed in 4/16 (25%) MLH1ph patients, versus 0/2 germline or somatic MMR-mutated patients.

**Detail:** Despite lower TMB and TIL scores, MLH1ph ECs retain meaningful sensitivity to immune checkpoint blockade. The sample size is small (n=16 MLH1ph evaluable) and responses ranged from complete response (n=2) to partial response (n=8) to stable disease (n=2) to progression (n=4). The median TMB of MLH1ph ECs receiving ICI was 33.8 mt/Mb. These data support continuing pembrolizumab eligibility for MLH1ph patients, while noting that longer-term prospective studies are needed to establish whether this group derives equivalent benefit.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1 (hypermethylation) · Endometrial carcinoma (recurrent/advanced)

## Open Questions

- Do MLH1ph ECs have a distinct immune microenvironment (e.g., T-cell exhaustion, immunosuppressive cytokines) beyond lower TIL density that mechanistically explains reduced immunogenicity?
- Can the lower immunogenicity of MLH1ph ECs be overcome with combination immunotherapy (e.g., checkpoint + LAG-3 inhibition or VEGF blockade)?
- Should early-stage MLH1ph ECs receive more aggressive adjuvant therapy given the exploratory finding of 70% vs 100% 2-year PFS?
- Do *ERBB2*/*ERBB3* mutations enriched in germline MMR-D ECs predict benefit from HER2-targeted therapy in this Lynch syndrome population?
- How does the degree of MLH1 promoter methylation (partial vs complete) correlate with TMB, TIL density, and ICI response?

## Limitations

- Retrospective single-institution cohort; referral bias limits generalizability of survival outcomes
- ICI response analysis severely limited by sample size (n=18 total; n=16 MLH1ph); insufficient power to detect significant differences
- TIL scoring was semi-quantitative (pathologist-scored, not digital pathology) and not deposited in cBioPortal
- Mutational signature analysis restricted to cases with ≥20 SNVs; some samples excluded
- Median follow-up of 25 months may be insufficient to capture late recurrences, especially for early-stage disease
- MLH1ph status determined by bisulfite methylation assay — degree/extent of methylation not captured
