## Summary

WES of 39 aggressive cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (cSCC) cases from the head and neck region at MD Anderson Cancer Center, sequenced to 115× average coverage. Aggressive cSCC carries the highest median tumor mutation burden (61.2 mut/Mb) of any cancer sequenced at that time — more than 4× that of melanoma and higher than HNSCC or lung squamous cell carcinoma. The dominant mutation signature is UV-induced (75% C>T transitions, 87% at pyrimidine-preceding C, plus 5.6% dinucleotide polymorphisms). Despite this extreme mutational background, 23 candidate driver genes were identified using MutSig, IntOGen, and two custom inactivation-bias algorithms: established drivers (TP53, CDKN2A, NOTCH1, AJUBA, HRAS, CASP8, FAT1, KMT2C/MLL3) and novel candidates (NOTCH2, PARD3, RASA1). KMT2C mutations were associated with poor clinical outcome and bone invasion. A subset of four nose-origin tumors showed a non-UV mutation signature resembling HNSCC. Full data are available in `cscc_hgsc_bcm_2014` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Highest tumor mutation burden of any cancer at time of publication — driven by UV signature

**Finding:** Aggressive cSCC has a median mutation burden of 61.2 mut/Mb — the highest reported for any cancer at the time of this study, exceeding melanoma by more than 4-fold and surpassing HNSCC and lung squamous carcinoma. The dominant cause is UV-induced damage: 75% C>T transitions (87% at pyrimidine-preceded C) and 5.6% dinucleotide polymorphisms (DNPs). Tumors were largely clonal (<15% of mutations at low allele fraction; only 4 samples had >15% subclonal genome fraction).

**Detail:** Total range: 86–12,112 somatic mutations per patient (median 108,034 across cohort; 16,588 genes). Tumors were largely clonal at diagnosis; prior treatment did not change mutation count or type, confirming UV as the dominant mutagenic force. POLE mutations were not detected, ruling out polymerase proofreading deficiency as an alternative explanation. Four nose-origin tumors showed non-UV signatures (39% C>T, similar to HPV-negative HNSCC at ~40%), possibly reflecting mucosal origin.

### F2: 23 candidate driver genes identified despite extreme UV mutational background

**Finding:** Four complementary statistical methods — MutSig (11 significant genes at q<0.1), IntOGen (292 at FDR<0.1, many false positives at this threshold), a chi-square inactivation-bias test (24 candidate TSGs), and a UV-corrected multinomial inactivation model — were combined. Genes significant by MutSig or by ≥2 other methods totaled 23 candidate drivers. TP53, CDKN2A, NOTCH1, NOTCH2, and AJUBA were significant by all four methods. Known drivers FAT1 and KMT2C (MLL3), and putative TSG RASA1, were confirmed by the two inactivation-bias methods.

**Detail:** The challenge of identifying drivers at 61.2 mut/Mb background required methods beyond standard frequency analysis. Two custom inactivation-bias algorithms were developed: a chi-square method comparing gene-specific inactivating/non-inactivating ratios to cohort-wide expectation, and a multinomial method accounting for gene-specific codon usage and UV signature context. Both approaches converged on the same core list, validating the approach. RBM46 and DCLK1 were dropped from MutSig hits due to low allele fractions suggesting passenger status.

### F3: cSCC mutational spectrum resembles HNSCC — dominated by tumor suppressor inactivation

**Finding:** The driver gene landscape of aggressive cSCC (TP53, CDKN2A, NOTCH1, NOTCH2, HRAS, CASP8, FAT1, AJUBA) is strikingly similar to that of HPV-negative head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) at both the gene and pathway level, suggesting shared oncogenic mechanisms despite different anatomical origins. Tumor suppressors dominate over oncogene activation.

**Detail:** Comparison with TCGA HNSCC (HPV-negative, n=243), TCGA lung squamous (n=178), and TCGA melanoma (n=278) confirmed that cSCC's mutation type and gene-level patterns align most closely with HNSCC. The enrichment for inactivating mutations (nonsense, frameshift, splice site) in nearly all identified drivers is consistent with a TSG-dominated biology. NOTCH1 and NOTCH2 function as tumor suppressors in stratified squamous epithelium; AJUBA is a LIM-domain protein with emerging tumor suppressor functions in squamous cancers. FAT1 is a Wnt pathway regulator and cadherin.

### F4: KMT2C mutations associate with poor outcome and bone invasion

**Finding:** KMT2C (also called MLL3), a histone H3K4 monomethyltransferase and known tumor suppressor, was significantly mutated in aggressive cSCC and its mutation was associated with worse clinical outcome and increased rate of bone invasion — providing the first genomic predictor of particularly aggressive cSCC behavior within this already-aggressive cohort.

**Detail:** KMT2C mutations were predominantly inactivating (consistent with TSG function), identified by both the chi-square and multinomial inactivation-bias methods. Association with bone invasion (a feature of extreme aggressiveness that upstages tumors by AJCC criteria) and poor outcome was statistically significant. KMT2C has established roles in active enhancer regulation and is recurrently mutated in bladder cancer, colorectal cancer, and HNSCC among others, supporting its function as a bona fide cancer driver across squamous contexts.

### F5: Novel tumor suppressors NOTCH2, PARD3, and RASA1 emerge as cSCC drivers

**Finding:** Three genes not previously established as cSCC drivers were identified: NOTCH2 (a NOTCH family tumor suppressor with roles in differentiation; found significant by all four methods alongside NOTCH1), PARD3 (a cell polarity regulator; significant by three methods), and RASA1 (a RAS-GAP and negative regulator of HRAS/RAS signaling; significant by the two inactivation-bias methods). Their inactivation patterns support loss-of-function tumor suppressor roles.

**Detail:** NOTCH2, like NOTCH1, appears to act as a squamous epithelial tumor suppressor — consistent with its known role in keratinocyte differentiation. PARD3 is a polarity complex scaffold (Par3/Par6/aPKC); its loss may disrupt epithelial architecture and promote invasion. RASA1 loss would relieve its GAP activity on HRAS/RAS, effectively phenocopying HRAS activating mutations and functionally complementing the HRAS hotspot mutations found independently in the cohort. Mutual exclusivity analysis between RASA1 loss and HRAS mutations would be informative but was not explicitly reported.

## Open Questions

- Does the similarity between cSCC and HNSCC mutation landscapes support shared therapeutic strategies (e.g., EGFR inhibitors, immune checkpoint blockade), and can existing HNSCC trial data be extrapolated to aggressive cSCC?
- Can KMT2C mutation status prospectively identify patients at risk of bone invasion and metastasis, enabling upfront intensified staging or adjuvant therapy?
- Do RASA1 loss and HRAS activating mutations have equivalent functional consequences, and are RASA1-mutant cSCCs sensitive to MEK or PI3K inhibitors?
- Why do nose-located tumors display a non-UV HNSCC-like mutation signature — are these truly cutaneous SCC or misclassified mucosal SCC, and how should they be managed?
- Given the absence of effective systemic therapies for aggressive cSCC, can the identified driver genes (particularly NOTCH pathway members) guide clinical trial design?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=39); statistical power limited for rare drivers and for survival analysis
- All cases from a single institution (MD Anderson), potentially skewing toward extreme cases
- Average tumor purity of 49.4% ± 22.4% may reduce sensitivity for subclonal mutations
- No RNA-seq or copy number array; structural variants and expression-level validation not performed
- Survival associations (KMT2C, total mutation burden) were not adjusted for all potential confounders
- No validation cohort for novel drivers (NOTCH2, PARD3, RASA1); external replication needed
