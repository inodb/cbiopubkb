## Summary

This pilot study from MSKCC characterizes the molecular differences between hepatocellular adenoma (HCA) and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) in patients without underlying hepatitis or cirrhosis, using small RNA sequencing and MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing. HCA miRNA profiles cluster intermediate between normal liver and HCC, suggesting a molecular continuum in hepatocarcinogenesis. Several miRNAs (miR-200a, miR-429, miR-490-3p, miR-452, miR-766, miR-1180) are co-dysregulated in both HCA and HCC, while the chromosome 19 miRNA cluster (C19MC) is selectively upregulated in HCC.

## Key Findings

### F1: HCA miRNA profiles are intermediate between normal liver and HCC

**Finding:** Unsupervised clustering of miRNA profiles places all three HCA subtypes (inflammatory, steatotic, β-catenin) between normal liver and HCC on a principal component analysis continuum.

**Detail:** 90 miRNAs were differentially expressed between tumors and normal liver. HCC exhibited 86 significantly dysregulated miRNAs vs. normal liver (FDR ≤0.05), while HCA showed only 10 significantly different miRNAs vs. normal liver, consistent with a partial molecular progression toward malignancy.

**Genes / cancer types:** miRNA clusters · Hepatocellular adenoma, Hepatocellular carcinoma

### F2: Six miRNAs are co-dysregulated in both HCA and HCC vs. normal liver

**Finding:** miR-200a, miR-429, and miR-490-3p are significantly downregulated in both HCA and HCC compared to adjacent normal liver; miR-452, miR-766, and miR-1180 are significantly upregulated in both, suggesting shared early oncogenic events.

**Detail:** Log fold changes for downregulated miRNAs ranged from −2.2 to −4.0 (miR-490-3p most suppressed), and from +1.0 to +2.3 for upregulated miRNAs. All comparisons FDR-adjusted p ≤0.05. miR-200a/429 are established EMT suppressors; miR-452 and miR-1180 have established oncogenic roles in HCC by targeting CDKN1B and TNFAIP3IP2, respectively.

**Genes / cancer types:** miR-200a, miR-429, miR-490-3p, miR-452, miR-766, miR-1180 · Hepatocellular adenoma, Hepatocellular carcinoma

**Data availability:** `not-available`
> miRNA sequencing data not deposited in cBioPortal.

### F3: C19MC chromosome 19 miRNA cluster selectively upregulated in HCC vs. HCA

**Finding:** Members of the chromosome 19 miRNA cluster (C19MC)—including miR-515-5p, miR-517a, miR-518b, and miR-520c-3p—were 3–4 fold higher in HCC than in HCA or adjacent normal liver, representing the most discriminating miRNA signature between adenoma and carcinoma.

**Detail:** These C19MC miRNAs were among the top hits in the 57 miRNAs that differentiated HCC from HCA (FDR ≤0.05). C19MC is normally expressed only in placenta and germline tissues; its activation in HCC may reflect epigenetic deregulation and could serve as a diagnostic marker.

**Genes / cancer types:** C19MC (miR-515-5p, miR-517a, miR-518b, miR-520c-3p) · Hepatocellular carcinoma vs. adenoma

**Data availability:** `not-available`
> miRNA data not in cBioPortal.

### F4: Distinct mutation patterns between HCA and HCC—TERT is HCC-specific

**Finding:** The most common mutations in HCC were TERT (7/10, 70%), TP53 (3/10, 30%), CTNNB1 (2/10, 20%), and APC (2/10, 20%). HCA harbored mutations in HNF1α (3/9), CTNNB1 (1/9), GNAS, FAT1, HGF, PIK3CA, and MLL2—but NO TERT mutations, consistent with TERT as a late event in adenoma-to-carcinoma progression.

**Detail:** Total 7 different mutations among HCA and 41 among HCC. The co-occurrence of CTNNB1 + TERT in HCC (both patients with CTNNB1 mutation also had TERT mutation) supports a model where CTNNB1 mutation is early and TERT is late. B-HCA with CTNNB1 mutation but no TERT underscores adenoma's position before this transition.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, TP53, CTNNB1, APC, HNF1α, GNAS, PIK3CA · Hepatocellular adenoma, Hepatocellular carcinoma

**Data availability:** `available`
> MSK-IMPACT mutation data in `hcc_msk_venturaa_2018`; includes TERT, TP53, CTNNB1, HNF1α, and other driver mutations.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Mutations tab and OncoPrint in cBioPortal. TERT promoter mutations are included.

### F5: HCA subtypes reflect distinct molecular programs with varying malignant potential

**Finding:** Three HCA subtypes were defined by IHC and sequencing: I-HCA (54%; CRP/SAA+, GNAS/FAT1/PIK3CA mutations), H-HCA (37%; LFABP loss, HNF1α mutations), and B-HCA (9%; nuclear β-catenin, CTNNB1 mutation), with B-HCA carrying the highest malignant transformation risk.

**Detail:** HNF1α mutations correlated 100% with H-HCA classification; CTNNB1 mutation identified the single B-HCA case. All HCCs were β-catenin IHC negative (no nuclear staining), suggesting the post-CTNNB1 transformation steps involve additional oncogenic hits (e.g., TERT).

**Genes / cancer types:** HNF1A, CTNNB1, GNAS, PIK3CA · Hepatocellular adenoma subtypes

**Data availability:** `available`
> Subtype-defining mutations accessible in `hcc_msk_venturaa_2018`; clinical subtype annotations may need to be integrated separately.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Mutations tab; cross-referencing with clinical data requires api or custom scripting.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Would B-HCA exhibit more HCC-like miRNA profiles with larger sample sizes, and could miRNA profiles guide malignant transformation risk assessment?
- Can C19MC members serve as diagnostic biomarkers to distinguish well-differentiated HCC from B-HCA in difficult pathology cases?
- Do TERT promoter mutations arise stochastically as a second hit in CTNNB1-mutant HCA, and what is the precise timeline of this transition?
- Could the shared miRNA dysregulations (miR-200a/429/490-3p) be early prevention/interception targets in high-risk HCA patients?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Small pilot cohort (11 HCA, 10 HCC); insufficient power for subgroup analyses by HCA subtype.
- Only 1 B-HCA sample, limiting conclusions about the highest-risk subgroup.
- No prospective follow-up to observe actual malignant transformation events.
- HCA and HCC cohorts differed significantly in age and sex distribution (HCC patients older, more male), potentially confounding comparisons.
- Adjacent normal liver used as comparator may not be entirely normal in HCC patients.
