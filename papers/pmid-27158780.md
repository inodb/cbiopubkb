## Summary

This large exome sequencing study compares the somatic landscape of 660 lung adenocarcinomas (ADC) and 484 lung squamous cell carcinomas (SqCC)—the two principal NSCLC subtypes. Recurrently mutated genes and focal copy number alterations are largely distinct between the two types, with lung SqCC more closely resembling head and neck squamous carcinoma (HNSC) and bladder cancer (BLCA) than lung ADC. Novel significantly mutated genes include *PPP3CA*, *DOT1L*, and *FTSJD1* in ADC; *RASA1* in SqCC; and *KLF5*, *EP300*, and *CREBBP* in both. Novel amplification peaks include *MIR21* (ADC), *MIR205* (SqCC), and *MAPK1* (both). Critically, lung ADCs lacking known RTK/Ras/Raf drivers harbor new pathway alterations in *SOS1*, *VAV1*, *RASA1*, and *ARHGAP35*, extending actionable coverage to 76% of all ADCs. Neoantigen load is high in both subtypes (47–53% with ≥5 predicted neoepitopes), supporting immunotherapy applicability regardless of histotype.

## Key Findings

### F1: Significantly mutated genes are largely distinct between lung ADC and SqCC — SqCC resembles other squamous cancers more than it resembles ADC

**Finding:** Only 6 genes (*TP53*, *RB1*, *ARID1A*, *CDKN2A*, *PIK3CA*, *NF1*) are significantly mutated in both tumor types. 38 genes are significant in ADC and 20 in SqCC (MutSig2CV q<0.1), with only 12% overlap. Lung SqCC most closely resembles HNSC and BLCA (>25% gene-list overlap), while lung ADC is most similar to GBM and CRC.

**Detail:** *TP53*, *CDKN2A*, and *PIK3CA* are significantly more mutated in SqCC than ADC (p<0.01). Genes specific to ADC include *STK11*, *RBM10*, *KEAP1*, *RAF1*, *RIT1*, *MET*. Genes specific to SqCC include *NFE2L2*, *KDM6A*, *RASA1*, *NOTCH1*, *HRAS*. Only 11/42 amplification peaks and 13/50 deletion peaks are shared between histotypes. This pattern suggests a common squamous lineage program governs carcinogenesis across tissue types rather than anatomic site.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `RB1`, `CDKN2A`, `PIK3CA`, `STK11`, `KEAP1`, `NFE2L2`, `KDM6A`, `NOTCH1` · Lung ADC vs SqCC

### F2: Novel significantly mutated genes expand the lung cancer gene landscape — PPP3CA, DOT1L, FTSJD1 in ADC; RASA1 in SqCC; KLF5, EP300, CREBBP in both

**Finding:** Multiple novel SMGs are identified exclusive to each subtype or shared (pan-lung). *PPP3CA* (calcineurin catalytic subunit) mutations cluster in the autoinhibitory domain and may be gain-of-function; they co-occur with *KRAS* mutations. *DOT1L* (H3K79 methyltransferase) is mutated in 3% of ADCs (truncating-enriched). *RASA1* (p120GAP) is enriched for frameshift mutations in SqCC. *EP300* and *CREBBP* share a mutational hotspot in the HAT domain.

**Detail:** *KLF5* (lung development transcription factor) has a novel recurrent mutation in the zinc finger domain in both ADC and SqCC. Its regulator *FBXW7* is also mutated and does not co-occur with *KLF5*. *FTSJD1* (cap methyltransferase) shows frameshift enrichment in ADC. *CUL3* (KEAP1 interaction partner) reaches significance in SqCC. Splicing factors *SF3B1* and *SNRPD3* also identified as recurrently mutated. Pan-lung joint analysis identified 14 additional genes not significant in either individual subtype.

**Genes / cancer types:** `PPP3CA`, `DOT1L`, `FTSJD1`, `RASA1`, `KLF5`, `EP300`, `CREBBP`, `CUL3`, `FBXW7` · Lung ADC and/or SqCC

### F3: RTK/Ras/Raf pathway is altered in 76% of lung ADCs including novel drivers SOS1, VAV1, RASA1, ARHGAP35

**Finding:** After classifying ADCs as "oncogene positive" (known RTK/Ras/Raf driver present) or "oncogene negative," 15 genes are significantly enriched in oncogene-negative samples, including known Ras GEFs *SOS1* and *VAV1*, and GAPs *RASA1* and *ARHGAP35*. Combined, 499/660 (76%) of ADCs harbor an alteration in known or putative Ras/Raf/RTK driver genes.

**Detail:** *SOS1* p.N233Y mutation falls in the autoinhibitory DH domain (analogous to Noonan syndrome mutation p.D309Y). *VAV1* p.S67Y is at the CH/Ac/PH domain interface known to increase GEF activity. *RASA1* and *ARHGAP35* are enriched for loss-of-function mutations (p<0.01). Novel fusions identified include *MET-CAPZA2* (tandem duplication), *TRIM24-NTRK2*, *KIF5B-MET*, and *TP63-NTRK2* in SqCC. Amplification peaks near *FGFR1/WHSC1L1*, *PDGFRA/KIT/KDR*, and *MAPK1* are only significant in oncogene-negative ADCs.

**Genes / cancer types:** `SOS1`, `VAV1`, `RASA1`, `ARHGAP35`, `KRAS`, `EGFR`, `ALK`, `MET`, `NTRK2`, `NF1` · Lung ADC

### F4: Novel copy number peaks — MIR21 (ADC), MIR205 (SqCC), MAPK1 (both), CCND3 (ADC), YES1 (SqCC)

**Finding:** Several novel focal amplification peaks are identified beyond the canonical lung cancer CNA landscape. *MIR21* amplification is found in ADC (also seen in breast cancer) and is a known prognostic microRNA in early-stage ADC. *MIR205* amplification distinguishes SqCC and may represent a lineage-specific alteration similar to *SOX2* amplification.

**Detail:** Top ADC amplification peaks: *NKX2-1*, *MYC*, *TERT*, *MCL1*, *MDM2*, *KAT6A*, *ZNF217*, *MYCL1*, *CCND3*, *MIR21*. Top SqCC amplification peaks: *SOX2*, *CCND1*, *WHSC1L1/FGFR1*, *MYC*, *EGFR*, *IGF1R*, *KDM5A*, *YES1*, *MIR205*. Pan-lung combined analysis reveals *MAPK1* (22q11) as a novel amplification peak in both subtypes. *B2M* (MHC component) is focally deleted and enriched for loss-of-function mutations in both subtypes, implicating immune evasion. *TRAF3* is a novel pan-lung focal deletion (also seen in HNSC).

**Genes / cancer types:** `MIR21`, `MIR205`, `MAPK1`, `CCND3`, `YES1`, `B2M`, `TRAF3`, `SOX2`, `MYC` · Lung ADC and SqCC

**Data availability:** `available`

### F5: Neoantigen load supports immunotherapy potential in both ADC and SqCC; smoking-related mutation burden drives neoepitope count

**Finding:** 47% of lung ADC and 53% of lung SqCC samples had at least 5 predicted neoepitopes. Neoepitope counts are significantly lower in never-smoker ADC vs ever-smoker ADC (p<0.001), but not significantly different between ADC and SqCC from ever smokers.

**Detail:** Mutational signatures include smoking-related C>A transversions (SI4), APOBEC-associated C>G/C>T (SI13/SI2), mismatch repair (SI15/SI6), a UV-related signature in ~1% SqCC (potentially skin-to-lung metastases), and an SI5 "molecular clock" signature. The SI4 smoking signature distinguishes ever vs never smokers better in ADC (AUC=0.87) than SqCC (AUC=0.62). Seven MMR-deficient tumors (4 ADC, 3 SqCC) had elevated indel rates and lower *MLH1* expression. Highly recurrent predicted neoepitopes include *TP53* p.V157F, *PIK3CA* p.E542K, *EGFR* p.G719A, *NFE2L2* p.E79Q.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PIK3CA`, `EGFR`, `NFE2L2`, `B2M`, `MLH1` · Lung ADC, SqCC

**Data availability:** `partial`

## Open Questions

- Do the novel SMGs (*PPP3CA*, *DOT1L*, *SOS1*, *VAV1*) identified here have clinical significance in the era of comprehensive genomic profiling of lung cancer?
- Can the 15–24% of ADCs still "oncogene negative" for RTK/Ras/Raf be further characterized with RNA-seq fusion analysis or whole-genome sequencing to find additional rare drivers?
- Does the squamous lineage program (SqCC sharing drivers with HNSC/BLCA) respond to the same targeted therapies across tissue types, enabling tumor-agnostic trials?
- Can *MIR21* amplification in ADC serve as a clinically actionable biomarker for prognosis or therapeutic targeting?
- Do the MMR-deficient lung cancers (MLH1-low tumors) respond to immune checkpoint inhibitors at rates comparable to CRC?

## Limitations

- Exome sequencing does not capture non-coding mutations, promoter alterations, or regulatory element changes; whole-genome studies required for regulatory discoveries
- Only one biopsy per patient — intra-tumoral heterogeneity not analyzed
- RNA-seq fusion analysis not available for all tumors; fusion and *MET* exon 14 skipping rates may be underestimated
- 15–25% of ADCs remain oncogene-negative for RTK/Ras/Raf; these may harbor ultra-rare drivers not detectable at this sample size
- Neoantigen/HLA analysis relied on RNA-seq for HLA typing; not all tumors had matching RNA data (n=971 with available RNA-seq)
- Survival analysis not significant after multiple hypothesis testing correction; prognostic implications of novel SMGs remain unclear
