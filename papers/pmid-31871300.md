## Summary

This study characterized the clinical and genomic features of all TRK fusion-positive cancers identified at Memorial Sloan Kettering during a multi-year center-wide prospective sequencing program of >26,000 patients. Among 76 confirmed TRK fusion-positive patients spanning 17 cancer types and 48 unique rearrangements (0.28% overall prevalence), TRK fusions were strongly associated with the absence of concurrent canonical oncogenic drivers (p<0.001) and lower tumor mutational burden (TMB; median 1.8 vs. 3.5 mutations/Mb, p<0.001, excluding MSI-H colorectal cases). TRK fusions persisted across all sampled timepoints in 82% of serially profiled patients, consistent with clonal driver status. Chemotherapy ORR was 63% and TRK inhibitor ORR was 65%, while immunotherapy response was limited to one MSI-H colorectal patient. These findings established the genomic context of TRK fusion-positive cancers and provided the largest deeply annotated cohort at the time of FDA tumor-agnostic approval of larotrectinib and entrectinib.

## Key Findings

### F1: TRK fusions are strongly enriched in uncommon histologies; overall prevalence only 0.28% across 26,000+ patients

**Finding:** The overall prevalence of TRK fusions was 0.28% (74/26,312) across all cancer types, but with markedly higher rates in specific histologies: salivary carcinoma 5.29% (12/227), thyroid cancer 2.22% (10/451), sarcoma NOS 1.17% (9/770), and uterine sarcoma 1.15% (2/174). In contrast, common cancers had very low rates: lung adenocarcinoma 0.16% (6/3,658), invasive breast (non-secretory) 0.08% (3/3,775), and colon 0.35% (8/2,306).

**Detail:** The enrichment in salivary carcinoma predominantly reflects *ETV6-NTRK3* fusions in mammary analogue secretory carcinomas (11/12 salivary cases). Among 76 patients, salivary (16%), thyroid (13%), sarcoma (12%), and colon (11%) were the most common cancer types. Despite sequencing >1,561 prostate cancer patients, zero TRK fusion-positive cases were identified, suggesting prostate cancer as a histology with very low or absent TRK fusion prevalence. The distribution was broadly similar to that in the pivotal larotrectinib development program, supporting that clinical trials enrolled broadly representative populations. 48 unique fusion events were identified, with *ETV6* and *EML4* as the most common upstream partners (both exclusively with *NTRK3*); *NTRK1/2* had diverse, non-recurrent upstream partners.

### F2: TRK fusions are strongly associated with absence of concurrent canonical oncogenic drivers and lower TMB

**Finding:** Among 65 TRK fusion-positive cases with broader genomic profiling, only 1.5% (1 patient) harbored concurrent activating alterations in select canonical MAPK pathway oncogenes, compared to 31.4% of 25,989 TRK fusion-negative cases (p<0.001). Excluding MSI-H colorectal cancers, median TMB was significantly lower in TRK fusion-positive vs. negative tumors (1.8 vs. 3.5 mutations/Mb, p<0.001).

**Detail:** The near-complete absence of concurrent canonical drivers (e.g., KRAS, BRAF, EGFR, ALK, RET mutations/fusions) in TRK fusion-positive cancers suggests that TRK fusions typically occur as the primary driver mutation without requiring co-driver mutations for oncogenesis. The one exception — a melanoma with concurrent *GON4L-NTRK1* and *NRAS* Q61R — was one of only six patients in the larotrectinib trial to show progressive disease as best response. Functional studies confirmed the GON4L-NTRK1 fusion was not expressed at the protein level by pan-TRK IHC, and the PDX-derived cell line was insensitive to larotrectinib but responsive to MEK inhibitor trametinib — consistent with NRAS-driven rather than TRK-driven oncogenesis. Lower TMB in TRK fusion-positive tumors (excluding MSI-H CRC) suggests simpler genomes, potentially reflecting the sufficiency of a single strong driver for transformation.

### F3: TRK fusions persist as clonal drivers in 82% of serially profiled patients

**Finding:** Among 17 patients with more than one tumor sample analyzed at different timepoints, the TRK fusion was detected in all sampled timepoints in 82% (14/17) of cases. Three discordant cases were identified: 2 breast cancer patients where fusions were found in metastatic but not primary samples, and 1 GBM patient who lost the *TPM3-NTRK1* fusion at progression (replaced by *EGFR* amplification after TRK inhibitor treatment).

**Detail:** The high stability of TRK fusions across time and disease sites in 82% of cases strongly supports their status as clonal, early driver events rather than passenger alterations. This biological finding partially explains the dramatic pan-cancer efficacy of TRK inhibitors: tumors with clonal TRK fusions as their sole driver are unlikely to have TRK-independent subclones capable of driving resistance. The GBM case is notable as a potential resistance mechanism — loss of the TRK fusion and acquisition of *EGFR* amplification under selective TRK inhibitor pressure suggests outgrowth of a TRK-independent clone. The two breast cancer cases with fusion only in metastatic samples may represent either subclonal fusions selected during metastasis or technical detection limits in primary samples.

### F4: Chemotherapy ORR of 63% and TRK inhibitor ORR of 65%; immunotherapy has limited activity except in MSI-H colorectal cancer

**Finding:** Among 51 patients who developed advanced/recurrent disease, the best ORR with chemotherapy-containing regimens across all lines of therapy was 62.5% (15/24; 95% CI: 40.6–81.2%). TRK inhibitor ORR was 64.7% (23/34; 95% CI: 46.5–80.3%). Among 12 patients treated with checkpoint inhibitors, only 1 (an MSI-H colorectal patient, who achieved a complete response lasting ≥3.5 years) responded — an ORR of 11.1% (95% CI: 0.3–48.2%).

**Detail:** The comparable ORR of chemotherapy and TRK inhibitors (63% vs. 65%) is striking, but TRK inhibitors are approved based on their tumor-agnostic efficacy across diverse cancer types in the context of FDA-accelerated approval. The low immunotherapy response rate (1/9 non-MSI patients had a response) is consistent with the lower TMB and absence of other immunotherapy biomarkers in TRK fusion-positive cancers. The MSI-H colorectal patients (86% of TRK+ CRC cases were MSI-H) represent a distinct subgroup where both TRK fusions and MSI co-occur, and immunotherapy remains effective. First-line PFS (excluding TRK inhibitors) was 9.6 months (95% CI: 4.8–13.2), and median OS from initial diagnosis was 19.8 years (95% CI: 9.1–NR), though the latter is heavily influenced by the 68% of patients who received TRK inhibitors.

### F5: TRK fusions in colorectal cancer are strongly associated with microsatellite instability (MSI-H): 86% co-occurrence

**Finding:** Among 7 TRK fusion-positive colorectal cancer patients with MSI testing results available, 86% (6/7) were MSI-H. No TRK fusion-positive cancer outside of colorectal was MSI-H. This association was consistent with prior reports linking kinase fusion acquisition to MSI-driven replication error in colorectal cancer.

**Detail:** The co-occurrence of TRK fusions and MSI-H in colorectal cancer reflects a mechanistic connection: MSI-driven replication error creates an environment permissive for acquisition of chromosomal rearrangements including kinase fusions. This has important therapeutic implications: MSI-H CRC patients with TRK fusions are candidates for both TRK inhibitors and immunotherapy, whereas TRK fusion-positive cancers in other histologies without MSI-H status appear to have minimal immunotherapy benefit. This finding also explains why TRK fusion-positive CRC cases should be excluded from TMB analyses comparing TRK fusion-positive vs. negative cancers, as MSI-H confers markedly elevated TMB independent of TRK fusion status.

## Open Questions

- Do TRK fusion-positive cancers without MSI-H or other immunotherapy biomarkers ever respond to checkpoint inhibitors, and should they be routinely offered immunotherapy?
- Can the presence of co-occurring canonical drivers (e.g., NRAS Q61R in the melanoma case) be used to predict primary TRK inhibitor resistance?
- What is the acquired resistance mechanism profile (e.g., solvent-front mutations, bypass alterations) of TRK fusion-positive cancers treated with first-generation TRK inhibitors in real-world settings?
- Does TRK fusion type (NTRK1 vs. NTRK2 vs. NTRK3; fusion partner identity) predict differential TRK inhibitor efficacy or resistance?
- Is the high rate of TRK fusions in MSI-H colorectal cancer exploitable as a therapeutic target in the context of concurrent immunotherapy?
- What fraction of TRK fusions in 'discordant' cases (detected in metastasis but not primary) represent true subclonal events vs. technical detection failures?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=76) prevents formal efficacy comparisons between cancer types or formal prognostic analysis relative to TRK fusion-negative controls
- Heterogeneity of cancer types makes interpretation of aggregate clinical outcomes (ORR, PFS, OS) challenging
- TRK fusions ascertained at variable timepoints relative to diagnosis (median 2 years); survival estimates may be biased by TRK inhibitor use in 68% of patients
- Only 17 patients had serial molecular profiling; clonal persistence analysis underpowered
- RNA-based MSK-Fusion assay missed some fusions detectable only by DNA; discordance rate of 26% between DNA and RNA for *NTRK2/3* introns too large to tile
- Immunotherapy outcomes based on only 12 patients; conclusions are hypothesis-generating only
- The study predates broader use of next-generation TRK inhibitors (LOXO-195/selitrectinib, repotrectinib) addressing resistance mutations
