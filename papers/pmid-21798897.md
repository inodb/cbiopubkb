## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing of 32 primary HNSCCs (expanded to 120 for prevalence) identified NOTCH1 as the second most frequently mutated gene (15%), with nearly 40% of mutations predicted to truncate the protein — establishing NOTCH1 as a tumor suppressor rather than oncogene in this context. HPV-positive tumors harbored far fewer mutations (4.8/tumor vs. 20.6/tumor) and lacked TP53 alterations, while FBXW7 was identified as a new HNSCC driver. The landscape reveals a predominantly tumor-suppressor-driven disease with few directly targetable oncogenic mutations, with implications for therapeutic strategy.

## Key Findings

### F1: NOTCH1 is a tumor suppressor mutated in 15% of HNSCC — inactivating mutations predominate

**Finding:** 28 NOTCH1 mutations were identified across 120 samples (15% frequency); 11/28 (39%) are predicted truncating (7 nonsense, 4 indels). Seven of 21 patients with NOTCH1 mutations had two independent mutations (presumably biallelic), and LOH was detected at the NOTCH1 locus in 2/3 CNV-analyzed mutant tumors. Together, biallelic inactivation likely occurred in ≥9/21 NOTCH1-mutant patients.

**Detail:** HNSCC NOTCH1 mutations are distributed predominantly in the N-terminal EGF-like ligand-binding domain and are distinct from hematopoietic malignancy hotspots (HD domain and PEST domain). This spatial distinction — combined with the truncation-rich mutation spectrum — positions NOTCH1 as a tumor suppressor in squamous epithelium (consistent with NOTCH1-null mouse epithelial tumor models), contrasting with its oncogenic role in T-cell leukemia. Statistical significance is P < 10⁻⁸, far exceeding chance expectations.

**Genes / cancer types:** NOTCH1, FBXW7 · Head and neck squamous cell carcinoma

### F2: HPV-positive tumors are a genomically distinct, hypomutated disease with wild-type TP53

**Finding:** HPV-positive HNSCCs have 4.8 ± 3 mutations/tumor vs. 20.6 ± 16.7 in HPV-negative tumors (P < 0.05). TP53 mutations were absent in all HPV-associated tumors but present in 78% of HPV-negative tumors. This mutation gap is independent of smoking status and is consistent with HPV E6 protein-mediated TP53 degradation substituting for somatic TP53 mutation.

**Detail:** The difference in genomic complexity between HPV+ and HPV- tumors mirrors clinical differences in prognosis and treatment response — HPV+ patients have improved overall and disease-specific survival. The HPV+ genetic profile (few mutations, no TP53, wild-type CDKN2A) suggests distinct oncogenic dependencies that may be exploited therapeutically with de-escalated treatment approaches being explored in clinical trials.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, CDKN2A, HPV (viral etiology) · HNSCC (oropharynx predominantly for HPV+)

### F3: Tobacco use associates with higher mutation burden but unexpectedly lacks G:C>T:A transversion enrichment

**Finding:** Tobacco-associated HNSCCs have 21.6 ± 17.8 mutations/tumor vs. 9.5 ± 6.5 in non-smokers (P < 0.05). Contrary to lung cancer data, no enrichment for G:C>T:A transversions (the tobacco-smoke signature) was observed; the mutational spectrum in HNSCC resembles non-smoking-related cancers in terms of base substitution context.

**Detail:** This finding suggests that tobacco's carcinogenic mechanism in HNSCC differs from lung — possibly involving reactive oxygen species, acetaldehyde, or other tobacco components producing a different mutational footprint. The near-absence of a tobacco-specific transversion signature has implications for distinguishing tobacco vs. other risk factor contributions in the HNSCC mutational landscape and contrasts with TCGA HNSCC data.

### F4: FBXW7 identified as novel HNSCC driver (5%); mutations cluster at hotspots blocking NOTCH1 degradation

**Finding:** FBXW7 (F-box/WD repeat protein 7), an E3 ubiquitin ligase that targets NOTCH1, cyclin E (CCNE1), and c-MYC for degradation, is somatically mutated in 6/120 HNSCCs (5%), with mutations at known hotspots blocking substrate degradation. FBXW7 and NOTCH1 function in the same pathway, and FBXW7 hotspot mutations may stabilize active NOTCH1 — suggesting pathway convergence.

**Detail:** This is the first description of FBXW7 mutations in HNSCC, though they occur in multiple other tumor types. Four of the 6 FBXW7 mutations are missense at known hotspots; 2 are indels. None showed homozygosity, consistent with FBXW7's reported haploinsufficiency behavior. The connection to NOTCH1 through substrate stabilization adds complexity: FBXW7 loss could activate NOTCH1 (oncogenic in hematopoietic tumors) or bypass the need for NOTCH1 mutation in squamous cells where NOTCH1 normally promotes differentiation.

### F5: HNSCC is predominantly a tumor-suppressor-driven disease — only 18% of HPV- tumors have targetable oncogene mutations

**Finding:** Among 28 HPV-negative discovery tumors, only 18% had activating mutations in a bona fide oncogene (HRAS or PIK3CA). In contrast, 89% harbored inactivating mutations in at least one bona fide tumor suppressor (TP53, CDKN2A, NOTCH1, FBXW7). Recurrent CNA gains include 11q13 (CCND1), 3q (PIK3CA), and 7p11 (EGFR).

**Detail:** The predominance of tumor suppressor inactivation over oncogene activation limits direct pharmacological targeting in the majority of HNSCCs. This finding suggests prevention, early detection, and immunological strategies may be more effective than targeted therapies for most patients. Among the minority with targetable alterations, PIK3CA (6%) and HRAS (4%) are candidates for PI3K and farnesyltransferase/MEK inhibitors respectively.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Does NOTCH1 inactivation in HNSCC promote dedifferentiation, epithelial-mesenchymal transition, or resistance to squamous differentiation therapies (retinoids)?
- Could FBXW7 loss and NOTCH1 loss cooperate in the same tumor (both inactivating the NOTCH1/FBXW7 axis via independent mechanisms)?
- What explains the lack of a tobacco-specific G:C>T:A transversion signature in HNSCC, and does this have implications for carcinogenesis models?
- Do HPV-negative HNSCCs with no targetable oncogene mutations (82% of patients) benefit from immune checkpoint blockade due to high tumor mutation burden?
- Is there a functional distinction between NOTCH1 truncating mutations (N-terminal, within EGF-like domain) and missense mutations in terms of tumor suppressor activity and clinical outcome?

## Limitations

- Discovery cohort is small (n=32); statistical power for discovering rare drivers is limited; overall confirmation rate by Sanger/454 was only 67% (17% failed, 16% untestable)
- Mutational spectrum analysis based on 32 tumors only — limited statistical confidence for the unexpected absence of tobacco-signature transversions
- Copy number analysis performed on only 42 of 120 tumor-normal pairs; CNA landscape is incomplete
- HPV testing methods and thresholds not fully standardized across both sequencing centers (JHU vs. HGSC Baylor)
- No RNA-seq or methylation data included; transcriptional consequences of NOTCH1 inactivation not directly assessed
