## Summary

Largest pan-cancer genomic profiling study of Asian (predominantly Chinese) cancer patients, using a validated 450-gene deep NGS panel (CSYS, OrigiMed, CLIA/CAP-certified) on 10,194 tumor–matched blood pairs spanning 25 principal tumor types. The study comprehensively characterizes the somatic mutation landscape, gene fusions, copy number alterations, TMB, MSI, and PD-L1 expression in the Asian oncology population. Systematic comparison to the MSK-IMPACT cohort (n=10,366) and TCGA pan-cancer atlas reveals broad similarity in mutation frequencies, with key differences in LUAD (higher EGFR, lower KRAS/KEAP1) and hepatobiliary tumors reflecting known East Asian epidemiology. 64% of Chinese patients harbor at least one OncoKB-actionable genomic alteration. Data accessible on cBioPortal as `pan_origimed_2020`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Pan-cancer mutation landscape: TP53, KRAS, TERT, EGFR, APC are the most altered genes

**Finding:** The most frequently altered cancer-related genes across 10,194 Chinese solid tumors were TP53 (58%), KRAS (18%), TERT (14%), EGFR (13%), APC (13%), CDKN2A (12%), and PIK3CA (11%). The most common recurrent hotspot mutations were KRAS G12, EGFR L858, and TP53 R273. TP53 hotspot patterns showed tumor-type specificity: R249S was enriched in LIHC (10%) and ICC (3%), while R175H/L dominated CRC and PAC.

**Detail:** 80,703 SNVs/InDels, 19,192 truncations, 17,779 gene amplifications, 1,688 homozygous deletions, and 3,111 fusions/rearrangements were detected. CNV analysis showed high frequencies of CDKN2A/B deletion, SMAD4 deletion, ERBB2 amplification, EGFR amplification, and MYC amplification in metastatic samples. ERBB2 amplification was enriched in BRCA (24% vs. 2% pan-cancer; FDR=7.6E-105) and 11q13.3 amplification (CCND1/FGF3/FGF4/FGF19) was enriched in ESCA (43% vs. 4%; FDR=3.6E-301).

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, KRAS, TERT, EGFR, APC, CDKN2A, PIK3CA, ERBB2, CCND1, SMAD4, MYC · Pan-cancer (25 tumor types)

### F2: Key East–West genomic differences: EGFR high in Chinese LUAD; KRAS/KEAP1 low vs. Western cohorts

**Finding:** Systematic propensity-score-matched comparison of the advanced OrigiMed (aOM) cohort with MSK-IMPACT and TCGA pan-cancer atlas revealed only 12 tumor-type:gene pairs with significantly different alteration frequencies (FDR < 0.05). The most prominent differences were LUAD: EGFR higher in aOM (consistent with known East Asian NSCLC biology), LUAD: KRAS lower, and LUAD: KEAP1 lower in aOM vs. both MSK and TCGA.

**Detail:** CRC: APC (71.9% aOM vs. 72.7% MSK; FDR=1) and SCLC: RB1 (84.2% vs. 71.1%; FDR=1) were strikingly similar between cohorts. Hepatobiliary tumor types showed some differences (ICC: KRAS). In NSCLC, female nonsmokers were enriched for EGFR mutations while male smokers were enriched for TP53, CDKN2A, PIK3CA, and KRAS, consistent with global reports. In younger gastric cancer patients, CDH1 mutations were more frequent; in older patients, TP53, NOTCH1, and FAT4 were more common.

**Genes / cancer types:** EGFR, KRAS, KEAP1, CDH1, TP53, NOTCH1, APC, RB1, TFE3 · LUAD, LUSC, CRC, GC, LIHC, SCLC, ICC, KIRC

### F3: 64% of Chinese cancer patients harbor actionable alterations; Level 1 driven by TMB-H and EGFR

**Finding:** Using OncoKB v3.6 criteria, 64% of patients (n=6,498) had at least one genomic variant with clinical actionability evidence (Level 1: 32%; Level 2: 1%; Level 3A: 1%; Level 3B: 13%; Level 4: 16%). Excluding TMB-H as a biomarker, 58% were actionable. The highest Level 1 actionability rates were in NSCLC (driven by TMB-H and EGFR mutations including L858R [20%] and exon 19 del [19%]), BRCA (PIK3CA mutations [31%] and ERBB2 amplification [24%]), and CRC (MSI-H [8%]).

**Detail:** The most common actionable variants population-wide were KRAS, EGFR, and PIK3CA SNVs/InDels; ERBB2 amplification; and ALK fusions—consistent with MSK cohort findings. An additional 4% of patients without OncoKB Level 1–4 variants were PD-L1 positive, providing an immune checkpoint biomarker rationale. In NSCLC, TMB-H was negatively associated with fusion-positive status (3% vs. 13% in TMB-H vs. TMB-L; P=1.31E-11), while MSI-H was positively associated with fusions (mainly NTRK; P=0.04).

**Genes / cancer types:** EGFR, KRAS, PIK3CA, ERBB2, ALK, NTRK1/3, ROS1, RET, FGFR2/3 · NSCLC, CRC, BRCA, SCLC, GC

### F4: MSI-H prevalence 2% pan-cancer; SCLC, NSCLC, ESCA have highest combined immunotherapy biomarker positivity

**Finding:** MSI-H was found in 186/10,194 tumors (2% overall), predominantly in CRC (55%, 102/186 of all MSI-H cases). TMB and MSI were compared in 2,723 tumors with concurrent PD-L1 IHC data: 30.3% were positive for at least one of MSI-H, TMB-H (≥10 Muts/Mb), or PD-L1 positivity. SCLC had the highest combined positive rate (48%), followed by NSCLC (46%) and ESCA (34%), suggesting large proportions of Chinese lung cancer patients may benefit from immunotherapy.

**Detail:** CD274 (PD-L1) amplification was found in 1% of tumors (85 cases), with 70% of evaluable amplified cases showing PD-L1 IHC positivity. CD274 amplification co-occurred with adjacent PDCD1LG2 and JAK2 amplification (89% and 82%, respectively), located at 9p24.3-9p22.2, and with high TP53 mutation frequency (78%). MSI-H and TMB-H are independent biomarkers with no significant correlation across most cancer subtypes.

**Genes / cancer types:** CD274 (PD-L1), PDCD1LG2, JAK2, TP53, POLE · NSCLC, CRC, SCLC, ESCA, and pan-cancer

### F5: 513 fusion events identified; ALK, ROS1, RET, FGFR2/3, NTRK1/3 are pan-tumor actionable fusions

**Finding:** 513 fusion events involving 31 driver genes were identified across the cohort. ALK fusions (n=139), ROS1 fusions (n=51), RET fusions (n=50), FGFR2/3 fusions (n=50), and NTRK1/3 fusions (n=30) were the most common pan-tumor actionable fusions, occurring across multiple tumor types. EWSR1 fusions were enriched in sarcomas and TFE3 fusions in KIRC. PRKACA fusions were exclusive to LIHC (fibrolamellar hepatocellular carcinoma). Novel fusion partners included GRIK2-ROS1, PARP12-BRAF, KIF13B-MET, and LRRC28-NTRK3.

**Detail:** 57 driver-partner relationships were detected spanning 71 genes total. KIF5B-ALK and EML4-ALK fusions showed novel exon combinations compared to the Quiver database. In NSCLC, ALK fusions were negatively associated with TMB-H status, while NTRK fusions were positively associated with MSI-H status—suggesting different immunotherapy combination strategies for different fusion-positive subgroups.

**Genes / cancer types:** ALK, ROS1, RET, FGFR2, FGFR3, NTRK1, NTRK3, BRAF, MET, EWSR1, TFE3, PRKACA · NSCLC, LIHC, STS, KIRC, and pan-cancer

## Open Questions

- Do the identified East-West differences in EGFR and KRAS frequencies (and lower KEAP1 in Chinese LUAD) translate into different treatment response rates for EGFR TKIs versus chemotherapy?
- What drives the TP53 R249S hotspot enrichment in Chinese LIHC (10%) — is this primarily hepatitis B virus-related aflatoxin exposure, and how does it affect treatment response?
- Can the NTRK fusion–MSI-H positive association in NSCLC be exploited therapeutically via combination of fusion-targeted therapy and immunotherapy?
- How does the CD274/JAK2/PDCD1LG2 co-amplification at 9p24 in Chinese cancer patients compare to the frequencies seen in Western lymphoma cohorts?
- Does the germline landscape of Chinese cancer patients differ substantially from Western patients in ways not captured by this somatic-only study?

## Limitations

- Germline data were not collected; analysis is somatic-only, precluding assessment of hereditary cancer predisposition differences between Chinese and Western patients
- Limited available clinical confounders for PSM (propensity score matching) — factors such as age, grade, tumor subtype, sequencing depth, and ancestry were incompletely captured across both cohorts
- Comparisons with TCGA were restricted to SNVs and InDels (not CNV or fusions) due to methodological differences
- 55% of patients had advanced-stage disease, potentially biasing comparisons to other cohorts with different stage distributions
- PD-L1 IHC data available in only 2,723/10,194 samples (27%), limiting immune biomarker analyses
