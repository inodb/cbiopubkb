## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing of 243 liver tumors from a European multi-center cohort revealed 4 operative mutational signatures (including 2 novel: signatures 23 and 24), with signature 24 linked to aflatoxin B1 (AFB1) exposure and signature 16 associated with alcohol/tobacco co-exposure. Fourteen significantly mutated genes and 161 putative driver genes across 11 recurrently altered pathways were identified; TERT promoter mutations (60%) emerged as the earliest event, while FGF3/4/19 amplification and CDKN2A loss characterize advanced, aggressive HCC. Twenty-eight percent of patients harbored at least one genomic alteration potentially targetable by FDA-approved drugs, establishing a roadmap for genomics-driven clinical trial design.

## Key Findings

### F1: Two novel mutational signatures identified — signature 24 (aflatoxin B1) and signature 23 (unknown)

**Finding:** De novo signature analysis identified 4 signatures; 2 are novel. Signature 24 is characterized by high C>A mutation rates, exclusively in patients born in subtropical Africa and infected with HBV; 3/5 MSig2 tumors carry the R249S TP53 mutation (characteristic of AFB1 exposure), directly linking signature 24 to aflatoxin B1 genotoxicity. Signature 23 occurred in a single hypermutated tumor with anthracotic pigment deposition and shows C>T predominance with strong strand bias, suggestive of an uncharacterized carcinogen–NER interaction.

**Detail:** Six known signatures (1A, 1B, 4, 5, 6, 16) plus two novel ones (23, 24) were operative across the 243 tumors. Signature 16 — associated with alcohol/tobacco synergy — defined MSig3 (19 HCC with 67% alcohol/tobacco exposure and 74% CTNNB1 mutation rate, P = 6×10⁻⁵), suggesting a genotoxic synergism producing a distinct molecular subtype. Validation in ICGC-Japan (452 tumors) and TCGA (198 tumors) confirmed signatures 6, 23, and 24.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53 (R249S hotspot), CTNNB1 · Hepatocellular carcinoma (HBV-related, alcohol/tobacco-related)

### F2: Etiology-specific driver gene associations: CTNNB1/alcohol, TP53/HBV, IL6ST/unknown etiology

**Finding:** Alcohol-associated HCC are significantly enriched for CTNNB1, TERT, CDKN2A, SMARCA2, and HGF alterations (P < 0.05). HBV-related HCC are significantly enriched for TP53 mutations. IL6ST mutations were exclusively found in HCC with no known etiology. HCV, metabolic syndrome, and hemochromatosis showed no significant gene-level associations.

**Detail:** These etiology-driver associations define three major co-alteration clusters: CTNNB1-cluster (alcohol/TERT), AXIN1-cluster, and TP53-cluster (HBV). Mutual exclusivity and co-occurrence patterns within and across clusters suggest cooperation and functional redundancy within pathways and may predict differential sensitivity to targeted agents (e.g., WNT inhibitors in CTNNB1-cluster tumors). The TP53/HBV association is further strengthened by the AFB1-specific R249S TP53 hotspot, which is not a typical random TP53 mutation.

**Genes / cancer types:** CTNNB1, TP53, AXIN1, IL6ST, TERT, HGF, CDKN2A, SMARCA2 · Hepatocellular carcinoma

### F3: TERT promoter mutation is the earliest genomic event; FGF3/4/19 amplification and CDKN2A loss mark advanced stages

**Finding:** TERT promoter mutations are already frequent in dysplastic macronodules (early pre-malignant stage) and remain prevalent across all HCC stages (60% overall), establishing TERT as the earliest driver. By contrast, FGF3/FGF4/FGF19/CCND1 amplification and CDKN2A alterations increase significantly in frequency from early to poor-prognosis HCC stages (P < 0.01). TP53 and CTNNB1 mutation frequencies also increase with progression.

**Detail:** Multivariate survival analysis confirms that CDKN2A inactivation and FGF/CCND1 amplification independently predict poor overall survival in resected HCC, controlling for classical clinical/histological factors. This positions CDKN2A and FGF3/4/19 as progression biomarkers with direct prognostic utility. TERT promoter mutations occur later in non-cirrhotic HCC (hepatocellular adenoma → HCC transformation), suggesting context-dependent timing.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, CDKN2A, FGF3, FGF4, FGF19, CCND1, TP53, CTNNB1 · Hepatocellular carcinoma

### F4: 28% of HCC harbor FDA-approved targetable alterations; 86% have drug-trial-level targets

**Finding:** Integration of 161 putative driver genes across 11 recurrently altered pathways reveals FDA-approved targetable alterations in 28% of HCCs: FLT kinase amplifications/mutations (6%), FGF3/4/19 (4%), PDGFRs (3%), EPHA4 (3%), JAK3 (3%), VEGFA (1%), HGF (3%), MTOR (2%), EGFR (1%), among others. 86% of patients have alterations targeted by phase I–III clinical trial drugs.

**Detail:** A notable finding is that RPS6KA3 mutations (7% of HCC) — an RSK kinase family member — cause ERK1/2 hyperactivation in vitro, positioning RPS6KA3-mutant HCC as potential candidates for MEK or ERK inhibitor therapy despite lacking upstream KRAS/BRAF mutations. Additionally, KEAP1/NFE2L2 mutations (oxidative stress pathway, 12%) predict sensitivity to HSP90 inhibitors (17-AAG/17-DMAG) via NQO1 upregulation — validated in 29 liver cancer cell lines (Pearson r = −0.56, P = 0.0015).

**Genes / cancer types:** FLT3, FGF3/4/19, PDGFRA/B, JAK3, VEGFA, MET, HGF, MTOR, EGFR, ERBB2, RPS6KA3, KEAP1, NFE2L2 · Hepatocellular carcinoma

### F5: Eleven major altered pathways define HCC biology — TERT, WNT, PI3K, TP53, MAPK, hepatic differentiation, and chromatin remodeling

**Finding:** Pathway annotation of 161 driver genes reveals 11 pathways altered in ≥5% of HCC: TERT promoter/telomerase (60%), WNT/β-catenin (54%), PI3K/AKT/mTOR (51%), TP53/cell cycle (49%), MAP kinase (43%), hepatic differentiation (34%), epigenetic regulation (32%), chromatin remodeling (28%), oxidative stress (12%), IL6/JAK/STAT (9%), and TGFβ (5%). Novel driver genes include WNT inhibitors ZNRF3/USP34/MACF1, and ACVR2A (TGFβ receptor).

**Detail:** The three major genetic clusters (CTNNB1, AXIN1, TP53) show distinct co-association and mutual exclusivity patterns that may reflect cooperative interactions within pathways, functional redundancy, or synthetic lethality. Genes within a single pathway distribute across different clusters, suggesting the clusters represent biologically distinct HCC subtypes rather than simple pathway groupings.

**Genes / cancer types:** TERT, CTNNB1, AXIN1, TP53, ARID1A, ARID2, NFE2L2, KEAP1, ZNRF3, ACVR2A · Hepatocellular carcinoma

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- What is the precise carcinogen responsible for signature 23 (associated with anthracotic pigment deposition)? Does it implicate air pollution-related compounds (coal dust, carbon particles)?
- Can KEAP1/NFE2L2-mutant HCC patients be reliably identified for HSP90 inhibitor (17-AAG/DMAG) clinical trials, particularly accounting for the NQO1*2 allele confound?
- Does the three-cluster genetic structure (CTNNB1, AXIN1, TP53) predict differential responses to sorafenib, lenvatinib, or immunotherapy?
- Does TERT promoter mutation in dysplastic macronodules predict progression to HCC, enabling surveillance biomarker applications?
- Can RPS6KA3-mutated HCC (7%) respond to MEK or ERK inhibitors analogously to RAS-mutant cancers, given the indirect ERK activation demonstrated in vitro?

## Limitations

- European cohort only (France, Spain, Italy); HBV-endemic Asia–Africa subtypes under-represented; AFB1-related HCC present only in 5 samples (MSig2), limiting statistical power for that group
- Exome mean depth of 72× is lower than contemporary standards; estimated 12% sensitivity loss vs. Sanger sequencing for specific genes (partly captured by systematic Sanger validation of 11 key genes in 155 samples)
- Structural variants (gene fusions), intronic mutations, and non-coding drivers outside TERT promoter are not captured by exome design
- Cell line HSP90 inhibitor validation limited to 29 cell lines; prospective clinical evidence for NQO1-guided patient selection not yet established
- Survival analysis based on resected cohort only; advanced/unresectable HCC patients not represented, limiting generalizability to all-stage treatment decisions
