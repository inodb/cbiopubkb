## Summary

Landmark WES study of 64 metastatic melanoma patients treated with ipilimumab or tremelimumab (discovery n=25, validation n=39) that established the genomic basis of clinical benefit from CTLA-4 blockade. Mutational burden was significantly associated with long-term clinical benefit but insufficient alone to predict response. Using patient-specific HLA typing and a bioinformatic neoantigen pipeline (NAseek), the authors identified a tetrapeptide neoantigen signature enriched exclusively in long-term benefit tumors. This neoantigen signature outperformed mutational burden for survival prediction and was validated in an independent cohort and by in vitro T-cell activation assays. Mutational data are accessible in `skcm_mskcc_2014` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Mutational burden associates with CTLA-4 benefit but is insufficient alone

**Finding:** There was a significant difference in mutational load (nonsynonymous coding mutations per exome) between long-term benefit and minimal/no-benefit patients in both the discovery (p=0.01, Mann-Whitney) and validation sets (p=0.009). High mutational burden correlated with improved overall survival in the discovery set (p=0.04, log-rank), with a trend in validation. However, tumors with >1000 mutations could still fail to respond, establishing that burden alone does not determine benefit.

**Detail:** Mutational loads ranged widely in both groups. The patient with the highest mutation count in the minimal-benefit group (1028 mutations, Patient SD7357) shared none of the neoantigen signature tetrapeptides. Similarly, in the validation set, patients NR9521 and NR4631 had >1000 mutations and still did not respond. Subgroup analysis in the discovery set suggested a dose-response relationship between mutation count and clinical benefit (four categories), but this was not strong enough for clinical utility. UV-induced transition-to-transversion ratios and nucleotide change spectra were similar between the discovery and validation sets, consistent with the UV mutational signature expected in melanoma.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRAF, NRAS (documented in clinical table); genome-wide somatic mutations · Melanoma

### F2: Shared tetrapeptide neoantigen signature predicts long-term CTLA-4 benefit

**Finding:** A bioinformatic pipeline (NAseek) translating all nonsynonymous missense mutations into mutant nonamers, filtering for MHC class I binding (≤500 nM affinity), and identifying patient-specific HLA-presented neoepitopes revealed 101 tetrapeptide sequences shared exclusively among long-term benefit patients in the discovery set. This neoantigen signature was independently validated in the 39-patient validation set and outperformed mutational burden for overall survival prediction (p<0.001, log-rank, in both sets).

**Detail:** Consensus tetrapeptide analysis identified a shared "neoantigen landscape" specific to responders. Not all tetrapeptides were in the same genes; they were distributed across diverse loci, all confirmed to be highly expressed in melanoma by TCGA RNA-seq data. Simulation testing with five independent statistical models confirmed the association was highly unlikely by chance (p<0.001 for four methods; p=0.002 for a fifth). Notably, neoantigen signature presence correlated more strongly with survival than mutational load alone. The neoepitopes also showed homology to viral and bacterial antigens in the Immune Epitope Database (IEDB), suggesting cross-reactivity with pathogen-primed T cells.

**Genes / cancer types:** FAM3C (harbors K193E mutation encoding TESPFEQHI neoepitope), CSMD1 (G3446E mutation encoding GLERE GFTF neoepitope) · Melanoma

### F3: In vitro T-cell activation confirms immunogenicity of predicted neoepitopes

**Finding:** Candidate neoantigen peptides synthesized and co-cultured with autologous PBMCs from ipilimumab-treated patients activated tumor-specific polyfunctional T cells. In patient CR9306, peptide TESPFEQHI (from FAM3C K193E mutation) elicited CD8+ IFN-γ/TNF-α responses absent for the wild-type TKSPFEQHI peptide; the response peaked at 60 weeks post-treatment. In patient CR0095, GLER-E-GFTF (from CSMD1 G3446E mutation) elicited a response peaking at 24 weeks.

**Detail:** Positive pools were observed for three of five patients tested. Individual peptide identification was performed in patients with adequate cryopreserved PBMCs. Intracellular cytokine staining confirmed polyfunctional response (IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-2, CD107a, MIP1β). Responses were absent in healthy donors. TESPFEQHI had predicted MHC class I affinity for B4402 of 472 nM vs. 18323 nM for the wild-type peptide. ESPF is a substring of a hepatitis D virus large delta epitope, and GLER-E-GFTF has 80% homology to a Burkholderia pseudomallei antigen (IEDB ID: 1027043). MART-1/MelanA EKLS tetrapeptide was also recovered, serving as a positive control for the computational approach.

**Genes / cancer types:** FAM3C, CSMD1 · Melanoma

### F4: Neoantigen landscape is shaped by non-random amino acid composition

**Finding:** The neoepitope tetrapeptides common to long-term benefit patients showed a distinct amino acid frequency distribution relative to those shared among non-benefit patients, indicating that the responding epitopes are not simply a consequence of higher mutational load generating more random neoepitopes, but reflect a structurally non-random set of immunogenic sequences.

**Detail:** Frequency analysis of amino acids composing the benefit-associated tetrapeptides (Figure S7A) revealed enrichment for specific residues. The shared neoepitopes in benefit patients were homologous to viral/bacterial antigens in IEDB more often than neoepitopes in non-benefit patients (Table S9), supporting the hypothesis that tumor neoepitopes resembling pathogen-derived epitopes are more likely to be recognized by pre-existing memory T cells and drive durable antitumor immunity after CTLA-4 blockade.

### F5: Study redefines "passenger" mutations as potential immune determinants

**Finding:** Whole-exome data show that exonic missense mutations in general confer increased MHC class I binding affinity for the mutant peptide vs. the wild-type counterpart (Figure S5A–B), suggesting that many previously categorized passenger mutations may serve as "immune determinants" contributing to tumor immunogenicity and shaping immune response regardless of whether the mutation drives oncogenesis.

**Detail:** The aggregate comparison of mutant vs. non-mutant peptides across all missense mutations in all patients showed that mutant peptides were predicted to bind MHC class I with higher affinity than wild-type counterparts (Figure S5). This extends the classical driver/passenger mutation dichotomy: some missense passengers may nonetheless generate neoantigens that influence immune surveillance. This concept informed subsequent large-scale efforts to build neoantigen prediction frameworks as clinical biomarkers across cancer types.

**Genes / cancer types:** Genome-wide somatic missense mutations · Melanoma

## Open Questions

- Can tetrapeptide neoantigen signature prediction be extended to predict benefit from PD-1/PD-L1 blockade or combination checkpoint therapy?
- What is the contribution of MHC class II neoepitopes (CD4+ T-cell responses) to durable CTLA-4 benefit?
- Do neoantigens homologous to pathogen-derived epitopes have higher affinity T-cell receptor interactions and, if so, are they more predictive of benefit?
- Can a prospective, WES-based neoantigen assay be clinically deployed for pre-treatment patient selection?

## Limitations

- Small discovery set (n=25); statistical power to identify all relevant neoepitopes is limited, and some true immunogenic epitopes may have been missed.
- Patients received varied prior treatments, and tumor samples were collected at different time points, potentially introducing heterogeneity in mutational profiles.
- MHC class II neoantigen prediction and relative contributions of different peptide lengths were not evaluated.
- In vitro T-cell assays have low sensitivity; absence of response in vitro does not exclude in vivo immunogenicity.
- HLA typing and neoantigen data are not in cBioPortal, making systematic re-analysis of the neoantigen signature computationally demanding.
