## Summary

First systematic whole-exome sequencing study of 22 gynecologic carcinosarcomas (malignant mixed Müllerian tumors, MMMTs) — 17 uterine and 5 ovarian — with matched normal tissue. Average sequencing coverage 191×. Four tumors were hypermutated due to somatic MMR gene mutations (*MLH1*, *MSH6*); the remaining 18 had an average of 43 mutations per tumor. Beyond established genes (*TP53* 67%, *KRAS* 23%, *PIK3CA* 41%), chromatin remodeling genes were the most striking finding: nearly two-thirds of cases harbored mutations in *ARID1A*, *ARID1B*, *MLL3*, *SPOP*, or *BAZ1A*. PI3K pathway alterations (PIK3CA/PIK3R1/PTEN) were actionable in >50% of cases. Chromatin remodeling dysregulation is proposed as a key oncogenic mechanism in carcinosarcoma tumorigenesis. Data available in `ucs_jhu_2014` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Chromatin remodeling genes mutated in nearly two-thirds of carcinosarcomas

**Finding:** Mutations in chromatin remodeling and modification genes — *ARID1A*, *ARID1B*, *MLL3*, *SPOP*, and *BAZ1A* — were identified in ~64% of carcinosarcomas. This is one of the highest frequencies of chromatin remodeling dysregulation observed across any cancer type characterized at the time of publication.

**Detail:** *ARID1A* truncating mutations occurred in 32% (7/22) of tumors; three of those also carried *ARID1B* mutations. *ARID1A* mutations were exclusive to ovarian cases among microsatellite-stable tumors (4/5 ovarian vs. 0/13 uterine MSS), suggesting distinct mutational processes by site of origin. *MLL3* (histone H3K4 methyltransferase) was altered in 27% (6/22). *SPOP* (DAXX ubiquitin ligase recruiter) was mutated in 14% (3/22). *BAZ1A* (chromatin assembly factor subunit) was mutated in 4/22 (18%). Mutations in these genes collectively implicate loss of chromatin accessibility control, nucleosome spacing, and epigenetic gene regulation as key carcinosarcoma drivers.

**Genes / cancer types:** ARID1A, ARID1B, MLL3 (KMT2C), SPOP, BAZ1A · Gynecologic carcinosarcoma (uterine and ovarian)

### F2: *TP53* mutations are far more common than previously appreciated (67%), confirming carcinoma lineage

**Finding:** *TP53* mutations were identified in 67% (16/22) of carcinosarcomas — significantly higher than earlier estimates of ~25%. Seven cases had *TP53* mutation frequencies above 80% (consistent with biallelic inactivation in all tumor cells). This, together with the presence of *PTEN* mutations (which are rare in endometrial sarcomas but common in carcinomas), supports the molecular classification of carcinosarcomas as carcinomas rather than sarcomas.

**Detail:** Clonal analysis showed that mutations in known cancer genes — including *TP53* and PI3K pathway genes — were present at high variant allele frequencies consistent with early, clonal events. The number of non-synonymous mutations per carcinosarcoma (average 43) was similar to endometrial carcinomas and significantly higher than typical sarcomas, reinforcing the carcinoma lineage. These data molecularly support the "conversion theory" — that carcinosarcomas arise from a single carcinoma cell undergoing sarcomatous differentiation.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, PTEN · Gynecologic carcinosarcoma

### F3: PI3K pathway activated in >50% of cases via *PIK3CA*, *PIK3R1*, and *PTEN*

**Finding:** The PI3K pathway was activated in over half of carcinosarcomas: *PIK3CA* hotspot mutations in 9/22 (41%), *PTEN* loss-of-function in 9/22 (41%), and *PIK3R1* mutations in 3/22 (14%). *PIK3CA* and *PIK3R1* mutations were mutually exclusive (as in endometrial carcinoma), while *PTEN* loss co-occurred with other pathway members. PI3K pathway alterations were found in both uterine and ovarian cases.

**Detail:** This high frequency makes the PI3K/mTOR/AKT axis the single most promising therapeutic target in carcinosarcoma. PI3K/mTOR/AKT inhibitors and MEK inhibitors are in clinical trials for endometrial cancer and other gynecologic malignancies, and carcinosarcoma patients may be eligible for basket trials based on these alterations. *KRAS* hotspot mutations were found in 5/22 (23%) and may sensitize tumors to MEK/BRAF inhibitors. One *ERBB3* hotspot mutation (V104M) was also identified.

### F4: MMR-deficient tumors (18%) harbor thousands of mutations and may be immunotherapy-sensitive

**Finding:** Four of 22 carcinosarcomas (18%) had a mutator phenotype (904–5,913 somatic alterations), all due to somatic mutations in mismatch repair (MMR) genes: *MLH1* (1 case), *MSH6* (3 cases). This MMR-deficient subgroup carries potential sensitivity to PD-1 checkpoint blockade. *MSH6* mutations in these tumors showed an overrepresentation of C:G to A:T transversions, a mutational signature consistent with impaired oxidative damage repair.

**Detail:** The prevalence of MMR deficiency in carcinosarcomas (~18%) is higher than in most solid tumors and is consistent with reports of MSI in endometrial carcinomas and other gynecological cancers. At the time of publication, anti-PD-1 immunotherapy (e.g., MK-3475/pembrolizumab) was in trials for MSI-high cancers, making this a directly actionable subgroup. Detection of somatic MMR gene mutations rather than epigenetic silencing may have implications for germline counseling.

**Genes / cancer types:** MLH1, MSH6, MSH2 · Gynecologic carcinosarcoma

### F5: HR pathway alterations (*BRCA1/2*, *FANCM*) and *FBXW7* mutations further expand actionable targets

**Finding:** Homologous recombination (HR) pathway alterations were identified: two somatic *BRCA2* nonsense mutations, one *BRCA1* frameshift, and two *FANCM* splice/frameshift mutations. A germline *BRCA2* nonsense mutation (K3326X) was detected in one patient with family history of cancer. *FBXW7* was mutated in 23% (5/22) of cases, with preclinical evidence suggesting HDAC inhibitor sensitivity for *FBXW7*-deficient tumors.

**Detail:** The presence of *BRCA1/2* and *FANCM* defects suggests that a subset of carcinosarcomas may be sensitive to PARP inhibitors or platinum-based cross-linking agents. The germline *BRCA2* finding (K3326X) has both clinical and familial implications. *FBXW7* encodes an E3 ubiquitin ligase that targets cyclin E; its loss may impair cell cycle checkpoints and render tumors vulnerable to HDAC inhibitor treatment. These findings were among the earliest descriptions of actionable alterations in carcinosarcoma.

**Genes / cancer types:** BRCA1, BRCA2, FANCM, FBXW7 · Gynecologic carcinosarcoma

## Open Questions

- Does the site-specific restriction of *ARID1A* mutations to ovarian carcinosarcomas (vs. uterine) reflect different cells of origin, mutational exposures, or prognosis?
- Can PARP inhibitors benefit carcinosarcoma patients with somatic *BRCA1/2* or *FANCM* alterations, and does *BRCA2* germline status predict sensitivity?
- What are the downstream transcriptional targets of *ARID1A/ARID1B* loss in carcinosarcoma, and can they be pharmacologically exploited?
- Does preclinical sensitivity of *FBXW7*-mutant tumors to HDAC inhibitors translate to carcinosarcoma clinical responses?
- Is the MSI-H/MMR-deficient subgroup (~18%) clinically distinct in terms of response to platinum-based chemotherapy and PD-1 blockade?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (N=22); limited statistical power for discovery of less-frequent recurrently mutated genes
- WES only; no RNA-seq, so fusion transcripts were not systematically evaluated
- No survival or treatment outcome data presented; clinical correlations not possible
- Germline analysis performed on only 84 genes; broader germline risk characterization not available
- All samples are primary tumors (except one recurrence); metastatic evolution not captured
