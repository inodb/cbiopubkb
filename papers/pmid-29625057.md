## Summary

This study establishes the first large biobank of patient-derived bladder cancer organoid lines (n=22 lines from 16 patients) spanning the full histopathological spectrum of urothelial carcinoma — from low-grade non-muscle invasive to high-grade muscle invasive disease. The organoids closely recapitulate parental tumor histology and molecular profiles, frequently retain intratumoral heterogeneity, and can be interconverted with orthotopic xenografts. Genomic analyses reveal clonal evolution during culture consistent with selection pressure, while drug response studies show partial correlations with mutational profiles (e.g., FGFR3-mutant lines responding to FGFR inhibitors). The platform provides a functional co-clinical model for studying tumor evolution and treatment resistance in the context of precision bladder cancer medicine. The associated cBioPortal study (bladder_columbia_msk_2018) contains the genomic and clinical data for the patient donors.

## Key Findings

### F1: Patient-derived bladder cancer organoids faithfully recapitulate parental tumor histology and molecular profiles

**Finding:** Bladder tumor organoids established from TUR biopsies mirror the histopathological classification of their parental tumors (papillary low-grade, high-grade urothelial carcinoma, CIS), retain expression of urothelial markers (GATA3, CK5, CK20), and show high concordance in somatic mutation profiles with matched patient tissue by MSK-IMPACT sequencing.

**Detail:** 22 lines established from non-muscle invasive (Ta, T1) and muscle invasive (T2) bladder cancer at ~70% efficiency. Organoids reproduce the architectural patterns of their parental tumors (papillary vs. solid growth) and maintain urothelial differentiation markers in culture. Mutation concordance between organoids and matched tumor biopsies was high for clonal driver mutations. Orthotopic xenografts generated from organoids formed bladder tumors that histologically resembled the original patient tumor, confirming their translational fidelity.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR3`, `TP53`, `ERBB2`, `RB1`, `CDKN2A` · Urothelial carcinoma (bladder)

### F2: Organoids retain parental tumor heterogeneity and undergo clonal evolution during culture

**Finding:** Many bladder tumor organoid lines retain subclonal mutation heterogeneity from the parental tumor, and genomic analyses over serial passages reveal clonal evolution consistent with selection of specific subclones in culture — including both gain of new mutations and loss of subclonal variants present in the primary tumor.

**Detail:** Comparison of variant allele frequencies between parental tumor and organoid lines at different passages shows shifting clonal architecture. Subclonal mutations present in the parental tumor were sometimes lost (due to outgrowth of a different clone) or enriched in organoids. Some organoid lines also acquired new mutations during culture not detected in the primary biopsy, suggesting ongoing mutagenesis or the expansion of very rare clones. This evolution is consistent with selection pressures of 3D culture conditions and has implications for interpreting drug response data — the clone(s) being tested may differ from the dominant tumor clone in the patient.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `FGFR3`, `KDM6A`, `ARID1A` · Urothelial carcinoma (bladder)

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Clonal mutation data for organoid vs. parental tumor comparisons are in paper supplementary materials. The cBioPortal study includes tumor-level mutation profiles but does not contain paired organoid passage-level data.

### F3: FGFR3-mutant bladder cancer organoids show selective sensitivity to FGFR inhibitors

**Finding:** Organoid lines harboring activating FGFR3 mutations (common in low-grade non-muscle invasive bladder cancer) display preferential sensitivity to FGFR inhibitors (BGJ398/infigratinib) compared to FGFR3 wild-type lines, providing direct functional evidence linking FGFR3 mutation status to FGFR inhibitor response in patient-derived models.

**Detail:** Drug response profiling across the organoid biobank revealed that FGFR3-mutant lines (e.g., SCBO-3) had markedly lower IC50 values for BGJ398 than FGFR3 wild-type lines. This correlation was validated in orthotopic xenografts derived from the same organoid line, where BGJ398 treatment significantly reduced tumor burden in vivo. These results are consistent with the clinical efficacy of erdafitinib (pan-FGFR inhibitor) in FGFR3-altered metastatic urothelial carcinoma (FDA-approved 2019). The organoid system thus provides a co-clinical platform for prospective drug sensitivity prediction.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR3` · Urothelial carcinoma (bladder)

**Data availability:** `available`
> FGFR3 mutation status (hotspot mutations: R248C, S249C, G370C, Y373C) is queryable in bladder_columbia_msk_2018. Drug response IC50 data for the organoid panel are in the paper's supplementary data, not in cBioPortal.

**Tool support:** `native`

### F4: TP53 mutation and ERBB2 alterations associate with high-grade disease and worse outcomes; partial drug response correlations

**Finding:** High-grade muscle invasive bladder cancer organoid lines more frequently harbor TP53 mutations and ERBB2 amplification/mutation, consistent with the known molecular stratification of bladder cancer. Drug sensitivity in these lines showed partial but imperfect correlation with mutation profiles, with some ERBB2-altered lines showing sensitivity to HER2-targeted agents.

**Detail:** TP53 loss-of-function mutations were enriched in high-grade (T2) organoid lines, while FGFR3 gain-of-function mutations predominated in low-grade Ta/T1 lines — recapitulating the well-established molecular dichotomy of bladder cancer. ERBB2 mutations/amplification in select lines conferred partial sensitivity to lapatinib and neratinib in vitro, though responses were heterogeneous. Importantly, drug responses to standard-of-care agents (cisplatin, gemcitabine) were not uniformly predicted by any single mutation, highlighting the complexity of chemotherapy resistance. One line showed acquired resistance to cisplatin associated with new TP53 mutations emerging after drug exposure.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `ERBB2`, `RB1`, `CDKN2A` · Urothelial carcinoma (bladder), muscle invasive

### F5: Serial organoid sampling from recurrent tumors captures genomic evolution associated with treatment resistance

**Finding:** Organoid lines established from sequential biopsies of the same patient before and after disease recurrence (e.g., after BCG or intravesical chemotherapy) reveal genomic changes consistent with clonal selection under treatment pressure, providing a model for studying acquired treatment resistance in bladder cancer.

**Detail:** For three patients with multiple biopsies, paired organoid lines (e.g., SCBO-3 and SCBO-3.2 from the same patient) showed divergent genomic profiles at recurrence compared to the initial diagnostic sample, with new mutations and copy-number alterations emerging in the post-treatment organoid. These changes included loss of heterozygosity at tumor suppressor loci and emergence of new subclonal drivers. This temporal sampling approach is a unique feature of the organoid biobank and directly models the clonal dynamics observed in longitudinal clinical sequencing studies.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `FGFR3`, `KDM6A`, `ARID1A`, `RB1` · Urothelial carcinoma (bladder), recurrent

**Data availability:** `partial`

## Open Questions

- Can drug sensitivity profiles from bladder cancer organoids prospectively predict clinical responses in individual patients, enabling true co-clinical trial design?
- Do organoid culture conditions (serum, ROCK inhibitor, Matrigel) systematically select for specific subclones and bias drug response results?
- What is the molecular basis of cisplatin resistance emerging in some organoid lines after drug exposure, and do these mechanisms match those seen in clinical specimens?
- Can FGFR3 inhibitor sensitivity be predicted by mutation subtype (e.g., R248C vs. S249C) or is it uniform across all activating FGFR3 alterations?
- How does tumor microenvironment (absent in organoids) contribute to drug resistance in vivo, and can co-culture systems (cancer-associated fibroblasts, immune cells) better model clinical response?
- Can bladder cancer organoids be used to model BCG immunotherapy response, which requires immune cell interactions absent in standard organoid cultures?

## Limitations

- Organoids lack tumor microenvironment (stromal cells, immune cells, vasculature), limiting modeling of immunotherapy responses and stromal-mediated drug resistance
- ~30% failure rate in organoid establishment; failed lines likely represent a specific biological subset (e.g., highly necrotic or fibrotic tumors)
- Clonal evolution during culture may make organoid drug responses diverge from those of the dominant in vivo tumor clone
- Small cohort (n=16 patients) limits statistical power for correlating mutation profiles with drug response outcomes
- Drug response measured as IC50 in 3D culture; translation to in vivo pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics is imperfect
- MSK-IMPACT panel sequencing; whole-genome structural variants and non-coding driver alterations not captured
- No long-term clinical outcome data linking organoid drug response to patient treatment outcomes
