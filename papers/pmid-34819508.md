## Summary

Methodological paper introducing Ollivier–Ricci (OR) network curvature as a prognostic biomarker for HGSOC patients (n=45) treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs). Copy number alterations (CNAs) from targeted or WES sequencing are mapped onto a protein–protein interaction (PPI) network, and total curvature is computed as an unsupervised summary statistic of genome-wide CNA-driven network dysregulation. Network curvature outperforms TMB (p=0.03), FGA (p=0.20), and LST (p=0.43) in stratifying overall survival (OS; p=0.00047). TP53 consistently ranks as the top gene by curvature change across all comparisons, validating the method's biological relevance. Curvature is not predictive in non-ICI-treated HGSOC, suggesting the signal may be immunotherapy-specific. Data available in `hgsoc_msk_2021` on cBioPortal.

## Key Findings

### F1: Ollivier–Ricci network curvature stratifies OS in HGSOC ICI patients (p=0.00047), outperforming TMB, FGA, and LST

**Finding:** Total OR curvature of a CNA-weighted PPI network stratifies ICI-treated HGSOC patients into high-risk (bottom 25%) and low-risk (top 75%) groups with highly significant survival difference (log-rank p=0.00047). This outperforms TMB (p=0.032), FGA (p=0.196), and LST (p=0.429) as prognostic biomarkers for this population.

**Detail:** The curvature is computed on a shared PPI network topology (HPRD) with patient-specific node weights based on gene-level CNA values. The Ollivier–Ricci curvature of an edge measures the degree of overlap between the neighborhoods of its endpoints — high positive curvature reflects redundant feedback loops (robustness); low or negative curvature reflects network destabilization. Total curvature contracts edge curvatures through scalar curvature to a single scalar per patient. The 25th percentile cutoff was empirically selected where the sorted curvature curve begins incrementing linearly; maximally selected log-rank statistics yielded a comparable split.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, KRAS, MYC, AKT1, CCNE1, CDKN1A, RB1 · High-grade serous ovarian carcinoma

### F2: TP53 consistently ranks as the top gene by curvature change across all comparisons

**Finding:** TP53 has the largest positive change in scalar curvature (Δκ = 0.209) between low-risk and high-risk groups. TP53 also ranks first in curvature change for comparisons of alive vs. deceased patients at last follow-up and for primary vs. metastatic tumor comparisons. This finding is consistent with TP53's known role as the dominant driver of HGSOC via aneuploidy induction.

**Detail:** Other top-ranked genes with positive curvature change (associating with worse OS) include ATXN1, EP300, SMAD2, PIK3R1, SRC, SMAD4, RB1, ESR1, and PRKCA. Top genes with negative curvature change (potentially protective) include CREBBP, SHC1, PTK2, AR, MYC, and JUN. MYC appearing in the negative (low-risk-associated) direction contrasts with its role as an oncogene, possibly reflecting specific network topology effects. 171 unique candidate genes across all curvature comparisons are reported.

### F3: Network curvature is not predictive of OS in non-ICI-treated HGSOC

**Finding:** When the same curvature pipeline is applied to HGSOC patients who did not receive ICIs, total curvature does not significantly stratify OS. This suggests the prognostic signal captured by curvature may be specific to the biological context of immunotherapy response, not a general survival biomarker.

**Detail:** The OS definition differs between the ICI cohort (from ICI start date) and the non-ICI control cohort (from time of diagnosis), which introduces a methodological caveat in direct comparison. The authors note that progression-free survival (PFS) is not significantly stratified by curvature in the ICI cohort, consistent with published data that ICIs can impact OS without clear PFS benefit. Curvature may capture network features relevant to immune recognition or immune evasion rather than inherent tumor aggressiveness per se.

### F4: FGA correlates with curvature (r significant, p=0.006) but provides less prognostic discrimination

**Finding:** Linear regression of total curvature on genomic parameters shows significant correlation with FGA (p=0.006) but not with TMB (p=0.97) or LST (p=0.09). Despite this correlation, FGA does not significantly stratify OS (p=0.196 vs. p=0.00047 for curvature), demonstrating that the PPI network topology adds prognostic information beyond simple genome alteration burden.

**Detail:** The correlation between curvature and FGA is expected given that FGA quantifies the genome-wide fraction of CNA-altered regions and curvature is a CNA-weighted network measure. The additional discriminatory power of curvature over FGA arises from the network connectivity dimension: curvature captures the functional interconnectedness of altered genes, while FGA treats all altered genome regions equivalently. This is the key conceptual contribution of the method.

### F5: HGSOC biology driven by TP53 loss → aneuploidy → CNA-driven amplification of KRAS, MYC, AKT1, CCNE1

**Finding:** The methodological rationale is grounded in HGSOC biology: near-universal TP53 loss leads to centrosome amplification, aneuploidy, and chromothripsis, producing recurrent amplification of cell cycle drivers KRAS, MYC, AKT1, and CCNE1. Since HGSOC has low TMB and is not driven by point mutations, CNA-based network analysis is particularly appropriate for this tumor type.

**Detail:** CCNE1 amplification leads to overexpression of cyclin E, which downregulates p21 (CDKN1A) and promotes centrosome duplication, creating positive feedback for further aneuploidy. AKT1 promotes TORC2 activity and MDM2-mediated p53 degradation. KRAS signals growth factor-driven cell cycle progression; MYC regulates hundreds of downstream targets. The CDKN1A gene is not mutated in HGSOC despite its role as a p53 target, suggesting functional preservation of p21 in non-p53 contexts. This biology motivates use of CNA values as node weights rather than mutation status in the curvature framework.

## Open Questions

- Can the Ollivier–Ricci curvature approach be prospectively validated in a larger, independent HGSOC ICI cohort and extended to other tumor types?
- Does the curvature signal predict which specific ICI regimens (anti-PD1, anti-CTLA4, combinations) a patient is most likely to benefit from?
- What is the mechanistic basis for the ICI-specificity of the curvature signal — does it reflect tumor immunogenicity driven by CNA-induced neoantigen load, or something more structural about network architecture?
- Can the method be applied using less expensive genomic data (e.g., low-pass WGS for CNA profiling) to make curvature-based stratification clinically practical?
- Why does MYC appear in the negative curvature change group (associated with lower risk) despite being a known oncogene in HGSOC?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=45 ICI-treated HGSOC); underpowered for multi-variable analysis and subgroup stratification
- Retrospective study with OS defined from ICI start date; patients had variable prior treatment histories and intervals from diagnosis to ICI
- Curvature computation is mathematically complex and not yet implemented in standard clinical bioinformatics pipelines; reproducibility requires expert implementation
- The 25th percentile threshold for risk stratification is data-driven and may not generalize to independent cohorts
- PPI network from HPRD represents average protein interactions, not tumor- or patient-specific interaction dynamics
- No correction for tumor purity or clonal heterogeneity in CNA-based node weights
