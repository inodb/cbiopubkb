## Summary

Landmark 2012 Nature study characterizing somatic mutation landscapes and clonal evolution in 104 primary TNBC cases using SNP6.0 copy number arrays (n=104), whole genome/exome sequencing (n=65), RNA-seq (n=80), and ultra-deep re-sequencing (median ~20,000×) of 2,414 validated SNVs. The study demonstrates that primary TNBCs display a wide, continuous spectrum of mutational and clonal complexity at diagnosis — from tumors with few mutations in one or two pathways, to tumors with hundreds of events across many pathways. Using a Dirichlet process clonal frequency model (PyClone), it reveals for the first time in an epithelial cancer the full spectrum of clonal genotype abundances, showing that basal-subtype TNBC exhibits greater clonal diversity than non-basal TNBC. TP53 and PIK3CA/PTEN are the dominant clonally-selected alterations, but even these canonical drivers can occupy non-founder clonal positions in some tumors.

## Key Findings

### F1: Primary TNBCs display a wide, continuous spectrum of somatic mutation burden at diagnosis — from few to hundreds of events

**Finding:** The distribution of somatic mutation counts across 65 TNBC cases is wide and continuous, ranging from tumors with very few somatic aberrations to those with hundreds. This variation is unrelated to the proportion of genome altered by CNAs (Pearson correlation near zero) or to tumor cellularity, indicating that the total mutation content reflects genuine biological differences in mutational processes or exposure — not technical artifact.

**Detail:** This key observation challenges the view of cancer as a uniformly genetically complex disease. Some primary TNBCs diagnosed with curative intent already harbor extensive clonal evolution (multiple pathways affected, high mutation burden), whereas others present with minimal genomic complexity. The clinical implication is that inter-patient heterogeneity in mutation burden at diagnosis may underlie the variable responses of TNBC to cytotoxic therapy. Approximately 20% of cases contained potentially clinically actionable somatic aberrations, including BRAF-V600E, high-level EGFR amplifications, and ERBB2/ERBB3 mutations.

### F2: TP53 is the dominant driver — mutated in 62% of basal and 43% of non-basal TNBC; PIK3CA/PTEN occupy clonally dominant but not always founder positions

**Finding:** TP53 is the most frequently mutated gene (overall ~54% of cases; 62% of basal, 43% of non-basal TNBC). PIK3CA mutations occur in 10.2% (7/65) and PTEN and RB1 in 7.7% (5/65) each. USH2A (9.2%), MYO3A (9.2%), and SYNE1/2 (9.2%) are also frequently mutated. Genes with evidence of single-gene selection (q<0.1): TP53, PIK3CA, RB1, PTEN, MYO3A, GH1.

**Detail:** While TP53 and PIK3CA/PTEN alterations tend to occur at the highest clonal frequencies (consistent with early clonal selection), ultra-deep re-sequencing reveals that in some tumors (e.g., SA219, SA236), p53 resides in lower clonal frequency groups, suggesting it was not the founding event. This challenges the universal founding status of TP53 in TNBC. PIK3CA mutations similarly show variable clonal positions. In contrast, mutations in cytoskeletal/motility genes (MYO3A, USH2A, integrins, laminins, myosins) occur at lower clonal frequencies, consistent with later acquisition during tumor progression.

### F3: Novel PARK2 intragenic deletions link a Parkinson's disease gene to TNBC as a tumor suppressor

**Finding:** Intragenic deletions in PARK2 (encoding parkin, an E3 ubiquitin ligase) are detected in 6% of TNBC cases, specifically linking PARK2 to TNBC as a tumor suppressor for the first time. Additional CNAs include RB1 deletion (5%), PTEN deletion (3%), and EGFR amplification (5%).

**Detail:** PARK2 was previously identified as a germline gene for early-onset Parkinson's disease but had not been established as a somatic tumor suppressor in breast cancer. The intragenic deletion pattern observed here is consistent with biallelic inactivation. This finding expands the repertoire of TNBC tumor suppressors beyond the well-characterized TP53, RB1, PTEN axis. Approximately 31.9% of somatic mutations in non-coding regulatory regions occurred within retinoblastoma (Rb) binding sites (vs. expected 2.5%; p=2×10^−19), further implicating Rb-regulated cell cycle network disruption in TNBC pathogenesis.

### F4: Only ~36% of somatic SNVs are expressed in RNA-seq — clonal abundance of mutations tracks with transcriptional representation

**Finding:** Integration of genome/exome sequencing with RNA-seq reveals that only ~36% of validated somatic SNVs are observed in transcriptome data. The proportion of low-abundance somatic SNVs observed in RNA is consistent with the distribution of wildtype, heterozygous, and homozygous expressed mutations, supporting the notion that low-abundance alleles represent rarer clones. 43 splice junction mutations with evidence of impact on splicing were identified, encompassing TP53, PIK3R1, and multiple novel genes.

**Detail:** This finding has important implications for cancer biology and drug target prioritization: the majority of somatic mutations are not transcriptionally active, potentially because they reside in minor subclones, are in genomic regions with low expression, or are subject to nonsense-mediated decay. The 36% expression rate closely mirrors findings in lymphoma (~42%), suggesting this is a general property of primary cancers. For cBioPortal purposes, this underscores that mutation data alone may overestimate the fraction of functionally active alterations in a tumor, and that RNA-seq integration is needed for complete functional interpretation.

### F5: Basal TNBC exhibits more clonal diversity than non-basal TNBC — clonal genotype spectrum characterizes the population at diagnosis

**Finding:** Using PyClone (Dirichlet process mixture model) applied to ultra-deep re-sequenced SNVs with local copy number and LOH context, the study demonstrates that primary TNBCs display a wide spectrum of clonal modes (distinct clonal genotype frequencies) at diagnosis. Basal-subtype TNBC (PAM50 classified) harbor significantly more clonal frequency modes than non-basal TNBC, indicating more extensive early clonal evolution. Some tumors show only 1–2 clonal frequency modes (minimal clonal evolution), while others exhibit multiple modes (extensive polyclonality).

**Detail:** The number of clonal frequency modes tends to increase with total mutation count but the relationship is not strictly linear, indicating that clonal diversification is partially independent of overall mutation rate. Mutations in cytoskeletal/motility pathways (actin, integrins, myosins) cluster in lower clonal frequency groups, consistent with subclonal acquisition during tumor progression — potentially driven by selection for invasion and motility. This landmark application of clonal frequency modeling to an epithelial cancer set the methodological precedent for subsequent PyClone and PHYLOGIC applications in cancer genomics.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PIK3CA`, `MYO3A`, `USH2A`, `SYNE1`, `SYNE2` · Triple-negative breast cancer (basal vs. non-basal)

## Open Questions

- Do tumors with fewer clonal frequency modes at diagnosis (minimal clonal evolution) have better responses to neoadjuvant chemotherapy than polyclonal tumors?
- Can PARK2 intragenic deletions serve as a biomarker for TNBC subtypes with distinct therapeutic vulnerabilities (e.g., proteasome pathway)?
- What proportion of TNBC cases with low mutation burden and few driver pathways are driven by non-coding or structural regulatory alterations?
- Does the observation that p53 is sometimes not a founding event challenge p53-centric models of TNBC initiation, and what alternative founder mutations drive these cases?
- Can the clonal frequency approach (PyClone) applied at diagnosis predict which patients will relapse from residual subclonal populations after cytotoxic therapy?
- Do cytoskeletal/motility gene mutations (MYO3A, USH2A) present at low clonal frequency at diagnosis increase in frequency at metastasis, confirming their role in cancer progression?

## Limitations

- Small primary discovery cohort (n=65 for WGS/WES), limiting statistical power for rare gene discovery
- Ultra-deep re-sequencing for clonal analysis restricted to 2,414 pre-validated SNVs; comprehensive genome-wide clonal modeling not feasible with this approach
- PyClone clonal frequency estimation assumes diploid neutral copy number for ~68% of SNVs; regions of complex CNA may have inaccurate clonal assignments
- RNA-seq data available for only 80/104 cases; expression integration incomplete
- No matched longitudinal (pre/post-treatment) specimens — clonal evolution trajectory inferred from cross-sectional primary tumor data only
- PAM50 classification from RNA-seq limits basal/non-basal analysis to the 80 RNA-seq cases
- Validation cohort (n=159) was sequenced for only 29 selected genes — not whole-exome, limiting independent discovery
- This is a 2012 pre-TCGA-era dataset; subsequent TCGA BRCA and METABRIC studies with larger cohorts have superseded some frequency estimates
