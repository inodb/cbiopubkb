## Summary

First whole-exome sequencing study of pancreatic carcinomas with acinar differentiation, a histologically and clinically distinct group from ductal adenocarcinoma that includes acinar cell carcinoma, pancreatoblastoma, and mixed acinar-ductal/neuroendocrine carcinomas. Analysis of 23 microdissected tumors (17 pure acinar cell carcinomas, 3 mixed acinar-ductal, 1 mixed acinar-neuroendocrine, 2 pancreatoblastomas) reveals a genomic landscape that is fundamentally different from pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma: no KRAS mutations, high chromosomal instability (mean FAL 0.27 vs. 0.15 for PDAC), and no single gene mutated in >30% of cases. Key discoveries include frequent JAK1 mutations (17%), recurrent BRAF hotspot mutations (13%), and therapeutically actionable alterations in >43% of cases spanning DNA repair pathway genes (BRCA2, PALB2, ATM, BAP1) and kinase targets (BRAF, JAK1). The study completes the first systematic exome survey of all major pancreatic neoplasm types and reveals each has a unique mutational profile.

## Key Findings

### F1: Acinar cell carcinomas are KRAS wild-type — confirming genomic distinction from pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma

**Finding:** None of the 23 acinar cell carcinomas and related neoplasms harbored KRAS mutations, in direct contrast to pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma where KRAS is mutated in >90% of cases. This confirms and extends prior limited gene-specific studies suggesting KRAS is not a driver in acinar differentiation and establishes acinar carcinomas as a molecularly distinct category at the exome level.

**Detail:** The absence of KRAS mutations in all 23 cases, combined with the near-universal KRAS dependency of PDAC, demonstrates that acinar carcinomas use entirely different oncogenic pathways. Instead, kinase pathway activation is achieved through BRAF hotspot mutations (13%) and JAK1 mutations (17%). The rare occurrence of GNAS mutations (2/23, both at hotspot codon 201) and RNF43 (1/23) suggests these IPMN-associated genes can occasionally be co-altered in mixed variants. The distinct genomic landscape has important diagnostic implications: KRAS wild-type status in a pancreatic tumor should prompt consideration of acinar or neuroendocrine lineage.

### F2: JAK1 mutations in 17% of acinar carcinomas — the most frequent novel kinase mutation, with therapeutic implications for JAK inhibitors

**Finding:** JAK1 is mutated in 4/23 (17%) of acinar carcinomas, making it one of the most frequently altered genes identified in this study and the first report of recurrent JAK1 mutations in pancreatic cancer. The types of mutations observed in JAK1 were consistent with potential functional relevance, occurring in a gene already the target of approved inhibitors (ruxolitinib for myelofibrosis).

**Detail:** JAK1 encodes Janus Kinase 1, a non-receptor tyrosine kinase critical for cytokine signaling via the JAK-STAT pathway. While JAK1 mutations were not all at canonical activating hotspots, the recurrence across 17% of cases far exceeds background expectations. JAK inhibitors (ruxolitinib, FDA-approved for myelofibrosis) are in clinical trials for solid tumors. The combined rate of potentially targetable alterations in this cohort — BRCA2 (4%), PALB2 (4%), ATM (4%), BAP1 (4%), BRAF V600E (13%), and JAK1 (17%) — reaches >43%, making this a therapeutically rich tumor type despite being rare.

### F3: High chromosomal instability with mean FAL of 0.27 — significantly greater than PDAC — and frequent 11p LOH in 52% of tumors

**Finding:** The fractional allelic loss (FAL) in acinar carcinomas ranged from 0–0.89 with a mean of 0.27, substantially higher than the mean FAL of 0.15 (range 0.015–0.32) previously reported for PDAC. Loss of heterozygosity at chromosome 11p (containing MEN1, WT1, and other loci) was present in 12/23 (52%) tumors including both pancreatoblastomas, and LOH at 17p (TP53) and 18q (SMAD4) was common (39% and 57%, respectively). FISH studies confirmed intratumoral chromosomal heterogeneity in 3 of 7 assessed tumors.

**Detail:** The high degree of chromosomal instability may explain the aggressive clinical behavior of acinar carcinomas and their resistance to standard chemotherapy regimens. FISH analysis of chromosomes 11, 15, and 22 revealed not only loss but also polysomy and striking intratumoral heterogeneity — one tumor (ACINAR06) showed gain/loss heterogeneity across five of six chromosomal regions assessed. This level of heterogeneity is consistent with ongoing chromosomal missegregation and challenges the efficacy of targeted therapies that require clonal alterations. The mechanism(s) underlying chromosomal instability remain unclear; spindle checkpoint and DNA repair gene mutations were not identified in these exomes.

### F4: Pancreatoblastomas are genomically distinct from acinar cell carcinomas — universal CTNNB1 mutations and very low mutation burden

**Finding:** The two pancreatoblastomas in the cohort (ACINAR17 and ACINAR19, both adult-onset) had remarkably few somatic mutations (17 and 18, versus an average of 131 for acinar cell carcinomas excluding outliers). Both pancreatoblastomas harbored CTNNB1 mutations — the same Wnt pathway gene universally mutated in solid pseudopapillary neoplasms — while no other acinar carcinoma in the study had a CTNNB1 mutation. This reinforces the molecular relationship between pancreatoblastoma and SPN and its genomic distinction from acinar cell carcinoma proper.

**Detail:** CTNNB1 mutations in pancreatoblastoma were previously described, and the LOH of chromosome 11p (present in both pancreatoblastomas) is a known hallmark. The low mutation burden in pancreatoblastomas is consistent with their unique biology as predominantly pediatric/young adult tumors that may arise through a different evolutionary trajectory. The mixed acinar-ductal carcinomas showed yet another distinct profile: 2/3 had CDKN2A homozygous deletion and 2/3 had BRAF mutations, but lacked TP53, SMAD4, RNF43, and GNAS alterations characteristic of classical PDAC.

### F5: Over one-third of acinar carcinomas carry potentially targetable alterations including BRCA2, PALB2, ATM, BAP1, BRAF, and JAK1

**Finding:** When all potentially actionable alterations are aggregated, >43% of acinar carcinomas carry at least one therapeutically relevant genomic event: BRCA2 (4%), PALB2 (4%), ATM (4%), BAP1 (4%), BRAF V600E (13%), and JAK1 (17%). This rate compares favorably with other solid tumors and provides a rationale for comprehensive molecular profiling of all acinar carcinomas at diagnosis.

**Detail:** BRCA2, PALB2, and ATM mutations implicate Fanconi anemia/homologous recombination pathway deficiency, conferring sensitivity to DNA crosslinking agents (mitomycin C) and PARP inhibitors. ATM-mutant tumors may additionally respond to DNA-PKcs inhibitors. BAP1 mutations (BRCA1-associated protein 1, a ubiquitin carboxy-terminal hydrolase) are emerging as actionable in other tumor types. BRAF V600E is targetable by FDA-approved vemurafenib as demonstrated in the companion UTSW PDA study (PMID25855536). None of the patients with somatic alterations in BRCA2, PALB2, or ATM had known constitutional disease-causing mutations, suggesting these are somatic events rather than germline susceptibility.

## Open Questions

- Do JAK1-mutant acinar carcinomas respond to JAK inhibitors (e.g., ruxolitinib), alone or in combination with chemotherapy?
- What is the sensitivity of BRCA2/PALB2/ATM-mutant acinar carcinomas to PARP inhibitors or platinum-based regimens in prospective trials?
- Do BRAF V600E-mutant acinar carcinomas (distinct from PDAC) show similar sensitivity to vemurafenib as the patient-derived PDAC model established in the companion UTSW study?
- What drives the extreme chromosomal instability in acinar carcinomas if spindle checkpoint and canonical DSB repair genes are not frequently mutated?
- Is the molecular overlap between pancreatoblastoma (CTNNB1, 11p LOH) and solid pseudopapillary neoplasm (CTNNB1 universal) evidence for shared cell of origin or convergent evolution?
- Can the distinct genomic landscape of acinar carcinoma (KRAS-wild type, JAK1/BRAF mutations) be used to improve pre-operative diagnosis from fine-needle aspiration specimens?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=23) with limited statistical power for precise mutation frequency estimation and rare event detection
- Histological subtypes are combined in analysis; pure acinar cell carcinomas vs. mixed subtypes may have distinct genomic profiles, but numbers are insufficient for stratified analysis
- High chromosomal instability with intratumoral heterogeneity may mean that exome sequencing of a single sample captures only a fraction of the genomic alterations present
- Two MSI-high outliers (701 and 404 mutations respectively) and one MSI-low outlier were excluded from per-tumor mutation count analysis, potentially biasing conclusions about mutation burden
- FISH validation performed on only 7 of 23 tumors; chromosomal instability may be even more heterogeneous than appreciated
- Clinical outcome data not systematically analyzed in this study — survival associations with individual gene mutations could not be assessed
- No transcriptomic data to validate expression consequences of the identified mutations or identify fusion transcripts
