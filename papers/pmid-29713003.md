## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing and RNA-seq profiling of 187 Korean breast cancers (88% premenopausal, mean age 39.3 years) from Samsung Medical Center (SMC) are systematically compared to the TCGA cohort (mean age 58.3 years, 72% postmenopausal). SMC harbours more HER2+ and Luminal B tumours and fewer Luminal A tumours than TCGA. TP53 and ERBB2 alterations are enriched in SMC. BRCA1/2 germline pathogenic mutations are significantly more prevalent in SMC (10.8% vs. 4.7%). HRD signature S3 is enriched in SMC TNBCs (85% vs. 52%). Virtual microdissection reveals that SMC premenopausal tumours have higher TIL factor and lower TGF-β signalling than TCGA postmenopausal tumours — differences residing predominantly in the tumour microenvironment. Data are available in cBioPortal as `brca_smc_2018`.

## Key Findings

### F1: Young Asian breast cancers are enriched for HER2+ and Luminal B subtypes with lower Luminal A prevalence compared to TCGA

**Finding:** SMC has significantly higher proportions of ER+/HER2+ subtype (16.1% vs. 5.4%, p=1.5×10⁻⁵) and lower proportion of ER+ (53.6% vs. 72.8%, p=3.3×10⁻⁶) than TCGA. The majority of SMC Luminal cases are Luminal B (58%) while TCGA is Luminal A-predominant (57%). IntClust5 (HER2-amplified, intermediate genomic instability) is enriched in SMC (p=0.02).

**Detail:** Three independent classification methods (IHC, PAM50, gene-expression NMC) are highly concordant (88–92%), validating the subtype distribution differences. The excess of Luminal B over Luminal A in SMC is not explained by histology (ductal carcinoma predominates in both cohorts) or tumour stage differences alone; multivariate analysis controlling for stage and histology confirms the shift. This suggests that younger Asian breast cancers have a fundamentally different biology, not merely a younger age-at-diagnosis effect.

### F2: BRCA1/2 germline pathogenic mutations are significantly enriched in younger Korean breast cancers (10.8% vs. 4.7% in TCGA)

**Finding:** Germline pathogenic mutations in BRCA1 or BRCA2 affect 10.8% of SMC vs. 4.7% of TCGA (logistic regression p=0.0027). Within SMC, BRCA1/2 mutations are enriched in younger patients (13.7% in YBC age ≤40 vs. 4.8% in IBC age >40, p=0.08). Overall, 18.8% of SMC harbour germline pathogenic mutations in at least one of 13 breast cancer predisposition genes.

**Detail:** Pathogenic mutations were defined as frameshift/nonsense variants or ClinVar disease-causing variants across 13 high-to-moderate penetrance breast cancer susceptibility genes. BRCA1 and BRCA2 are the dominant contributors. SMC patients with family histories of breast cancer are significantly enriched in younger patients (24% vs. 8%, p=0.02). Within TCGA, YBC (age ≤40) also had higher BRCA1/2 prevalence (12.0% vs. 3.4% in OBC), suggesting age-related enrichment is a universal rather than ethnicity-specific phenomenon — though baseline prevalence is higher in Korean patients.

### F3: TP53 somatic mutation prevalence is markedly higher in SMC (47.9% vs. 32.0% in TCGA), driven by subtype enrichment

**Finding:** TP53 somatic mutations affect 47.9% of SMC vs. 32.0% of TCGA (logistic regression p=5.0×10⁻⁵). TP53 mutation frequency varies dramatically by subtype in SMC: TNBC 87.5%, HER2+ 75%, ER+/HER2+ 64.3%, ER+ 23.5%. ERBB2 amplification is also enriched in SMC (20% vs. 9.1%). Six significantly mutated genes are identified by MutSigCV: TP53, PIK3CA, GATA3, CBFB, PTEN, CDH1.

**Detail:** The TP53 enrichment in SMC is explained in part by the higher proportions of HER2+ and TNBC subtypes. Co-occurrence of TP53 mutation with ERBB2 amplification in the ER+/HER2+ and HER2+ subtypes is consistent with APOBEC-driven mutagenesis. GATA3 mutations (12.4% in SMC vs. 9.1% in TCGA) are enriched in hormone-positive subtypes, while CDH1 mutations are more frequent in TCGA (10.1% vs. 2.4%), reflecting lower lobular carcinoma prevalence in SMC. Somatic mutation burden is higher in TCGA than SMC (1.4 vs. 0.90 mutations/Mb; p=0.01), consistent with age-related mutation accumulation.

### F4: HRD signature S3 is dramatically enriched in SMC TNBCs (85%) compared to TCGA TNBCs (52%), linked to younger age and BRCA status

**Finding:** The homologous recombination deficiency (HRD) mutational signature S3 is detected in 85% of SMC TNBCs (S3 score >0.2) vs. 52% of TCGA TNBCs (Fisher's exact test p=7.0×10⁻⁴). S3 score significantly correlates with younger patient age across both cohorts (Pearson r=−0.18, p=1.0×10⁻⁹). APOBEC signatures S2 and S13 are enriched in HER2+ and ER+/HER2+ tumours and in ERBB2-amplified tumours.

**Detail:** S3 enrichment in SMC TNBC is partly explained by younger age and higher BRCA1/2 germline mutation prevalence. Mutation burden in TNBC is most strongly correlated with S3, while S2/S13 drive mutation burden in non-TNBC. The high HRD rate in SMC TNBC has therapeutic implications: platinum salts and PARP inhibitors are particularly effective in HRD-positive tumours. S3 enrichment in SMC BRCA1/2 missense variant carriers (vs. wild-type) is observed in SMC but not TCGA, suggesting these variants have functional HRD impact in Korean patients.

### F5: Younger Asian premenopausal breast cancers have higher TIL infiltration and lower TGF-β signalling — differences dominated by tumour microenvironment

**Finding:** Virtual NMF microdissection of 1,678-sample RNA-seq compendium identifies a TIL factor (F9) that is significantly enriched in SMC premenopausal vs. TCGA postmenopausal tumours (p=1.55×10⁻⁶). TGF-β pathway expression signature and TGFB1 expression are higher in TCGA postmenopausal tumours. Of 827 differentially expressed genes (SMC-Pre vs. TCGA-Post), 51.6% are TME-attributed; only 13.2% are tumour-intrinsic.

**Detail:** F9-associated genes include CD8A and PD-L1 (CD274), and F9 correlates with cytotoxic T-cell and NK-cell signatures (CYT score). T-cell markers, interferon-α, IL-2 signalling, allograft rejection, and cytokine pathways are upregulated in SMC premenopausal tumours; stroma-associated TGF-β and 96% of tumour stroma pathways are upregulated in TCGA postmenopausal tumours. Korean BC status is independently associated with increased TIL and decreased TGF-β signalling in multivariate analysis controlling for molecular subtype and tumour purity. This TME difference may partly explain differential immunotherapy responsiveness and suggests that younger Asian patients may be prime candidates for immune-checkpoint blockade.

**Genes / cancer types:** `CD8A`, `CD274`, `TGFB1`, `TGFBR1`, `ESR1` · Korean/Asian breast cancer (all subtypes)

## Open Questions

1. Do the higher TIL levels in SMC premenopausal Asian breast cancers translate into improved responses to immune-checkpoint blockade compared to postmenopausal Western patients?
2. Can the 85% HRD rate in Korean TNBC be leveraged for PARP inhibitor trials even in BRCA1/2 germline wild-type patients, based on functional HRD signature detection?
3. What genetic factors (beyond BRCA1/2) explain the higher germline predisposition burden in Korean young BC patients, and do population-specific variants play a role?
4. Are the subtype distribution differences (more HER2+/Luminal B in SMC) primarily driven by age, ethnicity, or gene-environment interactions?
5. Do Asian-specific therapeutic guidelines need to be developed, particularly for TNBC (PARP inhibitors, platinums) and HER2+ disease?

## Limitations

- SMC is a single Korean institution enriched for premenopausal patients; may not represent the broader Asian BC population (e.g., Chinese, Japanese cohorts may differ).
- Comparisons with TCGA are potentially confounded by differences in age, menopausal status, histological composition (lower lobular in SMC), and sequencing protocols.
- WES rather than WGS limits detection of non-coding drivers, structural variants, and accurate mutational signature analysis in some tumours.
- Virtual microdissection (NMF) is a computational deconvolution; TIL enrichment was not validated with spatial or flow cytometry approaches.
- Germline mutation calling from WES without dedicated germline panels may underestimate prevalence of variants in non-coding and deep intronic regions.
