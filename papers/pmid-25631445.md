## Summary

TCGA profiled 279 head and neck squamous cell carcinomas (HNSCCs) across oral cavity, oropharynx, and larynx using whole-exome sequencing, copy number arrays, RNA-seq, methylation, miRNA, and reverse-phase protein arrays. The study stratified tumors by HPV status (36 HPV+, 243 HPV−) and identified 11 significantly mutated genes, four gene expression subtypes (atypical, mesenchymal, basal, classical), and candidate therapeutic targets in the majority of cases. HPV+ tumors are dominated by PIK3CA helical-domain mutations, TRAF3 loss, and E2F1 amplification; HPV− tumors show near-universal TP53 mutation, CDKN2A inactivation, and 3q26/28 amplification. Novel alterations include NSD1 inactivation (chromatin), AJUBA and FAT1 (Wnt/β-catenin), and CASP8+HRAS co-mutations in a favorable oral-cavity subset.

## Key Findings

### F1: HPV status defines two genomically distinct HNSCC subtypes with different driver landscapes

**Finding:** HPV+ HNSCCs are driven by PIK3CA helical-domain mutations, TRAF3 loss, and E2F1 amplification; HPV− tumors have near-universal TP53 mutation (86%), CDKN2A inactivation, and recurrent CNA of 3q26/28, 11q13/22.

**Detail:** HPV+ tumors retain an intact 9p21.3 (CDKN2A locus), whereas HPV− tumors show frequent homozygous deletion there. HPV+ tumors showed a higher rate of TpC mutations (APOBEC-like), while HPV− showed CpG transversions consistent with tobacco. TRAF3 loss (deletions 14%, truncating mutations 8%) was found exclusively in HPV+ tumors and is the first evidence linking TRAF3 to HPV-associated carcinomas. HPV+ and HPV− TP53 wild-type patients had favorable outcomes compared to TP53-mutant or 11q13/CCND1-amplified tumors.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PIK3CA`, `CDKN2A`, `TRAF3`, `E2F1`, `CCND1` · HNSCC (all anatomic sites)

### F2: Novel NSD1 inactivation as a recurrent chromatin-modifier alteration in HPV− HNSCC

**Finding:** NSD1 (H3K36 methyltransferase) is inactivated by truncating mutation (n=29) or focal deletion (n=4) in 33 HNSCCs (~12%), preferentially in HPV− classical-subtype tumors enriched in laryngeal cancers. NSD1 loss is associated with genome-wide hypomethylation.

**Detail:** NSD1 is similar to SETD2 (frequently mutated in ccRCC) and functions as a tumor suppressor in HNSCC. Its inactivation was correlated with the classical gene-expression subtype alongside TP53 mutation, CDKN2A loss, 3q amplification, and NFE2L2 pathway activation — a tobacco-related signature also seen in LUSC. Germline NSD1 carriers develop Sotos syndrome with predisposition to squamous cancers.

**Genes / cancer types:** `NSD1`, `SETD2`, `NFE2L2`, `KEAP1`, `CUL3` · HNSCC (larynx enriched)

### F3: HRAS+CASP8 co-mutation defines an oral-cavity subset with favorable outcome and low CNA burden

**Finding:** A distinct oral-cavity subset (~15%) is characterized by activating HRAS mutations co-occurring with inactivating CASP8 mutations and wild-type TP53, minimal copy number alterations ('M-class'), and significantly better clinical outcomes.

**Detail:** These tumors cluster separately by CNA analysis as a low-CNA subgroup, fitting the recently described 'M-class' cancer pattern (mutation-driven rather than CNA-driven). The HRAS–CASP8 pattern implicates disrupted RAS signaling and altered cell death/NF-κB as alternative oncogenesis routes. Co-amplification of 11q13 (CCND1, FADD, CTTN) and 11q22 (BIRC2, YAP1) was anti-correlated with CASP8 mutations, suggesting convergent targeting of the apoptosis/NF-κB axis.

**Genes / cancer types:** `HRAS`, `CASP8`, `BIRC2`, `FADD`, `YAP1`, `CCND1` · HNSCC (oral cavity)

### F4: Four gene expression subtypes with distinct genomic drivers and survival outcomes

**Finding:** Four previously validated HNSCC expression subtypes (atypical 24%, mesenchymal 27%, basal 31%, classical 18%) each show distinct enrichment for somatic alterations. Classical subtype resembles LUSC; basal subtype has NOTCH1 inactivation and HRAS/CASP8 co-mutations.

**Detail:** Classical subtype: TP53 mutation, CDKN2A loss, 3q amplification, NFE2L2/KEAP1/CUL3 pathway mutations, heavy smoking, larynx enriched. Atypical subtype: enriched for HPV+ tumors with PIK3CA helical-domain mutations (exon 9), no chromosome 7 amplification. Basal subtype: NOTCH1 inactivation, intact oxidative stress signaling, SOX2 low expression, HRAS–CASP8 co-mutations and 11q13/q22 co-amplification. Mesenchymal subtype: high innate immunity gene expression (CD56), low HLA class I mutation rate.

**Genes / cancer types:** `NOTCH1`, `SOX2`, `TP63`, `NFE2L2`, `PIK3CA` · HNSCC (all sites)

### F5: Actionable alterations in RTK/RAS/PI3K pathways found in majority of HNSCCs

**Finding:** EGFR/ERBB2 or FGFR1/3 alterations (mutation, amplification, or fusion) are the dominant receptor-level targets; PIK3CA mutation or amplification (~45% combined) and HRAS mutations are the main downstream targets. Therapeutic candidate alterations identified in most HNSCCs.

**Detail:** EGFR amplification (chromosome 7) clustered in HPV− tumors; FGFR3-TACC3 fusions found in 2 HPV+ tumors. PIK3CA is the only significantly mutated oncogene (q<0.01): 73% of mutations are canonical hotspots (E542K, E545K, H1047R/L); ~20% of tumors show focal amplification without mutation. Cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKN2A, CCND1, CDK6, RB1) and cell death/NF-κB genes also represent candidate targets. Integrative analysis identified co-alterations of TRAF3, HLA, PIK3CA, FADD, BIRC2, and CASP8 as convergent on NF-κB.

## Open Questions

- What is the clinical impact of HPV integration site variability (no recurrent integration locus found) on tumor biology?
- Do NSD1-inactivated tumors respond differently to epigenetic therapies (e.g., EZH2 inhibitors)?
- Can the HRAS+CASP8 subset be validated as a distinct entity with favorable prognosis in prospective cohorts?
- How do HPV− TP53 wild-type tumors (favorable outcome) differ mechanistically from TP53-mutant tumors?
- What is the functional role of AJUBA loss-of-function mutations in Wnt/β-catenin and Hippo pathway dysregulation?

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional primary tumor analysis; limited longitudinal or metastatic data.
- WGS performed in only 29 cases; structural rearrangement landscape incomplete in the full cohort.
- Subtype and HPV assignments rely on RNA-seq definitions; prospective HPV testing modalities differ clinically.
- Cohort is predominantly from oral cavity (62%); oropharyngeal tumors underrepresented relative to population incidence at time of study.
- Outcome data limited (median follow-up not specified); no treatment-stratified survival analysis.
