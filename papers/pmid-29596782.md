## Summary

The Multi-Center Mutation Calling in Multiple Cancers (MC3) project describes the design, execution, and results of a harmonized somatic variant calling pipeline applied to 10,510 tumor-normal exome pairs across 33 TCGA cancer types, producing the reference somatic mutation dataset for the TCGA PanCan Atlas. Seven mutation-calling algorithms (MuTect, MuSE, VarScan, Pindel, RADIA, SomaticSniper, Indelocator) are deployed uniformly using Docker containerization and Common Workflow Language (CWL) on cloud computing platforms. The resulting ensemble call set of ~3.5 million high-confidence somatic variants (open-access MAF) forms the foundation for all PanCan Atlas pan-cancer analyses, including driver gene discovery, mutational signatures, and tumor heterogeneity. The paper establishes best practices for large-scale reproducible cancer genomics: consensus calling outperforms single-algorithm methods, and flexible filter annotations support diverse downstream analytical objectives. All methods and data are publicly available via the NCI Genomic Data Commons (GDC).

## Key Findings

### F1: Ensemble consensus calling with 7 algorithms outperforms single-method variant calling in both sensitivity and specificity

**Finding:** Meta-calling using the intersection of two or more variant callers captures more true positive sites than any single caller while dramatically reducing false positives. For SNP calling, MuTect and MuSE individually detect the highest numbers of true positive sites among the validation set and have the highest pair-wise agreement. For indels, Pindel makes the most calls but with a high false-positive burden; two-caller intersection is less effective for indels due to increased false negatives.

**Detail:** The "two-caller rule" (requiring at least two of five SNP callers to call a variant) finds more valid sites than any specific pair of callers, demonstrating the "wisdom of crowds" principle in ensemble variant calling. Most variants that pass all filters are supported by all 5 callers. SomaticSniper had the lowest number of detected sites (highest specificity) but also missed the most validated somatic events. These results quantify the trade-off between sensitivity and specificity for individual callers and provide a principled basis for selecting filter stringency based on the downstream analytical objective (driver discovery vs. heterogeneity analysis).

### F2: Cloud computing, Docker containerization, and CWL enable reproducible deployment of genomic pipelines at scale

**Finding:** The MC3 project demonstrates that modern workflow technologies — Docker software containers plus Common Workflow Language (CWL) pipeline descriptions — enable reproducible, portable deployment of variant calling across multiple institutional compute environments (Broad Firehose, DNANexus cloud, UCSC cluster, ISB Cancer Genomics Cloud). All MC3 methods and containers are publicly available on GitHub and Docker/Quay repositories.

**Detail:** The previous paradigm of "results-oriented" genomics (groups running private pipelines on local data and sharing results) is replaced by a "methods-oriented" approach where algorithms are packaged and shared to be run on data wherever it resides. Specifically, Alignment + GATK processing + MuTect/Indelocator ran on Broad Firehose; Pindel, MuSE, RADIA, VarScan, SomaticSniper ran on DNANexus (1.8M core-hours); OxoG scores on ISB CGC. The combination of per-tool Docker containers with a CWL pipeline orchestrating them enables any institution to replicate the MC3 analysis or apply identical methods to new datasets — a prerequisite for reproducibility in large genomics consortia.

### F3: Rigorous filtering is essential — over 60% of full call set removed by non-exonic and panel-of-normals filters for open-access release

**Finding:** The controlled-access MAF contains 22.5M variants; the open-access MAF contains 3.6M (a >6-fold reduction). The two largest filter categories are: (1) non-exonic/non-capture regions ("NonExonic"/"bitgt" flags) removing >60% of calls, and (2) the Broad Panel of Normals (PoN) flagging ~30% of calls as potential germline or recurrent technical artifacts. LAML (acute myeloid leukemia) shows the most dramatic loss — only 44% of AWG calls recovered — due to tumor-contaminated normal samples from blood biopsies.

**Detail:** The open-access filtering pipeline protects against germline leakage (a privacy requirement for TCGA data release), removes technical artifacts, and focuses calls on protein-coding exonic variants relevant for driver gene analysis. Critically, the study shows that performing significantly mutated gene (SMG) analysis (MutSig2CV, MuSiC2) on unfiltered controlled-access data yields dramatically inflated false positives (MutSig2CV: 1,203 SMGs vs. 10 SMGs in open-access data for KIRC). Known KIRC drivers (VHL, PBRM1, SETD2, BAP1, MTOR, TP53, PTEN) are cleanly identified only from the open-access PASS variants. This underscores that variant filtering is not just a quality step but a biological necessity for meaningful driver gene analysis.

### F4: Cancer-type-specific mutation burdens are recapitulated; skin and lung cancers have highest median SNV counts

**Finding:** Across all 33 cancer types, SKCM (melanoma), LUSC, and LUAD have the highest median SNV counts per sample in the open-access MAF, consistent with known UV and tobacco mutagen signatures. LAML and OV have the lowest post-filtering mutation counts. This cross-cancer mutation burden landscape is directly used in the PanCan Atlas mutational signatures and driver discovery papers.

**Detail:** The MC3 open-access MAF contains 3.4M SNV events from 10,295 samples. The cancer-type mutation burden ranking (SKCM > LUSC/LUAD > BLCA > COAD/READ > UCEC at the high end; THCA, PCPG, ACC, TGCT at the low end) is consistent with known mutagenic exposures. Hypermutator samples (n=14 SNV, n=10 indel) were excluded from mutation burden plots for clarity. The MC3 MAF recovered 89.5% of PanCan12 original variant calls while increasing total call set size by 25%, with >90% concordance for HNSC, SKCM, BRCA, BLCA, COAD/READ, and UCEC.

### F5: MC3 demonstrates that large-scale multi-institutional genomics requires team science, shared methods, and flexible data release tiers

**Finding:** The MC3 project, involving >10 institutions and multiple cloud platforms, establishes five key lessons: (1) default tool parameters are insufficient — original authors' input was needed; (2) liquid tumors (LAML) require custom calling strategies; (3) providing annotated filter flags rather than hard-filtered files enables downstream flexibility; (4) reproducible containerized methods are essential as data volumes grow; (5) meta-calling outperforms single-method approaches. These lessons directly shaped the TCGA PanCan Atlas computational framework and remain the best-practice standard for pan-cancer mutation calling.

**Detail:** The three MAF tiers (controlled-access full, open-access PASS-filtered, and validation MAF) serve different use cases: controlled-access for sensitivity-requiring heterogeneity analysis; open-access for driver discovery and public sharing; validation MAF for benchmarking. The 33 cancer type–specific cBioPortal PanCan Atlas studies serve as the primary user-facing endpoint for this data resource, enabling the broader research community to query MC3-derived variants without requiring raw data access.

## Open Questions

- Should a next-generation MC3 use whole-genome sequencing (WGS) instead of WES to capture non-coding driver mutations, structural variants, and mutational signatures at higher resolution?
- How well do MC3 variant calls perform in low-purity and highly aneuploid tumors where FACETS-based corrections are most needed?
- Would rerunning MC3-equivalent pipelines on updated cloud platforms with newer callers (e.g., Mutect2, DeepVariant, GATK4) significantly change the driver gene landscape?
- How should MC3 filtering strategies adapt to tumor types (e.g., LAML, OV) where the standard germline-removal filters over-filter true somatic events?
- Is the "two-caller intersection" rule still optimal given advances in deep-learning-based variant callers that have dramatically different error profiles?
- What fraction of PanCan Atlas biological findings would be altered by using WGS instead of WES as the input data type?

## Limitations

- WES-based — non-coding mutations, structural variants, and events outside exome capture regions are not represented
- The seven callers were selected based on 2014–2016 best practices; newer callers (Mutect2, GATK4) were not available at project initiation
- Validation dataset (3,128 samples with targeted deep sequencing) was not randomly sampled — it was selected by original AWGs, biasing toward known high-confidence SMG regions
- LAML calls required manual Sanger sequencing interventions in the original AWG MAF not reproducible in MC3, leading to ~56% loss of original LAML variants in the open-access set
- hg19 genome build — MC3 predates GRCh38 and liftover introduces some coordinate uncertainty
- Tumor purity heterogeneity not uniformly corrected; low-purity samples may contribute more false negatives
- False negative rate is incompletely quantified because validation sites were not randomly sampled from the full call set (no background/uncalled-site sampling)
