## Summary

Exome sequencing of 151 myeloproliferative neoplasm (MPN) patients — supplemented by Sanger sequencing of CALR exon 9 across 1,345 additional hematologic cancers — identifies somatic frameshift mutations in *CALR* (encoding calreticulin) in 70–84% of MPNs with nonmutated *JAK2*. All 148 identified *CALR* mutations are exon 9 indels generating a +1 frameshift and a novel mutant C-terminal domain. CALR mutations are mutually exclusive with *JAK2* and *MPL* mutations and found primarily in essential thrombocythemia (ET) and myelofibrosis (MF), not polycythemia vera. Phylogenetic clonal analysis places CALR mutations at the earliest node, supporting an initiating driver role. This landmark study establishes CALR mutation testing as a clinically essential diagnostic tool for JAK2-negative MPN.

## Key Findings

### F1: CALR exon 9 frameshift mutations found in 70–84% of JAK2-unmutated MPN — identifying the long-sought second major MPN driver

**Finding:** Somatic frameshift mutations in *CALR* exon 9 are the dominant molecular event in MPN patients lacking *JAK2* or *MPL* mutations. CALR mutations were found in 26/31 (84%) of ET or MF patients with nonmutated JAK2/MPL, and in none of 120 patients with JAK2 or MPL mutations, establishing near-complete mutual exclusivity.

**Detail:** All 148 CALR mutations are indels in exon 9 generating a +1 frameshift, which predicts a mutant protein with a novel C-terminal domain. Two common variants account for the majority: L367fs*46 (52-bp deletion) and K385fs*47 (5-bp insertion/inverse duplication). CALR mutations were not found in solid tumors (1,517 tested) or healthy controls (550 tested), and were seen in only 8% of myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) cases and occasional other myeloid cancers, establishing high disease specificity. The discovery resolved the "JAK2-negative" diagnostic challenge that had hampered MPN classification.

### F2: Two dominant CALR mutation types (deletion L367fs*46 and insertion K385fs*47) share a novel oncogenic C-terminal domain

**Finding:** Despite 19 distinct CALR exon 9 variants, the two most frequent — a 52-bp deletion (L367fs*46, "Type 1") and a 5-bp insertion (K385fs*47, "Type 2") — account for the majority of cases. All variants produce a common +1 frameshift that eliminates the normal C-terminal ER-retention signal (KDEL) and generates a novel cationic C-terminal peptide, strongly suggesting the new C-terminus is the functional oncogenic element.

**Detail:** The 52-bp deletion is flanked by 7 bp of homologous sequence (microhomology-mediated deletion). The 5-bp insertion represents an inverse tandem duplication of five preceding nucleotides. Both classes and all minor variants result in the same +1 frameshift. Mutant calreticulin localizes to the endoplasmic reticulum without increased cell-surface or Golgi accumulation — suggesting the oncogenic mechanism involves altered protein–protein interactions in the ER rather than aberrant surface expression. The novel C-terminal domain is now understood to aberrantly activate the thrombopoietin receptor (MPL), explaining the thrombocytosis phenotype.

### F3: CALR-mutant MPN has distinct phenotype — higher platelet counts and lower hemoglobin vs. JAK2-mutant MPN

**Finding:** Patients with CALR-mutant MPN present with significantly higher platelet counts and lower hemoglobin levels than patients with JAK2-mutant MPN, establishing CALR mutation status as a clinically informative phenotypic discriminator beyond JAK2 status.

**Detail:** The phenotypic differences are consistent with the biology: JAK2 V617F preferentially activates erythroid and megakaryocytic lineages broadly (explaining polycythemia in PV), while mutant CALR selectively activates MPL (thrombopoietin receptor) on megakaryocytes, producing predominantly megakaryocytic hyperplasia and thrombocytosis without erythrocytosis. CALR mutations are found exclusively in ET and MF (not PV), reinforcing this lineage-specific effect. These phenotypic correlates have diagnostic and prognostic implications: CALR-mutant ET patients have different thrombosis risk profiles than JAK2-mutant ET.

### F4: CALR mutations originate in hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells and are clonal initiating events

**Finding:** Phylogenetic analysis of hematopoietic colonies reveals CALR mutations at the earliest clonal node, with JAK2 V617F and other mutations (epigenetic regulators, splicing factors) acquired later, establishing CALR as a disease-initiating mutation rather than a secondary cooperating event.

**Detail:** Hematopoietic colonies from CALR-mutant patients were individually genotyped for all mutations identified on exome sequencing. The CALR mutation was uniformly present in colonies carrying other somatic mutations but not vice versa. Detection of CALR mutation in hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells (HSPCs) directly confirmed stem cell origin. Clonal heterogeneity assessment (Bayesian Dirichlet process on exome data) confirmed subclonal architecture consistent with CALR as a founding event. This positions CALR mutation detection in peripheral blood as a highly sensitive diagnostic assay for MPN.

### F5: MPN mutational landscape reveals higher mutation burden in myelofibrosis and patterns mirroring myelodysplastic syndrome

**Finding:** Myelofibrosis carries significantly more somatic mutations per patient (median 13.0) than essential thrombocythemia (6.5) or polycythemia vera (6.5), consistent with MF as a more advanced disease stage. Co-mutation patterns — particularly ASXL1 with splicing factors and SRSF2 with epigenetic modifiers — parallel those in MDS.

**Detail:** 1,498 somatic mutations were identified and validated across 150 samples. SRSF2 mutations co-occurred with TET2, IDH1, and ASXL1 — a profile strikingly similar to MDS. ASXL1 co-occurred with U2AF1 and SRSF2 RNA splicing mutations. Novel CHEK2 missense mutations were found in one patient each across PV, ET, and MF. MPL mutations (thrombopoietin receptor) were confined to ET and MF with unmutated JAK2. The mutation spectrum was predominantly C→T transitions, similar to MDS and epithelial cancers.

## Open Questions

- What is the precise molecular mechanism by which the novel CALR C-terminus activates MPL/JAK-STAT signaling?
- Do CALR Type 1 (deletion) and Type 2 (insertion) mutations confer different clinical outcomes (thrombosis risk, transformation to AML)?
- Can CALR mutation subtype or clone size predict transformation from ET to MF or AML?
- Do co-mutations in TET2, ASXL1, or splicing genes modify the clinical phenotype or prognosis of CALR-mutant MPN beyond what CALR mutation alone predicts?
- Is CALR mutation status a predictive biomarker for response to JAK1/2 inhibitors (e.g., ruxolitinib) in MF, and does it differ from JAK2-mutant MF response?
- What are the mechanisms of CALR-mutation–negative, JAK2-negative, MPL-negative ("triple-negative") MPN — the ~15% not explained by any of these three genes?

## Limitations

- Exome coverage at the CALR locus was low (median 10 reads); Sanger sequencing of exon 9 was required as a complementary approach, introducing potential sensitivity variation
- Constitutional DNA contamination by neoplastic cells in T cells and buccal cells required special handling (in vitro T-cell culture); residual contamination may have affected variant calling
- The functional mechanism of the novel CALR C-terminus was not established in this paper (subsequently clarified in follow-up studies showing MPL activation)
- Protein subcellular localization studies did not reveal cell-surface enrichment, leaving the oncogenic mechanism open at time of publication
- Clinical outcome data (thrombosis, transformation, survival) not analyzed in this initial discovery cohort
- Small numbers of individual rare CALR variants limit subtype-level genotype-phenotype analysis
