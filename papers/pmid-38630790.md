## Summary

This paper characterizes diffuse pleural mesothelioma (DPM) with genomic near-haploidization (GNH) — a rare subtype (~4.8%) defined by genome-wide loss of heterozygosity affecting >80% of the genome, first flagged by the TCGA. Using MSK-IMPACT data from 290 DPM patients with allele-specific copy-number analysis (FACETS), the authors define the clinical, histologic, and molecular features of GNH DPMs. GNH DPMs are younger patients, more frequently biphasic, harbor abundant tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) and necrosis, rarely have classical asbestos exposure, are enriched for SETDB1 and TP53 mutations (SETDB1 being pathognomonic for GNH), and carry significantly worse prognosis despite younger age. Preliminary ICB response data and TIL enrichment suggest immunotherapy may warrant further evaluation. The full dataset is publicly available in the `plmeso_msk_2024` cBioPortal study.

## Key Findings

### F1: GNH defines a small but genomically distinct DPM subset at ~4.8% prevalence, with recurrent retained heterozygosity at chromosomes 5p and 7p

**Finding:** 10/210 (4.8%) evaluable DPMs demonstrated GNH (genome-wide LOH >80%), consistent with prior TCGA estimate (~3.2%). LOH score distribution is bimodal: GNH cases cluster around 90% LOH; non-GNH cases <30%. Half of GNH cases show evidence of genome duplication (≥2n copy states after LOH), suggesting GNH → genome doubling → partial heterozygosity restoration.

**Detail:** Allele-specific copy-number analysis with FACETS. Recurrent retained heterozygosity at chromosome arms 5p (DROSHA, IL7R, RICTOR, SDHA, TERT) and 7p (IKZF1, INHBA, ETV1, CARD11, PMS2, RAC1) across GNH cases, mirroring TCGA observations. These retained heterozygous regions may represent selective pressure to maintain gene function (imprinting or haploinsufficiency requirement). Methylation profiles (850k array, n=20 MSK + 73 TCGA cases) do not significantly differ between GNH and non-GNH, suggesting GNH is primarily a structural rather than epigenetic phenomenon.

**Genes / cancer types:** `DROSHA`, `IL7R`, `RICTOR`, `SDHA`, `TERT`, `IKZF1`, `CARD11` (retained); genome-wide · Diffuse pleural mesothelioma

### F2: SETDB1 mutations are pathognomonic for GNH DPMs and absent in non-GNH cases

**Finding:** SETDB1 mutations were found in 8/10 (80%) GNH DPMs and in 0/8 tested non-GNH DPMs (q=0.0035). Validated in a separate cohort: 4/4 SETDB1-mutant patients had confirmed GNH. SETDB1 mutation is effectively a molecular marker for GNH in DPM.

**Detail:** SETDB1 was not included in standard 341–468 gene IMPACT panels; all GNH and select non-GNH cases required re-sequencing with expanded panels or WES. Among cases with genome doubling, SETDB1 and TP53 co-mutation was seen in 2; variant allele fraction analysis showed SETDB1 mutations subclonal relative to TP53 (SETDB1:TP53 VAF ratios 0.676 and 0.53), suggesting temporal ordering: TP53 mutation → genome-wide LOH → genome doubling → monoallelic SETDB1 mutation. SETDB1 encodes a histone H3K9 methyltransferase involved in chromatin silencing; its role in GNH pathogenesis remains mechanistically unclear.

**Genes / cancer types:** `SETDB1`, `TP53` · Diffuse pleural mesothelioma

### F3: GNH DPMs present in younger patients with less asbestos exposure, and are dominated by biphasic histology with high TIL density

**Finding:** GNH DPMs occur at median age 58 vs. 71 years (p=0.008). Classical occupational asbestos exposure is less frequent (20% vs. 58%, p=0.02). Biphasic histology is found in 80% of GNH vs. 15% of non-GNH (p<0.0001). High TIL density (3+) in 90% of GNH vs. 20% non-GNH (p<0.0001). Tumor necrosis present in 90% vs. 24% (p<0.0001). PD-L1 positivity similar (55.6% vs. 34.1%, p=0.3).

**Detail:** Central pathology review blinded to GNH status. After controlling for biphasic histology, GNH DPMs remained significantly younger (p=0.02), less asbestos-exposed (p=0.003), with higher TILs (p=0.003) and necrosis (p=0.007). No patients in GNH group had prior radiation. Validation cohort (n=4 SETDB1-mutant/GNH, 505-gene panel): all biphasic, high TILs in 3/4, necrosis in 4/4, majority female (3/4), median age 63.5.

### F4: GNH is independently associated with worse overall survival (10.9 vs. 25.4 months), even after controlling for biphasic histology

**Finding:** Despite younger age, GNH DPM patients had significantly shorter median OS (10.9 vs. 25.4 months, log-rank p=0.004). This prognostic impact persisted when comparing GNH to non-GNH biphasic tumors only (19.5 vs. longer, p=0.016), indicating GNH is an independent adverse prognostic factor beyond histology.

**Detail:** TP53 alterations (60% in GNH vs. 14% in non-GNH, q=0.0035) and NF2 alterations (90% vs. 26.5%, q=0.0007) were significantly enriched in GNH. After controlling for biphasic histology, only TP53 association remained significant (q=0.04), as NF2 alterations are common in biphasic DPMs generally. BAP1 alterations (57% in non-GNH) were less frequent in GNH (20%, q=0.08), suggesting a mechanistically distinct genomic driver pathway. No germline pathogenic variants in GNH patients (vs. 10.8% in non-GNH, including BAP1, BRCA2, CHEK2).

### F5: Preliminary data suggest GNH DPMs may respond to immune checkpoint blockade

**Finding:** Of 3 GNH DPM patients treated with ICB (ipilimumab/nivolumab or pembrolizumab), 2 achieved a partial response — an encouraging signal given the typically poor response of DPM to immunotherapy. This is supported by high TIL density, biphasic histology, and PD-L1 positivity in GNH tumors.

**Detail:** ICB response data assessed blinded to GNH status by thoracic oncology specialist. 44 non-GNH DPMs treated with ICB for comparison. The preliminary ICB response data is anecdotal but consistent with reported patterns in TCGA/ICGC GNH cases. Standard of care ICB for DPM (ipilimumab/nivolumab, CheckMate-743) has established activity mainly in non-epithelioid (including biphasic) histology — the dominant subtype in GNH. Authors note this subset warrants prospective immunotherapy evaluation. No pathogenic microbial genomes (including SV40) detected in any DPM samples by off-target read analysis.

## Open Questions

- What is the mechanistic link between SETDB1 inactivation (H3K9 methyltransferase) and genome-wide LOH / near-haploidization? Does loss of SETDB1 promote chromosomal instability or select for haploid states?
- Do the retained heterozygous regions (chr5p, 7p) harbor haploinsufficient tumor suppressors or imprinted genes essential for viability in GNH cells?
- Is GNH a generalizable subtype in other mesothelioma sites (peritoneal, pericardial) or exclusive to pleural?
- Why do GNH DPMs occur at younger age with less asbestos exposure? Is there an alternative etiologic mechanism (radiation, germline SETDB1 variant, viral)?
- Can prospective ICB trials stratified by GNH status validate the preliminary response signal?

## Limitations

- Small GNH cohort (n=10 primary, n=4 validation) limits statistical power for clinical and molecular subanalyses.
- SETDB1 was not covered in primary IMPACT panel; retrospective re-sequencing introduced potential selection bias toward cases with available tissue/DNA.
- ICB response data (n=3 GNH patients) is insufficient for formal efficacy conclusions.
- Methylation analysis at 850k probe resolution may miss locus-specific epigenetic differences.
- All cases from a single tertiary cancer center (MSK); population-level GNH frequency may differ.
- LOH analysis (FACETS) requires ≥20% tumor purity; 80 of 290 patients excluded, potentially enriching evaluable cohort for higher-purity/more aggressive tumors.
