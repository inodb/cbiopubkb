## Summary

This PanCancer Atlas study performs the most comprehensive integrative molecular classification of cancer to date, profiling ~10,000 TCGA tumors across 33 cancer types using five platforms (aneuploidy, DNA methylation, mRNA, miRNA, and RPPA protein). iCluster integration identifies 28 molecular subtypes, the majority of which align with tissue of origin and histology rather than with any single molecular aberration. Cell-of-origin patterns dominate across all platforms—including DNA methylation, which retains tissue-specific signals even after excluding known tissue-specific CpG sites—supporting four major pan-organ axes: pan-GI, pan-gynecological, pan-squamous, and pan-kidney. The classification provides a molecular rationale for cross-cancer therapeutic strategies.

## Key Findings

### F1: Integrative clustering identifies 28 molecular subtypes dominated by cell-of-origin, not histology alone

**Finding:** Across all five molecular platforms and integrative iCluster analysis, the primary organizing principle of cancer molecular classification is the cell of tissue origin, with most subtypes aligning to organ systems rather than histological categories.

**Detail:** 28 iClusters identified from 9,759 tumors across 33 types. Copy number contributed 47% of integrative clustering weight, transcriptome (mRNA+miRNA) 42%, methylation 11%. For 16 of 33 tumor types, >80% of samples grouped together in a single iCluster. 8 iClusters were dominated by a single tumor type (LAML, LGG [IDH1-mut], OV, UCEC, THCA, PRAD, LIHC, LUAD). Most diverse iCluster (C20:mixed stromal/immune) contained 25 tumor types.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer (33 types)

**Data availability:** `available`
> All TCGA PanCan Atlas data available in cBioPortal. Individual tumor-type studies accessible with iCluster assignments.

**Tool support:** `native`
> OncoPrint, Groups Comparison, and Clinical Data tabs allow cross-cancer exploration. TumorMap visualization available at separate tool (tumormap.ucsc.edu).

### F2: DNA methylation retains cell-of-origin signal even after removing tissue-specific CpG sites

**Finding:** Cancer-associated DNA hypermethylation is organized primarily by tissue of origin even after excluding known tissue-type-specific methylation loci, indicating pre-existing chromatin marks rather than de novo methylation drive this patterning.

**Detail:** 3,139 CpG sites hypermethylated in ≥1 tumor type used (excluding tissue-specific baseline methylation sites); 25 methylation clusters identified from 10,814 tumors. Squamous cell carcinomas (HNSC, ESCA, LUSC, CESC) co-clustered in METH2 and METH3. Gastrointestinal adenocarcinomas (ESCA, STAD, COAD, READ) grouped in METH10–METH13. Gastrointestinal CIMP, EBV-associated hypermethylation, and MSI clusters each formed distinct methylation subtypes within pan-GI.

**Genes / cancer types:** DNA methylation · Pan-GI, pan-squamous cancer types

**Data availability:** `available`
> DNA methylation data for TCGA studies available in cBioPortal and via GDC/Broad Firehose.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Methylation cluster assignments available in supplementary data; detailed CpG-level analysis requires downloading IDAT/beta-value matrices and running clustering externally (R/BioConductor: minfi, ChAMP).

### F3: Four major pan-organ axes confirmed: pan-GI, pan-gynecological, pan-squamous, pan-kidney

**Finding:** Cross-cancer analyses reveal four robust pan-organ molecular groupings that span traditional histopathological boundaries and provide rationale for lineage-agnostic treatment strategies.

**Detail:** Pan-GI (COAD, READ, STAD, ESCA adenocarcinoma): 3 iClusters (EBV-CIMP [C1], MSI [C18], CIN [C4]) driven primarily by DNA methylation differences. Pan-squamous (LUSC, HNSC, CESC, ESCA squamous, BLCA): 3 iClusters (C10, C25, C27 [HPV]), all with chr3q amplification; HPV-positive CESC/HNSC cluster distinctly. Pan-kidney (KIRC, KIRP): C28, high hypoxia and immune checkpoint (PD-1, CTLA4) signaling; KICH clusters with ACC in C9 (hypodiploid). Pan-gyn (BRCA, OV, UCEC, UCS): distributed across multiple iClusters; CESC largely in pan-squamous C27.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ERBB2`, `TP53`, `KRAS`, `BRAF`, `IDH1`, `POLE` · Pan-GI, pan-squamous, pan-kidney, pan-gyn

**Data availability:** `available`
> TCGA PanCan Atlas studies on cBioPortal include iCluster assignments and organ system groupings.

**Tool support:** `native`
> Multi-study query in cBioPortal allows cross-cancer OncoPrint and pathway analysis. Pan-organ system comparisons possible natively; pan-gyn, pan-GI, pan-kidney, pan-squamous sub-analyses published as companion TCGA papers.

### F4: Mutational signatures align with iCluster subtypes: UVB in melanoma, tobacco in LUAD, POLE in UCEC/CRC

**Finding:** Tumor mutational signatures are tightly concordant with iCluster identity, reflecting the environmental and DNA repair context of each pan-organ group.

**Detail:** Somatic mutation data not used in iCluster generation but used for post-hoc characterization. C15:SKCM/UVM enriched for UVB signature. C14:LUAD enriched for tobacco (SBS4) signature. C8:UCEC and C4:pan-GI (CRC) enriched for POLE hypermutator signature. C18:pan-GI (MSI) enriched for CpG signature, toxin signature, and unknown signatures associated with mismatch repair deficiency. Overall somatic mutation burden highest in pan-GI, pan-squamous, SKCM/UVM, and LUAD iClusters.

**Genes / cancer types:** `POLE`, `MLH1`, `KRAS` · UCEC, CRC, LUAD, melanoma

**Data availability:** `available`
> Somatic mutation data in TCGA PanCan Atlas studies on cBioPortal; TMB accessible via the Mutations tab.

**Tool support:** `api`
> Mutational signature decomposition (COSMIC SBS) requires external tools (SigProfilerExtractor, deconstructSigs) applied to downloaded MAF files. TMB by iCluster queryable via cBioPortal API.

**Concepts:** #mutation-signatures #pan-cancer-map #MSI #BRAF #KRAS

### F5: Stemness signatures (mRNA and methylation-based) co-localize on TumorMap with distinct tumor subsets

**Finding:** High cancer stemness index (RNA and methylation-based) concentrates in specific pan-cancer regions of the TumorMap, notably in testicular germ cell tumors and a subset of AML.

**Detail:** Two Malta et al. (2018) stemness indices mapped onto 9,759-sample TumorMap; high mRNA-stemness enriched in TGCT, LAML, and certain LUAD/GBM subtypes. Methylation-based stemness enriched in TGCT but not LAML. Stemness proposed as driver of treatment resistance and metastatic potential. C3:mesenchymal (immune) and C20:mixed (stromal/immune) iClusters showed highest stromal and leukocyte fractions, respectively.

**Genes / cancer types:** Stemness index · TGCT, LAML, GBM

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Stemness index scores not directly queryable in standard cBioPortal interface; available as supplementary data in Malta et al. 2018 (PMID 29628290).

**Tool support:** `api`
> Stemness scores available for download; integration with cBioPortal clinical data requires API or data download and merge in R/Python.

### F6: iCluster classification improves management of cancer of unknown primary (CUP)

**Finding:** The integrated molecular subtypes provide a multi-platform molecular taxonomy that could improve tissue-of-origin assignment in cancer of unknown primary, affecting 1%–3% of newly diagnosed patients.

**Detail:** iCluster classification strongly recapitulates tissue of origin even for tumors with mixed histology or ambiguous primary. Both RNA-based and methylation-based tissue-of-origin classifiers (already in clinical use) are consistent with the iCluster taxonomy. High silhouette widths for single-cancer-type iClusters and pan-organ system iClusters suggest robust classification fidelity. Potential to reclassify ~1 in 10 cancer patients per earlier Pan-Cancer-12 analysis (2014), with larger dataset confirming cross-tissue associations.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer (CUP subset)

**Data availability:** `available`
> Molecular data for all 33 TCGA cancer types available in cBioPortal; iCluster assignments in supplementary tables.

**Tool support:** `custom`
> Tissue-of-origin classification requires running external classifiers (e.g., CancerType.AI, OncoNPC) on RNA or methylation data; not available natively in cBioPortal.

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Can pan-organ molecular groupings (pan-squamous, pan-GI, pan-kidney) directly guide lineage-agnostic clinical trial design and drug approval strategies?
- Does the cell-of-origin dominance in molecular classification persist in treatment-naïve vs. post-treatment tumors (e.g., does therapy erode tissue-specific signals)?
- What proportion of patients reclassified under molecular taxonomy vs. histopathology actually benefit from molecularly guided therapy?
- Can the multi-platform iCluster framework be applied to smaller, targeted clinical datasets (e.g., MSK-IMPACT) to achieve comparable classification?

## Limitations (from paper)

- Analysis based primarily on primary untreated tumors; metastatic or treatment-resistant disease may show different molecular organization.
- Aneuploidy data contributed weakly to clustering (47% weight in iCluster) and was poorly concordant with other platforms for ~1/3 of samples (AN7 group with minimal copy-number changes).
- RPPA protein data available for only 7,858 of 11,286 samples, limiting integrative analyses.
- iCluster is computationally intensive; not easily scalable to new sample classification without retraining.
- Genomic mutation data intentionally excluded from iCluster generation (used only post-hoc) due to data sparsity, limiting direct integration of somatic driver events into the taxonomy.
- Clinical outcome data not systematically integrated; molecular classification not directly linked to survival or therapy response in this paper.
