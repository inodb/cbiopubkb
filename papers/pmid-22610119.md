## Summary

Whole-exome sequencing of 112 prostate adenocarcinoma/normal pairs identified SPOP as the most frequently mutated gene (13%), along with novel recurrent alterations in FOXA1 (4%) and MED12 (5%). SPOP mutations cluster in the substrate-binding cleft of its MATH domain and are mutually exclusive with TMPRSS2-ERG and other ETS fusions, defining a distinct molecular subtype of ETS-negative prostate cancer. This subtype is further characterized by enrichment of 5q21 (CHD1) and 6q21 (FOXO3/PRDM1) deletions and depletion of TP53 and PTEN alterations.

## Key Findings

### F1: SPOP is the most frequently mutated gene in prostate cancer (6–15%), defining an ETS-negative subtype

**Finding:** SPOP harbors heterozygous missense mutations in 13% of discovery exomes and 6–15% across five independent cohorts (including 14.5% of metastases). All mutations cluster in the MATH-domain substrate-binding cleft at conserved residues (Y87, W131, F133 recurrently), disrupting ubiquitin ligase function and increasing tumor cell invasion.

**Detail:** SPOP mutations are completely mutually exclusive with TMPRSS2-ERG and other ETS rearrangements (P < 0.001, all five cohorts), are absent in 36 benign prostate samples and 6 cell lines, and are detected in HG-PIN adjacent to adenocarcinoma — establishing them as early driver events. SPOP-mutant tumors are enriched for 5q21 deletions (CHD1 locus, P = 1.4×10⁻¹¹) and 6q21 deletions (FOXO3/PRDM1, P = 3.4×10⁻⁷), and largely lack TP53 lesions and PTEN/PIK3CA alterations in primary disease.

**Genes / cancer types:** SPOP, CHD1, FOXO3, PRDM1, TMPRSS2-ERG · Prostate adenocarcinoma

### F2: FOXA1 Forkhead-domain mutations cluster at DNA-binding surface in 4–8% of tumors

**Finding:** FOXA1 harbors recurrent nonsilent mutations in 4/111 discovery exomes and 4/41 RNA-seq samples; all mutations affect conserved residues of the Forkhead domain adjacent to the DNA-binding surface, suggesting disruption of pioneer transcription factor function and AR-driven transcription.

**Detail:** FOXA1 modulates AR-driven transcription and activates CDKN1B (p27Kip1) expression. Mutations are distinct from any previously reported somatic change in this gene, and their clustering strongly implies functional selection. Their presence in the context of SPOP-mutant tumors (partial co-occurrence) reinforces the ETS-negative genomic background.

**Genes / cancer types:** FOXA1, AR, CDKN1B · Prostate adenocarcinoma

### F3: MED12 recurrent F1224L mutation in 5% of prostate cancers, distinct from leiomyoma hotspots

**Finding:** MED12 is mutated in 6/111 exomes; five cases share the F1224L substitution. MED12 prostate mutations affect different codons than the >70% frequency mutations in uterine leiomyoma, and arise in epithelial (not stromal) cells by LCM, implicating CDK8-mediated transcriptional dysregulation as a distinct mechanism in prostate tumorigenesis.

**Detail:** MED12 encodes a subunit of the Mediator complex and CDK8 sub-complex that regulates p53 and androgen signaling transcriptional programs. The tissue-specific codon preference suggests tissue-context-dependent substrate specificity of Mediator complex alterations.

**Genes / cancer types:** MED12, CDK8 · Prostate adenocarcinoma, uterine leiomyoma (comparator)

### F4: SPOP-mutant tumors show enriched 5q21/CHD1 and 6q21 co-deletions and depleted ETS, TP53, and PTEN lesions

**Finding:** CNA analysis of 169 copy-number-profiled tumors shows that SPOP mutation status predicts a distinct CNA landscape: enrichment of 5q21 (CHD1) and 6q21 (FOXO3/PRDM1) deletions, and mutual exclusivity with ERG amplification, TP53 loss, and primary-tumor PTEN/PIK3CA alterations. These co-occurrences suggest cooperative tumor suppressor loss in SPOP-driven tumorigenesis.

**Detail:** CHD1 undergoes disruptive rearrangements in prostate cancer independently; co-deletion with SPOP mutation suggests cooperation. In metastatic disease, PTEN co-occurrence with SPOP increases, implying that PI3K pathway activation may be a progression event specifically in SPOP-mutant tumors.

**Genes / cancer types:** SPOP, CHD1, FOXO3, PRDM1, PTEN, PIK3CA, TP53, ERG · Prostate adenocarcinoma

### F5: Prostate cancer mutation rate is low (1.4/Mb) with 12 significantly mutated genes identified

**Finding:** Excluding an MSH6 hypermutator, the discovery cohort shows a median of 30 non-silent mutations per tumor (~1.4/Mb), higher than prior whole-genome studies. MutSig identified 12 significantly mutated genes (q < 0.1) including known drivers TP53, PTEN, PIK3CA and novel ones SPOP, FOXA1, MED12, CDKN1B, THSD7B, SCN11A, ZNF595.

**Detail:** CDKN1B (p27Kip1) was mutated in 3 samples and deleted in 16, providing novel evidence for somatic substitution in this cell cycle regulator. The overall mutation rate is lower than most epithelial cancers but higher than prior prostate genome studies, likely owing to improved sensitivity from high-depth exome capture.

**Genes / cancer types:** TP53, PTEN, PIK3CA, SPOP, FOXA1, MED12, CDKN1B · Prostate adenocarcinoma

## Open Questions / Hypotheses

- Does SPOP mutation confer gain-of-function (altered substrate profile), dominant-negative activity, or subtle changes in substrate specificity? Which ubiquitin ligase substrates are specifically deregulated?
- Could SPOP mutation status serve as a biomarker for selecting patients for hedgehog pathway or other substrate-pathway inhibitors?
- Why do PTEN/PIK3CA co-alterations emerge more frequently in SPOP-mutant metastases than primaries — does SPOP mutation create a permissive context for PI3K addiction on progression?
- Do FOXA1 Forkhead mutations phenocopy AR resistance mechanisms seen in castration-resistant prostate cancer?
- Is SPOP mutation an early event uniformly across all ETS-negative prostate cancers, and does it predict indolent versus aggressive clinical course?

## Limitations

- Discovery cohort is treatment-naïve radical prostatectomy specimens only; castration-resistant and heavily treated tumors under-represented
- RNA-seq performed on only 22 exome-sequenced tumors; transcriptional consequences of mutations not fully characterized for most cases
- Functional invasion assays performed in vitro only; in vivo significance of SPOP loss in tumor progression not demonstrated
- LCM/epithelial confirmation performed on a limited subset for MED12; stromal admixture could affect allele fraction estimates in bulk sequencing
- Validation cohorts used Sanger sequencing, which may miss low-fraction clonal SPOP mutations
