## Summary

The most comprehensive paired pre/post-chemotherapy clonal evolution study of urothelial carcinoma (UC), using whole-exome sequencing of 72 prospectively collected tumors from 32 patients including 16 matched pre/post-chemotherapy sets and 2 rapid autopsy cases. Key findings: (1) the majority of mutations (71.6% on average) are not shared between pre- and post-chemotherapy tumors, demonstrating profound chemotherapy-driven clonal restructuring; (2) branching evolution and metastatic spread are early events, predating clinical diagnosis; (3) APOBEC mutagenesis (SBS2/13) is clonally enriched after chemotherapy and drives ongoing evolution; and (4) L1CAM and integrin signaling pathway mutations are clonally selected by chemotherapy, implicating cell-adhesion-mediated resistance. The study establishes the evolutionary framework for chemotherapy resistance in UC with direct implications for repeat metastatic biopsy and targeting resistance pathways.

## Key Findings

### F1: Only ~28% of mutations are shared between pre- and post-chemotherapy UC tumors

**Finding:** On average, only 28.4% (range 0.2%ŌĆō76.4%) of mutations are shared between pre- and post-chemotherapy tumor pairs within the same patient. This mutational divergence is consistent across primary-primary and primary-metastatic pairs (Wilcoxon p = 0.17). Even mutations in key driver genes including PIK3CA, KMT2D (MLL2), ATM, and TP53 are not consistently shared. Some post-chemotherapy tumors develop different mutations in the same gene (e.g., patient WCM077: TP53 p.Y234C in primary and TP53 p.G266V in lung metastasis).

**Detail:** Confirms with high-coverage targeted panel sequencing (Pearson correlation = 0.93 between WES and targeted VAFs, P<10^ŌłÆ171). The dramatic divergence indicates that biopsies of pre-treatment primary tumors are insufficient to characterize the molecular profile of subsequent chemotherapy-treated metastatic diseaseŌĆösupporting the need for repeat metastatic biopsies. The ATM/RB/FANCC signature (associated with chemotherapy sensitivity) was present in 73.3% of pre-chemotherapy tumors but only 37.9% post-chemotherapy (p = 0.05), consistent with selection against chemotherapy-sensitive clones.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `PIK3CA`, `KMT2D`, `ATM`, `RB`, `FANCC` ┬Ę Urothelial carcinoma

### F2: Branching evolution and metastatic spread are very early events in the natural history of UC

**Finding:** Phylogenetic analysis of 21 matched tumor sets reveals early branching evolution in every reconstructed treeŌĆöthe primary tumor occupies a branch position, not the root, meaning the ancestral clone gave rise to multiple diverging cell populations before clinical diagnosis. In a detailed 12-sample autopsy case (patient WCM117, tracked from TURBT through cystectomy to autopsy), at least 5 clonal expansion waves had already occurred before the time of initial diagnosis.

**Detail:** In WCM117, the TURBT primary tumor had 138 private mutations (including genes implicated in cisplatin response: POLD2, FOXP1, FGFR4, TRRAP, EGFR), which disappeared from the evolutionary record after neoadjuvant chemotherapyŌĆöthese clones were eradicated. Meanwhile, a separate clade that had branched at divergence node 5 gave rise to all distant autopsy metastases. The transition to metastatic state was marked by a non-silent mutation in TSPAN8, a pro-metastatic and angiogenesis-promoting gene, suggesting a key driver event for metastatic seeding. Clonal fractions in autopsy metastases were high (mean 83%, range 69%ŌĆō91%), reflecting clonal sweep after treatment selection.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `TSC1`, `CDKN2A`, `TSPAN8`, `POLD2`, `FOXP1`, `FGFR4`, `EGFR` ┬Ę Urothelial carcinoma

### F3: CDKN2A undergoes clonal selection under chemotherapy pressure from subclonal to homozygous deletion

**Finding:** Detailed multi-sample analysis of WCM117 revealed a subclonal heterozygous CDKN2A deletion in the pre-chemotherapy primary tumor that evolved into a homozygous, clonal deletion in all distant metastatic lymph nodes and liver lesions collected at autopsy, confirmed by FISH. Copy number landscape was otherwise relatively stable (intra-patient Hamming Distance median = 0.20 vs. inter-patient 0.53, p = 3├Ś10^ŌłÆ8).

**Detail:** Two CNA clusters identified by hierarchical clustering across 44 tumors: Cluster A (9p21/CDKN2A/CDKN2B/MTAP deletions, euploid background) and Cluster B (1q21.1/SETDB1/MLLT11 amplifications, 6p22.3/E2F3 amplifications, TP53 mutations enriched, p = 0.0001). Cluster A enriched for TCGA basal/squamous-like subtype. These CNA clusters are stable features that persist across tumor evolution and are independent of treatment effectsŌĆösuggesting CNA structure is an early, fixed tumor characteristic, while SNV landscape is plastic under therapy.

### F4: APOBEC mutagenesis is clonally enriched in post-chemotherapy UC and continues to drive evolution

**Finding:** Four mutational signatures identified in the cohort: APOBEC (Sanger signatures 2 and 13), age, smoking, and ERCC2-associated. APOBEC mutagenesis (C>T/G at TCW motifs) is significantly enriched in post-chemotherapy tumors (p < 0.05), and the clonality of APOBEC-induced mutations increases significantly post-chemotherapy. APOBEC3A is the dominant enzyme (enrichment p = 0.00001); APOBEC3B also enriched (p = 0.04); APOBEC3G decreased post-chemo. APOBEC-induced mutations are enriched in ABC transporter family (OR=2.7, p=0.038) and homologous recombination DNA repair pathway genes (OR=3.8, p=0.033).

**Detail:** Proposed mechanism: platinum-based chemotherapy increases ssDNA formation during 5ŌĆ▓ŌåÆ3ŌĆ▓ resection at DSBs during homology-directed repair. This ssDNA is accessible to APOBEC mutagenesis, increasing genomic diversity in post-chemotherapy cells. Low frequency of ERCC2 mutations post-chemotherapy is consistent with ERCC2 being enriched in cisplatin respondersŌĆöERCC2-mutant clones are eliminated by chemotherapy, not enriched. APOBEC-induced mutations in key resistance pathways may provide the molecular substrate for subsequent clonal expansions of resistant clones.

**Genes / cancer types:** `APOBEC3A`, `APOBEC3B`, `APOBEC3G`, `ERCC2` ┬Ę Urothelial carcinoma

### F5: L1CAM and integrin signaling pathways are clonally enriched mutations in post-chemotherapy UC

**Finding:** Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) of clonally enriched mutations in post-chemotherapy samples reveals significant enrichment in: L1-cell adhesion molecule (L1CAM) pathway (OR=1.9, FDR=0.12), integrin signaling (OR=2.8, FDR=0.02), and trans-membrane small molecule transport (OR=1.9, FDR=0.002, implicating multi-drug resistance genes). 83% of L1CAM pathway mutations and 90% of integrin pathway mutations are missenseŌĆöconsistent with potential gain-of-function activation of these pathways.

**Detail:** Cell-adhesion-mediated drug resistance (CAM-DR) via L1CAM-integrin cross-talk is a well-documented resistance mechanism in cholangiocarcinoma, ovarian, and pancreatic cancer; this study is the first to implicate it in UC at the clonal level. L1CAM is targetable by antibodies with efficacy in xenograft models of cholangiocarcinoma, ovarian, and pancreatic ductal carcinoma. Focal Adhesion Kinase (FAK) inhibitors targeting integrin signaling are in early-phase trials and may sensitize tumors to chemotherapy.

**Genes / cancer types:** `L1CAM`, integrins, ABC transporters ┬Ę Urothelial carcinoma

## Open Questions

- Can the clonal enrichment of L1CAM and integrin pathway mutations in chemotherapy-treated UC be therapeutically exploited with FAK inhibitors or anti-L1CAM antibodies in combination with chemotherapy?
- Is the APOBEC mutagenesis enrichment post-chemotherapy a driver of new resistance mutations or simply a passenger phenomenon of increased ssDNA availability?
- Would serial ctDNA monitoring of APOBEC-signature fraction serve as a dynamic biomarker of emerging clonal resistance?
- Given that ERCC2 mutations are depleted post-chemotherapy (clones selected against), would patients with ERCC2-mutant primary tumors show different relapse patterns?
- Does the early branching evolution (multiple clones predating diagnosis) imply that synchronous multi-site biopsy would outperform single biopsy for UC molecular profiling at any disease stage?

## Limitations

- Small sample size (32 patients, 16 matched pairs); underpowered to identify individual gene-level correlates of chemotherapy resistance
- Single-institution cohort (Weill Cornell Medicine); patient selection enriched for advanced disease
- WES provides breadth but less depth than targeted sequencing for low-frequency subclonal detection
- CLONET clonality estimates depend on tumor purity assumptions; heterogeneous samples may introduce error
- Chemotherapy-resistant UC and chemonaive UC controls are mixed; some muscle-invasive controls never received chemotherapy
- Rapid autopsy (n=2) provides the most complete evolutionary picture but represents extreme selection (death from disease)
- RNA sequencing not performed; transcriptional subtype and microenvironment not assessed
