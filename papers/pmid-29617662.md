## Summary

Using three RNA-seq fusion callers (STAR-Fusion, EricScript, Breakfast) applied to 9,624 TCGA tumor samples across 33 cancer types, this study systematically characterizes the landscape of driver gene fusions in human cancer. A total of 25,664 fusions are identified (63.3% WGS-validated). Fusions involving oncogenes are associated with target gene overexpression, while fusions involving tumor suppressors show reduced expression. Of 2,892 kinase fusions, 1,275 (44%) retain an intact kinase domain. Fusions drive development in 16.5% of cancer cases and serve as the sole driver in >1%. Druggable fusions involving *TMPRSS2*, *RET*, *FGFR3*, *ALK*, *ESR1*, and others are found in 6.0% of all samples. Fusion-bearing tumors have significantly lower mutation burdens, implicating reduced immunotherapy efficacy in this subpopulation but suggesting that fusion-derived peptides may be immunogenic neoantigen candidates.

## Key Findings

### F1: Fusions drive 16.5% of cancer cases; 1.8% have driver fusions with no co-occurring driver mutations — fusions are often the sole driver

**Finding:** 8.3% of cancer cases have both driver mutations and driver fusions; 1.8% have driver fusions only (with no driver mutations), with notably low mutational burden comparable to cases with no driver alterations. Fusions involving driver genes are mutually exclusive with mutations in the same gene across cancer types.

**Detail:** Of 8,955 TCGA patients with overlapping mutation and fusion calls: 48.6% have known driver mutations; 28.1% have mutations in driver genes; 8.3% have both driver mutations and driver fusions; 1.8% have driver fusions only. The mutual exclusivity between fusions and mutations in the same gene is observed across many patients and cancer types, confirming that fusions represent an alternative oncogenic mechanism. In LAML, 14% of tumors have fusions but no driver gene mutations, including *CBFB-MYH11*, *BCR-ABL1*, *PML-RARA*, and *NUP98-NSD1*. The low TMB in fusion-only tumors has direct immunotherapy implications.

### F2: Oncogene fusions upregulate expression; TSG fusions downregulate — 3′-kinase fusions are most likely to drive overexpression via strong partner promoters

**Finding:** Fusions involving oncogenes show outlier overexpression of the oncogene partner (6–28% of kinase fusions across cancer types). Fusions involving tumor suppressor genes show outlier underexpression (3–38% across cancer types). 3′-kinase fusions where the kinase is the 3′ partner show higher expression driven by the 5′ partner's stronger promoter.

**Detail:** For 3′-kinase fusions, 70.5% of cases show higher 5′ partner expression than the kinase, with fusion samples showing elevated kinase expression when the 5′ partner is highly expressed. Example: *TRABD-DDR2* fusion in HNSC where the stronger *TRABD* promoter drives *DDR2* overexpression, suggesting dasatinib sensitivity. *RET* fusions in THCA show overexpression of the 3′ *RET* oncogene. *CBFB-MYH11* fusions in LAML are associated with decreased *CBFB* (TSG) expression. *ERBB2* fusions in BRCA have local partners (*PPP1R1B*, *IKZF3*) and may be by-products of HPV integration-induced genomic instability. THCA has significantly more kinase fusions than any other cancer type (35.6%, Fisher's exact, p<2.2×10⁻¹⁶), mostly 3′-kinase fusions (94.0%).

### F3: TMPRSS2-ERG (38.2% of PRAD) is the most recurrent fusion in any cancer type; cancer-type-specific recurrent fusions define each histotype

**Finding:** *TMPRSS2-ERG* in prostate adenocarcinoma (PRAD) is the most recurrent example in any cancer type (38.2%). Other highly recurrent type-specific fusions: *CCDC6-RET* in THCA (4.2%), *FGFR2-BICC1* in CHOL (5.6%), *FGFR3-TACC3* in BLCA (2.0%), CESC (1.7%), LUSC (1.2%), and GBM. *EML4-ALK* is found in LUAD (1.0%).

**Detail:** Most cancer types have a median of 0–1 fusions per sample. Cancer types with lowest fusion burden: KICH, KIRC, KIRP, LGG, PCPG, TGCT, THCA, THYM, UVM (median = 0). The majority of fusions (majority of breakpoints) occur in coding sequence (CDS) of both partners. More fusions break in 5′ UTRs than 3′ UTRs (possibly due to more open chromatin or alternative promoter usage). 95.5% sensitivity for detection of fusions previously curated in TCGA marker papers.

### F4: 6.0% of cancer patients harbor druggable fusions — potential targets across 29 cancer types; ALK and ESR1 fusions highlight off-label opportunities

**Finding:** 574 of 9,624 samples (6.0%) have potentially druggable fusions based on literature evidence. Major recurrent druggable targets: *TMPRSS2* fusions in PRAD (205 samples), *RET* fusions in THCA (33 samples), *PML-RARA* in LAML (16 samples), *FGFR3* as potential target across 15 cancer types.

**Detail:** In LUAD, druggable fusions are significantly enriched in never-smokers vs ever-smokers: 15/75 (20%) vs 9/425 (2.1%) (chi-square, p<10⁻⁶). *ALK* fusions found in 20 samples across 8 cancer types (5 in LUAD); in 17/20 cases *ALK* is the 3′ partner with *EML4* as most frequent 5′ partner; fusion status corresponds to copy-number-neutral *ALK* overexpression—relevant for clinical ALK testing strategy. *ESR1* fusions in 16 samples across 5 cancer types (9 from BRCA): strict mutual exclusivity with *ESR1* mutations. When *ESR1* is 5′ partner, the AF1 transactivation domain is retained; when 3′ partner, AF2 is retained—functional activity preserved in both orientations. *WNK* kinase family fusions (*WNK1*, *WNK2*) found in 23 cases across multiple cancer types with associated mRNA upregulation.

### F5: Fusion-bearing tumors have low mutation burden — reduced immunotherapy efficacy predicted, but fusion-derived peptides offer neoantigen potential

**Finding:** Patients with driver fusions only show significantly lower mutation burden than patients with driver mutations (Mann-Whitney U, p<2.2×10⁻¹⁶). On average, 1.5 predicted neoantigens per fusion (range 0.33 in KICH to 2.88 in THYM); frameshift fusions generate more predicted immunogenic epitopes than inframe fusions (mean 2.2 vs 1.0).

**Detail:** The low mutation burden in fusion-driven cancers suggests reduced efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors in these patients, despite fusion peptides themselves potentially serving as immunogenic targets. Fusions with most samples having neoantigen candidates: *TMPRSS2-ERG*, *CCDC6-RET*, *FGFR3-TACC3*. However, the fusion product is only immunogenic in a small subset: most *TMPRSS2-ERG* patients do not generate predicted neoantigens. Without evidence of immune cell infiltration and immunogenicity, fusion neoantigen analysis remains exploratory. *TP53* is more commonly disrupted by mutation than fusion across most cancer types, but in SARC both mechanisms occur; in LAML, *CBFB* fusions occur without mutations.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TMPRSS2`, `ERG`, `CCDC6`, `RET`, `FGFR3`, `TACC3`, `TP53`, `CBFB` · Pan-cancer, PRAD, THCA, BLCA, LAML, SARC

## Open Questions

- Do the novel *WNK1/WNK2* kinase fusions identified across multiple cancer types have druggable kinase domain activity, and can WNK inhibitors exploit this?
- Can the 1.8% of tumors with driver fusions as the sole genomic driver be reliably identified through RNA-seq alone in clinical practice, given the lower mutation burden that reduces TMB-based detection?
- Does the strict mutual exclusivity of *ESR1* fusions vs mutations in breast cancer imply a common oncogenic mechanism (AR-pathway analog), and can ligand-independent activation be targeted similarly?
- Among the 15–24% of cancers with no detected fusions or known driver mutations, what proportion harbor fusions missed by RNA-seq (e.g., intronic breakpoints, low-expression fusion partners)?
- Can the fusion neoantigen immunogenicity signal be enhanced in combination with checkpoint inhibitors for the small subset of fusion-bearing patients with evidence of immune infiltration?

## Limitations

- RNA-seq-based fusion detection misses fusions with intronic breakpoints, low-expression gene partners, or those disrupting regulatory elements rather than coding sequence
- 63.3% WGS validation rate leaves ~37% of called fusions potentially artifactual; only 18.2% of fusions could be evaluated by WGS
- Fusion allele frequency not reliably estimable from RNA-seq; clonal evolution of fusions within a tumor cannot be assessed
- Multi-algorithm approach reduces false positives but fusion calling sensitivity/specificity varies by cancer type and tumor purity
- DEPO druggability database (used for druggable fusion assessment) was unpublished at time of study; requires independent validation
- Neoantigen analysis is speculative without clinical immunogenicity data or immune infiltrate measurements
- Normal tissue contamination and tumor purity differences across cancer types affect fusion detection comparability
