## Summary

UC-GENOME (NCT02643043) is a prospective biospecimen and clinical database study in 218 patients with metastatic urothelial carcinoma organized by the Bladder Cancer Advocacy Network (BCAN). This foundational paper presents integrated DNA and RNA sequencing with clinical correlates, characterizing the molecular landscape of metastatic UC. Key contributions: identification of Stroma-rich molecular subtype enrichment in metastatic versus non-metastatic cohorts; discovery that TP53 E285K—an APOBEC-attributable mutation—is preferentially enriched in metastatic disease; demonstration that APOBEC activity (SBS13) predicts treatment outcomes for both chemotherapy and ICI; and derivation of an integrated clinical-genomic model predicting ICI response (AUC = 0.84 in external validation). Despite 69.3% of patients having actionable genomic findings, only 5.0% received targeted therapy, highlighting the gap between genomic discovery and clinical translation.

## Key Findings

### F1: Stroma-rich molecular subtype is enriched in metastatic versus non-metastatic UC cohorts

**Finding:** Applying the Kamoun consensus subtype classification, the Stroma-rich subtype is significantly enriched in metastatic UC cohorts (UC-GENOME and IMvigor210) compared to non-metastatic cohorts (TCGA-BLCA and Kamoun), a difference that persists when restricting analysis to primary tumor samples only and is independent of tissue origin (primary vs. metastatic biopsy site).

**Detail:** The six consensus subtypes (Ba/Sq, LumP, LumU, LumNS, Stroma-rich, NE-like) are all represented in UC-GENOME with prototypical gene expression patterns. Stroma-rich tumors show elevated extracellular matrix, smooth muscle, and EMT gene expression; elevated FTBRS (Fibroblast-TGF-β Response Signature) and EMT_Stroma_core_18—both associated with ICI resistance. Ba/Sq subtype is enriched for females (32% vs. 19.6%, p = 0.08) and squamous differentiation (p < 0.0001). LumP is enriched for FGFR3 mutations (OR = 7.2, p = 6.1×10^−5).

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR3`, `TP53`, `RB1`, `KMT2D` · Metastatic urothelial carcinoma

### F2: TP53 E285K is an APOBEC-attributable hotspot specifically enriched in metastatic UC

**Finding:** TP53 E285K is the most frequent single TP53 variant in UC-GENOME (11%), with higher frequency in metastatic cohorts (UC-GENOME: 10.8%, IMvigor210: 6.9%) vs. primary non-metastatic TCGA-BLCA (5.9%). Across all Pan-TCGA tumors (>10,500), bladder cancer accounts for 39% (15/38) of TP53 E285 variants despite comprising only 5.6% of all TP53 mutations—indicating disease-specific enrichment.

**Detail:** E285K is an APOBEC-attributable hotspot mutation (one of the top 10 APOBEC-associated hotspot mutations). Of the top 10 APOBEC hotspot mutations, only TP53 E285K is enriched in metastatic vs. non-metastatic UC in this dataset, suggesting it may actively promote tumor progression rather than being a neutral passenger of elevated APOBEC activity. Proposed model: elevated APOBEC activity → TP53 E285K mutation → active promotion of metastatic progression. This model is consistent with APOBEC-high tumors generally having longer OS (arguing against E285K merely tagging APOBEC-high tumors), while E285K specifically marks a metastatic-promoting event.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53` (E285K) · Metastatic urothelial carcinoma

### F3: APOBEC activity (SBS13) predicts outcome for both chemotherapy and ICI; micropapillary histology is SBS2-enriched

**Finding:** Low cosine similarity to SBS13 (APOBEC) is associated with significantly decreased survival from the initiation of both chemotherapy (p = 0.013) and ICI (p = 0.011), establishing SBS13 as a predictive biomarker across treatment modalities. The association is treatment-specific, not merely prognostic (OS from diagnosis is not significantly different by SBS2/SBS13 groups). Micropapillary histology is specifically enriched for SBS2 (APOBEC) vs. squamous differentiation (p = 0.002) or other UC (p = 0.018).

**Detail:** Two mutational signature clusters identified by consensus clustering: K1 (high MMR signatures, MMR-deficient) and K2 (high APOBEC signatures SBS2/SBS13, also enriched for high TMB, p = 0.002). Tumors with squamous differentiation show elevated MMR defect signatures (SBS44) vs. micropapillary (p = 2.3×10^−5). High TMB independently associated with ICI clinical benefit (p < 0.05). Overall DNA sequencing demonstrated: TP53 (most frequent), RB1, chromatin modifiers (KMT2D, ARID1A, KDM6A, KMT2C, EP300, SMARCA4), DNA repair genes (BRCA2, PRKDC, ATM, ERCC2, FANCA), and kinase pathway hotspots (FGFR3 S249C, PIK3CA E545K). FGFR3-TACC3 fusions in 4% of evaluable samples.

**Genes / cancer types:** `TP53`, `RB1`, `FGFR3`, `PIK3CA`, `ERCC2`, `ATM`, `BRCA2` · Metastatic urothelial carcinoma

### F4: Ba/Sq and Stroma-rich subtypes have distinct immune microenvironments; immune phenotype predicts ICI benefit

**Finding:** Ba/Sq tumors are T-cell inflamed (elevated IFNG, T-cell signatures; enriched for Inflamed immune phenotype by CD8/Masson's Trichrome histology). Stroma-rich tumors are predominantly Excluded phenotype, with high FTBRS and EMT signatures associated with ICI resistance. The Inflamed phenotype is significantly enriched in Ba/Sq vs. Stroma-rich (p = 0.005), LumP (p = 0.01), and LumNS (p = 0.0009). Patients without ICI benefit are enriched for the Excluded phenotype (p = 0.0126).

**Detail:** Of 155 histologically evaluated tumors: 60% Excluded, 39.4% Inflamed, 0.6% Desert. ICI responders were split ~equally between Excluded and Inflamed (28/27), but ICI non-benefiters were significantly Excluded-enriched. LumP and LumU subtypes are enriched for memory B cells, activated dendritic cells, and plasma cells (CIBERSORTx). ICI clinical benefit positively associated with: TMB (p < 0.05), T-cell inflamed and IFNG IGS scores, B-cell abundance (Bindea B-cells, GO BCR signaling), Tfh cell proportion, and M1 macrophage proportion. FTBRS and EMT_Stroma were not associated with ICI response even within the Excluded subset.

**Genes / cancer types:** immune microenvironment · Metastatic urothelial carcinoma

### F5: Integrated clinical-genomic model outperforms TMB alone for ICI response prediction

**Finding:** An elastic net logistic regression model trained on IMvigor210 clinical and immunogenomic features achieves AUC = 0.84 in IMvigor210 validation, AUC = 0.82 in UNC-108 (independent cohort), and AUC = 0.65 (p = 0.009) in UC-GENOME—significantly outperforming TMB alone (AUC = 0.84 vs. 0.68, p = 0.038). The model is ICI-specific: not predictive of chemotherapy response (AUC = 0.536, p = 0.345).

**Detail:** Features favoring better ICI response in the final 25-predictor model: ECOG PS 0, high TMB, M1 macrophage signature. Features favoring worse ICI response: B-cell gene signature, Stroma-rich consensus subtype, claudin signature. ERCC2 mutations are validated in the metastatic setting as predicting higher chemotherapy response rates (consistent with prior neoadjuvant data) but are not associated with ICI response or improved OS on chemotherapy. Mutations in RB1, ATM, or FANCC (but not ERCC2) are associated with improved survival for ICI-treated patients, and with increased ICI clinical benefit (though not response rate), providing a potential DNA damage repair-based biomarker for ICI.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ERCC2`, `RB1`, `ATM`, `FANCC` · Metastatic urothelial carcinoma

## Open Questions

- Is the TP53 E285K mutation a bona fide driver of metastatic progression in UC, or is it a high-frequency APOBEC-attributable hotspot mutation that co-occurs with metastatic disease due to elevated APOBEC activity?
- Can the integrated elastic net ICI response predictor be simplified to a clinically deployable score combining TMB, ECOG, and Stroma-rich subtype classification?
- Why does FTBRS/EMT_Stroma fail to predict ICI resistance in the Excluded subset of UC-GENOME despite its predictive power in other datasets?
- What explains the large gap between actionable genomic findings (69.3%) and actual targeted therapy use (5.0%)—is it trial availability, physician awareness, or patient fitness?
- Does RB1/ATM/FANCC mutation status (associated with ICI benefit) predict response to ADC therapies (enfortumab vedotin, sacituzumab govitecan)?

## Limitations

- Sequencing primarily from primary tumors (87%), despite this being a metastatic disease cohort; may not capture metastasis-specific alterations
- Only 5% of patients received targeted therapy based on NGS—limits assessment of genomic biomarker utility in actual clinical decisions
- Elastic net model performs substantially better in IMvigor210/UNC-108 than in UC-GENOME (AUC 0.84/0.82 vs. 0.65), possibly due to differences in assays, antibodies used for ICI, or cohort composition
- RNA sequencing success in 176/218 patients; incomplete profiling reduces power for subtype-outcome analyses
- Histologic immune phenotype classified in 155/218 patients; pathologist-defined classification has inherent subjectivity
- Relatively short median follow-up (21.6 months) for a metastatic cohort with multiple lines of therapy
- No multi-region sequencing; tumor heterogeneity within patients not captured
