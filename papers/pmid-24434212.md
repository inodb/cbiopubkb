## Summary

Lohr et al. performed massively parallel sequencing (WES and WGS) on 203 matched tumor/normal multiple myeloma (MM) patient samples — the largest MM genome sequencing study at the time — to define significantly mutated genes, copy number alterations, and the extent of intra-tumor clonal heterogeneity. Eleven genes reached statistical significance (q < 0.1) using MutSigCV: *KRAS*, *NRAS*, *BRAF*, *FAM46C*, *TP53*, *DIS3*, *TRAF3*, *CYLD*, *RB1*, and *PRDM1*. The central and most striking finding is that nearly all MM tumors harbor extensive clonal heterogeneity — a median of ≥5 detectable subclones per tumor — with many driver mutations present in only a fraction of cells. This has immediate therapeutic implications: targeting a subclonal *BRAF* mutation with BRAF inhibitors was shown *in vitro* to paradoxically stimulate growth of *KRAS*- or *NRAS*-mutant non-targeted subclones, underscoring the requirement for clonality assessment prior to deploying targeted therapies.

## Key Findings

### F1: Eleven significantly mutated genes define the recurrent genetic landscape of MM in 203 patients

**Finding:** Using MutSigCV applied to 203 MM genomes, 11 genes reach q < 0.1: *KRAS* (~24%), *NRAS* (~20%), *FAM46C* (~11%), *DIS3* (~11%), *BRAF* (~9%), *TP53* (~8%), *TRAF3*, *CYLD*, *RB1*, *PRDM1*, and *CDKN1B* (via gene set). Five were previously identified in the smaller Chapman et al. pilot (38 patients); six (*BRAF*, *TRAF3*, *CYLD*, *RB1*, *PRDM1*, and pathway-level candidates) are confirmed or newly refined. *PRDM1* is established as a novel MM tumor suppressor through recurrent S552C and clustered missense/truncating mutations. *IRF4* harbors a recurrent hotspot (K123R) in 3 patients. 76% of all 203 patients carry at least one genetic event in a significantly mutated gene or pathway.

**Detail:** Mutation validation performed on 140 mutations (90.4% rate). A three-gene-set analysis (NF-κB pathway, histone-modifying enzymes, coagulation cascade) from the pilot study all retain significance (p < 0.05) in the full cohort. Six of 612 MSigDB gene sets also reach significance after multiple-testing correction, highlighting cell-cycle machinery (CDKN1B, CCND1) and MYC pathway components (MAX). GISTIC analysis of 153 CN-profiled patients identified 7 significant homozygous deletion regions encompassing CDKN2C, TRAF3, BIRC2, BIRC3, CYLD, PTPRD, and a novel 8p23.1 peak (BLK, MSRA, PINX1, SOX7). DIS3 mutations frequently co-occur with LOH (11% of patients), defining it as a two-hit tumor suppressor.

### F2: Nearly all MM tumors harbor extensive intra-tumor clonal heterogeneity — on average ≥5 subclones per tumor

**Finding:** Using the ABSOLUTE algorithm to estimate cancer cell fraction (CCF) for each somatic mutation, followed by Bayesian clustering of CCF distributions, the authors find that nearly all of the 153 MM samples with tumor purity > 0.7 show evidence of multiple clonal subpopulations. Most harbor at least 3 detectable subclones beyond the major clone; some harbor up to 7. MM is significantly more clonally heterogeneous than ovarian cancer sequenced to comparable depth and purity (8% of MM vs. 19% of ovarian cancer had ≤1 subclone; Fisher's exact p = 0.0024).

**Detail:** The ABSOLUTE algorithm models wild-type and mutant allele counts while accounting for local somatic copy number and sample purity (requiring SNP array or sequencing-derived copy number). The minimum detectable subclone is estimated at ~10% of the overall tumor — meaning the true number of subclones is likely substantially underestimated. The result implies that any single bone marrow biopsy will not capture the full breadth of clonal diversity, with additional subclones potentially residing in non-sampled skeletal sites. This finding extends earlier smaller WES/array CGH MM clonality studies to a population-level observation.

### F3: KRAS/NRAS/BRAF mutations are frequently subclonal and co-exist in independent subclones within the same tumor

**Finding:** Of 44 patients with coding *KRAS* mutations (purity > 0.7), 32 (73%) had clonal *KRAS* mutations, while 12 (27%) were subclonal (present in as few as 13% of cells). In a subset of patients, mutations in two of three MAP kinase pathway oncogenes (*KRAS*, *NRAS*, *BRAF*) co-occur in the same tumor — a seemingly paradoxical finding since these mutations activate the same pathway. Bayesian CCF clustering shows these co-occurring mutations are almost never simultaneously clonal (1 sample); instead, they occupy distinct subclones (9 samples), indicating parallel evolution of independent subclonal populations harboring non-redundant pathway activations.

**Detail:** The co-existence of KRAS, NRAS, and BRAF mutations in the same tumor (rather than being mutually exclusive as expected by pathway-level selection) indicates that subclonal populations can simultaneously evolve independent MAPK activation mechanisms in MM. In contrast, *DIS3* and *KRAS* mutations were often simultaneously clonal, pointing to cooperating rather than redundant functions. Treatment appears to accelerate fixation: significantly recurrent mutations are more often clonal in previously treated vs. untreated patients (p = 0.007, Wilcoxon rank sum), consistent with chemotherapy selecting for more fit subclones.

### F4: DIS3 mutations are enriched in non-hyperdiploid MM and act as a tumor suppressor via LOH

**Finding:** *DIS3*, encoding a ribonuclease involved in RNA processing (exosome complex), is mutated in ~11% of patients. Critically, DIS3 mutations are significantly more common in non-hyperdiploid MM (Fisher's exact p = 0.00013 vs. hyperdiploid). Loss of heterozygosity (LOH) was confirmed by ABSOLUTE in a substantial fraction of DIS3-mutated cases, establishing a classic two-hit tumor suppressor mechanism. Non-hyperdiploid MM also shows enrichment for TP53 mutations and a distinct biological profile, suggesting it may represent a molecularly distinct MM subtype requiring different therapeutic strategies.

**Detail:** The authors developed and validated a WES/WGS-based method for classifying MM as hyperdiploid vs. non-hyperdiploid (replacing FISH-based classification). Of 203 patients: 116 hyperdiploid, 86 non-hyperdiploid. DIS3 mutation with LOH occurs in 11% of all MM but is concentrated in the non-hyperdiploid subset. Why DIS3 loss-of-function mutations are so specific to MM (and non-hyperdiploid MM in particular) while being rare in other cancers remains mechanistically unexplained. FAM46C (encoding an atypical poly(A) polymerase) shares this RNA-biology theme and is also enriched in MM — collectively, RNA-binding protein mutations (DIS3 + FAM46C) occur in ~21% of all patients.

### F5: BRAF inhibitor treatment paradoxically stimulates KRAS/NRAS-mutant MM subclones — combined BRAF+MEK inhibition partially mitigates this

**Finding:** *In vitro* experiments demonstrate that the BRAF inhibitor PLX4720 (and dabrafenib) selectively kills BRAF-mutant U266 cells (BRAF K601N) while paradoxically stimulating MAP kinase pathway activity and proliferation in *KRAS*- or *NRAS*-mutant MM cell lines (MM1S: KRAS G12A; SKMM1: KRAS G12D; OPM2: NRAS/KRAS WT, but FGFR3 K650E). This paradoxical activation mirrors the known mechanism in melanoma (Poulikakos et al. 2010). Combined treatment with MEK inhibitor trametinib partially abrogates the paradoxical activation in BRAF-WT cells and enhances killing of BRAF-mutant cells, supporting combination BRAF+MEK inhibition specifically in patients with clonal BRAF mutations.

**Detail:** Western blot analysis shows PLX4720 suppresses pMEK and pERK in BRAF-mutant U266 cells but elevates them in BRAF-WT cell lines. Cell-counting flow cytometry after 5 days of dabrafenib shows dose-dependent growth inhibition in U266 vs. dose-dependent growth stimulation in KRAS/NRAS-mutant lines. The trametinib combination experiment in a 384-well proliferation assay shows synergy (increased killing) in BRAF-mutant cells but not in BRAF-WT lines. The clinical implication is stark: patients with subclonal BRAF mutations and co-existing KRAS/NRAS-mutant subclones could experience net tumor stimulation from BRAF monotherapy. This provided early evidence supporting combination BRAF+MEK as the strategy of choice in BRAF-mutant MM, and emphasized the need for clonality assessment before therapy.

### F6: Seven significant homozygous deletion regions identify known and novel MM tumor suppressor loci

**Finding:** GISTIC analysis of 153 copy-number profiled MM patients identifies 7 statistically significant regions of recurrent homozygous deletion. Known tumor suppressors confirmed include *CDKN2C*, NF-κB regulators *TRAF3*, *BIRC2*/*BIRC3*, and *CYLD*. Novel findings include homozygous deletions of *PTPRD* (tyrosine phosphatase that dephosphorylates STAT3, thereby suppressing IL-6 signaling — a known MM survival factor) and a peak at 8p23.1 containing candidate TSGs *BLK*, *MSRA*, *PINX1*, and *SOX7*, none previously linked to MM. *CYLD* is inactivated by both deletion and somatic mutation (5 patients), exemplifying convergent biallelic inactivation. 21 of 139 patients with high-density CN array and ABSOLUTE data harbor homozygous deletions in significant peaks.

**Detail:** Homozygous deletions were confirmed using ABSOLUTE to distinguish true homozygous events from LOH with remaining copy in diploid background. The NF-κB regulatory genes (TRAF3, BIRC2/3, CYLD) collectively define a major axis of MM pathogenesis — their deletion activates the alternative NF-κB pathway. PTPRD deletion is particularly interesting mechanistically: PTPRD loss would be expected to result in constitutive STAT3 phosphorylation, mimicking chronic IL-6 stimulation. The 8p23.1 candidates (BLK, MSRA, PINX1, SOX7) require functional validation to confirm tumor suppressor roles in MM.

## Open Questions

- Does the extent of clonal heterogeneity at diagnosis predict progression-free survival or treatment response in MM, and can routine single-biopsy WES provide reliable heterogeneity estimates for clinical decision-making?
- Can liquid biopsy (cell-free DNA) overcome the spatial sampling limitation of single bone marrow biopsies to more completely capture the clonal landscape of MM?
- Why are *DIS3* and *FAM46C* mutations so enriched in MM compared to other cancers — what MM-specific biological context makes RNA-processing gene loss oncogenic?
- Does combined BRAF+MEK inhibition provide clinical benefit in patients with clonal *BRAF* V600E or non-V600E mutations, and what is the response rate compared to BRAF monotherapy?
- Can pre-treatment clonality mapping (CCF of BRAF/KRAS/NRAS mutations) predict differential responses to MAPK pathway inhibitors?
- Do *PTPRD* deletions functionally suppress STAT3 in MM cells, and do they confer sensitivity to JAK/STAT inhibitors (e.g., ruxolitinib)?
- What is the functional significance of *PRDM1* mutations in MM — do they phenocopy PRDM1 deletion in promoting plasmacytic proliferation or suppressing differentiation?
- Does chemotherapy (e.g., bortezomib, lenalidomide) systematically select for specific subclonal genotypes, and can pre-treatment sequencing predict post-treatment clonal composition?

## Limitations

- Single-biopsy design per patient — the spatial heterogeneity of MM across skeletal sites is not captured; additional subclones in non-sampled locations are undetectable
- Minimum detectable subclone is ~10% of the tumor sample at 89× WES depth, meaning many small subclones are invisible
- WGS subset (n=26) at only ~30× depth provides insufficient sensitivity for subclone detection compared to the WES cohort
- Clinical annotation incomplete for a substantial fraction of patients (treatment history, cytogenetics, survival outcomes) — limits power to detect gene-outcome associations
- *In vitro* BRAF inhibitor paradox experiments performed in MM cell lines, not in patient-derived samples or xenograft models — clinical translatability unproven at time of publication
- ABSOLUTE-based CCF estimation requires high tumor purity (>0.7 threshold used); 50 patients excluded from clonality analysis on this basis, possibly biasing toward less heterogeneous samples
- No RNA-seq: transcriptional consequences of mutations, splice variants, and fusion transcripts are not characterized
- No matched relapse/progression samples for most patients — clonal dynamics under treatment cannot be directly measured in this cohort
- Study design predates immunotherapy and modern IMiD/proteasome inhibitor combination regimens; therapeutic implications may need re-evaluation in contemporary treatment contexts
