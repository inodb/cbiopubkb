## Summary

This prospective single-institution clinical trial enrolled 84 patients with AML or MDS at Washington University in St. Louis, plus 32 patients in an extension cohort, to characterize molecular determinants of response to 10-day courses of decitabine (20 mg/m²/day). Serial enhanced exome or gene-panel sequencing was performed in 67 patients (discovery cohort) and 54 patients underwent serial multi-timepoint sequencing. The pivotal finding was that *TP53* mutations predicted 100% bone marrow blast clearance with decitabine (21/21 patients, p<0.001) — a dramatic reversal of the dismal response rate to standard anthracycline-cytarabine chemotherapy in this same population — while overall survival remained similar regardless of *TP53* mutation status (median 12.7 vs. 15.4 months, p=0.79). The study also showed that mutation clearance was robust but never complete, with relapse driven by residual or pre-existing resistant subclones.

## Key Findings

### F1: TP53 mutations predict 100% bone marrow blast clearance with decitabine — a complete reversal of chemotherapy outcomes

**Finding:** *TP53* mutations in the founding clone at presentation were the strongest predictor of response to decitabine: 21/21 (100%) *TP53*-mutant patients achieved bone marrow blast clearance (<5% blasts) vs. 32/78 (41%) wild-type *TP53* patients (p<0.001). This contrasted sharply with standard anthracycline-cytarabine chemotherapy where *TP53* mutations predict only 20–30% initial response rates.

**Detail:** *TP53* mutations were identified in 21 of 99 sequenced patients (21%) and were almost exclusively found in patients with unfavorable-risk cytogenetics (20/21 cases). The dramatic 100% response rate in *TP53*-mutant patients to decitabine, compared to ~20-30% with standard chemotherapy, suggests that the poor prognosis associated with *TP53* mutations may be treatment-specific rather than reflecting intrinsic disease biology. Notably, only *TP53* and *SF3B1* showed consistent, rapid reductions in variant allele frequency to <5% across serial samples. The spectrum of *TP53* mutations was similar to that seen in other AML studies including TCGA. This finding established decitabine as a preferred induction therapy for *TP53*-mutant AML/MDS.

### F2: Unfavorable-risk cytogenetics predict superior decitabine response; 67% blast clearance vs. 34% in intermediate/favorable risk

**Finding:** Patients with unfavorable-risk cytogenetics had significantly higher bone marrow blast clearance rates with decitabine than those with intermediate or favorable risk (29/43, 67% vs. 24/71, 34%; p<0.001). Despite this higher response rate, overall survival was similar between cytogenetic risk groups (median 11.6 months unfavorable vs. 10 months favorable/intermediate; p=0.29).

**Detail:** Unfavorable karyotypes were defined as ≥3 abnormalities, deletions of chromosomes 5, 7, or 17, or chromosome 11 *MLL* rearrangements. 20 of 21 *TP53*-mutant patients had unfavorable karyotypes, suggesting the high response rate in this group is partially mediated by *TP53* mutation-driven sensitivity to decitabine. The equipoise in overall survival between cytogenetic risk groups was unexpected and contrasts with standard chemotherapy data where unfavorable-risk karyotype is associated with markedly shorter survival. This suggests that decitabine mitigates the survival penalty typically conferred by unfavorable cytogenetics, at least temporarily.

### F3: Mutation clearance with decitabine is robust but incomplete; relapse driven by pre-existing resistant subclones

**Finding:** Serial exome sequencing of 54 patients at multiple timepoints revealed consistent mutation clearance in all evaluated *TP53*-mutant patients, but clearance was never complete: average founding clone VAF at maximum clearance was 0.06–18.43%. All relapses were associated with outgrowth of one or more pre-existing subclones detectable before therapy.

**Detail:** Bone marrow blast clearance frequently preceded mutation reduction (15/54 patients), suggesting decitabine induces leukemic differentiation before eliminating cells — a differentiation response requiring months of therapy. The inability to achieve complete molecular remission is the mechanistic basis for the short duration of responses (typically <1 year). Differential subclonal sensitivity was observed: sensitive subclones sometimes existed within a largely resistant founding clone, and resistant subclones pre-existing at baseline expanded at relapse. "Rising clones" with clonal hematopoiesis-associated mutations (DNMT3A, PPM1D) were observed in 7/22 responders, mirroring findings with standard induction therapy.

### F4: Decitabine causes equivalent global DNA hypomethylation in responders and non-responders, ruling out methylation as a response biomarker

**Finding:** Among patients with bone marrow biopsies analyzed by Illumina 450K methylation arrays, the reduction in methylcytosine content from day 0 to day 10 of cycle 1 was similar in responders and non-responders (p=0.19), and similar in *TP53*-mutant and wild-type patients. Steady-state plasma decitabine levels also did not correlate with response (p=0.09).

**Detail:** These negative findings rule out two hypothesized mechanisms of response selectivity: (1) differential drug delivery/metabolism (plasma levels equivalent between response groups) and (2) differential methylation sensitivity (equal hypomethylation across response categories). No canonical methylation signature driven by *TP53* mutations could be identified, suggesting *TP53*-mutant cells are not epigenetically primed for decitabine response through a methylation mechanism. The mechanisms underlying *TP53* mutation-specific decitabine sensitivity remain unclear and are a major open question in the field, with subsequent studies proposing roles for immune activation, p53-independent apoptosis, and differentiation induction.

### F5: Allogeneic stem cell transplantation had the greatest impact on overall survival, independent of TP53 status

**Finding:** Among all factors analyzed, consolidation with allogeneic stem-cell transplantation (allo-SCT) had the greatest effect on overall survival (Cox proportional-hazards model with stepwise regression; p<0.001). Importantly, transplantation outcomes were not adversely affected by *TP53* mutation status, with patients achieving decitabine responses remaining eligible for potentially curative transplantation.

**Detail:** This finding has immediate clinical implications: decitabine can serve as an effective bridge to transplant in *TP53*-mutant patients who would otherwise be ineligible due to refractory disease with standard chemotherapy. The ability of decitabine to achieve 100% blast clearance in *TP53*-mutant patients creates a window for transplantation that is unavailable with standard induction regimens. The median survival among the 12.7 months for *TP53*-mutant patients is substantially higher than the 4–6 months historically reported with standard chemotherapy for this population. This observation directly motivated subsequent studies of hypomethylating agents + venetoclax in *TP53*-mutant AML.

## Open Questions

- What is the molecular mechanism by which *TP53* mutations confer hypersensitivity to decitabine rather than resistance?
- Can complete molecular remission be achieved by combining decitabine with other agents (e.g., venetoclax) in *TP53*-mutant AML/MDS?
- Do *TP53*-mutant patients benefit equally from azacitidine, or is there decitabine-specific efficacy?
- Can pre-existing subclonal architecture at baseline predict which patients will relapse rapidly vs. achieve durable responses?
- What is the long-term benefit of allo-SCT consolidation after decitabine bridging in *TP53*-mutant AML?
- Does the observed "rising clonal hematopoiesis" during decitabine-induced remission affect long-term outcomes after transplant?

## Limitations

- Single-institution, prospective but uncontrolled trial without a comparator arm; survival data are not adjusted for treatment selection bias
- Small *TP53*-mutant subgroup (n=21) limits statistical power for subgroup analyses
- Serial exome sequencing performed in only the discovery cohort (n=39/54 evaluated); extension cohort had only gene-panel sequencing
- Follow-up was limited (median 19 months in survivors); long-term survival data not available
- Eligibility criteria (age ≥60 for de novo AML, relapsed AML, transfusion-dependent MDS) limit generalizability to younger patients
- Mechanism of *TP53* mutation-specific decitabine sensitivity not resolved; multiple mechanisms remain plausible
- Clonal hematopoiesis in remission samples may confound mutation clearance interpretations
