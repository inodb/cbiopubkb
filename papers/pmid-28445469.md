## Summary

The TRACERx (TRAcking Cancer Evolution through therapy (Rx)) study's first ctDNA report applies a tumor-specific phylogenetic approach to circulating tumor DNA profiling in the first 100 enrolled early-stage NSCLC patients. Using multi-region exome sequencing (M-Seq)–derived phylogenetic trees to design bespoke patient-specific multiplex-PCR NGS assay panels targeting both clonal and subclonal SNVs, the study: (1) establishes clinical predictors of ctDNA detectability (histology, lymphovascular invasion, Ki67, tumor volume); (2) demonstrates that tumor volume quantitatively predicts clonal plasma VAF; (3) shows that post-operative ctDNA detects impending relapse with a median 70-day lead-time over clinical imaging; (4) identifies adjuvant chemotherapy resistance in real-time; and (5) validates that ctDNA tracks the specific subclone(s) seeding metastatic relapse using phylogenetic characterization — with actionable findings (ERBB2 amplification, KRAS amplification) identified in relapsing subclones.

## Key Findings

### F1: ctDNA is detected pre-operatively in 48% of early-stage NSCLCs; LUSC (97%) vs LUAD (19%) — with non-adenocarcinoma histology, lymphovascular invasion, and high Ki67 as independent predictors

**Finding:** In 96 evaluable early-stage NSCLC patients, ctDNA was detected (≥2 SNVs) pre-operatively in 46/96 (48%). Detection rates are dramatically histology-dependent: 97% (30/31) of lung squamous cell carcinomas (LUSC) vs. only 19% (11/58) of lung adenocarcinomas (LUAD). Even stage I LUSC had 94% detection (16/17) vs. 13% (5/39) for stage I LUAD. Multivariable analysis identified three independent predictors: non-adenocarcinoma histology, lymphovascular invasion, and high Ki67 proliferation index. PET FDG-avidity also predicted ctDNA detection (AUC = 0.84, *P* < 0.001, n=92). KRAS, EGFR, or TP53 driver status in LUADs was not associated with ctDNA detection.

**Detail:** The histology difference reflects LUSC's higher necrosis rates vs. LUAD — necrosis is hypothesized to drive passive ctDNA release. ctDNA-positive LUADs formed a subgroup of more necrotic tumors compared to ctDNA-negative LUADs. Additional univariable predictors: total cell-free DNA input, pathological tumor size, Ki67 index, lymph node involvement, lymphovascular invasion. Clonal SNVs were detected in all 46 ctDNA-positive patients (median 94% of targeted clonal SNVs detected; range 11–100%). Subclonal SNVs were detected in 27/40 (68%) ctDNA-positive patients with subclonal SNVs targeted, with a median of 27% of targeted subclonal SNVs detected — significantly less than clonal SNVs, consistent with lower cancer cell fraction.

**Genes / cancer types:** `KRAS`, `EGFR`, `TP53` · NSCLC (LUAD, LUSC)

### F2: Tumor volume quantitatively predicts clonal plasma ctDNA VAF — 10 cm³ predicts ~0.1% VAF, corresponding to ~302 million malignant cells at the detection limit

**Finding:** Among 37 ctDNA-positive patients with CT volumetric data, tumor volume shows a strong log-linear correlation with mean clonal SNV plasma VAF (Spearman's ρ = 0.63, *P* < 0.001). Linear modelling predicts that 10 cm³ tumor volume yields ~0.1% mean clonal VAF (95% CI 0.06–0.18%). Based on a density of 9.4×10⁷ cells/cm³ pure tumor, a 0.1% VAF corresponds to ~302 million cancer cells. This quantitative relationship defines the fundamental detection limit of ctDNA-based early detection for NSCLC and shows that CT-detectable 4mm nodules (~0.034 cm³) would have predicted VAF of ~1.8×10⁻⁴% — at or beyond current technology limits.

**Detail:** Both pathologic tumor size and CT volumetric tumor volume correlate with mean clonal VAF. This relationship is consistent with a prior NSCLC dataset (the line of best fit was concordant). Tumor purity was multiplied by volume to estimate cancer cell volume for VAF prediction. For subclonal SNV detection, effective subclone size (mean CCF × cancer cell volume) is the relevant metric: detected subclonal SNVs mapped to subclones with median estimated size of 4.06 cm³ vs. 1.70 cm³ for undetected (Wilcoxon *P* < 0.001). Shared subclones (present in >1 M-Seq region) were detected in 61% of ctDNA-positive patients vs. 33% for private subclones — confirming that subclone volume, not just mutation type, drives ctDNA detection.

### F3: Post-operative ctDNA detects impending NSCLC relapse in 93% of patients with a median 70-day lead-time over clinical imaging; 4 patients had >6-month lead-times

**Finding:** In a 24-patient longitudinal sub-cohort (10 relapse-free, 14 with confirmed NSCLC relapse), blinded ctDNA profiling detected relapse in 13/14 (93%) confirmed relapse patients prior to or at clinical recurrence. The median lead-time (ctDNA positivity to CT-confirmed relapse) was 70 days (range 10–346 days). Four of 13 cases had lead-times >6 months. In 3 cases (CRUK0080, CRUK0004, CRUK0062), ctDNA was detectable within 30 days of surgery and increased despite adjuvant chemotherapy — demonstrating adjuvant resistance. One patient (CRUK0013) had ctDNA clear post-adjuvant and remains relapse-free at 688 days.

**Detail:** At least 2 SNVs were detected in only 1/10 (10%) relapse-free patients (CRUK0013, with eventual ctDNA clearance). ctDNA detected intracerebral relapse 54 days before clinical CT detection (CRUK0029). Adjuvant chemotherapy resistance identification: 3 patients had increasing SNV counts post-surgery despite platinum chemotherapy, all relapsing within 1 year. Adjuvant response: CRUK0013 — 20 SNVs at 72h post-op, 13 SNVs pre-adjuvant, then undetectable post-adjuvant through day 667. This demonstrates ctDNA's value for monitoring adjuvant treatment response in real-time, which could enable early therapy switching in non-responders.

**Genes / cancer types:** `ERBB2`, `KRAS`, `ARID1A` · NSCLC (LUAD, LUSC)

### F4: Phylogenetic ctDNA tracking identifies the specific subclone seeding relapse — including actionable targets (ERBB2 amplification, KRAS amplification) in relapsing subclones

**Finding:** By mapping post-operative ctDNA SNVs back to M-Seq derived phylogenetic trees, the study identifies which phylogenetic branch and subclone seeds metastatic relapse in individual patients. In CRUK0004, the relapsing subclone contains an ERBB2 (HER2) amplification (>15 copies, triploid background) — a targetable event in NSCLC. In CRUK0013, a relapsing subclone identified at lymph nodes contains a KRAS amplification (>15 copies, triploid background). Phylogenetic characterization at post-mortem (CRUK0063, via PEACE study) shows that 6 of 7 metastatic sites descended from a common ancestral subclone (phylogenetic cluster 8 → cluster 12).

**Detail:** CRUK0063 post-mortem integration: M-Seq from 5 primary regions (day 0), 1 relapse biopsy (day 467), and 6 post-mortem metastatic sites revealed that all 7 metastatic regions arose from single ancestral subclone (cluster 8); 6 shared a later branching (cluster 12); para-aortic metastasis had a private subclone (cluster 9). Retrospective 103-SNV ctDNA panel tracked metastatic subclonal burden longitudinally: clonal SNVs detected at day 151 (189 days before the 19-SNV panel detected them), subclonal signal from cluster 8 at day 242. At day 466 (post-relapse), 18/20 SNVs from clusters 8, 11, 12 were detected — concordant with the 6 metastatic sites sharing these clusters. Mean clonal VAF responded to palliative radiotherapy and chemotherapy, then increased at day 767 with para-aortic progression (not seen on CT 112 days prior).

**Genes / cancer types:** `ERBB2`, `KRAS`, `ARID1A`, `OR5D18`, `OR10K1` · NSCLC

### F5: Clonal mutations are more sensitive ctDNA markers than subclonal mutations — median 94% detection rate for clonal vs. 27% for subclonal SNVs — with direct implications for assay design

**Finding:** Among ctDNA-positive patients, clonal SNVs targeted by assay panels had a median detection rate of 94% (range 11–100%) in plasma. Subclonal SNVs had a median detection rate of only 27% (range 0–91%), significantly lower than clonal SNVs (within-patient comparison, Wilcoxon signed-rank, *P* < 0.001). Mean plasma VAF of clonal SNVs was higher than subclonal SNVs. These results establish clonal alterations as the superior basis for ctDNA detection, while subclonal SNV detection provides complementary phylogenetic characterization of which tumor branch is expanding.

**Detail:** 40 of 46 ctDNA-positive patients had subclonal SNVs targeted by assay panels; 27 (68%) of these had at least one subclonal SNV detected. The median 94% detection of targeted clonal SNVs demonstrates high assay sensitivity for clonal signal above the 0.1% VAF threshold. The low median 27% for subclonal SNVs reflects genuine biology (smaller subclone volume, lower CCF) rather than assay failure. This finding informs clinical assay design: clonal-SNV-first panels maximize sensitivity for residual disease detection; including subclonal SNVs adds phylogenetic resolution at the cost of individual detection sensitivity. For monitoring purposes, tracking the dominant (clonal) signal is most reliable.

**Genes / cancer types:** Pan-cancer principle; quantified in NSCLC

**Data availability:** `partial`
> Clonal vs. subclonal SNV designation (from M-Seq phylogenetic analysis) is in the TRACERx supplementary data and dataset. The nsclc_tracerx_2017 study in cBioPortal contains multi-region mutation data from which clonality can be inferred, though not directly annotated in the standard cBioPortal mutation table.

## Open Questions

1. Can the 70-day ctDNA relapse lead-time translate into a clinical benefit from earlier salvage therapy — and can NCT01888601 extension cohorts demonstrate this in a randomized setting?
2. What is the minimum ctDNA-detectable tumor volume for clinically actionable early detection of lung cancer at screening (LDCT-identified nodules, ~0.034 cm³)?
3. Can clonal SNV-based ctDNA platforms (targeting tumor-specific somatic SNVs) be replaced or supplemented by tumor-naïve methylation or fragmentomics approaches for patients where M-Seq tumor phylogenetics is unavailable?
4. Do the ERBB2-amplified or KRAS-amplified relapsing subclones identified in ctDNA predict response to HER2-directed or KRAS G12C inhibitor therapies?
5. How does the 5% adjuvant chemotherapy benefit in stage I-II NSCLC change when restricted to ctDNA-positive post-operative patients (i.e., those with confirmed residual disease)?

## Limitations

- Bespoke patient-specific multiplex-PCR assay panels require prior tumor tissue sequencing and panel synthesis (~$1,750 per patient for 5 plasma samples); not scalable to broad population screening without cost reduction.
- Platform sensitivity is >99% at VAF ≥0.1%, but tumor volumes below ~10 cm³ predict VAFs below the platform's practical detection limit — relevant for stage I adenocarcinomas.
- Low ctDNA detection rate in LUAD (19% pre-operatively) limits clinical utility for the most common NSCLC subtype at early stages; LUSC is well-detected but is less common.
- Only 24 patients had longitudinal post-operative profiling; power to detect statistically significant outcome differences by ctDNA status is limited.
- Phylogenetic characterization requires multi-region tumor tissue sequencing (standard of care only in clinical trials), limiting immediate clinical translation.
- ctDNA negativity post-operatively does not rule out relapse (1 false-negative relapse case in 14); sensitivity is not 100%.
