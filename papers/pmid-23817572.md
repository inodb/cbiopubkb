## Summary

This 2013 Nature Genetics study — part of the ICGC PedBrain Tumor Project — performed whole-genome sequencing of 96 pilocytic astrocytomas (matched tumor-blood DNA) with RNA-seq in 73 cases and mate-pair sequencing in 68. Pilocytic astrocytoma, the most common childhood brain tumor, had an extremely low somatic mutation rate (<0.1/Mb, mean 1.6 non-synonymous SNVs per tumor). Strikingly, every tumor in the cohort harbored a MAPK pathway alteration — validating pilocytic astrocytoma as a prototypic single-pathway disease. The dominant event was *KIAA1549:BRAF* fusion (70 cases), predominantly in cerebellar tumors. Novel findings included: recurrent activating *FGFR1* point mutations (N546K/K656E) and internal tandem duplication (ITD) in non-cerebellar tumors; *QKI:NTRK2* and *NACC2:NTRK2* fusions in 3 samples; novel *BRAF* fusions (*RNF130:BRAF*, *CLCN6:BRAF*, *MKRN1:BRAF*, *GNAI1:BRAF*); and *PTPN11* (SHP-2) hotspot mutations in *FGFR1*-mutant tumors. Additionally, the same *FGFR1* mutations were identified in a subset of *H3F3A*-mutated pediatric GBM (with *NF1* loss), linking pilocytic astrocytoma biology to a molecularly distinct GBM subset. This study is the primary data source for the `past_dkfz_heidelberg_2013` cBioPortal study.

## Key Findings

### F1: 100% of pilocytic astrocytomas harbor a MAPK pathway alteration — a true single-pathway disease

**Finding:** All 96 pilocytic astrocytomas harbored at least one MAPK pathway alteration, with no other significantly mutated genes identified by Genome MuSiC analysis. Only *BRAF*, *FGFR1*, *KRAS*, and *NF1* achieved statistical significance. Mutual exclusivity of most alterations (P < 0.0001) supports a model where a single oncogenic hit is sufficient for tumorigenesis.

**Detail:** MAPK pathway events were distributed across: *KIAA1549:BRAF* fusion (70/96; 73%), novel *BRAF* fusions (4 cases), *BRAF* V600E (4 cases), *BRAF* ins599T (1 case), *FGFR1* mutations/ITD (6 cases in WGS cohort; 9 additional in replication cohort), *NTRK2* fusions (3 cases), *KRAS* mutations (2 cases), and *NF1* germline + somatic second hit (3 cases). With the exception of *FGFR1* + *PTPN11* co-occurrence, each tumor typically harbored only one pathway alteration. The somatic mutation rate was markedly lower than medulloblastoma or other pediatric tumors, consistent with the benign clinical behavior and low proliferative index of most pilocytic astrocytomas.

**Genes / cancer types:** `BRAF`, `KIAA1549`, `FGFR1`, `NTRK2`, `KRAS`, `NF1`, `PTPN11` · pilocytic astrocytoma (PA)

### F2: Novel recurrent FGFR1 mutations (N546K and K656E) identify a therapeutically targetable subset in non-cerebellar tumors

**Finding:** Hotspot mutations in *FGFR1* at N546 (asparagine-to-lysine) and K656 (lysine-to-glutamate) were identified in 5 tumors in the WGS cohort and confirmed in 9 additional non-cerebellar cases in an independent validation cohort. A further case harbored an *FGFR1* kinase domain internal tandem duplication (ITD). All *FGFR1*-mutant tumors were extra-cerebellar, predominantly in midline locations.

**Detail:** N546K alters FGFR1 auto-phosphorylation, increasing kinase activity and transforming potential; K656E is also transforming in vitro. Phospho-FGFR1 IHC showed strong diffuse positivity in all 7 *FGFR1*-mutant cases and no positivity in 11 WT tumors. All samples showed strong phospho-ERK1/2 staining, confirming downstream MAPK pathway activation. *FGF2* (bFGF), the primary FGFR1 ligand, was significantly overexpressed in pilocytic astrocytomas vs. other astrocytic tumors (P < 0.001), independent of *FGFR1* mutation status — suggesting ligand-mediated FGFR1 activation as a broader oncogenic mechanism. Mutations at homologous codons in *FGFR2* and *FGFR3* are common in bladder, skin, and endometrial cancers, underscoring the functional relevance of these kinase domain hotspots. The *FGFR1* ITD (case ICGC_PA89) duplicated the entire kinase domain (~4.5 kb), analogous to activating FLT3-ITDs in AML.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR1`, `FGF2` · pilocytic astrocytoma (non-cerebellar / midline subset)

### F3: Novel NTRK2 fusions (QKI:NTRK2, NACC2:NTRK2) define a new oncogenic mechanism in non-cerebellar pilocytic astrocytomas

**Finding:** Two novel *NTRK2* fusion gene types were identified in 3 samples (2 with *QKI:NTRK2* and 1 with *NACC2:NTRK2*), all in non-cerebellar tumors lacking *KIAA1549:BRAF* or other known MAPK alterations. Both fusions involve 5′ partners with dimerization domains predicted to drive ligand-independent TrkB dimerization and MAPK activation.

**Detail:** QKI (Quaking, chromosome 6) contains a QUA1 dimerization domain; NACC2 (chromosome 9) also contains dimerization-capable domains. N-terminal TrkB truncation has been shown to induce transformation of neural crest cells. NTRK2 (TrkB) activates the MAPK pathway as one of its downstream effectors, explaining its functional equivalence to other pathway hits in pilocytic astrocytoma. *NTRK1* and *NTRK3* have established fusion oncogene roles in other tumor types (*TPM3:NTRK1* in papillary thyroid cancer; *ETV6:NTRK3* in secretory breast cancer and congenital fibrosarcoma). Fusions were confirmed by RT-PCR and Sanger sequencing. *NTRK2* fusions are now clinically actionable targets for larotrectinib and entrectinib (pan-TRK inhibitors, FDA-approved for TRK fusion-positive solid tumors).

**Genes / cancer types:** `NTRK2`, `QKI`, `NACC2` · pilocytic astrocytoma (non-cerebellar subset)

### F4: Novel BRAF fusions and a BRAF kinase insertion (insVLR) expand the spectrum of BRAF activation beyond KIAA1549

**Finding:** Four novel *BRAF* fusion types were identified: *RNF130:BRAF* (2 cases from a reciprocal t(5;7) translocation), *CLCN6:BRAF*, *MKRN1:BRAF*, and *GNAI1:BRAF* (1 case each). An additional novel *BRAF* insertion — p.R506_insVLR — was identified in one tumor and demonstrated equivalent ERK activation to BRAF V600E by immunoblot and confirmed homodimerization by co-immunoprecipitation.

**Detail:** All novel BRAF fusions result in loss of the N-terminal auto-inhibitory region of BRAF, the same mechanism underlying *KIAA1549:BRAF* activation. BRAF is therefore a highly promiscuous fusion partner in pilocytic astrocytoma. The *BRAF* insVLR mutation introduces three residues (Val-Leu-Arg) into the interdomain cleft (between Arg506 and Lys507) — a region linked to BRAF activity and homodimerization. Protein modeling predicted that the insertion stabilizes active BRAF homodimers. Experimentally, insVLR increased ERK1/2 phosphorylation to the same level as V600E in NIH3T3 cells, confirmed by Western blot. These data suggest that preclinical evidence of BRAF inhibitor paradoxical activation (via fusion-mediated dimerization) applies broadly to non-V600E BRAF events — a critical consideration for treatment planning.

### F5: FGFR1-mutant pilocytic astrocytomas share a molecular signature with a subset of pediatric GBM carrying H3F3A K27M and NF1 loss

**Finding:** One *FGFR1*-mutant pilocytic astrocytoma (ICGC_PA69) co-harbored *H3F3A* K27M and somatic *NF1* alterations. Re-analysis of prior pediatric GBM exome data identified 3/48 GBM samples (6%) with the same triple-mutation constellation (*FGFR1* activation + *H3F3A* K27M + *NF1* loss), all *TP53* wild-type — defining a molecularly distinct subset.

**Detail:** H3F3A K27M mutations are a defining feature of diffuse intrinsic pontine glioma (DIPG) and pediatric GBM — tumors with uniformly poor prognosis. The identification of *FGFR1* activation in a subset of H3F3A K27M-mutant tumors (3% of GBM) provides a therapeutic opportunity: FGFR inhibitors may be active where *TP53*-dependent resistance mechanisms are absent (K27M GBM with TP53 mutations is the dominant co-occurring event, but these triple-mutant cases are TP53 WT). PTPN11 (SHP-2) hotspot mutations (E69K, E76A — identical to Noonan syndrome mutations) were found in 2 *FGFR1*-mutant PAs, suggesting cooperative RAS/MAPK pathway amplification. SHP-2 activation inhibits RasGAP recruitment, effectively mimicking NF1 loss, potentially accelerating FGFR1-driven tumorigenesis.

**Genes / cancer types:** `FGFR1`, `H3F3A`, `NF1`, `PTPN11` · pilocytic astrocytoma; pediatric GBM (H3K27M subset)

**Data availability:** `available`

## Open Questions

- Do *FGFR1* N546K- or K656E-mutant pilocytic astrocytomas — particularly surgically inaccessible midline tumors — respond to erdafitinib, pemigatinib, or other FGFR inhibitors in clinical trials, and does co-occurring *PTPN11* mutation modify response?
- Should all *NTRK2* fusion-positive pilocytic astrocytomas receive larotrectinib or entrectinib as first-line treatment instead of or before radiation, given their FDA-approved status for TRK fusion-positive solid tumors?
- What determines whether *FGFR1* mutation drives pilocytic astrocytoma vs. pediatric GBM — is it the presence of additional *H3F3A* K27M and *NF1* hits, the cell of origin, or tumor microenvironment?
- Can BRAF inhibitor paradoxical ERK activation (via dimerized fusion BRAF) be overcome by MEK inhibitors (trametinib) or second-generation RAF inhibitors (naporafenib, lifirafenib) in *KIAA1549:BRAF*-fusion PAs?
- Is the *FGFR1*-ITD (kinase domain tandem duplication) mechanistically equivalent to point mutations N546K/K656E, and does it confer similar sensitivity or resistance to FGFR inhibitors?

## Limitations

- WGS cohort (n=96) had low statistical power to detect mutations in genes altered at <5% frequency, given the very low background mutation rate; some true drivers may be missed.
- NF1-associated tumors (optic pathway gliomas) are underrepresented (~3%) because tissue from these surgically managed tumors is rarely available for research.
- NTRK2 fusions identified in only 3 cases — too rare for robust frequency estimation or survival association.
- Cross-tumor comparison with pediatric GBM is based on re-analysis of a separate exome sequencing dataset (Schwartzentruber et al.); formal integrated analysis not performed in this study.
- RNA-seq was not available for all 96 tumors (73/96), potentially underestimating fusion frequency in the non-RNA-seq subset.
- The clinical outcome data for individual patients are not comprehensively reported; long-term survival impact of specific MAPK pathway alterations (other than BRAF V600E) remains to be determined.
