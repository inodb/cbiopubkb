## Summary

This study presents the first comprehensive molecular characterization of 62 high-grade unclassified renal cell carcinomas (uRCC) — tumors that cannot be assigned to any established RCC histological subtype and currently have no standard therapy. Using MSK-IMPACT targeted sequencing (230 genes), RNA-seq, SNP arrays, FISH, and immunohistochemistry, the authors identify recurrent somatic mutations in 29 genes and define four molecularly distinct subsets accounting for 76% of the cohort: (1) NF2 loss with dysregulated Hippo-YAP signaling (26%, worst survival); (2) mTORC1 hyperactivation via MTOR/TSC1/TSC2/PTEN mutations (21%, best survival); (3) FH-deficient tumors (6%, HLRCC spectrum); and (4) ALK translocation (2%). An additional 21% carry chromatin/DNA damage regulator mutations. The NF2 loss subset is entirely novel in RCC and defines a biologically aggressive entity with potential therapeutic targets via YAP inhibition or WEE1 inhibition in SETD2-mutant/H3K36me3-deficient cases.

## Key Findings

### F1: NF2 loss defines a 26% subset of uRCC with dysregulated Hippo-YAP signaling and aggressive outcome

**Finding:** NF2 mutations (18%) combined with 22q12 copy-number loss identify a subset of 16 tumors (26%) characterized by biallelic NF2 inactivation (69% have concurrent NF2 mutation + LOH), nuclear YAP/TAZ accumulation, and active YAP transcriptional programs confirmed by GSEA. This subset has significantly worse cancer-specific survival than other uRCC groups. Concurrent SETD2 mutation and 3p loss are enriched in this subset (44% SETD2 vs. 9% in non-NF2 cases, p=0.004).

**Detail:** NF2 loss in RCC is a novel finding — NF2 mutations are essentially absent in ccRCC (0–1%), pRCC (0–6%), and chRCC (0%). In NF2 loss uRCC, YAP/TAZ nuclear staining is significantly stronger and phospho-YAP is lower (indicative of active, unphosphorylated YAP). YAP1 knockdown in NF2-deficient cell lines (ACHN, LB996-RCC) reduces S/G2M proliferating cells and colony formation. 1p and 3p losses co-occur in >50% of NF2 loss cases; SETD2 mutation leads to complete loss of H3K36me3 in all 7 NF2-loss/SETD2-mutant cases tested. NF2 loss uRCC does not overlap histologically with pRCC type 2 or collecting duct RCC.

### F2: mTORC1-hyperactive uRCC (21%) shows mutually exclusive pathway activation and better clinical outcome

**Finding:** Mutations in MTOR, TSC1, TSC2, and PTEN occur mutually exclusively in 16 (26% by mutation frequency, 21% by confirmed mTORC1 hyperactivation via IHC) uRCC cases. 13 of 16 demonstrate high p-4EBP1 and p-S6 by IHC confirming functional mTORC1 activation. This subset has the best clinical outcome among uRCC subgroups and is associated with potential sensitivity to mTOR inhibitors.

**Detail:** Novel MTOR mutations L2427R (recurs 3 times) and V2475M were discovered. L2427R was confirmed hyperactive by functional S6K phosphorylation assay in 293T cells; V2475M showed baseline activity (likely passenger). All 7 TSC1/TSC2-mutant tumors showed high p-4EBP1 (H-score=300). The mTORC1 subset is mutually exclusive with the NF2 loss subset. Despite mTORC1 hyperactivity, NF2 loss tumors do not show mTORC1 activation (consistent with organ-specific context dependence of Hippo-mTOR signaling). This subset resembles ccRCC patients who achieve long-term benefit from mTOR inhibitor therapy.

### F3: FH-deficient uRCC reveals germline HLRCC cases and a somatic FH deletion mechanism

**Finding:** Four tumors (6%) are FH-deficient by 2SC positivity/FH IHC loss. Three harbor germline FH mutations (HLRCC), and one harbors somatic homozygous FH deletion — establishing a somatic mechanism of FH inactivation that may phenocopy HLRCC. FH-deficient tumors have the worst clinical outcome in the entire cohort, similar to known HLRCC behavior.

**Detail:** A single FH somatic missense (G401V, in tumor T71) was FH IHC positive/2SC negative and lacked HLRCC histological features, suggesting it is a passenger. The three proven germline HLRCC cases demonstrate that uRCC pathological classification encompasses HLRCC — emphasizing that genetic counseling should be offered when FH deficiency is suspected. FH and 2SC IHC are insufficient alone to distinguish somatic FH inactivation from true HLRCC, requiring germline testing. Only somatic homozygous FH deletion (not point mutation) phenocopied HLRCC in this series.

### F4: Chromatin modifier mutations recur across uRCC independently of NF2 and mTOR subsets

**Finding:** 29 recurrently mutated genes were identified with NF2 (18%), SETD2 (18%), and BAP1 (13%) as the three most frequent. Outside the NF2 loss subset, chromatin modulation genes (SETD2, BAP1, KMT2A/C/D, PBRM1) and DNA damage response genes (TP53, CHEK2, BRCA2) are mutated in 21% of tumors collectively. VHL mutations — the hallmark of ccRCC — were found in only 1 of 62 uRCC, confirming distinct biology.

**Detail:** Epigenetic regulator mutations include KMT2C (10%), KMT2D (5%), ATRX (7%), DNMT3A (5%), SMARCB1 (5%). TP53, NOTCH2, and KLF6 are mutated at 5% each. PBRM1, SETD2, and BAP1 recur in uRCC despite absent VHL — consistent with their role in chromatin regulation being VHL-independent. These chromatin mutations may be actionable (e.g., SETD2 loss creates H3K36me3-deficient synthetic lethality with WEE1 inhibition). Rare MET (H1094Y) and BRAF (Y472C) mutations were also found and provide patient-level targetable options.

### F5: Differential clinical outcomes by molecular subset support biology-informed prognosis and therapy selection

**Finding:** The four molecular subsets display distinct cancer-specific survival (CSS) trajectories: FH-deficient (worst) > NF2 loss (poor) > chromatin/DNA-damage (intermediate) > mTORC1 hyperactive ≈ uncharacterized (better). These associations held despite the small cohort (n=62) and suggest that molecular stratification should guide clinical management decisions for this rare cancer.

**Detail:** Overall, 42% (n=26) of patients developed metastatic disease and 35% (n=22) died of RCC, underscoring the aggressive natural history. SETD2 or BAP1 mutation alone did not significantly stratify outcomes within this cohort (likely underpowered). The TPM3-ALK fusion discovered in one case (T12, confirmed by FISH) represents an emerging RCC entity with potential ALK inhibitor sensitivity. Altogether, 76% of uRCC tumors were assigned to a biologically characterized subset — unprecedented for this histological category.

## Open Questions

- Can YAP/TAZ inhibitors (e.g., verteporfin, TEAD inhibitors) achieve meaningful responses in NF2 loss uRCC in clinical trials?
- Does synthetic lethality between H3K36me3 deficiency (SETD2-mutant NF2 loss cases) and WEE1 inhibition translate clinically?
- What drives uRCC tumorigenesis in the 24% of tumors without any identified molecular feature? Are they truly "uncharacterized" or do they harbor epigenetic or noncoding alterations not captured by IMPACT?
- Do germline FH-deficient cases and somatic FH-deletion cases truly have equivalent clinical behavior, or does somatic FH loss present with a different natural history?
- Is the mTORC1 hyperactive uRCC subset as sensitive to mTOR inhibitors as long-term responder ccRCC patients with similar mutations?

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=62); survival analyses lack statistical power for subgroup-specific conclusions.
- Only 7 tumors had RNA-seq, limiting transcriptomic validation to a subset.
- MSK-IMPACT v1 (230 genes) misses alterations in non-panel genes; whole-exome or whole-genome data not available for most cases.
- No matched normal germline sequencing for most cases (only T02 used unmatched normal), limiting systematic germline analysis.
- Primarily retrospective MSKCC cohort; representation of non-NCI-designated cancer center patients with limited access to genomic profiling is absent.
