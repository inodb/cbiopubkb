## Summary

Single-center retrospective study (MSK, 2023) characterizing the genomic landscape of advanced hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) via targeted NGS of circulating cell-free DNA (cfDNA) using the 129-gene MSK-ACCESS assay in 51 histologically confirmed patients, with paired tumor (MSK-IMPACT) data in 37. Genomic alterations were detected in 92.2% of cfDNA samples. Mutation frequencies in plasma closely matched published tissue-based HCC cohorts for all major drivers. Plasma-tissue concordance was substantial to near-perfect for the most common genes (TERT, TP53, CTNNB1, ARID1A). Actionable mutations were found in 37% of patients, and cfDNA-exclusive alterations were found in 27% of paired cases—often actionable. Higher variant allele fraction (VAF) correlated with tumor burden (AFP ≥400, liver tumor volume) but not with overall survival in treatment-naïve patients.

## Key Findings

### F1: cfDNA detects tumor-associated alterations in 92.2% of advanced HCC patients, mirroring tissue-based frequency

**Finding:** NGS of cfDNA using MSK-ACCESS detected genomic alterations in 92.2% of histologically confirmed advanced HCC patients. The most frequently mutated genes in plasma were *TERT* promoter (57%), *TP53* (47%), *CTNNB1* (37%), *ARID1A* (18%), and *TSC2* (14%). When compared with a separate published cohort of tissue-sequenced HCC (MSK-IMPACT, n=121), mutation frequencies in plasma were statistically indistinguishable for TERT, CTNNB1, ARID1A, and TSC2. TP53 was enriched in the cfDNA cohort (51% vs 32%, P=0.024), possibly reflecting higher tumor shedding in TP53-mutant advanced disease.

**Detail:** The high detection rate likely reflects the advanced disease stage (74% AJCC stage IV), the sensitivity of the MSK-ACCESS assay (dual barcodes, error suppression), and the known elevated cfDNA shedding rates in HCC. The most common oncogenic pathways affected were WNT-β-catenin (45%) and PIK3-AKT-mTOR (25%). No samples showed evidence of microsatellite instability. The four patients with no detected cfDNA alterations had low AFP (<400 ng/mL), low total tumor volume, or had received systemic therapy (including one complete radiologic responder).

### F2: Plasma-tissue concordance is substantial to near-perfect for major HCC driver genes

**Finding:** Among 37 patients with paired tumor and cfDNA data, plasma cfDNA detected 92.5% of alterations previously identified in tissue. Gene-level concordance (kappa) was: CTNNB1 (κ=0.83, near-perfect), ARID1A (κ=0.91, near-perfect), TP53 (κ=0.74, substantial), TERT (κ=0.67, substantial), TSC2 (κ=0.65, substantial), PIK3CA (κ=0.36, fair). The fair agreement for PIK3CA likely reflects lower allele frequency or spatial heterogeneity.

**Detail:** Median interval between tissue and cfDNA sampling was 9.8 months (range 0.1–70.6), introducing temporal heterogeneity as a potential source of discordance. In 10/37 (27%) paired cases, cfDNA identified alterations not found in the matched tumor biopsy—consistent with spatial heterogeneity and the liquid biopsy's ability to sample multiple tumor clones simultaneously. These cfDNA-exclusive alterations included diverse driver events across pathways. Findings confirm that cfDNA and tissue sequencing are complementary, and both may be needed for comprehensive profiling.

### F3: Actionable mutations detected in 37% of patients, including cfDNA-exclusive alterations

**Finding:** Clinically actionable mutations (OncoKB-annotated) were identified in 37% of all patients via cfDNA, including *TSC1/2* (18%), *BRCA1/2* (8%), and *PIK3CA* (8%). Of the 10 patients where alterations were detected exclusively in cfDNA (not in matched tissue), 4/10 (40%) had OncoKB level 3b actionable alterations. Two patients received everolimus based on MTOR-AKT pathway alterations identified initially in tissue and confirmed in cfDNA; one had stable disease (5.4 months), one had progressive disease (2.5 months).

**Detail:** Level 3b OncoKB evidence indicates clinical evidence in a different cancer type. TSC2 alterations may sensitize to mTOR inhibitors (everolimus/sirolimus); BRCA1/2 mutations to PARP inhibitors; PIK3CA mutations to PI3K inhibitors. The finding that 40% of cfDNA-exclusive alterations were actionable underscores the clinical importance of complementary liquid biopsy profiling in HCC, where tissue biopsies are technically challenging. Active clinical trials for TSC2 (NCT05103358) and CTNNB1 (NCT04008797, NCT05091346) were noted.

### F4: Higher cfDNA VAF correlates with tumor burden (AFP, tumor volume) and treatment-naïve status

**Finding:** In multivariable analysis, higher average VAF was independently associated with AFP ≥400 ng/mL (β=0.43, P=0.007), greater liver tumor volume (β=0.14, P<0.001), and absence of prior systemic therapy (β=−0.52, P<0.001). VAF did not significantly correlate with overall survival in the 24 treatment-naïve patients (HR 1.18, 95% CI 0.78–1.80, P=0.43).

**Detail:** These findings establish cfDNA VAF as a surrogate of tumor burden in HCC, consistent with reports in other solid tumors. The negative correlation with prior therapy suggests either tumor debulking or reduced shedding from treated lesions. The lack of OS correlation in this small treatment-naïve cohort (n=24) does not exclude prognostic utility but highlights the need for larger prospective studies. Patients post-complete radiologic response to immunotherapy showed undetectable cfDNA, reinforcing the potential for cfDNA-based response monitoring.

### F5: cfDNA NGS is a viable alternative to tissue biopsy in HCC where tissue acquisition is infeasible

**Finding:** HCC is unique in that diagnosis may be made by imaging alone (without tissue), and biopsies are complicated by co-occurring cirrhosis. This study establishes MSK-ACCESS–based cfDNA profiling as a clinically valid alternative to tissue genotyping in advanced HCC, with high detection rates and strong concordance for key driver genes. The study provides the first systematic assessment of MSK-ACCESS in a histologically confirmed, paired-sample HCC cohort.

**Detail:** Prior cfDNA studies in HCC were limited by heterogeneous populations, lack of histologic confirmation, or absence of paired tissue. This study addresses all three limitations. The 92.2% detection rate is notably higher than other solid tumors, attributed to advanced disease stage, HCC biology (high cfDNA shedding), and assay sensitivity. In contexts where tissue is unavailable (common in cirrhotic patients), cfDNA can guide enrollment in molecularly targeted clinical trials. The recommendation is to combine blood and tumor sequencing where feasible to capture the full spectrum of actionable alterations.

## Open Questions

- Does cfDNA VAF have prognostic significance in larger prospective HCC cohorts (including early-stage or post-transplant patients)?
- Can serial cfDNA monitoring during systemic therapy (atezolizumab+bevacizumab, sorafenib) reliably detect early progression or acquired resistance in HCC?
- What is the minimum tumor volume or AFP level required for reliable cfDNA detection in HCC?
- Do cfDNA-exclusive alterations (detected in plasma but not tissue) represent spatial heterogeneity, circulating clones, or clonal hematopoiesis?
- Would broader-panel or WGS cfDNA improve actionability rate or concordance in HCC?
- Can CTNNB1 or TSC2 alterations detected in cfDNA predict response to matched targeted therapies in ongoing basket trials?

## Limitations

- Single-center retrospective study with potential selection bias; all patients from MSKCC
- Small sample size (n=51 cfDNA, n=37 paired); underpowered for survival analyses and rare alteration discovery
- Heterogeneous timing of cfDNA vs tissue collection (median 9.8 months); temporal evolution may confound concordance estimates
- MSK-ACCESS panel covers 129 genes; does not capture FGFR4, MET amplification, or structural variants routinely detectable by broader platforms
- No MSI detected, consistent with HCC biology, but the assay was not designed with full MSI evaluation
- OncoKB annotation reflects knowledge at time of study; actionability classifications evolve as new evidence emerges
- cfDNA yield not correlated with VAF, suggesting yield alone is not a reliable proxy for tumor shedding
- Outcome data for matched targeted treatments limited to 2 patients treated with everolimus
