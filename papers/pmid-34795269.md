## Summary

Mechanistic study establishing MAPK pathway activation as a recurrent and clinically relevant mechanism of resistance to anti-HER2 therapy in HER2-amplified breast cancer. Genomic profiling of 733 *ERBB2*-amplified breast tumors (primary and metastatic) revealed enrichment of MAPK pathway activating mutations (*NF1* loss, *ERBB2* activating point mutations, *KRAS*/*BRAF* activating mutations) in metastatic versus primary tumors (p=0.020), and in those with shorter PFS on first-line anti-HER2 therapy. Patients without MAPK alterations had a median first-line PFS of 21 months versus 9.9 months in MAPK-altered patients (HR 2.03; p=0.011). Mechanistically, MAPK activation via *NF1* loss uncouples MAPK signaling from HER2-mediated control, driving a pathway-dependence switch from PI3K/AKT to MEK/ERK. MAPK-activated, HER2i-resistant cells lose AKT dependence but are dramatically sensitized to MEK/ERK inhibition — validated in xenograft and PDX models — establishing MEK/ERK inhibition as a rational therapeutic strategy for this resistance subgroup.

## Key Findings

### F1: MAPK pathway alterations are enriched in metastatic vs. primary HER2+ breast tumors and predict worse PFS on anti-HER2 therapy

**Finding:** MAPK pathway activating mutations (NF1 loss, ERBB2 activating mutations, KRAS/BRAF mutations) were significantly enriched in metastatic versus primary HER2-amplified breast tumors (p=0.020), while PI3K/AKT pathway enrichment in metastatic samples did not reach significance (p=0.07). In 145 patients sequenced prior to first-line anti-HER2 therapy, MAPK-altered tumors had a median PFS of 9.9 months (95% CI 5.5–17) versus 21 months (95% CI 17–30) in MAPK-WT tumors (HR 2.03; 95% CI 1.18–3.51; multivariate p=0.011).

**Detail:** Among 733 ERBB2-amplified tumors: PIK3CA mutations were present in 30%, PTEN loss in 2.6%, and AKT1 mutations in 0.3%. NF1 loss was detected in 8% of metastatic tumors; ERBB2 activating point mutations (co-occurring with amplification) in 7% of metastatic tumors — consistent with selection for MAPK activation during treatment. The PFS association of MAPK alterations was independent of PIK3CA/AKT1/PTEN co-mutations (HR 2.25; 95% CI 1.29–3.93; adjusted p=0.0043), establishing MAPK activation as an independent resistance predictor. No mutual exclusivity or co-occurrence between PI3K/AKT and MAPK pathways was observed (p=0.42), implying the two pathways can co-occur without functional interaction. A paired case study showed acquired NF1 intragenic inversion (extending 27 Mb, bisecting NF1 RAS-GTPase domain) in a progressing lymph node but not in the pre-treatment primary tumor, providing direct in vivo evidence of MAPK pathway acquisition under selective pressure.

**Genes / cancer types:** NF1, ERBB2, KRAS, BRAF, PIK3CA, PTEN, AKT1 · HER2-amplified breast carcinoma

### F2: *NF1* loss is sufficient to confer resistance to FDA-approved HER2 kinase inhibitors (lapatinib, neratinib, tucatinib)

**Finding:** shRNA- or CRISPR/Cas9-mediated NF1 depletion in four HER2+ breast cancer cell lines (SKBR3, MDA-MB-361, BT-474, HCC1954) conferred resistance to all three FDA-approved HER2 kinase inhibitors — lapatinib, neratinib, and tucatinib — in both metabolic and colony formation assays, with resistance evident as early as 3–5 days and more prominent at >2 weeks of treatment.

**Detail:** Two independent shRNA sequences and CRISPR knockout yielded consistent resistance phenotypes, ruling out off-target effects. NF1-deficient cells showed marginally elevated baseline RAS-GTP and phospho-MEK/ERK. Upon HER2 inhibitor treatment, HER2 phosphorylation was equivalently suppressed in shNF1 and shRenilla controls (confirming equivalent target engagement), but phospho-MEK and phospho-ERK failed to be potently or durably suppressed in NF1-deficient cells — indicating that NF1 loss renders MAPK signaling HER2-independent. Re-expression of NF1 via doxycycline-inducible vector in HER2i-resistant shNF1 cells restored HER2 inhibitor sensitivity, confirming on-target resistance mechanism. Parallel testing of HER2 L755S, BRAF V600E, and KRAS G12V overexpression in SKBR3 cells all increased proliferation under lapatinib/tucatinib treatment, demonstrating that multiple MAPK-activating mechanisms converge on the same resistance phenotype.

### F3: MAPK activation drives a pathway-dependence switch from PI3K/AKT to MEK/ERK, sensitizing cells to MEK/ERK inhibitors

**Finding:** HER2i-resistant NF1-deficient SKBR3 cells underwent a dramatic switch in pathway dependence: AKT inhibitor sensitivity was markedly reduced (MK2206 IC₅₀ shift: 197 → 4,475 nM), while MEK inhibitor sensitivity was markedly increased (trametinib IC₅₀: 738 → 13 nM; ERK inhibitor IC₅₀: 2,851 → 250 nM). This MEK sensitization was equivalently observed in KRAS G12V- and HER2 L755S-expressing SKBR3 cells, confirming pathway-level convergence.

**Detail:** In HER2+ parental cells, PI3K/AKT is the dominant effector pathway (established in the field), and MEK/ERK inhibition has minimal single-agent efficacy. MAPK activation via NF1 loss entrains CDK2 kinase activity in a MEK-dependent manner (mechanistic link to cell cycle deregulation). SHP2 inhibition was insufficient to block phospho-MEK/ERK in NF1-deficient cells, indicating that MAPK activation occurs downstream of SHP2. In vivo validation in BT-474 shNF1 HER2i-resistant xenografts showed significant 37% growth inhibition with trametinib monotherapy (not observed in shRenilla controls). An HER2+/NF1-null patient-derived xenograft (from trastuzumab-refractory patient) was unresponsive to trastuzumab alone but showed significant tumor control with trametinib and combination regimens, directly translating the cell line findings to a clinical-grade PDX model.

### F4: CDK2 activation is the mechanistic link between MAPK activation and cell cycle deregulation in anti-HER2-resistant cells

**Finding:** MEK-dependent activation of CDK2 kinase is the key downstream effector translating MAPK pathway activation into anti-HER2 therapy resistance. In HER2+ cells, AKT signaling entrains cell cycle regulators (p27, cyclin D1); upon MAPK pathway dominance (NF1 loss), MEK-driven CDK2 activation bypasses this AKT-dependent cell cycle control, allowing continued proliferation despite HER2/AKT pathway suppression.

**Detail:** CDK2 activation was confirmed mechanistically as MEK-dependent in NF1-deficient resistant cells, providing a molecular explanation for why MEK inhibition (rather than downstream CDK2 inhibition alone) effectively blocks proliferation. This is distinct from previously described resistance mechanisms (PIK3CA mutation, PTEN loss, ERBB3 mutation, cyclin E amplification, CDK4/CDK6 amplification) and establishes a new molecular class of HER2 therapy resistance. The CDK2 link further suggests that CDK2 inhibitors, or MEK+CDK2 combinations, could be therapeutically relevant — though this was not directly tested in vivo in this study.

### F5: ERBB2 activating point mutations (7% of metastatic tumors) co-occur with ERBB2 amplification and mediate resistance

**Finding:** Among 733 ERBB2-amplified tumors, ERBB2 activating point mutations (e.g., L755S, V777L) were identified in 7% of metastatic tumors — more than twice the rate in primary tumors — and were functionally validated to confer resistance to HER2 kinase inhibitors by the same MAPK-activation mechanism as NF1 loss. These co-occurring ERBB2 mutations activate MAPK signaling independently of (and in addition to) the ERBB2 amplification.

**Detail:** The enrichment of ERBB2 activating mutations at metastatic sites supports selection under anti-HER2 therapy pressure. In cell line experiments, exogenous HER2 L755S expression in SKBR3 cells conferred resistance to lapatinib and tucatinib — despite the background of endogenous ERBB2 amplification — confirming that the point mutation contributes MAPK-activating function beyond amplification-driven HER2 overexpression. This finding implies that sequencing of metastatic biopsies should include assessment not only for resistance mutations in downstream pathways (PIK3CA, NF1) but also for activating ERBB2 point mutations as co-resistance mechanisms.

## Open Questions

- Do MEK inhibitors (e.g., trametinib, cobimetinib) combined with anti-HER2 therapy improve outcomes specifically in MAPK-altered (NF1 loss, ERBB2 point mutations) HER2+ breast cancers in the clinic?
- Can serial ctDNA or liquid biopsy monitoring detect MAPK pathway mutation emergence before clinical progression and guide preemptive therapy switch?
- Is the MAPK switch in driver pathway reversible — do NF1-null, HER2i-resistant tumors revert to PI3K/AKT dependence if MEK pressure is applied and HER2 signaling is then re-engaged?
- Do CDK2 inhibitors synergize with MEK inhibitors in MAPK-activated, HER2i-resistant cells, given MEK-dependent CDK2 activation as the mechanistic link?
- Does the MAPK resistance mechanism extend to antibody-drug conjugates (trastuzumab deruxtecan, T-DM1), which do not rely solely on HER2 kinase inhibition?

## Limitations

- PFS analysis restricted to 145 patients with pre-treatment genomic profiling; remaining patients were sequenced on metastatic tissue at variable timepoints, limiting causal interpretation
- Cell line models (SKBR3, BT-474, MDA-MB-361, HCC1954) may not fully represent the heterogeneity of clinical HER2+ metastatic breast cancer
- Only one NF1-null PDX model tested in vivo; broader PDX panel needed to generalize MEKi sensitivity findings
- The study does not evaluate combination anti-HER2 + MEKi regimens in the clinical cohort; efficacy predictions are preclinical only
- Long-term durability of MEKi response in MAPK-activated HER2i-resistant cells was not assessed
